The Khamenei Directive

Two senior Iranian officials, speaking on Thursday evening to Reuters and to Haaretz on condition of anonymity, confirmed that the office of the Supreme Leader Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei has issued a binding internal directive: Iran’s stockpile of near-weapons-grade enriched uranium — the principal currency of the past six weeks of Pakistan-mediated nuclear talks — must remain inside the country, and cannot be transferred to a third state under any negotiated framework. The directive is the explicit institutional rejection of the load-bearing US demand inside the talks the Trump administration said on Tuesday had entered their “final stages.” It arrives in the same forty-eight-hour cadence as a Tehran-Muscat float of a permanent Strait of Hormuz toll framework the White House has already publicly rejected and as Brent crude holding above $104 a barrel in Asia trading on Friday morning. Foreign minister Abbas Araghchi, speaking to Iranian state television the same evening, said Tehran “cannot trust the Americans at all,” and that the “shaky” ceasefire is being preserved “to give diplomacy a chance.” The Stratagem reads the directive as the BATNA-fixing move — the deliberate, public narrowing of the negotiable space, designed to force the framework back onto the only currency the Iranian establishment can absorb: sanctions relief and security guarantees against the verified, on-soil cap of enrichment levels, not the surrender of the stockpile itself. The directive is the position. The position is the demand the talks must now compose themselves around.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Tehran & Washington·22 May 2026

Tehran —A directive issued by the office of a Supreme Leader does not normally leak. When it does, the leak is the directive. Two senior Iranian officials, in separate conversations to Reuters and to Haaretz on Thursday evening, used identical formulations: “the consensus within the establishment is that the stockpile of enriched uranium should not leave the country.” The repetition is not accidental. The line is the institutional position of the Iranian state, composed in the same forty-eight-hour cadence in which Trump told reporters the talks had entered their “final stages” and in which Iran-and-Oman floated a permanent Hormuz toll the White House had already rejected. The directive is not a negotiating leak. It is a negotiating fix.

Chris Voss’s work on the BATNA — the best alternative to a negotiated agreement — is the practitioner reading the Tehran directive earns. The single move that most reliably reshapes a negotiation is not a concession or a threat but a public, costly, irreversible narrowing of the negotiator’s own walk-away alternative. Khamenei’s office has now done exactly that on the question that has organised six weeks of Pakistan-mediated talks. The stockpile-surrender demand the US negotiating cohort was institutionally pricing as the load-bearing concession — the visible deliverable that would let the Trump administration carry a deal into the autumn cycle — has been publicly removed from the table by the only Iranian principal whose word can remove it. The talks can continue. The frame in which they continue is now the Iranian frame: verified, on-soil enrichment caps inside an IAEA architecture, in exchange for sanctions relief and security guarantees against further Israeli or US strikes. Anything else is no deal. The BATNA the directive composes is “no deal,” and the directive’s function is to make “no deal” institutionally cheaper to Tehran than to Washington.

“Sending the material abroad would leave the country more vulnerable to future attacks by the United States and Israel.” The sentence the senior source gave Haaretz is the structural reading on its own face. The Iranian establishment has watched, in compressed sequence, the 2003 Libya disarmament cycle end in a 2011 cruise-missile cycle that ended in Gaddafi’s execution in a roadside ditch; the 2015 JCPOA end in a 2018 abrogation that ended in 2026 with US air-strikes inside Iranian territory; and the past six weeks of US blockade architecture compose itself around an enrichment programme that has, structurally, deterred precisely the larger escalation the US blockade was designed to compel. The stockpile is not a chip Tehran trades for sanctions relief. The stockpile is the deterrent the past nine months proved was load-bearing. Mearsheimer’s reading on the structural logic of nuclear hedging by regional powers under hegemonic pressure is the analytic backbone: the only states that have surrendered nuclear material under inspection regimes are states whose regimes were subsequently changed. The directive is the Iranian establishment’s reading of its own historical record.

The BATNA as red line.

Sun Tzu on the discipline of the constructed position — let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt — is the inverse reading on the choice to make the directive public through senior-source leaks rather than to carry it inside the talks as a private constraint. A private constraint can be renegotiated inside the room. A public constraint cannot be renegotiated without the principal absorbing the reputational cost of publicly walking back the position. By leaking the directive through Reuters and Haaretz simultaneously on the same Thursday evening, the office of the Supreme Leader has institutionally locked the position inside the architecture of the Iranian state itself. Araghchi, Pezeshkian, the negotiating delegation in Islamabad — none of them can now offer the stockpile without producing the institutional fracture inside the Iranian state the directive’s public composition was designed to prevent. The discipline is the discipline of a principal who knows that the strongest position in a negotiation is the position the principal cannot, structurally, walk back.

Robert Greene’s Twenty-Second Law — use the surrender tactic: transform weakness into power — is the closing tactical reading on the Khamenei posture. Iran cannot win an extended air war with the US-Israeli coalition; the past nine months have institutionally proved that. The Iranian conventional military, the IRGC, and the proxy architecture have each, in measurable terms, been compressed inside the post-March cycle. The directive accepts the structural compression and trades it for the harder symbolic position. By publicly conceding nothing — by accepting no transfer of the stockpile, no abandonment of enrichment, no on-paper renunciation — Tehran composes the position from which the only deal that can be reached is the deal the Trump administration can publicly claim as a deliverable inside the autumn cycle, on terms that institutionally preserve the Iranian deterrent. The surrender is the refusal to surrender the chip. The power accrues to the principal who has nothing left to give and refuses to give it.

The final-stages trap.

La Rochefoucauld’s maxim that “we promise according to our hopes and perform according to our fears” is the practitioner reading on the Trump administration’s public “final stages” framing of the talks. The framing is the hope. The directive is the fear. The administration has spent six weeks publicly pricing a deal as the load-bearing autumn deliverable; the political-coalition cost of returning from Pakistan empty-handed is now institutionally priced into the Tehran negotiating position. Iran reads the “final stages” framing as the documentary evidence that the US side has compressed its own optionality on a walk-away alternative; the directive is composed precisely against that compression. Trump can absorb the directive in three ways: accept the Iranian frame and claim the deal as a stockpile-verification regime rather than a stockpile-surrender; reject the frame and pivot publicly to military escalation, which the post-blockade oil price ($104 Brent, climbing) makes domestically expensive; or hold the public “final stages” framing while quietly accepting the Iranian terms inside the language. The third path is the path the administration’s political-coalition architecture rewards. The directive composes the trap inside which the third path is the institutionally easiest exit.

Two layers carry this story. Tactical: the directive is the BATNA-fixing move — the costly, public, irreversible narrowing of the Iranian walk-away alternative that locks the frame the talks must compose themselves around. Structural: the Iranian establishment’s reading of the 2003-Libya, 2015-JCPOA, and 2026-blockade sequence is that the stockpile is the deterrent that prevented larger US-Israeli escalation, and that surrendering it is structurally indistinguishable from surrendering regime survival. What is not driving this is a Khamenei personal preference; the directive is the institutional consensus of the establishment, leaked in identical formulation to two outlets on the same evening, and would survive any change of office inside the Supreme Leader’s building. The Stratagem reads the load-bearing tests of the next sixty days as the Trump administration’s public response (does the “final stages” framing absorb the directive or rupture against it), the IAEA verification framework (does an on-soil cap-and-inspect architecture compose itself inside the window the directive permits), the Israeli strike posture (does Tel Aviv read the directive as the institutional admission that an air strike is the only remaining instrument to remove the stockpile, or as the institutional cover for a deal that leaves the deterrent intact), and the Hormuz toll cycle (does Iran convert the toll framework into a parallel revenue architecture or absorb the White House’s rejection inside the broader negotiating frame). The leak is the position. The position is the deal. The deal is the cycle.

That single act narrows the negotiable space — the deal must now compose itself around verified, on-soil enrichment caps rather than around stockpile transfer — and shifts the political-coalition cost of “no deal” from Tehran to Washington. The structural layer carries the second half of the reading. The Iranian establishment is reading its own historical record — Libya 2003 to Tripoli 2011; JCPOA 2015 to abrogation 2018 to air-strikes 2026 — and concluding that the stockpile is the deterrent the past nine months proved load-bearing. The directive is the institutional consequence of that reading, not a personal preference of the man in the office.

What's not driving this: Khamenei’s personality or the specific identity of the Supreme Leader. The directive is the institutional consensus of the establishment and would survive any change of occupant inside the building. Reading the move as a single principal’s caprice misreads the structure.

What would break this reading:

  • Tehran agrees inside the next sixty days to transfer any portion of the near-weapons-grade stockpile to Oman, Russia, or a neutral third state — the “BATNA-is-publicly-locked” reading was wrong, the directive was a negotiating bluff that the Supreme Leader’s office walked back under sufficient pressure, and the Iranian institutional architecture is less rigid on the stockpile question than the article credited.
  • Trump publicly walks away from the talks inside the next thirty days and orders fresh strikes on Fordow or Natanz — the “Washington-absorbs-the-directive-inside-the-final-stages-framing” reading was wrong, the political-coalition cost of accepting the Iranian frame is sharper than the article credited, and the administration’s walk-away optionality on military escalation has not been institutionally compressed by the post-blockade oil price.

The committee held the federal funds target at 3.50 to 3.75 percent on an 8–4 vote; three of the four dissenters opposed the cut-implying language inside the statement, and Governor Stephen Miran — since departed — voted for a lower rate. Law of the Day · No. 22

Use the surrender tactic: transform weakness into power.

Greene’s Twenty-Second Law: when you are weaker, never fight for honour’s sake; choose surrender instead. Surrender gives you time to recover, time to torment and irritate your conqueror, time to wait for his power to wane. Do not give him the satisfaction of fighting and defeating you — surrender first. By turning the other cheek you infuriate and unsettle him. Make surrender a tool of power. The discipline is to read the cohort’s structural compression honestly, accept the field that cannot be won on its current terms, and compose the position from which the only remaining concession is institutionally impossible to give.

Today’s reading: the directive, the dissent, the anniversary. Each story turns on the cohort that has accepted its own structural compression and converted the compression into the strongest available position. In Tehran the load-bearing fact is that Iran cannot win an extended air war with the US-Israeli coalition; the Khamenei directive accepts that compression and trades it for the harder symbolic position by publicly conceding nothing — the surrender is the refusal to surrender the chip. In Washington the load-bearing fact is that the FOMC discipline-cohort cannot publicly commit to a hike inside the appointing administration’s political-coalition cycle; the four-vote dissent accepts that compression and trades it for the harder institutional position by refusing to absorb the cut-implying language — the surrender is the refusal to vote a hike. In Berlin the load-bearing fact is that the Merz cabinet cannot compose substantive reform inside the Brandmauer-imposed coalition arithmetic; Merz’s explicit rejection of minority government and snap elections accepts that compression and trades it for the procedural position that the institution holds together by the absence of the alternative — the surrender is the refusal to compose the architecture inside which the cohort would have to fight.

The Practitioner’s Note

The BATNA-fixing move.

The three stories on Friday’s table each turn on a single discipline: the cohort that has compressed its own walk-away alternative publicly and irreversibly composes the position from which the only available framework is the framework it can institutionally absorb. Khamenei’s office leaked the directive to two outlets in identical formulation on the same Thursday evening; the move is irreversible inside the Iranian state. The April-FOMC dissenters voted against the cut-implying language and did not propose a hike; the move pre-positions the chair-elect inside an architecture from which accommodation is institutionally costly. Merz told Welt this week that minority government and snap elections are both ruled out; the move publicly fixes the cabinet’s walk-away to “hold the compressed coalition through the autumn cycle.” In each case, the principal has taken the strongest position by publicly removing the alternatives. The cohort that cannot give the chip away is the cohort the negotiation is now structured around; the cohort that cannot publicly cut is the cohort whose posture composes the cycle; the cohort that cannot compose the alternative is the cohort whose chancellorship is preserved by the absence.

In your own affairs: the discipline is to read your own walk-away arithmetic against the cycle’s structural compression. Most actors preserve their optionality by holding multiple alternatives privately and choosing tactically inside the cycle; the harder discipline is to publicly remove the alternatives that compress the position you are actually willing to hold, and to let the cycle compose itself around the position that remains. A salary negotiation in which you publicly commit to a number below which you will walk away is a negotiation in which the counter-party negotiates against the number, not against you. A capital-allocation decision in which you publicly compose the criteria the allocation must meet is an allocation in which the cohort proposing the allocation has to compose against the criteria rather than against your discretion. A relationship in which you publicly fix the standards the cohort must meet is a relationship in which the cohort must compose itself against the standards rather than against your patience.

The hard question: in the contest you are holding this week, are you the cohort that has preserved every alternative inside private optionality — or the cohort that has publicly compressed its own walk-away to the position it is institutionally willing to hold? If the former, what alternative is structurally weakening your actual negotiating position by remaining publicly available? If the latter, what is the cycle now composing around the position you have already publicly fixed — and have you read what it documents?
