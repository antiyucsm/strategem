The Outsourced War

Sudanese army air-defence batteries engaged a strategic-range drone over Khartoum North and Omdurman on Tuesday evening — the third such interception in ten days, the latest beat in a campaign Khartoum’s Joint Chiefs have since May 5 attributed to a Bahir-Dar-to-Khartoum supply architecture routing Chinese-built UAVs through Ethiopian airspace. Brigadier General Asim Awad Abdelwahab presented drone serial-number evidence in a May 6 press conference; Sudan recalled its ambassador from Addis Ababa; Khartoum International was shut for three days after the May 5 strike. The United Arab Emirates has publicly denied direct military involvement even as Middle East Eye and ACLED reporting has tracked new Chinese-drone supply routes through Bahir Dar and the Central African Republic; Ethiopia has counter-charged Khartoum with materially supporting the Tigray People’s Liberation Front; the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam filling cycle and the Tigray reconstruction architecture sit underneath the drone file as the structural pressure the bilateral cannot detach from. The Stratagem reads the convergence as the coordination signature of a Gulf-Horn architecture in which a regional war is fought by drone-by-proxy, the discipline of getting others to do the work while the principals carry the public denial, and the beginning of the window in which the Khartoum air-defence tape, the Quad ceasefire framework, the GERD filling cycle, and the El Fasher-and-Kordofan ground tempo are all repriced against the same outsourced-war configuration.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Khartoum & Addis Ababa·11 June 2026

Khartoum —The detail that matters in the Tuesday-evening engagement over Omdurman is not the explosion the residents heard and it is not the SAF claim of interception. It is the serial number. For six weeks now, the Sudanese army has been able to produce in a press conference the part-number evidence on the drones it shoots down, trace the launch back to Bahir Dar airport in northern Ethiopia, name the procurement chain to Chinese manufacturers, and place the financial sponsorship inside the same Abu Dhabi architecture that ACLED and Middle East Eye have been tracking through the supply routes via the Central African Republic. Three governments are denying involvement. The forensic chain is public. This is not the fog of war; it is the opposite. It is the coordination signature so explicit that no participant any longer pretends the war is a civil one. The Greene seventh law — get others to do the work for you, but always take the credit — reads the architecture from the patron’s side. The credit here is being declined; the work is being done; the discipline applies in inversion. The patron who does the work and lets the proxy take the cost is the patron who has cracked the law in its operational form.

Begin with the structural reading the journalistic register has skipped. Sudan sits on the longest North-South seam in Africa: between the Sahel and the Horn, between the Red Sea and the Sahara, between the Arab world and sub-Saharan Africa, with the Nile carrying the agricultural and demographic mass of every state from Cairo to Kampala. The Sudanese civil war is a structural inevitability of a state whose elites have for half a century extracted rents at the seam and whose military and paramilitary architectures were built to fight over the extraction rather than over governance. Mearsheimer’s offensive realism specifies that regional powers move into the perimeter of failed neighbours when the cost of inaction exceeds the cost of intervention; the Gulf monarchies, Ethiopia, Egypt, Turkey, and Iran have all moved into Sudan in the past three years, and the United Arab Emirates has moved further than anyone else. The UAE’s investment is structural before it is political: a foothold on the Red Sea, a stake in the Nile basin’s gold-and-food-security architecture, an extension of the Yemen-Libya-Sudan logistical envelope through which the post-2017 Emirati foreign policy projects power without the cost of formal occupation. Ethiopia’s involvement is structural before it is opportunistic: the GERD has restructured the basin’s water and energy economy against the Egyptian and Sudanese downstream position, and Addis Ababa cannot allow a Khartoum government hostile to the dam to consolidate. The drone war is the modality of the structural contest. It is not the cause of it.

Sun Tzu’s reading that the line of supply decides the line of battle reads the operational register the headline tape has missed. The RSF’s sustained ground tempo across Darfur and Kordofan since the El Fasher capture in October 2025 is not the product of indigenous arms; it is the product of a sustained Chinese-drone-and-light-vehicle supply line whose physical routing through Bahir Dar, through the Central African Republic, and through the Libyan border depots has been tracked in the open ACLED dataset for two years. The SAF’s air-defence response over Khartoum is calibrated against the same physical fact: the drone that the gunners engaged on Tuesday evening had to travel roughly nine hundred kilometres from its loitering basin to reach the capital, and that travel is only possible if the launch infrastructure, the navigation architecture, and the post-launch satellite-guidance chain are continuously serviced by a state-grade logistics operation. No non-state actor sustains nine-hundred-kilometre strategic-drone operations. The signature is the signature of a state. The state that publicly denies the operation is the state that operationally runs it. Greene’s third law — conceal your intentions — sits at the diplomatic register: the UAE’s public denial is not a lie about the conduct of the war; it is the legal instrument by which Abu Dhabi preserves the optionality of the Quad ceasefire framework in which it is, with Washington, Cairo, and Riyadh, one of the official mediators. The patron mediates the war the patron is fighting. The conflict of interest is the architecture, not a flaw inside it.

What the drone war installs.

Read the immediate consequences three ways. For Khartoum, the Tuesday interception is the operational confirmation that the SAF’s air-defence layer, restored over the past nine months with Egyptian, Russian, and Turkish components, can now intercept the drone the RSF launches from a Libyan or Ethiopian depot — the regime no longer loses the symbolic capital city to a single strategic strike, and the Burhan government can sustain the central-bank, oil-revenue, and diplomatic-recognition architecture from a Khartoum that is contested but not erased. For Addis Ababa, the Sudanese ambassador recall is the diplomatic floor under which the Ethiopian government has decided to operate: the cost of the public accusation has now been priced in, the GERD filling cycle continues, and the cooperation with the UAE on the procurement-and-overflight architecture has not been formally repudiated. Ethiopia’s counter-charge that Khartoum is materially supporting the Tigray People’s Liberation Front against the Addis government carries the same coordination signature in the opposite direction; the bilateral has now stabilised at the level of mutual proxy warfare without direct kinetic exchange, and the bilateral cost of escalation to direct conflict remains higher than the structural pressure justifies for either capital. For Abu Dhabi, the architecture is the cleanest version of the doctrine the Yemen and Libya files installed: a structural intervention sustained at the logistical and procurement layer, mediated through proxies who carry the political and reputational cost, and preserved inside a multilateral diplomatic framework the patron continues to chair. Confucius’s observation that the superior man does not have the heart to do what he is not willing to acknowledge reads the architecture inversely: the patron who is willing to run the war but not willing to acknowledge it is the patron whose Confucian discipline has been replaced by the Greene-and-Talleyrand discipline of separating action from attribution. The doctrine is the doctrine. The denial is the instrument.

What the drone war tests.

Three indicators across the coming weeks. The first is the Khartoum air-defence tape itself. If the SAF continues to intercept the strategic drone before it reaches the capital’s civilian and military centre, the regime has stabilised the symbolic centre and the war returns to the Darfur-and-Kordofan ground theatre where the RSF’s advantage remains; if any drone successfully reaches the central oil-or-power installation, the regime’s air-defence claim is overstated and the symbolic-capital file becomes acutely vulnerable again. The second is the Quad framework calendar. If the United States, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE produce a substantive humanitarian-truce framework in the next ninety days, the architecture of patron-mediated diplomacy has held despite the public attribution of the drone supply; if Khartoum walks out of the Quad track over the UAE’s continued mediator role, the contradiction between sponsorship and mediation breaks the framework and the war runs without an internationally recognised diplomatic channel for the rest of the year. The third is the GERD-and-Tigray pressure tape. If Sudan’s counter-pressure on the Tigray file produces an Ethiopian operational distraction in the north, the drone supply chain through Bahir Dar compresses and the SAF-RSF balance shifts; if the GERD filling cycle proceeds without bilateral incident and the Tigray file stays at the political register, the operational architecture holds and the war sustains at its current intensity through the dry-season ground campaign in August. La Rochefoucauld’s observation that we confess small faults only to persuade people that we have no large ones reads the patron diplomacy: the UAE will continue to confess to the small fault of humanitarian-aid logistical entanglement to persuade the international community that no large fault — the drone supply — is occurring. The supply continues. The denial sustains. The doctrine holds.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What’s driving this: Two layers carry the war. The first is coordination at the patron-proxy register: the strategic-drone campaign that the RSF runs against Khartoum cannot be operationally sustained without a state-grade logistical chain, and the open-source attribution chain (drone serial numbers, Bahir Dar overflight, Chinese-manufactured platforms, CAR-and-Libya physical routing) is the public signature of a coordination architecture between Abu Dhabi, Addis Ababa, and the RSF that the three governments publicly deny. The denial is not the absence of the architecture; it is the legal-and-diplomatic instrument by which the architecture is sustained inside the Quad framework the UAE chairs. The second is structural: the Horn-of-Africa frontier is the geographic seam at which the Gulf, the Nile basin, and the Sahel intersect, and the structural pressure on the UAE to extend its Yemen-Libya-Red-Sea logistical envelope into Sudan is the same pressure that explains its presence in every other state along the seam. The drone war is the modality of the structural contest; the proxy is the instrument by which the cost of the contest is held outside the patron’s formal balance sheet.

What’s not driving this: The consciousness-state register that reads the war as the failure of one or another leader’s personal judgement is not load-bearing for the question being asked. Burhan and Hemedti are operating from inside structural pressures whose logic would deliver the same war under different commanders; the substitution of personalities would not substantially change the configuration. The metaphysical layer is silent here too; this is a coordination-and-structure event, not a civilisational-rhythm one.

What would break this reading:

  • The UAE publicly accepts removal from the Quad mediator role and admits to RSF sponsorship in exchange for a face-saving multilateral framework — the “deniability is the architecture” reading was wrong; the patron has chosen to absorb the political cost rather than sustain the war.
  • Khartoum and Addis Ababa exchange direct kinetic strikes (not proxy or drone-by-proxy) inside the next sixty days — the “bilateral stabilises at the mutual-proxy register” reading was wrong; the GERD-and-Tigray structural pressure has overrun the deniability discipline.
  • A genuine SAF-RSF ceasefire is signed and held for thirty days inside the Quad framework — the “structural impasse with patron-mediated proxy war” reading was wrong; either the patron architecture has collapsed faster than expected or the SAF’s air-defence improvement has shifted the cost calculus.
  • The strategic-drone campaign continues to penetrate Khartoum’s central installations at scale — the “SAF air-defence has restored the symbolic capital” reading was wrong; the patron supply has scaled past the defensive recovery and the regime’s consolidation is overstated.

Sources used: Mearsheimer’s offensive realism reads the Gulf-Horn architecture as the structural extension of regional power into the perimeter of a failed state; the UAE is doing what an offensive realism textbook would predict, not deviating from it. Greene’s seventh law on getting others to do the work reads the patron-and-proxy doctrine as the operational form of the law in inversion: the patron declines the credit and lets the proxy absorb the cost. Greene’s third law on concealing intentions reads the UAE’s public denial as the diplomatic instrument that preserves the optionality of the Quad framework. Sun Tzu on the line of supply reads the Bahir-Dar-to-Khartoum architecture as the operational fact against which every tactical event on the ground is finally calibrated. La Rochefoucauld on the small confession reads the UAE’s humanitarian-aid posture as the instrument by which the larger sponsorship is concealed; Talleyrand on the separation of action from attribution reads the patron diplomacy as the doctrinal form the Yemen and Libya files already taught.

The Borrowed Premise

The Bureau of Labor Statistics released the May 2026 CPI on Wednesday morning showing a 4.2 percent headline year-over-year reading — the highest annual print since April 2023 — with a 0.5 percent month-over-month rise driven overwhelmingly by a 3.9 percent monthly energy component and a 7 percent monthly gasoline move that together carry above sixty percent of the headline acceleration. Core CPI printed 0.2 percent month and 2.9 percent year against consensus 0.3 and 3.0; the energy index is up 23.5 percent on the year, gasoline up 40.5 percent. Kevin Warsh was sworn in as Federal Reserve Chair last month; his first FOMC opens on June 16, the decision and the Summary of Economic Projections land on June 17, and the Wall Street Journal opinion page in which Warsh argued before confirmation that “AI will be a significant disinflationary force” is the public document inside which the doctrine that will now have to survive the first print was installed. Polymarket-implied odds of no change at the June meeting sit above ninety-six percent. The Stratagem reads the convergence as the Aristotle test of the practical reason constrained by the material reality that resists the borrowed premise, the Munger two-track reading the disinflation argument requires, the Greene fifth-law reading of the reputational instrument the new chair will defend at the first meeting, and the beginning of the window in which the dots, the hyperscaler capex tape, and the public-market repricing of the disinflation doctrine are all repriced against the same borrowed-premise configuration.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington & New York·11 June 2026

Washington —The detail that matters in Wednesday’s BLS release is not the 4.2 percent headline and it is not the 0.2 percent core. It is the composition. Sixty percent of the headline acceleration came from energy. The proximate driver of the inflation print is not the demand tape, not the labour-market tape, not the goods-disinflation versus services-inflation divide. It is the price of a barrel of Brent, and the price of a barrel of Brent is set by a Strait of Hormuz that has been substantially closed to Iranian exports since April under the standing US naval blockade, by a ceasefire architecture between Tehran and Tel Aviv that the Trump administration has spent six weeks trying to extend, and by an Israel-Lebanon incursion calendar inside which the regional escalation risk has not yet meaningfully decompressed. The proximate cause of the highest CPI print in three years is the Iran-war energy pass-through. The new Federal Reserve Chair’s entire pre-confirmation argument was that AI productivity would be the structurally disinflationary force allowing rate cuts. Greene’s fifth law — so much depends on reputation; guard it with your life — reads the configuration. The first meeting under a chair whose public premise has been borrowed from a productivity wave whose disinflationary effect has not yet shown up in the actual price index is the meeting at which the borrowed premise is forced to survive its first encounter with the data.

Begin with the Aristotelian register. The practical reason — phronesis, the deliberative virtue specific to the particular case — is the discipline by which a wise actor reads the moment against the universal principle rather than asserting the principle and forcing the moment to bend to it. Warsh’s argument that AI productivity will be disinflationary is a perfectly defensible universal claim about the medium-term cycle. It is also a claim about the average price level eighteen to thirty-six months out, not about the energy-driven CPI print landing on the desk of the first FOMC meeting. The error pattern Aristotle names is the substitution of the general for the particular: the actor who knows the principle so well that he stops reading the present arrives at the wrong move in the present. The Federal Reserve has institutional memory of this exact failure. The Burns Fed of the early 1970s borrowed the premise that oil-shock inflation was transitory and supply-driven and would resolve through productivity gains and supply normalisation; the borrowed premise was right in shape and wrong in calendar, and the cost was the entire 1970s inflation cycle and the Volcker recession that ended it. Lee Kuan Yew’s observation that the test of a doctrine is whether it survives contact with the cost it imposes reads the institutional posture: a doctrine that lets you cut rates against your declared inflation mandate has a cost; the cost is the loss of the credibility instrument the post-2022 framework painfully rebuilt. The June 17 decision will not be the test. The September and December decisions, after two more energy-driven CPI prints, are the tests.

Munger’s discipline of two-track analysis is the second instrument. Track one: the substantive economic claim. Track two: the psychological-and-institutional incentives shaping the actor making the claim. On track one, the AI-productivity disinflation argument has a body of supporting research, a plausible mechanism (substitution of capital for labour in services, reduction in unit cost of cognitive work, acceleration of supply-side responsiveness), and a few early indicators in the unit-economics of customer-service automation and code-generation cost curves. The argument is not wrong; it is incomplete. The energy-shock pass-through is an exogenous variable the AI productivity wave does not address inside the meeting horizon, and the chair’s job is to set policy against the print, not against the multi-year thesis. On track two, the chair’s incentive is the one Greene’s thirteenth law — appeal to self-interest, never to mercy or gratitude — reads inversely: the chair who campaigned on a thesis is now staked to it, the political constituency that nominated him on the disinflation argument expects rate cuts, the public-market repricing of the AI cycle has been partially priced against the same thesis, and the cost of abandoning the thesis at the first meeting is the loss of the reputational instrument that earned him the chair. La Rochefoucauld’s observation that the mind is always the dupe of the heart reads the same register: the doctrine the chair has publicly committed to is the doctrine the chair will read the data through, and the marginal energy-driven print will be coded as transitory because the doctrine requires it. Dalio’s late-cycle big-cycle framework sits at the structural register: when a debt-burdened economy meets a supply shock against an inflation-anchor regime, the cycle has historically converged on either the wage-price spiral or the recession-by-tightening path; the disinflation thesis is the third path, the one the cycle has rarely delivered without a productivity miracle on the calendar the cycle imposes.

What the print does.

Read the immediate consequences three ways. The metaphysical layer is silent. Law of the Day · No. 7

Get others to do the work.

Greene’s seventh law is the thread through Thursday’s three stories, applied at three registers that look entirely different until the instrument is named. The law’s usual gloss reads as the courtier’s ruse — use others’ effort, take the credit, conserve your strength. The deeper version, the one each of Thursday’s principals is operating from, is structural: the wise actor identifies the institution, the proxy, or the wave whose work the actor’s own posture cannot do as well, and arranges for that work to be done while the actor retains the credit-attribution and the discretion. In Khartoum, the credit is being declined and the work is being done; the United Arab Emirates is running a strategic-drone war through the RSF and the Bahir-Dar supply chain while publicly chairing the Quad framework that is supposed to end it. The inversion of the seventh law is also a form of the seventh law: the patron lets the proxy carry the cost and absorbs the structural position the war secures without ever having to acknowledge the operation. In Washington, the Fed chair is borrowing the AI-productivity wave to do the disinflationary work his rate path requires; the productivity gain is being asked to substitute for the demand compression a hold-or-hike regime would have delivered, and the borrowed premise is the doctrine the borrowing rests on. In Berlin, the chancellor is letting the Bundestag carry the public-anchoring work that the European Council’s subsequent compromise will be measured against; the floor is installed by the legislative deliberation, and the chancellor takes the credit at the Brussels meeting where the legislative work would not directly apply. The Practitioner’s Note

Borrow the work that is yours to direct.

Each of Thursday’s stories turns on the same instrument read three ways: an actor identifies the wave, the institution, or the proxy whose work substitutes for the actor’s own visible move, retains the discretion the substitution preserves, and absorbs the credit-attribution the structural position delivers. The UAE’s strategic-drone supply through Bahir Dar costs Abu Dhabi nothing it is not already paying through the Yemen-and-Libya logistical envelope; the cost of running the war directly would be the political-and-multilateral cost the Quad framework is precisely structured to prevent. The Fed chair’s borrowed AI-productivity premise costs the chair nothing rhetorically; the cost of delivering the cut against an energy-shock print without the borrowed premise would be the institutional credibility the post-2022 framework rebuilt. The chancellor’s Bundestag floor costs Merz nothing the coalition arithmetic does not already absorb; the cost of installing the position at the Council without the Bundestag floor would be the southern-and-eastern compromise the Council’s majority would otherwise extract.

In your own affairs: the law cuts at three registers. At the operational register, identify the wave, institution, or proxy whose work would substitute for your own visible move — the lawyer, the framework, the partner whose effort delivers what your direct push would cost. At the doctrinal register, identify the wave or doctrine that is doing the structural work your reasoning needs and ask honestly whether the wave is operationally on the calendar you require or only on the calendar your reasoning would prefer it on. At the institutional register, identify the deliberative process — the board, the committee, the legislative body — whose public deliberation can install the anchoring you would otherwise have to install at higher cost. Greene’s seventh law is the standing discipline against doing yourself what others, institutions, or waves can do for you while you retain the discretion the borrowed work preserves.

The hard question: in your own life, where is the work you are doing yourself that an institution, a wave, or a proxy could be doing on your behalf — and where is the work you are borrowing from a wave that may not be operationally on the calendar you need it on? The first failure is the failure of the prideful operator who insists on direct action where a borrowed instrument would deliver the same result at lower cost; the second failure is the failure of the borrower who stakes his credibility on a wave whose timing he cannot control. Greene’s seventh law is the discipline of asking which is which.
