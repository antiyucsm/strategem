The Forgotten Front

Sudan's war crossed its third year this week. Nearly seven hundred civilians were killed by drones in its first quarter. While Iran absorbs global attention, the Gulf powers are testing their weapons — and their architecture — on a nation the outside world has agreed to stop seeing.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Khartoum & Port Sudan·19 April 2026

Port Sudan —Three years and four days after Sudan's war began, the country is dividing not by treaty but by use. The Sudanese Armed Forces hold the north, centre, and east, including the ruined capital. The Rapid Support Forces hold Darfur, most of Kordofan, and have now opened a new front along the Blue Nile border with Ethiopia. Between January and March the United Nations documented 693 civilians killed by drones alone — more than any prior quarter of the war, and eighty percent of the children recorded killed in the same window. The frontline has not moved. The weapons have. The architecture behind the weapons has, too.

This is a war nobody wants to look at because looking requires naming its coalition. The RSF is financed and armed through a UAE-centred network — Capital Tap Holding and seven Emirati companies sanctioned by the US Treasury in January 2025, with the Saudi Crown Prince reportedly asking Trump in February to sanction the UAE directly. The SAF is backed by Egypt and, less visibly, Saudi Arabia. Iran's war absorbs the attention; Sudan is where the Gulf's own rivalries are being fought at low cost, in somebody else's country.

"War is the continuation of politics by other means." — Clausewitz. Sudan is the continuation of Gulf politics by other people's deaths.

The shape that will not close.

Three scenarios remain: fragmentation into de facto partition, a decisive SAF push backed by Cairo and Riyadh, or a fragile internationally-brokered ceasefire. After three years, only the first is actually happening. Al-Burhan's army retook Kadugli on 3 February and broke the siege of Dilling two weeks later; it secured el-Obeid in early March. Hemedti's RSF responded by shifting east into Blue Nile state and consolidating its hold on Darfur after the October 2025 fall of El Fasher — a capture a UN fact-finding mission has since called genocide in everything but name, with more than six thousand killings documented in the first three days alone.

The historical analogue is not Rwanda and not Syria. It is Spain, 1936–1939. External powers testing systems — German aircraft at Guernica, Soviet armour at Brunete — while the belligerents ground each other into a stalemate neither could end without foreign decision. In Sudan the tested system is not aircraft but the commercial-off-the-shelf armed drone: Turkish Bayraktars and Akıncıs on the SAF side; Chinese Wing Loongs routed through the UAE on the RSF side. Seven hundred civilian deaths in a quarter is what the technology does when no foreign power will impose cost on its supplier.

Why the impasse holds.

Mearsheimer's structural realism reads this directly. Each external backer prefers a stalemate in which its client controls a share of Sudan's resources to a victory by the other side's client. The UAE's stake is in the Red Sea corridor and in RSF-controlled gold flows that now move through Dubai; Egypt's stake is in the Nile waters and in a friendly SAF government in Khartoum. Neither has an incentive to end the war on the other's terms, and both have capacity to prevent a decisive result. The war therefore has no reason to end. Its backers are getting what they paid for.

Lee Kuan Yew's reading of small-state survival applies in reverse. What Sudan lacks is what Singapore and Switzerland possessed at founding: a balancing coalition of great powers each of which would lose by allowing the others to dominate. Sudan has the opposite — a balancing coalition of middle powers each of which gains by prolonging the contest. The Berlin conference of April 15, co-hosted by Britain, France, Germany, the US, the AU and the EU, raised $1.5 billion for humanitarian aid and produced no political pressure on the two external backers who matter. Aid without a sanctions regime on UAE companies is treatment of a wound that is being deliberately re-opened.

What the drone changes.

Greene's Law 23 — concentrate your forces — explains why the drone war produces this casualty pattern and not another. Neither army has the manpower or logistics to hold more than a narrow corridor, so each concentrates on contested cities and relies on unmanned strike to degrade what it cannot occupy. A Kadugli funeral, a North Darfur wedding in Kutum, a South Kordofan market — these are not errors. They are the new tactical doctrine: attrit the civilian base of the enemy's faction until displacement does the territorial work. Thirty-four million Sudanese now need humanitarian support; nine million are displaced. Those are operational metrics for the doctrine, not tragic side-effects of it.

Ripperger's framework on spiritual warfare — where the technical question and the moral one diverge — is where the reading ends. Every actor in this war can defend every decision as strategically rational. Yet the pattern itself, read from outside the actors' self-understanding, is a civilisation's lower bound: a country being dismantled for the convenience of neighbours who will not be held to account, by a drone economy whose manufacturers will not be named, under the indifference of a great-power system whose attention is elsewhere. Confucius: the superior man may indeed have to endure want, but the mean man, when he is in want, gives way to unbridled license. The architecture in which unbridled license is financed by investment accounts in three continents is the architecture to read.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: The war persists because the external backers — UAE, Egypt, Saudi Arabia — each gain more from a frozen contest in which their client holds territory than from any plausible ceasefire that would redistribute it. That is the structural reading: a balancing coalition of middle powers with aligned incentives to prolong, not end. The drone economy is the tactical overlay that translates that preference into seven hundred civilian deaths per quarter without moving the line on the map.

What's not driving this: The belligerents' military capacity. Both sides have enough arms to keep fighting and neither has enough to win. The decisive variable is not what Burhan or Hemedti can do. It is what Abu Dhabi and Cairo will keep paying for.

What would break this reading:

  • US imposes secondary sanctions on UAE companies in the Capital Tap network before end of Q2 — the external-backer architecture cracks and Hemedti's supply chain tightens.
  • Saudi-UAE reconciliation produces a joint ceasefire framework before September — the middle-power rivalry was shallower than the reading.
  • Either Burhan or Hemedti is removed from command by his own side before year-end — the internal constraint mattered more than the external one.
  • A decisive territorial breakthrough (SAF retakes a Darfur capital, or RSF enters Port Sudan) before Q3 — the stalemate premise was wrong.

Sources used: Mearsheimer's structural realism for why each external backer prefers stalemate to the other's victory; the Spanish Civil War 1936–1939 as the template for middle-power proxy systems being tested on a small state; Greene's Law 23 (concentrate your forces) for the drone war's civilian-attrition doctrine; Ripperger's distinction between strategic rationality and moral pattern for the reading's closing move.

The Chokepoint

TSMC's Q1 revenue hits a record $35 billion. Profit jumps 58%. High-performance computing is 61% of the book. The AI economy's only real bottleneck is a single island, eighty miles from a rival hegemon — and every hyperscaler's balance sheet is now staked on it.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Hsinchu & New York·19 April 2026

Hsinchu —Taiwan Semiconductor reported first-quarter revenue of NT$1.134 trillion — roughly $35 billion — on 16 April. Net profit rose 58% year-on-year. High-performance computing, which is the category where Nvidia's accelerators and their imitators live, delivered 61% of the top line. Full-year capex now guides to the high end of a $52–56 billion range. Full-year revenue growth is guided above 30%. Second-quarter revenue is guided to $39–40.2 billion, a 10% sequential step. The island's most important company is running at the limit of its capacity, and the limit is rising.

Inside Taiwan, TSMC is already more than 40% of the total market capitalisation of the Taiex. Inside the global AI economy, it is the entire foundry layer for every frontier model — Nvidia's H- and B-series, AMD's MI-series, Google's TPUs, Amazon's Trainium, Apple's custom silicon. Samsung is a node behind on yield. Intel Foundry, after three reorganisations, is still selling design wins, not wafer output. The AI economy has chosen a single supplier, and the single supplier sits on a single island.

"Invert, always invert." — Jacobi, via Munger. The interesting question is not why TSMC's numbers are this good. It is what has to stay true for another four quarters of these numbers.

The chokepoint as fact.

Zeihan's geography-first framing deals with this cleanly: when a critical input depends on a single geography, great-power strategy converges on that geography, and the civilian economy that relies on the input discovers it has rented a piece of its balance sheet to a security question. The US CHIPS Act was the first visible payment of that rent — $52.7 billion in subsidies to reduce, over a decade, the share of leading-edge wafers fabricated inside what the Pentagon calls the first island chain. Four years in, the Arizona fabs are running at 4nm in limited volume; the 2nm roadmap remains in Hsinchu and Kaohsiung.

Mearsheimer's offensive realism adds the second half of the frame. A rising hegemon sitting opposite a single-node chokepoint has structural incentive to either capture it or deny it. Taiwan's strategic value is the argument against the invasion; the value is also the argument for a quarantine, a coercive grey-zone pressure campaign, a submarine-cable severing — anything that reprices the chokepoint without triggering direct war. Every major US hyperscaler's capex plan for 2026–2029 is an implicit bet that none of those moves happen inside the planning horizon.

The capital architecture.

Yesterday's edition noted the circular-capital pattern inside the $122 billion OpenAI round. TSMC's results read it from the other side. Nvidia's revenue — which funds both the circular flows and the demand that justifies them — is approximately one-third of TSMC's book. TSMC's $52–56 billion capex is approximately 60% of ASML's expected EUV revenue for the year. The wafer fab equipment buying cycle, the design-IP leasing cycle, and the hyperscaler lease cycle are now a single coordinated loop running through three balance sheets that share no regulator.

Greene's Law 23 — concentrate your forces — describes what the system is doing correctly. A concentrated supply chain is faster, sharper, and captures the innovation-surplus that a distributed chain spreads thin. It also describes what the system is doing dangerously. Concentration raises the return on any single point of failure, and the points of failure are now known: TSMC Fab 18 at 3nm; ASML's Veldhoven export pipeline; the Burmese rare-earth corridor that moves scandium and gallium into Taiwanese substrate production. A serious incident at any of the three compresses global AI capex growth inside a quarter.

What breaks the number.

Invert the 58% profit jump. What would make a 2027 reader look back at this quarter as the top of a cycle?

Hyperscaler capex deceleration is the first candidate. Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, Google, Oracle collectively guide to roughly $700 billion of infrastructure spend in 2026. That number is funded by two assumptions — enterprise AI adoption continues to compound, and the model economics justify the inference build-out. A single weak guide from one of the five breaks the assumption visibly. The second candidate is a rival model class — a successful Chinese frontier model, a credible open-source MoE architecture with materially lower inference cost — that reprices the marginal GPU from capex to impairment. The third is geographic: a quarantine, a weather event, an accident that takes any of TSMC's Gigafabs offline for more than a fortnight.

Lee Kuan Yew observed that small states survive by being useful to more than one great power and indispensable to none. TSMC has achieved the opposite: indispensable to one, visibly useful to none of the others, with the indispensability growing every quarter. The earnings report is the proof of the first clause. The geopolitical calendar is the proof that the second clause has not yet mattered — and the longer it has not mattered, the more expensive it becomes for it ever to matter.

The reading.

The Hsinchu number is not a stock story. It is the visible edge of a structural fact. The global economy has built the cognitive infrastructure of the next quarter-century on a single foundry in a single geography, and priced the arrangement as permanent. In Munger's language, the system has embedded a low-probability catastrophic tail into its base case and called it a cost of doing business. Sun Tzu's rule applies in reverse: the supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting. The supreme art of AI capital is to build a supply chain so concentrated that its protection becomes someone else's problem. That arrangement holds — until it doesn't.

The lenses used.

Filed Brussels / Berlin / Paris
Rearmament · NATO · EU Commission

Two Brussels, One Army

A trillion-euro rearmament drive has two addresses in the same city and no single commander. NATO and the EU Commission are both in Brussels; both claim final authority on European defence. Merz sits at the hinge — the biggest budget, the thinnest mandate, and a flight to Paris to make his choice visible.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Brussels & Berlin·19 April 2026

Brussels —On 16 April the Financial Times reported a "fierce dispute" between NATO and the European Commission over who governs the rearmament drive — an effort that in the Commission's own numbers reaches €800 billion under the ReArm Europe plan, and in NATO's projections approaches a trillion dollars a year by decade-end. NATO has long opposed Brussels taking on defence powers; the Commission argues that the fiscal space is European and the procurement rules must be, too. The Council has already activated the fiscal escape clause for seventeen member states. The money is arriving. The chain of command is not.

Germany is where the dispute lands. The 2026 German defence budget is around €82.6 billion — a €20.2 billion increase over 2025. Finance minister Klingbeil has committed to 2.4% of GDP in 2025, 3.5% by 2029, and to align long-term spend with NATO's 5% guideline by 2035. Friedrich Merz flew to Paris this week for the European conference on securing the Strait of Hormuz. Germany is, for the first time since reunification, the continent's largest defence buyer. And it is doing this with 80% of its voters disapproving of the chancellor ordering the cheques.

"War is the mere continuation of politics by other means." — Clausewitz. When the policy has two authors, the war has two plans.

What the dispute is actually about.

The surface argument is governance. Beneath it lies procurement. NATO's interoperability framework pulls European defence spending into transatlantic primes — Lockheed Martin's F-35, RTX's Patriot, Boeing's tankers — because the alliance's standards for ammunition, avionics and command-and-control are written around US systems. The Commission's SAFE instrument — €150 billion in loans, already disbursing to eight member states — comes with "European preference" clauses designed to redirect those flows into Airbus, KMW-Nexter, Rheinmetall, Dassault, Leonardo, Saab. The same €1 trillion procures either a transatlantic defence industrial base or a sovereign European one. It cannot procure both.

Kroeber and Gavekal would read this exactly the way they read China's industrial disputes: as a coalition war disguised as a governance question. The NATO side is backed by the governments most invested in the US alliance — Poland, the Baltic states, the Nordics — and by the defence ministries that have to fight tonight. The Commission side is backed by the governments most invested in industrial sovereignty — France, Italy, and, ambiguously, Germany — and by the finance ministries that will service the debt. Each side can legitimately claim to represent "Europe." That is the problem.

Berlin's two flags.

Merz's trip to Paris is a signal. Germany is joining a European coalition to secure Hormuz — a mission designed, crewed, and politically framed in Paris and London rather than in Washington. The same week, Berlin has quietly deepened its Rheinmetall contracts for NATO-standard 155mm production, reinforced its Patriot deployments in Romania under NATO command, and continued to buy F-35s for its nuclear-sharing mission. The two commitments are not contradictory. They are the observable evidence that Berlin is not yet making a choice and intends to remain useful to both architectures for as long as the ambiguity can be sustained.

Talleyrand would recognise this as the classic small-coalition move: stay indispensable to every side until the moment of decision, and never be the state that collapses the compromise. It works while the dispute is about rhetoric. It stops working when either architecture demands an exclusive contract — a European-preference clause that disqualifies a US prime, or a NATO standard that disqualifies a European one. The first such demand arrives, per Commission officials, in the autumn SAFE rulebook. Germany's choice is measured in months, not years.

The consent problem.

Morning Consult's April survey put Merz's disapproval at 80% — the highest for any elected head of government it measures. A chancellor with an 80% negative rating who is simultaneously the continent's largest defence buyer is an experiment that modern Germany has not run. The closest analogue is Helmut Schmidt in 1979–80, carrying NATO's double-track decision against a mass peace movement inside his own SPD. Schmidt prevailed on the NATO track and lost his chancellorship; the Bundeswehr's Euromissile deployments survived him by almost a decade. The historical lesson is that an unpopular chancellor can execute a structurally required rearmament, but the execution outlives his career.

Aristotle's formulation of the political: the polis is prior to the individual, as the whole is prior to the part. What is happening under Merz is the polis reassigning itself to a role — underwriter of European defence — that its citizens have not consciously elected. Consent is trailing the commitment. The AfD and Die Linke each sense this gap from opposite sides and run on it; the coalition's only language for the change is a fuel subsidy and a defence-budget line item. Neither is the explanation that the moment requires.

What the reading names.

The structural layer is doing most of the work. Once the US signalled that NATO guarantees are now transactional rather than structural, Europe either rearms or accepts strategic dependency on a reluctant underwriter. That bound set of choices does not depend on who holds office in any European capital. The coordination layer is where the outcome is being decided: whose procurement rules govern the rents, which industrial coalition captures the decade of spending. And the consciousness-state layer is the constraint the other two underestimate — a polity asked to pay for a rearmament whose strategic purpose has not been articulated to it runs a legitimacy deficit that compounds every year the guns arrive faster than the explanation. Mearsheimer has the structure. Greene has the coalition. Aristotle has the consent. All three are load-bearing; none alone is sufficient.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: A structural force — the US has moved NATO guarantees from structural to transactional, and Europe has no choice but to rearm — is meeting a coordination war over who captures the resulting trillion in annual procurement. Each architecture is rational for the coalition that runs it; neither can win the whole. Germany's role is to make the choice visible, because its budget is large enough that its vote moves the equilibrium. A third layer — consent — is doing background work: every month the guns arrive faster than the public story of why they are arriving, the legitimacy deficit compounds.

What's not driving this: Personality. Merz's 80% disapproval, von der Leyen's ambition, Rutte's institutional instincts — all real, all visible, all less load-bearing than the industrial-coalition arithmetic. A different cast of European leaders would produce a very similar dispute because the coalitions themselves are producing it.

What would break this reading:

  • The autumn SAFE rulebook drops "European preference" under US pressure before November — NATO wins the governance war and the dispute resolves transatlantically.
  • Germany announces an exclusive Rheinmetall-Airbus-Leonardo procurement framework that disqualifies US primes before year-end — the Commission wins and the transatlantic base erodes.
  • Russia-Ukraine ceasefire closes before Q3 and rearmament momentum collapses — the structural premise was a crisis premium, not a secular shift.
  • A German federal snap election in 2026 — the consent constraint has already broken and the reading has to re-centre on domestic legitimacy, not industrial coalitions.

Law of the Day · No. 23

Concentrate Your Forces.

Conserve your forces and energies by keeping them concentrated at their strongest point. You gain more by finding a rich mine and mining it deeper than by flitting from one shallow mine to another — intensity defeats extensity every time. When looking for sources of power to elevate you, find the one key patron, the fat cow who will give you milk for a long time to come.

Today's reading: each story is a map of where concentration lives and where it is being contested. Taiwan's leading-edge fabs are a mine so rich that a single island has become the node through which artificial intelligence, advanced weapons, and half a decade of American corporate earnings must pass. Europe's rearmament is an attempt to concentrate a continent's industrial base — under a Brussels that cannot decide which Brussels commands. Sudan is the inverse case: two forces that refuse to concentrate decisively, and so burn the country instead.

  • When you find the rich mine — the one client, the one platform, the one relationship that compounds — dig deeper before you dig wider.
  • A chokepoint is a concentration that someone else controls. Map yours before the counterparty does.
  • Concentration without command is not power — it is a target. Europe's €1 trillion is learning this.

The Practitioner's Note

Find the rich mine.

Every one of today's three stories is about who has concentrated and who has not. TSMC is the richest mine in modern industry; the market prices it at roughly fifteen times earnings because the mine is also a chokepoint, and the chokepoint is also a hostage. The Germans are pouring €82 billion into a defence industry they do not yet control, under a command structure no one has yet agreed. The Sudanese war continues because neither side can concentrate enough to finish it, and both have patrons willing to fund the inconclusion.

In your own affairs: identify the one position, one skill, one relationship that has compounded the most for you in the last three years. Now ask what you are doing to deepen that mine — not broaden your portfolio, deepen that mine. Most of your energy leaks into the shallow seams.

The hard question: the rich mine is always also a dependency. Where does your concentration make you a hostage, and to whom?

Today's three stories each turn on a single question: where does your concentration live, and who controls the chokepoint around it? Sudan burns because no one can concentrate; Taiwan rules an industry because the concentration became a chokepoint; Europe pours money into a rearmament whose command structure has not yet concentrated. Apply the test to your own life — the mine you are deepening, the skill you are compounding, the relationship you rely on. Where is the chokepoint, and whose hand is on the valve?
