The Cauca Drones

Five days before Colombians vote, a rebel faction that walked away from the 2016 peace accord is dropping grenades from quadcopters onto police stations in Cauca, Nariño, and the coffee belt that runs north toward Antioquia. The Defence Ministry counted 333 drone incidents in 2025, up from 61 a year earlier. The Electoral Observation Mission calls one-third of the country — 386 municipalities — vulnerable to armed-group interference. Senator Iván Cepeda Castro, the candidate of the ruling Pacto Histórico, polls at 38.7 percent on the AtlasIntel aggregate; right-wing outsider Abelardo de la Espriella of the Movimiento de Salvación Nacional has closed to 37.3. The Stratagem reads the Sunday vote not as an isolated security crisis but as the documentary moment at which a peace-process politics that has organised Colombian national life since 2016 confronts an armed-group cohort that has spent the intervening decade composing the capability to render that politics structurally non-viable. The drones are the receipt. The runoff is the cycle.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Bogotá & Popayán·26 May 2026

Bogotá —A commercial quadcopter, modified to carry a grenade or an IED, costs roughly two hundred dollars and requires perhaps twenty minutes of training to operate. Through 2025 the EMC dissident faction — the FARC splinter that rejected the 2016 accord under alias Iván Mordisco — and the Clan del Golfo deployed those drones 333 times against Colombian police and military targets, up from 61 the year before. The incident curve is the curve of cheap precision diffusing into a civil-conflict architecture that the state has institutionally designed against rifles, mortars, and improvised land mines. The drones are not the threat the integrated Plan Colombia architecture was composed against. They are the threat that the post-2016 peace process never anticipated — and the threat the Sunday vote will hand to whichever cohort takes office in August.

Mearsheimer’s structural reading is half the analytic. Colombia is, in classical terms, a state with incomplete control over its own territory: 386 municipalities under varying degrees of armed-group authority, an estimated 27,000 combatants under arms across at least six rival cohorts, and a geography — jungle, mountain, river — that institutionally favours the dispersed armed actor over the centralised state. The post-2016 peace process did not change that arithmetic; it changed the names of the cohorts that hold the periphery. President Gustavo Petro’s “total peace” doctrine read the cycle as susceptible to negotiation. The drone incident curve is the cohort-external answer: where the state offers talks, the cohorts that profit from coca, gold, and extortion compose the capability to render the talks structurally unnecessary. The Sunday vote is the moment at which the architecture is publicly assessed.

“The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting.” Sun Tzu on the discipline of victory through positioning is the harder reading on what the armed-group cohort has institutionally composed across the campaign. The drones, the candidate threats, the polling-station targeting, the displacement of voters in the 386 vulnerable municipalities — none of it is required to win the war. It is required to shape the political conditions under which whichever cohort wins the war will negotiate. A Cepeda government inherits a peace-process platform whose credibility now sits below the floor the EMC dissidents and the Clan del Golfo have institutionally pre-set; a de la Espriella government inherits a mandate for hard-line military response inside an environment the cohort has institutionally pre-composed as ungovernable through force alone. The cohort that holds the drone capability holds the cycle inside which either administration must operate.

The peace-process ceiling.

Dalio’s big-cycle framework on the long arc of political-economic settlement reads the post-2016 Colombian arrangement as a cycle that has now reached its institutional ceiling. The 2016 Havana accord composed the country’s national-security architecture against an opposing cohort — the legacy FARC — that the architecture defeated by absorbing. What the architecture was not composed against was the fragmentation cycle that followed: the EMC dissidents under Mordisco, the Segunda Marquetalia under Iván Márquez, the ELN, the Clan del Golfo, the multiple smaller cohorts that captured the territorial vacuum the demobilising FARC left behind. By 2026 the fragmentation has compounded past the threshold inside which a single negotiation cycle can re-compose the architecture. The cohort that runs the next government will discover, inside the first hundred days, that the institutional instrument it inherits was designed for a cycle that has structurally ended.

Lee Kuan Yew’s discipline on small-state survival inside a hostile structural environment is the practitioner overlay the Colombian cohort has institutionally underweighted. LKY’s doctrine reads the cohort’s long-cycle viability as resting on three pillars: a state monopoly on coercion within national territory; an economic architecture inside which the formal sector grows faster than the informal; and an institutional cadre inside which corruption is contained below the threshold at which it captures the state itself. The 2025-2026 cycle has compressed all three. The state monopoly on coercion has been visibly eroded by the drone-and-IED campaign; the cocaine economy now represents an institutional sector whose annual receipts approach the official defence budget; and the parallel cycle of state-prosecutor investigations into the Petro administration’s campaign-finance and Casa de Nariño operations has institutionally compressed the cohort’s long-cycle credibility. The Sunday vote is composed inside that compression.

The runoff arithmetic.

Two layers carry the reading. Structural: the post-2016 Colombian political-economic settlement has reached the cycle position at which its founding instruments — the peace process, the centralised civilian-led security architecture, the formal-sector growth assumption — no longer hold against the cohort-external pressure the fragmentation cycle has composed. Tactical: the drone-and-IED campaign is the armed-group cohort’s discipline composed inside Sun Tzu’s rule that the war is won by shaping the conditions inside which the political cycle operates, not by direct engagement with the state’s capability. What is not driving this is a sudden ideological realignment of the Colombian electorate; the polling cohort has held remarkably stable through the campaign, and the May 31 first-round arithmetic almost certainly composes a June 21 runoff between Cepeda and de la Espriella. The Stratagem reads the load-bearing tests of the next two-to-four quarters as the first-round turnout inside the 386 vulnerable municipalities (does the violence-driven depression of rural turnout institutionally compress Cepeda’s base or hand the runoff to the right-wing cohort), the runoff consolidation dynamic (does the Valencia and Quintero vote consolidate behind de la Espriella or fragment across abstention), the Petro administration’s post-election security cadence (does the cohort compose a substantive operation against the EMC and the Clan del Golfo or hold the architecture inside the discipline of the “total peace” doctrine), and the Washington cohort’s cadence on Plan Colombia successor funding (does the Trump administration condition further security cooperation on a hard-line successor or hold the cooperation independent of the political outcome). The drones are the receipt. The receipt is the cycle the next government inherits.

The Abu Dhabi Departure

The United Arab Emirates formally left OPEC on May 1. It was the cartel’s third-largest producer, the holder of its most useful spare capacity, and the member whose USD145-billion upstream programme through 2030 has been pulling national output from sub-four million barrels a day in 2020 toward a five-million-barrel ceiling by 2027. The break was not theatrical — no walkout, no recrimination at the Vienna podium — but it was structural. Twenty-five days later, Brent is holding near USD97 after a USD140 spike during the Iran war, the OPEC+ cohort has staged a symbolic 188,000-barrel-per-day production rise in its first meeting without Abu Dhabi, and the US Treasury cohort’s tacit endorsement of the move sits inside the cartel’s reading rooms as the documentary signal that the post-1973 architecture has reached its inflection. The Stratagem reads the Abu Dhabi departure not as a fintech-style headline but as the cycle position at which the cartel’s structural authority over the price of the marginal barrel is publicly contested for the first time in fifty-three years. The first defection is the cycle. The cycle composes the position the next cohort reads itself inside of.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Abu Dhabi & Vienna·26 May 2026

Abu Dhabi —Founded in Baghdad in 1960 by five producers who wanted control of their own pricing power back from the seven Anglo-American majors, OPEC has spent sixty-five years holding the architecture of the global oil market through a single disciplined mechanism: quotas. The mechanism is not subtle. Member states agree to produce less than they could, and the resulting scarcity supports a price the cartel collectively benefits from. The mechanism works only if no member of consequence defects. The UAE has now defected. Not in protest; not in crisis; but with the cohort discipline of a state that has spent the last decade composing the capability to defect, the diplomatic cover to defect, and the export infrastructure to defect without absorbing the institutional cost the cartel could otherwise have imposed.

Munger’s mental-model on incentives is the first load-bearing analytic. The UAE’s exit is over-determined by structural arithmetic. The quota Abu Dhabi has held under OPEC+ since 2017 has run roughly one million barrels per day below the country’s actual production capacity. The 145-billion-dollar upstream programme through 2030 was always going to compose a national output above any quota the cartel could realistically award. Inside the cartel, the UAE was paying a quarter of its potential revenue annually for the privilege of supporting a Saudi-led pricing architecture that institutionally disadvantaged it. Outside the cartel, it can sell every barrel it produces. Munger’s rule — show me the incentive and I’ll show you the outcome — reads the May 1 departure not as a strategic surprise but as the cycle position at which the incentive structure could no longer be papered over with diplomatic accommodation.

“Wars are won by the deeper purse.” The maxim, attributed variously to Frederick the Great and to Adam Smith, is the harder reading on what the Abu Dhabi cohort has institutionally composed. The UAE’s post-2008 sovereign-wealth architecture — ADIA, Mubadala, ADQ — holds an estimated 1.5 trillion in deployed capital, and the country’s breakeven oil price sits below thirty dollars. Saudi Arabia’s breakeven, by contrast, runs above eighty: Vision 2030 capex, NEOM, the post-Aramco fiscal architecture, and the social-contract subsidies that Riyadh cannot publicly dismantle each compose a price floor below which the cohort runs an unsustainable deficit. In any price war the UAE could choose to wage, the structural arithmetic favours Abu Dhabi. Riyadh knows this. The May 3 OPEC+ meeting, the first without Abu Dhabi, accepted the asymmetry by reading from the same script as previous cycles — a symbolic 188,000-barrel-per-day production rise inside language that emphasised cohort cohesion — without composing the substantive coercive response the cartel might historically have attempted.

The cartel ceiling.

Dalio’s big-cycle framework reads the post-1973 OPEC architecture as a cycle that has now reached the late-stage inflection at which the founding instrument has institutionally compressed past its load-bearing position. OPEC was composed against a structural condition — rising marginal demand, finite reserve discoveries, a concentrated reserve base in a small number of cooperating states — that each of the four constituent assumptions has now been compressed past. US shale, Brazilian pre-salt, Guyana, and Canadian heavy each compose a non-OPEC supply architecture that the cartel cannot meaningfully discipline. Demand growth has flattened across the post-2024 EV-and-LNG cycle. Reserves are now widely distributed across a non-cooperating cohort. And the surviving members face the institutional problem that the cartel’s diminished influence on the marginal barrel composes a parallel diminution in each member’s individual political-economic returns from cohort membership. The Abu Dhabi departure is the documentary signal that one member has read the cycle as fully inflected. Indonesia in 2008 was peripheral; Qatar in 2019 was strategic but small; the UAE in 2026 is structurally central. The next departure compounds the cycle.

Lee Kuan Yew’s discipline on the small-or-middle-power cohort inside great-power competition is the practitioner overlay on the Abu Dhabi move. LKY’s doctrine reads the cohort’s long-cycle position as resting on the maximum number of substantive instruments the principal cohorts cannot simultaneously coerce. The UAE has spent the post-2017 cycle composing precisely that architecture: a parallel diplomatic posture inside the Abraham Accords with Israel, a deepening commercial-and-technological architecture with China, a residual security architecture with the United States, a sovereign-wealth diversification cycle into Indian, European, and African infrastructure, and now an independent production architecture outside the OPEC quota cycle. The post-May-1 cohort architecture is not the act of a state defying a cartel; it is the cohort discipline composed against a cycle in which the founding architecture institutionally constrained the cohort below its preferred long-cycle trajectory. The cartel was the constraint. The departure is the cohort architecture composed against the constraint.

Two layers carry the reading. Structural: the post-1973 OPEC architecture sits at the Dalio-cycle late-stage inflection at which each of the four founding-condition assumptions has compressed past its load-bearing position, and the cartel’s authority over the marginal barrel is now publicly contested by a non-OPEC supply architecture, a flattening demand cycle, and an internal departure of structural consequence.
