Law of the Day · No. 23

Concentrate Your Forces.

Power itself always exists in concentrated forms. Single-mindedness, against opponents who are less focused — such an arrow finds its mark every time.

Yet know the limit. When tied to a single source, if that source falls, you fall with it. Concentration is a sword with two edges.

  • Find the single point whose absence decides the contest.
  • Place your weight there. Do not thin it across fronts.
  • Identify what happens if that point fails — plan for it.
From the Classics

Clausewitz & Sun Tzu.

Be very strong; first in general, then at the decisive point. There is no higher and simpler law of strategy than that of keeping one's forces concentrated.

— Carl von Clausewitz

Attack where he is unprepared. Appear where you are not expected.

— Sun Tzu

The Practitioner's Note

On your own Hormuz.

Each life contains choke points. A single client, a single relationship, a single income — points whose pressure decides the whole.

Of your adversary: where is their Hormuz? What single point, if squeezed, decides more than its size?

Of yourself: where is your Hormuz? Diversify that point — or fortify it until it cannot be squeezed.

Lead Analysis · Power Dynamics

Concentration of Force

Washington closes Iran's arteries at Hormuz. Read it not as crisis, but as case study — a demonstration of where power is applied, not how much of it.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Islamabad & Washington·15 April 2026

Islamabad —A state with superior force rarely uses all of it. It concentrates it, places it where absence would cost the other side everything, and waits. That — more than any kinetic act — is what the United States did this week with Iran. The choice of where to apply pressure, rather than how much, is the instructive element.

Following a twenty-one-hour summit in Pakistan that ended without agreement, U.S. Central Command began enforcing a blockade of Iranian ports. Within the first twenty-four hours, no ship had passed. Six merchant vessels complied with direction and turned back. No shots were fired.

The arrow, not the army.

Iran's adversarial position rests on two instruments: its enrichment program, and its capacity to disturb oil markets through allied proxies. Both are now fixed publicly as American "red lines." To negotiate over them is to admit their existence; to refuse is to invite the arrow.

The design is Clausewitz's: be strong in general, then very strong at the decisive point. The decisive point at Hormuz is not military — it is the transit of Iranian economic life. One narrow waterway carries everything an extended campaign would take months to degrade.

"A single-mindedness of purpose and total concentration on the goal… such an arrow will find its mark every time." — Law 23.

Unpredictability as cadence.

Within a single news cycle, the White House moved from blockade announced to talks might resume in forty-eight hours. That is not indecision. It is Law 17 applied in motion: cultivate unpredictability. Iran must calibrate not against a position, but against a moving pattern. Proxies find the rules altered mid-response; allies watch ground shift before they commit.

Invert before you admire.

Munger's discipline: ask what makes this a disaster. Asymmetric retaliation by proxies — the blockade's "clean" frame shatters. Brent sustained above one-hundred-and-twenty dollars — the political cost reverses onto Washington. Allied fatigue — the near-shoring consensus erodes. None is improbable. Each converts concentration from virtue to vulnerability.

The reader is not asked to approve or condemn, only to read. By Friday, the tape will have told us more.

The Circular $122 Billion

OpenAI closes the largest private round in history — funded, in part, by the very hardware vendor it most depends upon. Read the ledger, not the headline.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed San Francisco·15 April 2026

San Francisco —When the supplier funds the purchaser, one should pause before admiring the deal. OpenAI's record $122 billion round — valuing the company at $852 billion — was led by Amazon ($50B), Nvidia ($30B) and SoftBank ($30B). Each has a different reason to be in the room. Only one has a reason that circles back to its own income statement.

Nvidia's investment in its largest customer is, in ledger terms, an investment in the durability of Nvidia's own demand curve. The chips OpenAI must buy to justify $852 billion in enterprise value do not buy themselves. By funding the buyer, the seller underwrites the buying.

Incentive-caused bias, at scale.

This is the cleanest public example of what Munger called incentive-caused bias — a force so powerful that normally decent people will rationalize decisions they would never make if the reward structure were different. It is not accusation. It is arithmetic.

Consider the hyperscaler capex for 2026: Amazon ≈ $200B, Google ≈ $180B, with total industry spend projected near $700 billion. A share of that flows, inevitably, through Nvidia. A share of what Nvidia receives is then reinvested into the buyers of the chips. The capital is not obviously misallocated — but it is undeniably circular.

"Never, ever think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives." — Charles T. Munger.

The Lollapalooza.

Munger's Lollapalooza names the compounding effect of several forces converging — social proof (every hyperscaler spending), authority (the Son / Huang / Altman axis), commitment and consistency (each round larger than the last), incentive-caused bias (vendor-financed demand). Read together, they explain the speed of the valuations, not merely their size.

For the student of power: OpenAI executes Law 11 — keep people dependent on you. A platform on which 900 million work each week, and on which hyperscalers now have $80B co-staked, is a dependency, not a product. Dependency, made mutual, becomes gravity.

Invert before you admire.

If hyperscaler capex slows by even 30 percent — a capacity absorption event, a regulatory intervention, a rival model class — the circular flow reverses. Nvidia's revenue falls; its own equity stakes mark down; the valuations they supported cool; OpenAI's next round prices differently. Three links in the chain, one direction of travel.

Watch Nvidia's April 16 earnings. Watch AWS AI's capacity, still reported at $15B run-rate and still capacity-constrained. Watch the first serious mention of pricing discipline. That will be the signal.

Berlin, Bystander

Germany's "fiscal reawakening" meets the strait. Merz learns what every medium power eventually learns — sovereignty ends at the price of Brent.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Brussels·15 April 2026

Berlin —The most consequential economic decision of the German quarter was not made in Berlin. It was made aboard a U.S. carrier somewhere west of the Strait of Hormuz. Growth forecasts for 2026 and 2027 have been cut. Inflation, which the Merz government had planned to outrun, returns through the fuel pump. The eight-and-a-half-percent minimum-wage rise intended to unlock real wage gains now sits against a moving inflation line.

The uncomfortable lesson is not new — it is older than the Federal Republic itself. A mid-sized export economy in a long peace is structurally exposed to the wars of others. Germany writes the rules of its labor market; it does not write the price of Brent crude.

The cost of the silent chair.

Where the Stratagem's first section described Washington as agent and Tehran as target, Germany occupies a third category worth naming carefully: the silent chair. No seat at the table of decision. Every line of the outcome delivered to its balance sheet.

This is not weakness. It is position. But position untended becomes liability. Law 20 counsels: do not commit inadvertently to anyone. Germany's energy posture, its export dependence on China, its security reliance on Washington — each is a commitment compounded over decades. None is easily reversed in a single political cycle.

The board does not pay its cost in battles lost. It pays in forecasts cut — quarter after quarter.

The domestic echo.

Here the Munger lesson applies inwards. The AfD does not need to author policy. It needs only to name the cost. Every inflation print not produced by Berlin becomes, in the populist register, a failure of Berlin. Inflation imported through oil is politically indistinguishable from inflation created at home. Voters feel the pump; causation is a story for editors.

Merz's fiscal pivot — the cautious reawakening of state investment that S&P called the story of 2026 — was always going to be judged against external conditions. It is being judged against worse ones than its authors expected.

What the student takes away.

Three readings, portable to one's own affairs. First, identify the silent chairs in your life — contracts, suppliers, reputations shaped by decisions you will not be invited to make. Second, name the structural commitments whose costs reveal themselves only in crises. Third, do not confuse calm with control. Much of what feels stable is merely the absence, for now, of a pressure test.
