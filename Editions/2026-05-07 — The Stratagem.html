Lead Analysis · Israel Strikes a Hezbollah Commander as the Iran Deal Nears Closure

The Beirut Breach

An Israeli strike on the Dahieh suburb of Beirut on Wednesday killed Malek Ballout, identified by a source close to Hezbollah as the operations commander of the elite Radwan force. Eleven other people were killed in coordinated strikes across the south and east of Lebanon. Prime Minister Netanyahu confirmed personally that he had ordered the operation. It is the first Israeli strike on the Beirut metropolitan area since the US-brokered Lebanon ceasefire was extended on 23 April, and it lands precisely during the seventy-two-hour window in which Tehran is reviewing a Washington proposal that would, on the most-quoted Iranian formulation of it, settle the Strait of Hormuz first and the nuclear file later. The Lebanese front is the lever; the strike is the price quoted to the principal who is reading the deal.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Beirut & Tel Aviv·7 May 2026

Beirut —The killing of Malek Ballout is a price quotation, not a tactical event. The Radwan force is Hezbollah's elite ground formation; the operations commander is the man who runs the cell-and-pair architecture from which any Hezbollah response to an Israeli ground operation would have to be assembled. Removing him is a real combat-power cost. But the timing is the message. The strike landed on the morning Tehran's negotiating delegation entered formal review of the Washington memorandum that would lift the US naval blockade of Iranian ports in return for a uranium-enrichment cap and stockpile arrangement. Netanyahu did not strike Beirut on the morning of the deal because the deal made the strike easier. He struck Beirut on the morning of the deal because the deal threatens to remove his leverage, and the strike was designed to remind every party at every table that the leverage is held in Israeli hands and applied on an Israeli calendar.

Read the architecture the strike is testing. The 16 April Lebanon ceasefire — extended for three weeks on 23 April — was not a Lebanese-Israeli arrangement; it was the regional sub-clause of the Iran-track de-escalation, an end to Israeli operations in Lebanon being one of Tehran's central asks. The deal Iran has been reviewing this week is the principal contract; the Lebanon ceasefire is collateral. Killing the Radwan operations commander is a way of opening the collateral document, marking it, and handing it back. It says: the principal contract may close, but the collateral remains an Israeli prerogative. The Lebanese state, which holds the formal monopoly on coercion inside its territory, was not warned; the Hezbollah leadership, which holds the actual monopoly, was given a corpse to read.

"In war, then, let your great object be victory, not lengthy campaigns." — Sun Tzu, The Art of War, II. Israel's preference is not the long Lebanon campaign. It is the visible, calibrated, high-value strike that changes the price of every move the other side considers. Removing Ballout costs Hezbollah a year of internal succession; it costs Israel one airframe, one weapons load, and a single line of cabinet authorisation. The asymmetry is the doctrine.

The leverage seam.

Mearsheimer reads regional architectures as the product of relative power, and the relative power on the Lebanon vector has not changed since the 8 October 2023 trade. Hezbollah lost its senior command tier in the September 2024 pager-and-radio operation, lost Hassan Nasrallah a week later, lost its Bekaa supply chain through Syria when the Assad regime fell in December 2024, and entered the 2026 round on a depleted ammunition base with no functioning Iranian land-bridge. The Radwan force is what survived. Removing its operations commander while the strategic patron is reviewing a deal that would freeze Israel's Lebanese options is the maximum-leverage move available inside an asymmetric balance — it cashes in Hezbollah's depletion before any deal locks the Israeli hand. La Rochefoucauld: we are never made so ridiculous by qualities we possess as by those we affect to have. The Lebanese ceasefire architecture has been carrying the affected quality of mutuality; the Beirut strike is the unmasking.

Greene's Law 15 — crush your enemy totally — is the doctrine the strike fragment-quotes. Greene's reading of crush-your-enemy is not maximalist territorial conquest; it is the elimination of the residual capacity of an adversary to retaliate at the moment when retaliation would otherwise cost most. Killing Ballout in the seventy-two-hour window during which Tehran is constrained from authorising a serious Hezbollah response — because authorising it would terminate the Iran deal review — is the cleanest application of the principle since the 27 September 2024 Nasrallah strike was timed against the UN General Assembly week. The form is the same: identify the seam in the adversary's coalition, time the operation to the moment in which the seam is open, and absorb the cost of the act inside a window in which the principal cannot punish you without paying a worse cost elsewhere.

The Tehran question.

The Iranian decision now is the load-bearing variable. Tehran can read the strike three ways. As a deal-killer, in which case it walks the delegation home from the Washington memorandum, accepts the continuing oil-blockade cost, and authorises a Hezbollah response that resets the Lebanon front to active fighting. As a deal-raiser, in which case it absorbs the strike, completes the deal, and treats Lebanon as a write-off whose costs are smaller than the costs of the blockade and the sanctions stack. Or as a deal-converter, in which case it incorporates the strike into a new clause that demands explicit US guarantees on Lebanon as a condition of signing — converting a betrayal into a leverage point. Voss on tactical empathy reads the Iranian calculus cleanly: the move that preserves Tehran's options is the third one, because it allows the principal contract to close while extracting the regional concession the strike has just made unavoidable. The cost is that it depends on Washington being able to deliver Israeli restraint, and Washington has spent six months demonstrating that the principal it is negotiating for does not always deliver his own coalition.

Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of small-state defence is the cleanest practitioner overlay on what Lebanon is becoming. A state whose monopoly on coercion has been ceded to a non-state actor, whose ceasefire is held by a foreign broker, whose airspace is open to its declared adversary, and whose senior internal-security architecture cannot prevent a strike on a metropolitan suburb is not a sovereign state in the operational sense; it is a postal address for foreign-power decisions. President Joseph Aoun's statement of condemnation on Wednesday afternoon will produce no consequence, because the Lebanese state has no consequence to deliver. The next move is Iran's; the move after that is Israel's; the move after that may be Washington's. The party whose name is on the building is reading every move from the lobby.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Coordination: an Israeli strike on a Hezbollah commander timed to the seventy-two-hour window in which the Iranian patron is constrained from authorising a response is not an opportunistic kinetic event; it is a coordinated leverage move executed by a coalition — Netanyahu's office, the IDF Northern Command, the cabinet's security sub-group — that has read the diplomatic calendar as carefully as the target dossier. Tactical: the operation cashes in the asymmetric depletion of the adversary at the maximum-leverage moment, on the doctrine that the value of a strike is the function of the constraint inside which it lands. What is not driving this is grand-strategic ambition for a permanent Lebanese reconfiguration. The Israeli political object is narrower — to keep the Lebanon file open and Israeli-priced while the Iran file closes. The Stratagem reads the next two weeks as the window in which Tehran's three options collapse into one. The third option — the deal-converter — is the rational move. The lesson of the last six months is that rational moves are not always available to actors operating inside a coalition whose internal politics no longer track the external incentives.

Fed Fracture · Four Dissents at the April Meeting — the Most Since 1992 — on the Eve of the Chair Handover

The Four Dissents

The detailed breakdown of the April FOMC vote — released this week — shows four dissents on the decision to hold the federal funds rate at 3.50 to 3.75 percent. It is the largest set of dissents at a single meeting since late 1992. The composition is the story. Stephen Miran dissented for a 25 basis-point cut. Beth Hammack, Neel Kashkari, and Lorie Logan dissented in the other direction — they supported the hold but opposed the inclusion of an easing-bias clause in the statement. One vote pulling toward a cut now, three votes pulling against the language that signals cuts later: the committee fractured on opposite axes inside the same meeting nine days before its chair leaves office. The doctrinal argument the Powell handover article framed last week as a question for the autumn has just been pulled forward. The institutional crack is visible.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington & New York·7 May 2026

Constitution Avenue —Four dissents at one FOMC meeting is not a count, it is a posture. The post-Volcker norm is that the chair builds consensus before the meeting and the dissent rate is the public signal of how much consensus the chair could build. One dissent is a normal independent-thinker presence. Two dissents is a debate visible to the staff and quietly understood. Three is a problem the next chair inherits. Four — last seen at the September 1992 meeting under Greenspan, in a different inflation regime and with a different committee architecture — is institutional fragmentation under stress. The April vote that produced the four dissents happened inside the last full month of Jerome Powell's chairmanship; the next meeting will be the first under his successor; the gap between is being filled with a public record that the committee no longer agrees on the framing of its own decisions, let alone on the rate path itself. The architecture has always carried internal disagreement. The architecture has rarely admitted it on this scale at a moment of leadership transition.

Read the geometry of the four. Stephen Miran dissented for an immediate 25 basis-point cut — the dovish-political pole, formally arguing that the labour-market deceleration and the residual demand-side effects of the elevated rate path warrant easing despite the oil shock. Beth Hammack, Neel Kashkari, and Lorie Logan dissented from the opposite pole — they supported the rate hold but refused to sign on to the inclusion of easing-bias language inside the statement, on the reading that signalling future cuts inside an inflation environment with headline CPI at 3.3 percent and the Iran-war fuel premium still feeding through is an institutional unforced error. The geometry matters. One dissenter against three on opposite axes is not a committee searching for a centre; it is a committee whose centre has been hollowed out by a chair who could not hold both flanks inside a single signal. The April meeting reads as the moment the post-Volcker consensus stopped being a consensus and started being an outgoing chair's last vote count.

"When the army is restless and distrustful, trouble is sure to come from the other feudal princes. This is simply bringing anarchy into the army, and flinging victory away." — Sun Tzu, The Art of War, III. The four-dissent meeting is the institutional equivalent. A divided committee on the eve of a contested handover, with an oil shock feeding inflation and an appointing principal openly campaigning for rate cuts, is a posture the adversary — bond markets, the FX market, the political opposition — reads as instability. The structure has not failed yet. It has visibly cracked.

The fragmentation seam.

Aristotle on factions inside a constitution is the right frame. The Federal Reserve System's institutional design — twelve-member FOMC, seven board governors, five rotating regional presidents, a chair who chairs the Board of Governors and the FOMC simultaneously — was built precisely to make the central bank's decisions look like the decisions of an institution rather than of an individual. The dissent rate is the public metric of the chair's success at building that look. Powell's eight-year tenure has averaged below one dissent per meeting; Greenspan's nineteen-year tenure averaged about the same. Yellen's chair-tenure averaged half that. The April 2026 four-dissent meeting is two and a half standard deviations from the post-Volcker average. The political pressure architecture — the President's public framing of the rate path as a personal grievance, the nomination of a successor on the explicit promise of pivot, the cabinet-level coordination of the public framing — has been visible since 2025. What was not visible until the April vote count is that the pressure has reached the dissent-decision threshold of three sitting members of the committee on the hawkish side and one on the dovish side. The cracks are not the political pressure. The cracks are what the committee has done with it.

Dalio's big-cycle framework gives the macro overlay. Late-stage debt cycles produce political pressure on the central bank as fiscal arithmetic deteriorates; the central bank's institutional independence is the single most consequential feature of the post-1980 American financial architecture; the visibility of dissent inside the FOMC is the public market for the institution's credibility premium. The bond market read the April statement and absorbed the headline. The minutes — and now the named dissent breakdown — give the same market a different picture: a committee that did not agree on the language of its own forward guidance during the last meeting under the chair who built the language. The credibility premium is currently priced inside the institution. It is being asked, by a four-dissent meeting on the eve of a handover, whether it should be priced inside the next chair's first nine months or inside the next chair's first vote count.

The doctrine question.

La Rochefoucauld on the visible signs of inner disagreement: quarrels would not last long if the fault were only on one side. The four dissents are the public proof that the doctrinal argument inside the committee is not a Powell-vs-Trump argument; it is a Hammack-Kashkari-Logan-Miran argument that has its own internal axes and has been gathering since the oil shock began feeding into the data. Hammack's pre-Cleveland Fed career on the Treasury markets desk gives her the institutional read on what easing-bias language does to the front-end curve in the current rate regime; Kashkari's Minneapolis Fed presidency has held a hawkish posture through the 2024-2026 cycle on the demographic-and-services-inflation reading; Logan's Dallas Fed presidency has held a different hawkish posture on the financial-conditions-and-balance-sheet reading. Miran is the lone-dove pole on a labour-market-deceleration reading. Four committee members, four different operating frameworks, three of them aligned against the chair's preferred forward guidance. The successor inherits the committee, not the consensus.

Confucius on names not aligning with substance is the cleanest read of what is being asked of the next chair. Warsh's confirmation by 15 May, his first FOMC in June, his first non-routine policy decision in the autumn — each of these will be the public test of whether the new chair can rebuild a committee centre on terms that hold both the hawkish-three and the dovish-one inside a single statement. The institutional name of the office is “Chair”; the substance of the office is the chairing of a committee. The April meeting demonstrated that the substance is the harder of the two problems. Mearsheimer on the structural pressure on actors inside an institution: the committee will pull the chair toward consensus more than the chair pulls the committee. The next nine months are not the first test of Warsh's policy preferences. They are the first test of Warsh's chair-craft inside a committee that has just publicly declined to be chaired without dissent.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Coordination: an FOMC committee whose internal architecture has been a centred-consensus committee for the post-Volcker era is now a four-dissent committee on opposite axes, and the geometry of the dissent is the public proof that the chair's centre has been hollowed by inflation, oil, and the political pressure compounding into the framing decisions. Institutional: the Federal Reserve's credibility premium has always been priced on the architecture's ability to absorb internal disagreement and produce a single voice; the April meeting is the first public datum that the architecture has visibly failed at the second part. What is not load-bearing today is the rate level; the dissents on rates were one against three against the chair, and the headline decision held. What is load-bearing is the forward-guidance language, the dissent rate at the June meeting under the new chair, and whether the September decision can be made with a dissent count below the political-credibility threshold the bond market will start pricing if the architecture continues to fragment. The Stratagem reads the next two FOMC meetings as the test the post-Volcker convention has not had to pass since 1992. The architecture has cracked. The question is whether it holds the shape of the institution while it is being repaired.

The Mutual Defence Clause · The EU Quietly Builds the Architecture It Spent Forty Years Refusing

The Brussels Blueprint

European Union ambassadors held private discussions in Brussels this week on operationalising Article 42.7 of the Treaty on European Union — the bloc's mutual defence clause — and Cypriot President Nikos Christodoulides confirmed at the European Council that the Commission has been tasked with preparing a blueprint for how the article would be triggered, what assistance member states would actually deliver, and on what timetable. The clause has existed in treaty law since 2009. It has been invoked once, by France after the November 2015 Bataclan attacks, with no implementing architecture beyond the foreign-minister statement that registered the invocation. The trigger this week is the April drone strike on the British airbase at Akrotiri in Cyprus during the closing phase of the Iran war — a strike on the territory of an EU member that the broker of the Lebanon ceasefire, Washington, did not warn the host state about. Cyprus is in the EU and not in NATO. The blueprint Brussels has now commissioned is the operational architecture the Union spent four decades refusing to build under the assumption that the United States would carry the burden the architecture is now being designed for.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Brussels & Nicosia·7 May 2026

Brussels —The blueprint is the move because the assumption it replaces has expired. The post-1949 European security order was built on a division of labour: NATO carried the kinetic guarantee, the European Communities carried the economic integration, and Article 42.7 — when the Lisbon Treaty added it in 2009 — was a doctrinal-symmetry clause that mirrored NATO's Article 5 without ever being meant to operate as one. The clause says that if a member state is the victim of armed aggression on its territory, the others are obliged to provide aid and assistance "by all means in their power" in line with the UN Charter. There is no implementing architecture, no command structure, no force-generation mechanism, no doctrine on what counts as armed aggression, and no mechanism for the Council to authorise an aggregate response. France invoked the clause in November 2015 and received twenty-six bilateral expressions of solidarity, no operational support beyond what was already bilaterally agreed, and no continental architecture for a future invocation. The clause has remained nominally active and operationally vacant for sixteen years. The drone strike on Akrotiri in April demanded an operational answer from a system that had never built one.

Read the architecture the strike exposed. The Akrotiri base is British sovereign territory inside Cyprus; it carries the British nuclear-and-electronic-intelligence capacity in the eastern Mediterranean. The drone that struck it during the April Iran-war phase was probably Iranian or Iraqi-proxy in origin; the British response was operational and bilateral. Cyprus, however, is an EU member, and the strike inside Cypriot territory triggered the question of what the Union itself owed Nicosia in operational terms. The answer was nothing — no doctrine, no architecture, no mechanism. Cyprus invoked no clause because no clause would have produced any operational response. Christodoulides spent the late-April European Council building the consensus that the gap is now intolerable. Von der Leyen's Commission has accepted the blueprint commission. The first draft is to be circulated to capitals by the end of June; the formal Council debate is scheduled for the September European Council under the Cypriot rotating presidency. The instrument exists. The architecture is being commissioned to make the instrument usable.

"Hence to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting." — Sun Tzu, The Art of War, III. The function of an operational mutual-defence architecture, once it exists, is not the response it produces but the strikes it deters that the absence of architecture invited. The post-1949 European order outsourced the deterrence function to NATO; the blueprint Brussels is now drafting is the recognition that the outsourcing arrangement has become contingent on the politics of the principal contractor in a way the clause's drafters never priced.

The umbrella seam.

Mearsheimer reads alliance architecture through the cost the dominant power is willing to absorb to underwrite the security of clients whose contributions do not match the consumption. The post-2024 American posture has compressed that cost-benefit calculation into a public set of demands: 5 percent of GDP on defence, the Pentagon's 5,000-troop withdrawal from Germany announced 2 May, the threats against Italian and Spanish basing rights, the rhetorical decoupling from any European security guarantee that does not run through a transactional negotiation. The structural fact is not that the United States will leave NATO — the institutional architecture and the Senate's role in any treaty exit make that outcome a tail event. The structural fact is that European capitals can no longer plan the next decade on the assumption that the umbrella opens automatically when needed. Article 42.7 is the clause the treaty drafters wrote precisely for the scenario in which the umbrella's automaticity comes into question. The blueprint is what would have to exist to convert the clause from a doctrinal-symmetry artifact into an operational instrument. The structural read is that this would have happened anyway in the next decade. The trigger this week is what determines whether it happens on a five-year or a fifteen-year calendar.

Helen Thompson on the political economy of European autonomy is the operational overlay. The fiscal envelope required to make 42.7 functional — defence-industrial base capacity, command-and-control architecture, force-generation mechanisms, doctrine, training, and the political consensus on aggregate response — is a multi-decade project that cannot be financed inside the existing European budget framework. It would require either Eurobond issuance at a scale that has been politically vetoed since 2010, or a dedicated defence-fund treaty modification that requires unanimous ratification, or a piecemeal national-budget acceleration that the current fiscal positions of France and Italy cannot absorb without bond-market consequence. The blueprint commission inside the von der Leyen architecture is starting from the assumption that the third path is the only feasible one inside this decade — and that the institutional design will need to encode the financing fragility into the operational architecture. The clause's draftsman never imagined the financing problem because the clause was always assumed to be a reinforcing layer beneath NATO's primary architecture. The trigger of the blueprint is the recognition that the layering assumption no longer holds.

The veto question.

Greene's Law 19 — know who you are dealing with — runs through the question of which member states will and will not sign on to an operational 42.7 architecture. Hungary's Orbán has spent the past three years extracting concessions from the Council process by threatening unanimous-vote vetoes; the operational mutual-defence architecture would carry an unanimous-vote requirement at the activation step, and the Hungarian veto would be the load-bearing question if the architecture were tested in a contingency that involved a state Budapest considered politically unfavourable. Slovakia under Fico, the new Czech government, and any future populist-right government in Italy or France would carry the same signal-jamming capacity. The blueprint will have to encode either an enhanced-cooperation path that excludes the veto holders or a coalition-of-the-willing structure that operates beneath the formal 42.7 invocation. Talleyrand on the durability of treaty architecture: treaties are kept as long as they serve the interests of the parties to them, and not a moment longer. The architecture being drafted is therefore not a single instrument; it is a layered system that must be operable when the formal instrument is blocked, on a calendar measured in days, by adversaries who will read the veto holders' positions before launching the next strike.

Aristotle on the constitution as the structure that organises offices is the institutional frame. The post-Lisbon European treaty architecture distinguishes the Common Foreign and Security Policy from the Common Security and Defence Policy, places both inside intergovernmental decision-making, and grants the High Representative — currently Kaja Kallas — the responsibility for executing the policies the Council authorises. The blueprint will sit inside that architecture and will have to operate without forcing a treaty modification, because treaty modification requires unanimous ratification across all 27 member states and the political will for that does not exist this decade. The institutional creativity required is therefore considerable. It is the same creativity the eurozone applied to the 2010-2012 sovereign-debt-crisis architecture — the European Stability Mechanism, the OMT programme, the banking union, all built outside formal treaty modification through institutional improvisation under crisis pressure. The defence equivalent is now in early drafting. Confucius on the rectification of names: the clause says "mutual defence." The blueprint is the work of making the substance match what the name has implied for sixteen years.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: an alliance architecture whose primary contractor has visibly compressed its underwriting commitment, leaving the European secondary clause as the next-best institutional vehicle for the security function the primary architecture has historically provided, and a discrete trigger event — the Akrotiri drone strike on EU member territory — that demanded an operational answer the system had never built. Institutional: a treaty clause that has existed for sixteen years without an implementing architecture, now being commissioned through the Commission's blueprint process under the explicit recognition that the architecture must be operable without treaty modification and must encode workarounds for the veto-holder member states. What is not driving this is European federalist ambition; the architecture is being built inside the existing intergovernmental decision-structure precisely because federalist routes have been politically foreclosed. What is load-bearing is whether the September Council debate produces the political will to ratify the blueprint at the speed the structural pressure now requires, and whether the architecture encodes a coalition-of-the-willing layer beneath the formal-invocation layer that is operable when the unanimous vote is blocked. The Stratagem reads the next eighteen months as the window in which the dormant clause either becomes the architectural anchor of a partially autonomous European defence posture or remains the symbolic clause it has been since 2009. The Akrotiri strike was the trigger. The Council in September is the test.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: A structural change in the underwriting of European security that has compressed the cost-benefit calculation by which Washington has been willing to absorb the burden of underwriting client states whose contributions did not match their consumption. The Pentagon's 2 May 5,000-troop withdrawal announcement, the threats against Italian and Spanish basing, and the demand for 5 percent of GDP on defence have together delivered a public message capitals have correctly read as a signal to plan the next decade on a different assumption. The Akrotiri drone strike was the trigger event that demanded an operational answer the system did not have — Cyprus is in the EU and not in NATO, the strike landed inside EU territory, and the absence of any operational mutual-defence architecture inside the Union became visible in a way that previous structural pressure had not made unavoidable. The mechanism is structural-institutional compounding: an alliance architecture whose primary contractor has visibly compressed its commitment is being substituted for by the secondary clause whose drafters never expected it to operate as the primary instrument, and a treaty clause that has existed without an implementing architecture for sixteen years is being commissioned through the institutional creativity that has carried previous European crisis-instruments outside formal treaty modification.

What's not driving this: A US withdrawal from NATO. The institutional architecture and the Senate's role in any treaty exit make the binary withdrawal a tail event that the blueprint is not being designed for. Reading the blueprint as a NATO-replacement project misses the cleaner read: the blueprint is being built precisely because the European capitals expect NATO to formally persist while its operational guarantee on the European theatre becomes contingent in ways the post-1949 architecture has not had to price.

What would break this reading:

  • The September Council debate produces a unanimous endorsement of the blueprint — the veto holders have either been bought off or have accepted the architecture inside the rotating-presidency political theatre, and the institutional creativity required to encode the coalition-of-the-willing workaround was not, in fact, the load-bearing question.
  • The Hungarian or Slovak governments collapse before the September debate — the veto-holder problem has been removed at the actor level rather than at the institutional level, and the blueprint can be drafted as a unanimous-vote instrument rather than as a layered coalition architecture.
  • A Trump-administration policy reversal restores the public commitment to NATO's primary architecture — the structural pressure that triggered the blueprint commission has been withdrawn, and the political will inside the Council to absorb the financing burden of the operational architecture dissipates inside the next budget cycle.
  • The blueprint's first draft circulates with a unanimous-vote-only operational layer — the Commission has read the Council politics as not yet ready for the coalition-of-the-willing workaround, and the architecture being built reproduces the same vacancy the existing clause has carried since 2009.

The Practitioner's Note

Architectures crack before they fall.

The three stories on Thursday's table are each about an architecture that has been operating under an inherited assumption that has expired without being formally retired. The Lebanon ceasefire architecture has assumed that Israeli strikes inside Beirut are off the table while the broker negotiates the principal Iran contract; the Wednesday strike has retired that assumption without retiring the ceasefire.
