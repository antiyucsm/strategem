The Garrison Threat

In a Truth Social post late Wednesday, President Trump announced that the United States is "studying and reviewing the possible reduction of Troops in Germany, with a determination to be made over the next short period of time." The post landed twenty-four hours after Chancellor Friedrich Merz, in a press appearance, said Europe is "suffering considerably" from the Hormuz blockade and urged the conflict be resolved. Trump had already accused Merz on Tuesday of thinking "it's OK for Iran to have a Nuclear Weapon." The garrison at Ramstein, Wiesbaden, Stuttgart, and Grafenwöhr has been the physical anchor of the post-1945 transatlantic order. The threat to move it is the structural break that two months of Iran war have made cheap to make — and expensive, even by his own pattern, to walk back.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Washington & Berlin·30 April 2026

Washington —The sequence is twenty-four hours long. On Tuesday Trump posted that "The Chancellor of Germany, Friedrich Merz, thinks it's OK for Iran to have a Nuclear Weapon. He doesn't know what he's talking about!" Wednesday morning Merz, asked at a Berlin press appearance about the row, said his personal relationship with Trump remained "as good as ever" but that he had "had doubts from the very beginning about what was started there with the war in Iran" and that "we are suffering considerably in Germany and in Europe from the consequences of, for example, the closure of the Strait of Hormuz." Hours later Trump posted on Truth Social: "The United States is studying and reviewing the possible reduction of Troops in Germany, with a determination to be made over the next short period of time." Roughly 38,000 US personnel are stationed across Ramstein, Wiesbaden, Stuttgart, Spangdahlem, Grafenwöhr, and the medical complex at Landstuhl. EUCOM and AFRICOM both headquarter inside the German lay-down. The threat is not new — Trump made a similar one in 2020 that the Biden administration cancelled — but the strategic geometry around it is.

Read the move. The American garrison in Germany has, since 1945, been the physical instrument by which the United States both reassures Europe and constrains it — the alliance is also the leash. To threaten the garrison over a German chancellor's public skepticism of an American war is to convert the reassurance instrument into a leverage instrument in front of every other allied capital that watches what Berlin is being asked to swallow. The threat may not be executed; that is, in 2026, the second-order question. The first-order question is what every other allied government has just observed about the price of public dissent.

"In all fighting, the direct method may be used for joining battle, but indirect methods will be needed in order to secure victory." — Sun Tzu. The Iran war is the direct method; the threat to a third country's garrison is the indirect one. Merz is not the principal in the Iran negotiation; he is the lever the principal in Washington has chosen to grip.

The thumbscrew.

Greene's Law 33 — discover each man's thumbscrew — is the cleanest read on the move. The German chancellor's structural vulnerability is the same one that has defined the Bundesrepublik since Adenauer: the country's defence depends on a security guarantee written in bases it does not own. Pull the bases and the guarantee thins; thin the guarantee and the AfD reads as the party that was right about the alliance being unreliable; let the AfD read that way and the CDU/CSU's domestic politics inverts. Trump is not picking the German chancellor's vulnerability at random. He is picking the one Merz cannot publicly answer in the German interest without confirming the AfD's frame for him at home. The threat works whether or not it is executed, and Merz's Wednesday line about the relationship being "as good as ever" is the audible sound of the screw being turned.

Mearsheimer's offshore-balancing thesis is doing structural work underneath. The American interest in keeping a heavy garrison in central Europe was, for forty years, the containment of the Soviet Union and, after 1991, the management of a unipolar peace. The interest in keeping it on a 2026 calendar is harder to articulate now that the United States has launched a Middle East war in which its European allies will not participate. A garrison whose strategic mission is unsynchronised with its host's political consent is, structurally, on borrowed time. What is unusual in 2026 is not the strategic logic of drawdown — Mearsheimer has argued it for two decades — but the procedural improvisation by which the drawdown is being threatened: Truth Social post, Iran-war framing, no NSC paper, no allied consultation, no congressional notification. The move is structurally over-determined and procedurally chaotic at the same time.

What Berlin can do.

Voss on negotiation reads Merz's available moves with surprising precision. The Chancellor cannot win the public argument with Trump on Trump's chosen ground; the asymmetry of the platform alone forecloses it. He can refuse to engage on the chosen ground at all — the tactical mirror of "no deal is better than a bad deal" — and absorb the visible cost of being seen to be silent. He can move the dispute behind the closed-door channel where Wadephul, Pistorius, and the Kanzleramt's foreign-policy adviser actually negotiate with State, Pentagon, and the NSC. He can let the Bundeswehr's chief of defence make the procedural points in uniform — that EUCOM's sustainment depends on the German lay-down, that AFRICOM's medevac chain runs through Landstuhl, that the operational continuity of the US presence in Africa is German real estate. None of these moves are public victories. All of them are the discipline of refusing to be drawn into the kind of fight the principal opposite chose precisely because he wins it on social media.

Talleyrand on managing a stronger principal applies under the procedural one. The classic instrument is to make the principal's threat expensive to consummate without being expensive to walk back from. The German bases generate billions in regional rents in Bavaria, Hesse, and Rheinland-Pfalz; the construction lobby on the American side has been the silent stabiliser of every previous threat. Merz does not need to win the argument. He needs to ensure that the cost of execution is paid first by the American constituencies that elected the man making the threat. The drawdown announced in 2020 never started; the lobby that prevented its start is still in place. The play is to thicken the lobby, not to engage the man.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: an overstretched hegemon late in its alliance posture is using the host-government dependency as leverage to coerce a co-belligerency it cannot win on the merits. Tactical: the principal has identified a thumbscrew that works whether or not it is turned all the way, and the host government has only the indirect, behind-the-door instruments available against a public, platform-based pressure. What is not load-bearing today is the formal NATO architecture. Article 5, the Washington Treaty, the integrated command structure — none of these are at stake in the Wednesday post. The post is about a bilateral garrison the United States can announce moving without taking the alliance apart. The escalation that would test the alliance is the next one, not this one — and the next one is now a measurable distance closer.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: A tactical thumbscrew applied to a structural vulnerability. The American garrison has, since 1945, been the physical instrument by which Germany's defence is guaranteed and its foreign policy constrained. The Iran war has produced a moment in which the host government's public consent is necessary to the war's domestic legitimacy in Europe, and Germany has refused to give it. The principal in Washington has identified that the host's dependency on the garrison is the lever by which the consent can be coerced — and that the threat is operational whether or not it is executed, because every other allied capital is now pricing the cost of public dissent. The mechanism is leverage application, not strategic withdrawal; the calibration is to a co-belligerent ask, not to a long-horizon force-posture review.

What's not driving this: The formal NATO architecture. Article 5, the Washington Treaty, the integrated military command — none of these are at stake in the Wednesday post. The threat is to a bilateral garrison, an instrument the United States can move without taking the alliance apart. Reading the move as a NATO-withdrawal story conflates the bilateral lever with the multilateral one and obscures what is actually being tested.

What would break this reading:

  • An NSC paper or DoD posture review surfaces showing the troop-reduction language was the published output of a months-long process — the move was structural-strategic and the timing relative to Merz was coincidence.
  • Merz publicly reverses on Iran inside two weeks and the troop language quietly drops — the thumbscrew worked precisely as designed and the leverage instrument is now confirmed in the toolkit.
  • Rutte or another major NATO capital publicly invokes Article 5 logic against the unilateral garrison threat — the bilateral move has triggered the multilateral architecture the principal expected to be able to keep separate.
  • A specific brigade-level redeployment order is signed and published before July — the threat was not leverage but stated intent, and the structural drawdown reading deserves more weight than the tactical-thumbscrew one.

The Advance Estimate

At 8:30 ET this morning the Bureau of Economic Analysis releases the advance estimate of Q1 2026 GDP. The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow nowcast sits at 1.2% annualised, unchanged for nine days; the prediction-market consensus has clustered in the 2.0–3.0% bin, leading at 30%. The gap is the read. Underneath sits a quarter that contains both March retail sales up 1.7% month-on-month and the start of the Hormuz oil shock — gasoline at $4.23, Brent $115, the highest pump price in four years. The first quarter is the last clean read on the US economy before the war's price effects fully metabolise into the headline series. The advance number is the timestamp on the world before that arithmetic comes due.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington·30 April 2026

Washington —The advance estimate prints at 8:30. The Atlanta Fed's nowcast has held at 1.2% for nine consecutive readings, anchored to weak Q1 inventory build, soft housing starts, and a manufacturing read still below 50. The prediction-market crowd has the print clustered above two — the 2.0–3.0% bin leads at 30%, the 1.0–2.0% bin at 24%, the under-1% tail at 14%. The gap between the official nowcast and the consensus is the kind of pre-print spread the BEA's revision history typically resolves toward the lower estimate, but the data this quarter is unusually contradictory. March retail sales surged 1.7% month-on-month after a frigid February — consumption has not broken. Q4 2025 was revised down to 0.5% annualised. Personal income data lands alongside the print at 8:30; March PCE inflation reads at the same minute.

Read the arithmetic. The first quarter contains both the strong-consumer data and the start of the Hormuz oil shock — the blockade began 13 April, but futures and pump prices began pricing it through February as the ceasefire fragility became visible. WTI averaged $89 in Q1, against $76 in Q4. The drag from a ten-dollar oil shock has been studied in the literature: each $10 sustained adds roughly 0.3 percentage points to headline CPI and removes roughly 0.15–0.2 from real GDP growth on a four-quarter horizon. The first quarter only contains the leading edge of that drag. Q2 contains the rest. The advance estimate is, in effect, the last quarter that can plausibly print above 1.5% before the war's price arithmetic fully arrives.

"To know your enemy, you must become your enemy." — Sun Tzu. Munger reads the same sentence in the inversion: tell me where I'm going to die so I won't go there. The advance estimate is a snapshot of the past; the question the snapshot is being read against is whether the wartime stagflation reading is correct for the next four quarters. The data the BEA prints is the floor under that question, not the answer to it.

What the nowcast is doing.

The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow is not a forecast in the academic sense. It is a real-time aggregation of the same component data the BEA assembles. The 1.2% number means: given the inputs the BEA has already ingested, the model believes the print falls there. The persistence of the nowcast across nine readings is a signal that the late-quarter monthly indicators (March retail sales, durable goods, trade balance, inventories) have been arriving in a configuration the model reads as soft. The market consensus at 2.5% is the human read on the same data plus the soft-survey priors and the normal upward bias of pre-print expectations. Munger's discipline on this kind of split is to weight the systematic mechanism over the human prior. Atlanta Fed wins more of these than the consensus does, especially when Q4 was already weak.

Dalio's big-cycle frame supplies the longer arithmetic. The United States is in the late-debt-cycle stage in which fiscal stimulus is constrained, monetary stimulus is constrained, and the marginal economic policy response to a wartime shock has to come either from oil-supply diplomacy or from rate suppression that the inflation prints will not yet allow. The first-quarter data is being read against that backdrop. A 1.2% print rather than a 2.5% print does not change the regime; it shortens the window in which the regime can be sustained without policy response.

The consumption question.

The single most-watched line in this morning's release will be Personal Consumption Expenditures. Real PCE held remarkable resilience through the early phase of the inflation cycle and through the rate-cycle peak of 2024–25; the consumer has been the load-bearing variable of every soft-landing reading. The structural question entering Q2 is whether the consumer holds against the gasoline-cost shock that arrived in mid-April but did not fully bite into March. The labour market has slowed but not broken — the April nonfarm payrolls release is now eight days away. The early read on Q1 PCE will tell the FOMC and the markets whether the consumption engine is fragile or merely flat. Confucius on "the gentleman in adversity holds firm" applies, less rhetorically than at first glance: the structural question is whether the household sector holds firm or whether it has begun the substitution toward credit and savings depletion that precedes the breaks in the cycle.

Lee Kuan Yew on capital, applied here, is a useful corrective to the recession-watch frame. The American consumer has the largest cushion of any consumer base in the world — household balance sheets net positive after the 2020–22 transfer payments, mortgage rates locked at sub-4 fixed for the majority of homeowners, equity prices near all-time highs. The cushion absorbs the oil shock for a quarter or two before it shows in the spending data. The structural risk is not the recession this quarter. It is the slow erosion of the cushion that begins to register in the data three or four quarters from now, after the consumer has worked through the inventories, the savings, and the credit-card capacity in that order.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the analysis. Structural: a late-debt-cycle reserve-currency hegemon is testing the resilience of its consumer base against an exogenous oil shock that did not begin during this quarter and will not end during the next. The advance estimate is the last clean snapshot before the price arithmetic of the war arrives in the headline series. Tactical: the 1.2-versus-2.5 nowcast-versus-consensus gap is a market-process signal that the soft-survey priors are still optimistic relative to the systematic component data. What is not load-bearing today is the political read. The administration will spin the print up or down by 30 basis points either way; the structural reading does not bend on rhetorical handling. The number prints at 8:30, the regime continues afterwards, and the Q2 release in late July is the one that will tell whether the wartime stagflation reading was right or whether the consumer absorbed more of the shock than the literature predicted.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: A structural test of the consumer cushion against a sustained exogenous oil shock that began before the print quarter ended and will continue past Q2. The advance estimate is the last clean snapshot of the US economy before the wartime price arithmetic — $4.23 pump prices, Brent at $115, sticky core CPI — fully metabolises into the headline series. The 1.2-versus-2.5 nowcast-versus-consensus gap is a market-process signal that the systematic component data is reading softer than the human prior, and that the BEA print is more likely to validate Atlanta Fed than the prediction-market crowd. The deeper structural force is the late-debt-cycle stage in which fiscal stimulus is constrained, monetary stimulus is constrained on inflation grounds, and the policy-response window for absorbing a wartime shock has narrowed to oil-supply diplomacy plus household balance-sheet runway.

What's not driving this: Recession-by-Q4 mechanics. A 1.2% Q1 print is below trend, not below zero; the household balance sheet absorbs another quarter or two before the consumption substitution effects show in the spending data. Reading the print as the prologue to a 2026 recession misses the cleaner read: it is the timestamp on a regime that erodes rather than breaks, and the breaks (if they come) are dated three to four quarters out.

What would break this reading:

  • The advance print arrives above 2.5% with March PCE consumption strong — the cushion is thicker than the literature predicts and the wartime stagflation regime has more runway than the structural reading allows.
  • The print arrives below 0.5% with sharp PCE decline — the consumer broke earlier than the cushion arithmetic implied and the recession-by-Q3 path is open.
  • A Hormuz de-escalation announcement lands inside Q2 — the structural shock removes itself faster than the literature's four-quarter persistence assumption, and the print becomes the floor of a recovery rather than the ceiling of a slowdown.
  • The auction calendar shows visible long-end demand erosion through May — the fiscal arithmetic downstream of the print breaks before the consumption arithmetic does, and the regime fails on the supply side of capital, not the demand side.

The Frankfurt Tightrope

The Governing Council of the European Central Bank decides on rates today. Markets and economist surveys converge on the hold at the 2.0% deposit-facility rate set in March; the live question is the June meeting, at which JP Morgan, Barclays, and the Goldman desk all forecast a 25-basis-point hike, pricing the bank's first move off the floor in the cycle. Eurozone CPI is running at 2.5% and the ECB's own March projection has lifted the 2026 forecast to 2.6% on the back of the Hormuz oil shock. Underneath sits a growth pattern at 0.9% — stagflation in the textbook form. The bank cannot fight both prints with one instrument. The tightrope is which one Lagarde tells the press the bank is fighting today.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Frankfurt & Berlin·30 April 2026

Frankfurt —The Governing Council meets today. The decision will be a hold; the press conference at 14:45 CET will be the substance. Lagarde walks into a room in which the headline inflation print is 2.5% and rising, the bank's own staff projection has lifted 2026 inflation to 2.6%, and growth has been marked down to 0.9% on the latest Spring Forecast. The Hormuz blockade has been in place since 13 April — the leading edge of the price shock has not finished arriving in the eurozone series. Major banks have priced 25 basis points of tightening for the June meeting at 89% on the swaps; Polymarket's yes-on-hike-in-2026 prints at the same level. The bank that spent eighteen months cutting rates from 4.0% to 2.0% is now asked to consider raising them — not because the underlying economy has accelerated, but because an exogenous oil shock has reset the inflation arithmetic the bank's mandate is bound to defend.

Read the seam. The European Central Bank, alone among major central banks, has a single mandate — price stability — defined in treaty rather than dual mandate. The Federal Reserve can and must trade off employment against inflation; the ECB cannot, formally. In a wartime stagflation, that asymmetry binds. The bank cannot say it is choosing growth; the bank can only say it is choosing the inflation horizon at which the print returns to target. The communication choice is not "do we cut or hike" — it is "which date do we name as the moment 2% reasserts itself, and how much credibility do we spend on naming it."

"Whoever fights monsters should see to it that in the process he does not become a monster." — Nietzsche, repurposed for the central-bank seat. The bank that fights an oil-driven inflation by raising rates into a 0.9% growth print risks becoming the procyclical agent of the recession its mandate does not require it to prevent. Lagarde knows the asymmetry. The press conference will be the calibration of the asymmetry.

What the swaps are pricing.

The 89% pricing of a June hike is not a forecast that the eurozone economy will overheat. It is a forecast that the bank's mandate, mechanically applied, requires a single hike to demonstrate that inflation expectations remain anchored when an oil shock pushes the headline above target. The literature on this — Bernanke's standard treatment of supply shocks, the ECB's own 2008 case study after the oil run-up — argues that monetary tightening into a supply shock is mostly counter-productive on growth without buying much on inflation, because the price shock is exogenous and runs its course. The institutional argument for the hike is different: if the bank does not move, expectations un-anchor; if expectations un-anchor, the bank's credibility — its only real tool when its rate is already low — erodes. The hike is not an inflation-fighting measure. It is a credibility-defence ritual.

Helen Thompson's reading of European political economy applies underneath. The structural pressure on the eurozone — defence rearmament accelerating, energy reconfiguration after Hormuz, demographic aging that constrains growth — does not produce the demand-side inflation a hike is calibrated to fight. It produces a supply-side cost ratchet that monetary policy can only partly absorb. The bank's instrument is the wrong one for the regime. Lagarde will not say so this afternoon. The institutional move is to communicate that the bank is doing what its mandate requires, and to leave the political question of whether the regime is monetary or fiscal for the finance ministers in the Eurogroup to take up.

The German seam.

Merz's Berlin sits inside the asymmetry as the most-exposed member-state vector. Germany is the largest net energy importer in the eurozone, the most-exposed industrial economy to the Hormuz cost shock, and the country whose chemicals, autos, and machinery sectors will absorb the second-round inflation effects through wage negotiations into 2027. A 25-basis-point hike from the ECB lands on a German economy already projecting a fifth consecutive quarter of below-trend growth, an unemployment rate at 6.4% trending up, and an industrial-production print that has not been net positive since Q3 2024. The same chancellor whose troops Trump threatened to pull on Wednesday now faces an ECB action that, mechanically, deepens the German recession he is being held responsible for. Two pressures, applied to the same office, with no synchronised institutional response available. La Rochefoucauld on the simultaneous load applies — fortune does not so much change men as bring them out.

Mearsheimer's structural read on the eurozone, less famous than his great-power one, is useful here. The currency union without fiscal union is, in the structural sense, a peacetime architecture. It works under conditions of stable energy supply, low geopolitical risk, and contained internal divergence between members. The Iran war breaks all three at once. The bank's rate decision is being asked to do work the architecture was not designed for; the institutional answer to that mismatch — eurobond issuance for joint defence, NextGenerationEU repurposed for energy resilience — exists in proposals that have not yet cleared the Council. The ECB is the only Union-level instrument operational in the current crisis. That is precisely why the press conference today is doing more institutional work than the rate decision is.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the analysis. Structural: an exogenous wartime oil shock has reset the eurozone inflation arithmetic against an underlying growth pattern at 0.9%, producing the textbook stagflation regime the currency union's architecture was not designed to manage. Tactical: the bank's communication choice today is whether to defend the credibility of the 2% target by hike-pricing into a recession, or to absorb the credibility cost by holding. The pricing of the swaps tells you the bank is expected to defend credibility. What is not load-bearing today is the rate vote itself. The hold is unanimous-on-leak; the action is in Lagarde's framing of June. The chancellor in Berlin has no instrument of his own to influence either decision; his role is to absorb the consequences of both, and to do so in front of an electorate that has already moved its support to the AfD.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: An exogenous wartime oil shock has reset the eurozone inflation arithmetic — headline at 2.5% rising, staff projection lifted to 2.6% for 2026 — against an underlying growth pattern at 0.9%. The currency union without fiscal union is a peacetime architecture; the Iran war is asking it to manage a stagflation regime its single-mandate central bank was not designed to absorb. The structural force is the architectural mismatch. The tactical force is the bank's communication choice: defend the credibility of the 2 pct target by pricing a June hike, or absorb the credibility cost by holding optionality. The 89 pct swap pricing of a June hike tells you the institutional answer the bank is being expected to produce, even though the literature on tightening into supply shocks argues most of the work the hike does is on credibility, not on the price level itself.

What's not driving this: Demand-side overheating. Eurozone aggregate demand is not running above potential; the inflation print is supply-driven through the energy channel and through second-round wage effects in industrial sectors already contracting. Reading the bank's June pricing as a normal tightening cycle misses the cleaner read: the move (if it comes) is a credibility ritual, not a demand-management one.

What would break this reading:

  • Lagarde explicitly names June as the meeting at which the bank will tighten, and the swaps repricing pushes the next-meeting probability above 95 pct — the credibility-defence reading is confirmed and the bank is on a published path that constrains its future optionality.
  • A Hormuz de-escalation announcement lands inside Q2 and headline CPI begins falling toward target — the supply shock arithmetic resolves itself and the bank can hold without paying the credibility cost.
  • A second-round wage agreement in IG Metall or in French unions clearly catches the inflation pass-through — the supply shock has metabolised into core inflation, the regime is no longer pure stagflation, and the case for the hike strengthens beyond credibility into substance.
  • A eurobond issuance for joint defence or for an energy-resilience instrument is announced inside Q3 — the architectural mismatch is being addressed at the fiscal level, and the bank's mandate is no longer doing all the institutional work alone.

Sources used: Helen Thompson on the political economy of European energy and the architectural strain a wartime oil shock places on the currency union; Bernanke and the ECB's own 2008 case study on tightening into a supply shock as a credibility move rather than an inflation-fighting one; Mearsheimer on the eurozone as peacetime architecture and the structural strain wartime regimes apply to it; La Rochefoucauld on simultaneous load (fortune does not so much change men as bring them out) for Merz's exposure inside the same week as the Trump troop threat; Munger's incentive-caused-bias frame for reading the swap pricing as institutional expectation rather than economic forecast.

Law of the Day · No. 33

Discover each man's thumbscrew.

Everyone has a weakness, a gap in the castle wall. That weakness is usually an insecurity, an uncontrollable emotion or need; it can also be a small secret pleasure. Either way, once found, it is a thumbscrew you can turn to your advantage. Find the right thumbscrew and you control the man.

Today's reading: three theatres, three thumbscrews. Trump has located Germany's structural vulnerability — the host-government dependency on a garrison it does not own — and turned the screw with a Truth Social post timed to the same afternoon Merz publicly criticised the Iran war. The thumbscrew works whether or not it is executed, because every other allied capital is now pricing the cost of public dissent. The advance estimate is the United States's own thumbscrew turned on itself: a wartime oil shock applied to a late-debt-cycle economy whose only remaining cushion is the household balance sheet, and the BEA's print at 8:30 ET is the read on how much of the cushion the first quarter has already spent. The ECB sits inside the most exquisite thumbscrew of the three — a single mandate that requires the bank to defend a price target the regime no longer permits, against a growth print the same mandate is institutionally forbidden from naming.

  • The garrison threat is a leverage instrument because the host's defence is written in bases it does not own. The screw is operational the moment it is named, not the moment it is turned.
  • The first-quarter GDP print is the timestamp on a regime that erodes rather than breaks. The screw is the slow exhaustion of the consumer cushion, three quarters out, that no political reading of the print can unwind.
  • The ECB's mandate is the institutional thumbscrew applied to itself — credibility-defence required by treaty into a recession the same treaty does not permit the bank to name as a competing concern.

The Practitioner's Note

Find the screw, name the cost.

The three stories share a single architecture at the layer below the headlines. Power locates a vulnerability in a counterpart, applies pressure, and reads the response. The pressure is the instrument; the response is the data. The asymmetry is that the screw works whether or not it is turned all the way — its operational value is in the threat, not in the consummation. Trump does not need to move a single brigade to make Merz's Wednesday silent. The wartime oil shock does not need to break consumption to register in the Q1 print. The ECB does not need to hike to spend the credibility today.

In your own affairs: when you find that someone is squeezing you, the discipline is not to win the public argument they have chosen. Refuse the chosen ground. Move the dispute to the channel where the actual decision lives. Make execution expensive to the constituencies on the other side of the screw, not to yourself. The 2020 garrison threat never started; the lobby that prevented its start is still in place. The thicker the lobby, the lighter the screw becomes — without the visible defeat that makes the screw turn harder next time.

The hard question: in the encounters of your own week, what was the screw that was being turned on you? Did you fight on the ground the prober chose, or did you make the cost of execution someone else's? And whose constituencies, on your side of the table, are thick enough to absorb the next one?
