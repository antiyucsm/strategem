The Empty Chair

Asia’s premier defence summit opens in Singapore today, and the most eloquent thing in the room is a vacancy. US Secretary of War Pete Hegseth will headline the first plenary with a speech on America’s strategy for peace in the Indo-Pacific, watched by allied ministers for any sign that Washington still means it after a year spent on the Iran war. China, for its part, has declined to send its defence minister for a second year, dispatching a delegation of uniformed scholars instead. The Stratagem reads the asymmetry, not the agenda: the power that must reassure is the power that shows up to talk; the power that feels no need to reassure sends academics and lets the chair stay empty. Presence is the tell of need. Absence, deployed deliberately, is the flex.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Singapore & Beijing·29 May 2026

Singapore —The Shangri-La Dialogue is a market in reassurance, and like any market it reveals who needs what. For two decades it has been the one venue where the United States, China, and the smaller powers of the Indo-Pacific test each other’s intentions in the same hotel ballroom. This year the test is lopsided before a word is spoken. The American secretary of war arrives to deliver the keynote and to spend three days in bilaterals soothing ministers who have watched Washington pour munitions and attention into the Middle East. Beijing sends a delegation of scholars from its National Defence University and keeps its defence minister at home. One side has come to persuade. The other has come to watch the persuading.

Begin with the structure, because it sets the asymmetry the absence exploits. The United States is the offshore balancer of Asia — the outside power that underwrites the regional order so that no single state can dominate the rest. That role rests on a promise: that American power will arrive when it is needed. The Iran war has put a question mark over the promise. A hegemon underwrites one theatre comfortably and two only with strain, and the smaller powers of the region — Manila, Hanoi, Canberra, Tokyo — read every reallocation of a carrier group as a data point about whether they will be defended or abandoned. Mearsheimer’s realism is blunt about what they do if they conclude the guarantee is hollow: they hedge toward the local giant, because in an anarchic system survival is the organising concern and proximity is destiny. Hegseth’s speech is an attempt to refill the promise with words. The audience knows that words are cheap and carriers are not.

“Be so subtle that you are invisible, so mysterious that you are intangible; then you will hold your opponent’s fate in your hands.” Sun Tzu names what Beijing is doing by not coming. China’s empty chair is not the snub of indifference; it is a calculated withdrawal. Greene’s sixteenth law — use absence to increase respect and honour — holds that what is constantly present loses value, and what withdraws becomes prized and pursued. By declining the stage, China denies the United States a counterpart to corner on the South China Sea or Taiwan, forces the Americans to argue against an empty chair, and signals to the region that it does not feel the need to explain itself. Presence is what you offer when you want something. Absence is what you can afford when you do not.

The persuader and the observed.

The worldview’s reading of power sharpens the contrast. Visible power is weaker than unseen power: the announced commitment invites the demand to prove it, while the unannounced one compounds unchallenged. Hegseth must be visible — that is the entire function of the trip — and visibility is a vulnerability, because every reassurance can be measured against the next deployment and found wanting. China’s scholars, saying nothing of consequence, leave nothing to be measured. Meanwhile the load-bearing variable in the ballroom is not capability but the collective state of the smaller powers: a region operating from anxiety perceives abandonment in ambiguity, and anxiety is contagious across a coalition. The danger for Washington is not that it loses an argument at the summit. It is that the mood of the room hardens into the assumption that the guarantee is fraying — and once that assumption sets, hedging becomes rational, and the hedging itself erodes the alliance the summit was meant to shore up.

What the chair tests.

So watch the gradations, not the communiqués. The test of Hegseth’s speech is not its applause but whether it is followed by something material — a force posture, a basing agreement, an arms package — within weeks, because only steel converts words into deterrence. The test of China’s absence is whether it reads as confidence or as contempt: absence honours the absentee only while the audience still wants him there, and a Beijing that skips the region’s forum too often risks ceding the room to the Americans by default — the inverse of the very law it is playing. And the test of the region is the bilaterals nobody photographs — whether Manila and Hanoi and Tokyo leave Singapore having quietly banked an American commitment, or having quietly decided to buy their own insurance. The summit’s headline will be Hegseth’s plenary. Its meaning will be in the chair no one sat in, and in what the watching capitals concluded from the man who came to talk and the man who did not bother.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Two layers carry it. The structural one is the United States as Asia’s offshore balancer, now visibly stretched: the Iran war has forced a reallocation of attention and munitions, and a hegemon underwrites two theatres only with strain. The variable that decides the outcome is not American hardware — that lead is intact — but allied confidence that the guarantee will arrive when called, because in an anarchic system the smaller powers hedge toward the local giant the moment they judge the distant patron unreliable. The second layer is tactical: China’s deliberate absence is a move, not a no-show. Withholding the principal denies Washington a stage opponent, signals that Beijing feels no need to explain itself, and lets American reassurance read as American need.

What's not driving this: The balance of forces. The US still fields more capability in the theatre than China; if this were a hardware contest the summit would be a formality. It is a credibility and perception contest — which is why a speech and an empty chair, not an order of battle, are the load-bearing facts.

What would break this reading:

  • China reverses and sends its defence minister or a senior political figure after all — the “absence as calculated flex” reading was wrong, and the empty chair was scheduling or weakness, not strategy.
  • Hegseth’s speech is followed within weeks by concrete posture — new basing, a major arms package, a visible deployment — and allies publicly reassured — the “words are cheap, guarantee fraying” reading was wrong, and the reassurance had steel behind it.
  • A South China Sea or Taiwan Strait incident during or just after the summit forces a kinetic American response — the “both sides bank managed stability” reading was wrong, and coercion, not theatre, is the operative mode.
  • The middle powers sign new US-aligned agreements at Singapore rather than hedging — the “anxious, hedging region” reading overstated the drift, and the guarantee was credited more than the article assumed.

Sources used: Mearsheimer’s offensive realism is the structural spine — offshore balancing, anarchy as the absence of an enforcer, and the hedging reflex of small powers toward the nearer giant when the distant guarantor looks stretched. Greene’s sixteenth law, use absence to increase respect and honour, and Sun Tzu on formlessness read China’s empty chair as a move rather than a snub. The worldview’s claim that visible power is weaker than unseen power explains why the persuader is the more exposed party, and the consciousness-state layer — the contagious anxiety of a coalition — names the variable that actually decides whether the alliance holds.

The Tenant’s Bargain

Snowflake’s stock leapt by more than a third on Wednesday on a blowout quarter and a single number: a six-billion-dollar, five-year commitment to Amazon Web Services, the largest cloud pledge the data company has ever made. Wall Street read it as a demand signal — proof that enterprises are pouring money into Snowflake’s agentic-AI tools. The Stratagem reads the other side of the ledger. Snowflake’s landlord is also its rival: AWS sells Redshift, a direct competitor, and now collects six billion dollars from the tenant it competes with, paid partly in Amazon’s own silicon. The market sees a company growing into its platform. We see a company deepening its dependence on it — and calling the leash a partnership. The applause measured the upside; the contract measured who owns the growth.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed San Mateo & Seattle·29 May 2026

San Mateo —Read the press release and you would think Wednesday was an unalloyed triumph. Snowflake beat its quarter, raised its guidance, and reported that customer spending on its platform had outrun every prior forecast; the shares rose more than thirty percent. Buried in the celebration was the structural fact that matters more than the beat: to serve that growth, Snowflake has committed six billion dollars over five years to Amazon Web Services — up from $2.5 billion in 2023 and $1.2 billion at its 2020 IPO. The company does not own the data centres its software runs in. It rents them — and it has just signed the longest, largest lease in its history with a landlord that sells a competing product down the hall.

Begin with where the rents accrue. The worldview treats coordinated capture as the normal mode of power at scale, and the cloud layer is its cleanest contemporary example. Three hyperscalers — Amazon, Microsoft, Google — own the compute on which the software economy now runs, and every application company above them, however fast it grows, grows as their tenant. Snowflake’s revenue is AWS’s revenue at one remove: the more data Snowflake’s customers query, the more Amazon collects in hosting, and this deal routes a slice of that spend into Amazon’s own Graviton processors, so the landlord captures the silicon margin too. It is the same circular pattern the worldview flagged in the frontier-AI rounds — supplier and customer bound into a single flow where each link lifts the other — only here it wears the costume of a routine procurement contract. Each step is individually rational. Read whole, the chain is a structure in which the platform owner wins at every node, and the tenant’s growth is the platform owner’s growth.

“Invert, always invert.” Munger’s rule is the discipline the market skipped on Wednesday. Do not ask why the commitment is bullish; ask what would make it a mistake. A six-billion-dollar spending pledge is, read from the other end, a six-billion-dollar fixed cost and a deepening of lock-in to a single vendor that also competes with you. It is bullish only so long as the agentic-AI demand that justifies it keeps compounding; if that demand air-pockets, the commitment becomes an obligation to a rival, sized for a boom and payable in a slowdown. The applause measured the upside. Inversion prices the thing the applause ignored.

The landlord who sells a rival product.

The sharpest lens is the worldview’s claim about dependence: the most durable hold is not coerced but voluntary, because the dependent defends the arrangement as its own choice. Snowflake is not trapped on AWS; it chose AWS, expanded on AWS, and has now bet its next five years on AWS — while AWS sells Redshift, its own data warehouse, to the very customers Snowflake is fighting for. A tenant who could in principle move to another cloud, and instead signs a larger lease with the landlord who competes with him, has converted optionality into commitment. The worldview’s rule on suppliers is exact: the supplier you cannot replace is more durable than the contract you can cancel. Snowflake can cancel the contract. It cannot, in any timeframe that matters, replace the supplier — and the deeper it builds on Amazon’s silicon, the truer that becomes. Dependence accepted as strategy is dependence still.

What the bargain tests.

So watch the metrics the press release will not headline. Watch gross margin: Snowflake’s cost of revenue is largely its cloud bill, and a $6B commitment is a wager that it can raise price and efficiency faster than Amazon raises rent — if margins compress, the lease was a trap. Watch whether the agentic-AI surge is durable usage or a one-time wave of experimentation that recedes once enterprises tally the returns; the whole capex cycle this deal is one tile of rests on that question. Watch whether Amazon leans on its dual role — pricing, integration, the quiet favouring of Redshift — now that it holds a five-year guaranteed cheque. And watch the broader tell: when a fast-growing software company’s single largest decision is how much to commit to the platform it cannot leave, the platform, not the company, is where the power sits. The market bought the growth. The growth is real. The question the market did not ask is who owns it.

The North Sea · With a Bid Deadline of June 1 Germany Re-Tenders Two Pre-Examined North Sea Offshore-Wind Sites Worth Some 2.5 Gigawatts — the Identical Sites That Drew Not a Single Bid in the August 2025 Zero-Subsidy Auction — After the Bundestag Confirmed the 2026 Round Will Run Again Under the Old Uncapped Negative-Bidding Model, With the Two-Sided Contracts-for-Difference the Industry Demands Deferred to a Structural Reform Now Pushed to 2027, One Major Utility Chief Urging the Auction Be Cancelled Outright — the No-Bid Verdict Landing as Economy Minister Katherina Reiche’s Government Scales Back Renewable and Hydrogen Ambitions Under a “Reality Check” Monitoring Report, Keeps Coal Online Longer, Pivots to New Gas Plants, and Reopens the Nuclear Exit Chancellor Friedrich Merz Now Calls a “Serious Strategic Mistake,” All Against German Industrial Power Prices Running Near Double American Levels, an Iran-War Energy Shock, and an Ifo Business Climate at Its Lowest Since the Pandemic

The North Sea

Germany will re-tender two North Sea wind sites on June 1 — the same two that drew not a single bid last August. The Bundestag has confirmed that the 2026 round runs again under the old rules — uncapped negative bidding, no contracts-for-difference — with structural reform pushed to 2027, even as developers warn the model is broken and one major utility’s chief urges scrapping the auction outright. The Stratagem reads the empty auction, not the megawatts. The bidders’ absence in August was the most honest verdict German energy policy has received: capital will not finance the flagship of the Energiewende on the terms the state still insists on. The government is re-running a failed move and calling the delay a plan.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Bremerhaven·29 May 2026

Berlin —The most eloquent thing in German energy policy this spring is a number that never appeared. Last August the government put two pre-examined North Sea sites — some 2.5 gigawatts of offshore wind — out to tender under a zero-subsidy, negative-bidding design, and received no bids at all. Not a low bid, not a disappointing one: none. On June 1 the same two sites go back out under essentially the same rules, because the Bundestag has confirmed the 2026 round will run on the old model and deferred the structural fix to 2027. An auction that failed once is being run again, unchanged, in the hope of a different result.

The structural reading is the one the worldview reaches for first, and here it is brutal. Energy is the binding constraint on German industrial power — the variable that decides whether the country’s chemical, steel, and machine-building base survives the decade. Since the severing of cheap Russian gas, German industrial electricity has run at roughly double American prices, and the Iran-war oil shock has pushed energy inflation higher still; the Ifo business-climate index sits at its lowest since the pandemic. The Energiewende — the wager that wind and solar would replace both nuclear and Russian gas — always rested on capital agreeing to build the renewable base on the state’s terms. The no-bid auction is that wager being called. Facing uncapped negative bidding, grid-connection delays, and wake-effect losses, developers concluded that at the price on offer the project does not clear. Capital did not protest. It simply did not show up — and an empty room is a more honest signal than any white paper.

“Be consistently not stupid,” Munger advises, “instead of trying to be very intelligent.” The consistently-not-stupid move after a no-bid auction is to ask what the silence is telling you and change the terms — precisely what the industry, and even a major utility chief urging cancellation, demands through two-sided contracts-for-difference that would put a floor under the investment. Instead the state re-runs the failed design and postpones the fix a year. Reality, as Munger’s tradition insists, has the final say; the no-bid is reality’s invoice, and Berlin has chosen to file it rather than pay it.

The reality check.

Watch the language, too. The government’s broader retreat from the Energiewende — lowered renewable ambitions, scaled-back hydrogen, coal kept online longer, a pivot to new gas plants, and an openly reconsidered nuclear exit Chancellor Merz now calls a “serious strategic mistake” — arrives wrapped in the authority of a “reality check” monitoring report. The phrase does real work. It reframes a politically painful reversal as a neutral, technocratic adjustment, so that a project sold for two decades as moral necessity can be quietly downsized without anyone having to own the about-face. This is the coordination layer’s signature in its mildest and most legal form: not a conspiracy but a framing, propagated to convert a U-turn into prudence. Aristotle would note the gap between the constitution a state proclaims and the one it actually operates — the proclaimed energy order is still the Energiewende; the operative one is increasingly gas, coal, and a second look at the reactors.

What the North Sea tests.

So watch June 1. If the re-run draws bids, the August failure was a pricing glitch the market has digested; if it draws silence again, the state has confirmed that its terms, not the weather, are the problem — and the 2027 reform becomes the real auction, two years late. Watch whether the contracts-for-difference the industry demands actually arrive, because a floor under the investment is the difference between a build-out and a brochure. Watch industrial electricity prices against French and American benchmarks, because that gap is the deindustrialisation clock, and every month it stays double is a month of capacity quietly deciding to expand somewhere else. Dalio’s cycle is unsentimental about what this is: a mature economy whose energy base has become a liability discovers that the political project and the structural reality have diverged, and that reality holds the deciding vote. The megawatts are not the story. The empty room is. A state can legislate a target. It cannot legislate a bid.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Two layers carry it. The structural one is energy as the binding constraint on German industrial power: since cheap Russian gas was severed, industrial electricity has run near double US prices, the Iran-war shock has worsened it, and the no-bid auction is capital’s verdict that the state’s terms simply do not clear — a capital strike, expressed as silence rather than protest. The second is institutional: the Bundestag re-runs the failed model and defers reform to 2027, while a “reality check” monitoring report launders a painful Energiewende reversal into neutral technocracy. The mechanism that decides the outcome is price — whether the state offers a floor (CfDs) capital will build on — not ideology.

What's not driving this: Green ideology. This is not a culture-war swing against climate policy; it is the cost arithmetic reasserting itself. The targets are being downsized because capital will not build at the offered terms and industry cannot pay double, not because belief changed — which is why the fix is a pricing instrument, not a manifesto.

What would break this reading:

  • The June 1 re-tender draws strong bids under the old model — the “the state’s terms are the problem” reading was wrong, and August was a one-off glitch the market has since digested.
  • Germany adopts two-sided contracts-for-difference for the 2026 round before the auction rather than waiting for 2027 — the “institutional inertia, re-run the failure” reading was wrong, and the state faced reality fast.
  • German industrial electricity prices converge toward French and US levels within the year — the “energy as binding deindustrialisation constraint” reading overstated it, and relief arrived.
  • The Energiewende targets are restored or accelerated rather than downsized — the “structural reality overrides the political project” reading was wrong, and the project still commands the policy.

Sources used: The worldview’s structural layer is the spine — energy-security politics as the binding constraint on industrial power, read through the trusted Helen Thompson lens, and Dalio’s account of a mature economy whose own base turns liability. Munger does the method: be consistently not stupid, face the no-bid as reality’s invoice, and do not re-run a move that already failed. The coordination layer reads the “reality check” framing as a mild, legal signature — a propagated phrase that converts a U-turn into prudence — and Aristotle names the gap between the constitution a state proclaims and the one it operates.

Law of the Day · No. 16

Use absence to increase respect.

Greene’s sixteenth law is the cleanest thread through Friday’s three stories: what is constantly present loses value, and what withdraws is prized and pursued. The worldview sharpens it from the other end — visible power is weaker than unseen power. The announced commitment invites the demand to prove it; the publicized red line invites the test. Presence is what you offer when you want something; absence is what you can afford when you do not, and the silence of the party who does not show up is often the most honest signal in the room. But the law carries its own warning, the inverse Greene also names: absence honours the absentee only while the audience still wants him there. Withdraw too long and you are not prized but forgotten.

Today’s reading: the chair, the lease, the auction. In Singapore, China keeps its defence minister home and sends scholars — absence as the flex of a power that feels no need to explain itself — while America’s loud reassurance betrays the doubt the reassurance is meant to cure. In San Mateo, the inverse: Snowflake surrenders the one thing that would have given it leverage — optionality, the credible ability to walk — and binds itself to its landlord for applause, mistaking presence at the table for power over it. In the North Sea, capital’s absence is the verdict: a German offshore auction that drew no bids at all, the emptiest room speaking more plainly than any target the state can legislate.

The Practitioner’s Note

The loudest thing is often the seat no one fills.

Each of Friday’s stories turns on the same instrument read three ways: who withholds presence, who throws it away, and who simply does not appear. Beijing skips the summit and lets the absence speak; the move works because confidence can afford silence and need cannot. Snowflake does the opposite — it gives up the credible ability to walk and signs a larger lease with the landlord that competes with it, converting the optionality that was its only leverage into a commitment dressed as a partnership. And the German state learns the hardest lesson of all: that the most eloquent verdict on a policy is the auction that draws no bids, the room capital declines to enter. A target can be announced. A bid cannot be commanded.

In your own affairs: the law cuts three ways. When you have power, scarcity protects it — do not be so constantly available that your presence stops being prized, and do not announce a commitment you will then be made to prove. When you are negotiating, guard your optionality as the asset it is, because the ability to walk away is the whole of your leverage and the moment you surrender it the price moves against you. And when you are reading a situation, weigh the silences: the person who did not come, the offer no one made, the bid that never arrived often tell you more than the loudest voice in the room.

The hard question: where in your week is your presence a tell of need rather than a show of strength — the meeting you took because you could not afford to miss it? Where have you traded the ability to walk for the comfort of a seat at the table, and called the leash a partnership? And whose absence have you misread as indifference when it was a verdict? Greene’s sixteenth law is the discipline — use absence deliberately, but remember it honours you only while you are still wanted, and that the silence of others is data you ignore at your cost.

“A state can legislate a target and cannot legislate a bid. Germany sold the Energiewende for two decades as the moral necessity of the age, and the policy ran on an unspoken assumption: that when the state put the sites out to tender, capital would come. Last August it put two North Sea fields out and capital did not come — not at a discount, not reluctantly, not at all. That silence is the most honest sentence in German energy policy, and the government’s answer is to run the same auction again on June 1 and call the year’s delay before reform a plan. The deeper verdict is structural: industrial electricity at double American prices is a deindustrialisation clock, and every month it ticks is a month of chemical and steel capacity quietly deciding to build somewhere else. The reactors are reconsidered, the coal kept warm, the gas plants greenlit, the renewable targets trimmed under the cover of a ‘reality check.’ The check has already cleared.
