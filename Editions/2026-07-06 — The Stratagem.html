The Riyadh Quintet

On Wednesday, July 1, Foreign Policy formally named the Middle-Eastern coordination architecture that has been visibly assembling since the March-to-April US-Israeli war on Iran: Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Turkey, Pakistan, and Egypt — a five-state consultation grouping organised around the double mandate of containing Tehran and bounding further Israeli expansion. Beneath the diplomatic layer sits a security quadrilateral (Turkey-Saudi-Egypt-Pakistan) whose material substrate is Pakistani nuclear deterrence, Saudi oil, Egyptian Suez transit, and Turkish NATO-adjacent conventional force. The United Arab Emirates is publicly absent from a Gulf architecture that would, on any conventional reading, be organised around Riyadh and Abu Dhabi together. The Quintet is the first Gulf architecture in fifty years arranged without Abu Dhabi in the room — the shape structural pressure produced in the absence of the American security guarantee the June 17 memorandum did not restore.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Riyadh, Ankara & Islamabad·06 July 2026

Riyadh —The useful thing to notice about the new Middle-Eastern quintet is not that it exists. It is who is not in it. The scaffolding has been visible for months: a Saudi-Pakistani defence framework quietly upgraded in April, Turkish-Saudi arms cooperation ratified in May, an Egyptian-Saudi bilateral on Suez transit reserves signed on June 22. Each move landed inside its own domestic news cycle; none was individually a signal. What Foreign Policy called on Wednesday was not the moment the bloc was formed; it was the moment the bloc was named. And the naming reveals the geometry — five capitals in, one out. The Emirati absence is not a scheduling accident. It is the coordination architecture disclosing which of the two natural Gulf principals has taken the seat, and which has declined it on terms the seat could not accommodate.

Begin with Mearsheimer. The hegemon that retreats from the theatre it once policed does not leave the field empty; it forces the middle powers to arrange the balancing themselves. The June 17 US-Iran memorandum reopened Hormuz and deferred the nuclear question, but the Gulf capitals reading the fine print did not read a restored American security guarantee. They read an American principal buying six months on a file it does not know how to close. The structural response is textbook offshore-balancing reversion: when the hegemon steps back, the region’s own middle powers combine to bound the ambitious neighbour. Riyadh’s arithmetic is that Saudi capacity alone cannot deter Iran; Pakistan’s nuclear arsenal, Turkey’s NATO-adjacent conventional force, Egypt’s Suez leverage, and Qatar’s mediation architecture — combined — can. The bloc is the shape structural pressure produced in the absence of a guarantor.

Then read the coordination signature. Talleyrand’s discipline on the durable coalition reads this file precisely: the coalition that will not sign the treaty is the coalition that survives the next war. The Quintet is architectural, not treaty-form — a security quadrilateral beneath a diplomatic layer that includes Doha as the mediation node. There is no founding document, no press-conference photograph of the five heads of state, no shared budget line. There are, instead, four aligned bilateral instruments landing on a shared cadence and a fifth mediator publicly holding the room. Each party retains the option to deny the bloc’s formal existence; each is operationally executing the bloc’s working thesis. This is what coordination looks like when the parties know they will be watched.

What the Quintet installs.

The Emirati absence is the coordination-layer’s second signature and the harder one to read. Abu Dhabi has spent five years positioning itself as the American principal’s preferred Gulf counterparty — the Israeli-cooperation architecture inside the Abraham Accords, the Signal-Group commercial track, and the AI compute stack that runs on Emirati sovereign capital and American chips. That posture is publicly incompatible with a bloc whose second mandate is to bound Israel. The MBZ cohort has read the file and declined membership on terms Riyadh could not soften. Greene’s twentieth law is precise: commit to no side prematurely, so that you retain the option to be the essential broker to both. The UAE is playing that hand — but the cost of the hand is that the bloc will now form without it. Whatever seat MBZ assumed he would keep inside the post-war Gulf architecture is being priced this month against the reality that the seat is publicly not there. The January weapons-supply rupture with Riyadh made the exclusion inevitable; the July naming has made it visible.

Sun Tzu’s discipline is to know the ground before you fight on it. The Quintet’s material substrate is unusually deep for a bloc formed in four months: Pakistan holds the region’s only Sunni-cohort nuclear arsenal, Saudi Arabia is the world’s second-largest oil producer, Egypt controls Suez, Turkey sits inside NATO. What the bloc does not have is the American security guarantee it is replacing, and the gap is the file’s central risk. Dugin’s Eurasianist reading of the Turkish pivot is the darker note: Erdogan’s simultaneous participation in a Riyadh-led bloc and in the standing BRICS-plus dialogue with Beijing signals a Turkey that has begun to hedge the Atlantic alliance rather than reinforce it. The Chinese offer — naval patrol arrangements, dual-use port infrastructure, tokenised trade rails — is not the American offer. It is the hedge every Gulf capital now holds against the possibility that the American offer will not be there when the next crisis requires it.

What the calendar tests.

Three variables across the next sixty days will reveal whether the Quintet is a diplomatic silhouette that dissolves under an American re-guarantee or the structural shape of the post-war Middle East. First, the US response: if the Trump administration files a public defence letter to Riyadh, restores the F-35 pipeline to the UAE, and publishes a July-cycle security-cooperation instrument that undoes the Emirati exclusion by dragging the Quintet back inside the American umbrella, the bloc has been read as insurance against a guarantor the guarantor has chosen to remain. Second, the Saudi-Iranian channel: if the Chinese-brokered March 2023 architecture is publicly rebuilt and a Riyadh-Tehran gesture inside sixty days removes containment from the bloc’s mandate, the Quintet becomes a Sunni-cohort balancing instrument without an adversary to balance against. Third, the Turkish register: if Ankara publicly ratifies a China-inclusive port arrangement inside the same window, Dugin’s reading is being operationally installed and the Quintet is being run as an Atlantic-hedge rather than an Atlantic-supplement. The historical rhyme is the Baghdad Pact of 1955 — the moment the retreating British hegemon watched a regional bloc form around Turkey, Iraq, Iran, Pakistan, and Britain against Soviet pressure. That bloc collapsed inside three years, not because its logic was wrong but because the guarantor could not sustain the commitment the arithmetic assumed. Whether the Quintet survives the American question that felled the Baghdad Pact depends less on the July naming than on the September cadence.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington, New York & San Francisco·06 July 2026

Washington —The useful thing to notice about the stablecoin yield fight is not the specific dollar figure. It is the map. The American banking system has, for ninety years, run a simple bargain: banks take deposits at short duration, lend at long duration, capture the spread, pay depositors below the funds rate, and in exchange offer FDIC insurance, payments rails, and a monetary-transmission mechanism the state cannot cheaply replace. Stablecoin issuers now offer the same product — a dollar-denominated liquid instrument — on a competitive spread that comes from something the banks structurally cannot access: routing Treasury-bill yield through directly to the holder without capturing the deposit-lending spread. The July 18 rulemaking is the moment the question of whether that arbitrage is permitted to persist becomes an operative rule.

Begin with Munger. Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome. Banks in 2026 pay depositors an average of roughly 1.5 per cent on checking-and-savings; their US deposit base is about eighteen trillion dollars. If Circle, Tether, and PayPal USD can advertise yield-bearing rewards at three to five per cent inside a permitted-issuer framework — even if the yield is technically routed through an affiliated distributor rather than the issuer itself — a fraction of that base migrates. Fifteen per cent migration is 2.7 trillion dollars out of core banking. Munger’s inversion cuts hard: what breaks the American banking model in 2026 is not credit risk, capital ratios, or an exotic regulatory arbitrage; it is a monetary substitute that offers the same product at a better price on a technology stack that scales differently from the branch network. Banks have known this since the money-market fund episode of the early 1980s. They know how to lobby against it.

Then read the coordination signature. The GENIUS Act itself was passed in July 2025 under heavy stablecoin-issuer lobbying — Circle, Coinbase, Kraken, PayPal, joined by a hyperscaler cohort that runs the tokenisation rails on which the framework operates. The implementing regulations, due July 18, are being written inside a Treasury-OCC-FDIC triangle where the incumbent bank lobby has spent six months elevating the “yield-loophole” frame at every consultation. The CLARITY Act compromise — rewards permitted on transactional activity, prohibited on idle balances — is not a substantive line. It is a coalition line. Banks want it moved to “no rewards”; issuers want it left where it is. The Senate delay past July 4 is that coalition’s public deadlock. What looks like a technical regulatory question is, at the higher level, a coordination battle between two incumbent networks for which one gets to intermediate the American deposit base for the next decade.

What the Loophole reveals.

Greene’s laws on the specific thumbscrew read the file exactly. The stablecoin lobby has spent 2025-2026 surrendering on the issues that do not matter — 1:1 reserves, monthly audits, restrictions on foreign issuers, permitted-issuer perimeters — in exchange for the framework that permits them to operate at all. Law 22: surrender in the small becomes command of the large. The banks have identified the yield-loophole as the specific thumbscrew that permits the largest migration; Law 33: discover each man’s thumbscrew, and press it. The July 18 rulemaking will decide whose reading of “rewards on activity” becomes operative. If the language survives with the issuer-friendly reading, the affiliated-distributor architecture Coinbase and Circle have already pre-built goes live inside Q3. If the language is tightened to bar rewards inside any affiliated structure, the migration slows and the CLARITY Act becomes cosmetic. Fitts’s financial-plumbing discipline is the harder read: the tokenisation rails, the stablecoin regulatory architecture, the Treasury PIPE, and the BlackRock IBIT compound sit inside a single system where the same equity pools benefit at every step. The lobby fight is visible; the pool underneath is not.

The historical rhyme is 1980. The Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act killed the Regulation-Q interest-rate ceiling that had let money-market funds outrun banks through the late 1970s. The Reagan-era compromise gave the banks a decade to adjust; they used it to build the interest-rate-derivatives book, the securitisation architecture, and the fee-income substitutes that carried them into the 1990s. Aristotle’s discipline on the polity is the second reading. A monetary system in which the state permits two competing deposit-taking architectures to operate on materially different rules is not neutral; it is choosing which one wins. The GENIUS Act framework is the choice, taken. The July 18 rulemaking is the choice’s ratification. The banking system is being told, on the same schedule that the 1980 Act told the thrift industry, that it has a decade to adjust — or a shorter one, depending on how the rewards language survives.

What the calendar tests.

Three variables across the next ninety days will reveal whether the Loophole is the crack banks say it is or the safety valve issuers say it is. First, the July 18 final rule: if the Treasury-OCC-FDIC triangle publishes a joint rulemaking that permits activity-based rewards through an affiliated-distributor perimeter, the issuer reading has won and migration begins inside Q3; if the rule prohibits any structure that indirectly returns yield to the holder, the bank reading has won and CLARITY becomes a cosmetic instrument. Second, the CLARITY signing calendar: if the Senate parliamentarian clears the reconciliation text and a floor vote lands before Labor Day, the framework becomes durable; if the July delay lengthens into a September delay, the political capital that carried GENIUS in 2025 begins to age. Third, the Fed cycle: if the July FOMC surprises with dovish language and Q4 cuts price in, the yield-arbitrage spread compresses on its own and the migration pressure eases before the regulatory battle resolves; if the dot plot holds at the hawkish June median, the arbitrage widens and the lobby fight sharpens. The base case is that activity-based rewards survive inside a narrow perimeter, that Coinbase and Circle route around the perimeter through affiliated distributors, and that the migration begins slowly and compounds. The polity discovers, ex post, that it has already quietly chosen.

From the Desk of Continental Affairs·Filed Wolfsburg, Hanover & Berlin·06 July 2026

Wolfsburg —The useful thing to notice about Thursday’s vote is not that it will happen. It is what has already been priced into the room the board will enter. Volkswagen’s supervisory board is not a corporate board in the American sense; it is a constitutional instrument that has been running for six decades on the assumption that German auto exports would continue to underwrite German industrial employment. Both sides of that assumption are, publicly and for the first time in sixty years, failing simultaneously. Cheap Russian pipeline gas — cut in 2022. Access to the Chinese consumer market at scale — BYD and NIO have absorbed the mid-market Volkswagen priced against for two decades. Access to the American consumer market at scale — the four billion euros of annual US tariff cost holds. The board that meets Thursday to vote on one hundred thousand redundancies is voting on how to reduce the size of a firm whose structural position is deteriorating faster than it can restructure.

Begin with the structural reading. Zeihan on the German model is exact: post-1990 Germany rebuilt its industrial base on three enabling conditions, all of them external. All three have moved against Germany inside the last thirty months and none is reversible on a four-year horizon. What Volkswagen is being asked to do this quarter is not a corporate restructuring in the ordinary sense — a manager decides the labour force is one-fifth too large and cuts one-fifth. It is a corporate restructuring under the premise that even the reduced firm will not, at 2026 cost curves, be competitive against a Chinese domestic industry whose labour costs are one-fifth German labour costs and whose EV platform runs a technology lead the German cohort has publicly acknowledged. The Merz Programme announced on July 3 — ten billion euros of tax relief targeted at lower and middle-income earners — was Chancellor Merz’s answer to the wider economic-drift file. The Wolfsburg vote is what the Merz Programme cannot answer. Fiscal transfers can cushion the wage-earner who loses his job. They cannot restore the cost curve that made the job viable.

Then read the coordination signature. Volkswagen is not Renault, not Fiat, not General Motors. Twenty per cent of the voting shares are held by the state of Lower Saxony under the 1960 Volkswagen Law — a founding constitutional instrument that has survived four European Court of Justice challenges and remains in force. Roughly fifty-three per cent is held by the Porsche Automobil Holding SE controlled by the Piech and Porsche families. The supervisory board’s Mitbestimmung structure gives IG Metall functional veto power over any decision the union cohort collectively opposes. Four cohorts — the Piech-Porsche family, the Blume executive cohort, the IG Metall works council, and the Weil-led Lower Saxony state government — must all publicly ratify the same instrument for the restructuring to hold. No combination of three of the four is sufficient. This is what makes Volkswagen a national asset in the Lee Kuan Yew sense and a national problem in the same sense. A firm the state cannot let fail is a firm the state must eventually intervene inside.

What the Vote reveals.

Greene’s twenty-second law reads the tactical layer. Surrender in the small becomes command of the large. IG Metall has publicly refused the surrender — “we would prevent them with all our might” — and this is exactly what the union’s role in the Mitbestimmung architecture requires. The maximal announcement is the negotiating anchor, not the negotiated settlement. Historically — the 2004 Wolfsburg “5000 x 5000” cycle, the 2014 Kassel restructuring, the 2018 Emden electrification transition — the pattern has been that the initial number is set at the ceiling, the union bargains it down thirty to fifty per cent, the plant closures shrink to two rather than four, the job cuts land at sixty thousand rather than one hundred thousand, and both sides publicly declare victory. The July 9 vote will authorise the maximal cut on the assumption that the actual number will be renegotiated inside the Q3 works-council cycle. But this pattern held while the Chinese market compensated. In the sixty years the Mitbestimmung architecture has operated, no German auto restructuring has been executed against a permanently deteriorating structural position. The 2004 cycle was resolved because the Chinese market was still growing. This cycle does not have that solvent.

The historical rhyme is British Leyland 1975. The Wilson Labour government nationalised the collapsing British auto conglomerate on the reasoning that it was too large to fail and too structural to disaggregate. The state ran BL for eleven years, poured seven billion pounds of 1975 money into it, and eventually broke it up under Thatcher at the moment the political capital ran out. Peter Piech’s own 1993 Volkswagen restructuring is the closer German precedent. Piech inherited a firm losing 1.9 billion Deutschmarks a year and forced through the Mitbestimmung architecture a Vierertagewoche — four-day working week — that cut labour cost thirty per cent without a single named redundancy. The Piech instrument required the union’s active co-authorship; the Blume instrument, as currently framed, does not have that. The Vierertagewoche instrument is on the table this week inside IG Metall’s counter-proposal, but the executive cohort has publicly signalled that it will not carry the requisite hours cut this cycle. The gap between what the union will accept and what the executive believes is required is what Thursday’s vote will publicly register.

What the calendar tests.

Three variables across the next ninety days will reveal whether Thursday’s vote produces the negotiated compromise the sixty-year Mitbestimmung architecture is engineered for or the constitutional-industrial crisis the current structural position points toward. First, the Weil-cohort posture: if the Lower Saxony government publicly bridges between IG Metall and the executive cohort on a Vierertagewoche instrument, the compromise geometry holds; if Weil publicly aligns with the union’s refusal of any plant closure, the vote fails and the board reconvenes in September under a different frame. Second, the Merz-cohort response: if the Chancellor announces a Wolfsburg-specific industrial-policy instrument inside two weeks — targeted energy-cost relief, a Sektor-Kaufhaus procurement architecture, an EV-battery-cluster subsidy that inherits the closed plant footprint — the Merz Programme is being extended to cover the restructuring; if the response is silence, fiscal is being told to stay on its side of the constitutional line and the industrial reckoning is being left to the corporate architecture alone. Third, the China cohort: if Beijing signals inside the same window a market-access gesture that returns even three points of Volkswagen’s Chinese market share, the structural position marginally improves and the maximal cut becomes politically unnecessary; if the Chinese domestic industry consolidates further at Volkswagen’s expense, the July 9 announcement will be re-run at higher scale in September. The base case is a negotiated settlement at roughly seventy thousand cuts and two closures — but on a structural position that will require the same restructuring again in 2028.

Law of the Day · Munger on inversion

Invert the assumption before the assumption inverts you.

The discipline is Munger’s and it is the most-taught, least-practised idea in Poor Charlie’s Almanack. Invert, always invert. Take the assumption that has carried the system for a generation and ask: what if it stopped being true tomorrow? The question is not idle. Almost every crisis of the last two centuries has been the story of a system built on an assumption that quietly changed while the participants continued to price as though it had not. Empires assume their guarantors will remain. Firms assume the cost curve that made them competitive will continue. Households assume the wages that carried the mortgage will keep rising. The moment the assumption inverts is the moment the participants discover which of their arrangements were engineered against the change and which were engineered inside the assumption that was quietly disappearing.

Today’s reading: the Riyadh Quintet, the Yield Loophole, the Wolfsburg Vote. Each is a system whose founding assumption has publicly stopped being true and whose participants are being forced, this quarter, to price the reality without the assumption. In Riyadh, the assumption that the American security guarantee would remain continuous. In Washington, the assumption that the ninety-year bank-deposit oligopoly would remain the only lawful intermediary of the dollar. In Wolfsburg, the assumption that German industrial employment could be sustained on external demand and cheap external inputs.

  • The Riyadh Quintet is the inversion at the great-power register — the middle powers combining because the underwriter has quietly ceased continuous underwriting.
  • The Yield Loophole is the inversion at the monetary register — a substitute for the bank deposit that offers the same product at a better price on a technology stack the incumbent cannot replicate.
  • The Wolfsburg Vote is the inversion at the industrial register — the sixty-year employment compromise being asked to run against a cost curve it was never engineered to run against.

— Question for the Day —

Three operations on Monday’s calendar, each a different register of the same architecture: a durable arrangement whose founding assumption has quietly inverted while the participants continued to price as though it had not. The Riyadh Quintet is what happens when middle powers stop assuming a continuous American security guarantee. The Yield Loophole is what happens when banks stop being the only lawful intermediary of the dollar deposit base. The Wolfsburg Vote is what happens when a sixty-year industrial-employment compromise is asked to run against a cost curve it was never engineered against. Apply the test to your own week — name the three assumptions underneath your professional position, your household arrangement, and your investment book that you have not stress-tested against a change in the underwriter. Which of them is quietly inverting on a calendar that has not yet forced you to notice? Munger’s discipline is old and unfashionable: invert, always invert; identify the assumption you would be most embarrassed to have missed if it inverted; test whether the arrangement you have priced against its continuance survives the test. The observer who identifies the inverting assumption early is behaving prudently; the observer who quietly positions against the inversion while loudly advocating that the assumption still holds is committing the specific historical crime each generation is asked to name. Ask yourself which side of that line you are standing on before you deploy the discipline.
