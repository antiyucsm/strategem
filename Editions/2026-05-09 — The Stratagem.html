Lead Analysis · Taiwan's Opposition-Led Parliament Approves a $25 Billion US-Arms Package and Bargains the President Down to Sixty-Two Percent of His Ask

The Taipei Appropriation

Taiwan's Legislative Yuan on Friday afternoon passed the Special Act for Safeguarding National Security and Strengthening Asymmetric Combat Capabilities Procurement, authorising NT$780 billion — about $25 billion — for the purchase of United States military equipment over the coming budget cycle. The bill was put forward jointly by the opposition Kuomintang and the Taiwan People's Party rather than by President Lai Ching-te's DPP government; only 107 lawmakers were present at the third reading and 59 voted in favour. The procurement envelope as passed is roughly sixty-two percent of the NT$1.25 trillion supplementary defence package the Lai administration had requested. The KMT-TPP authorship is the story under the story: the parties favoring closer ties with Beijing are the parties writing the cheque to Lockheed and Raytheon, after months of stalling Lai's larger ask and absorbing public pressure from a US senator's late-April push for a "comprehensive" budget. The procurement list reportedly carries HIMARS, additional artillery, missile platforms and air-defence systems. The vote is a deterrent posture written by the people who least want the deterrent confrontation it preserves the option of.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Taipei & Washington·9 May 2026

Taipei —A defence appropriation reveals the parties through which it is passed more cleanly than the system it claims to defend. President Lai Ching-te's government had asked the Legislative Yuan for NT$1.25 trillion — roughly $40 billion — in a special supplementary defence package, the largest single procurement request in the island's history, structured to absorb the Pentagon's pending Foreign Military Sales pipeline and to stand up an asymmetric-defence inventory of one-way drones, mobile air defence, coastal missile platforms and command-and-control survivability. What the chamber passed on Friday afternoon, on a third reading, was a different bill: NT$780 billion, roughly $25 billion, ring-fenced for US-arms purchases only, drafted and sponsored by the Kuomintang and the Taiwan People's Party rather than by Lai's DPP. The bill is the deterrent the opposition was prepared to write. It is also a public bargaining-down of the president's posture by a parliament whose majority parties stand for the lighter cross-strait posture the bill itself materially extends.

Read the geometry of the move. KMT-TPP holds the Yuan majority and has spent the cycle since the 2024 election compressing Lai's executive room — freezing parts of the regular defence budget, recalling DPP legislators, contesting the constitutional framework of the office. A $40 billion ask was inadmissible to that majority; a zero outcome was inadmissible to the United States, which spent April publicly pressing the chamber through Senator Pat Toomey's successor and the AIT to pass a "comprehensive" budget before the summer recess. The compromise is the answer to both pressures. The opposition writes the cheque, captures the political credit for "responsible" defence spending against the DPP's "excessive" demand, denies Lai the political dividend of having staged the package itself, and lands roughly sixty-two percent of the Pentagon's preferred envelope. Lai signs because the alternative is the zero outcome. Beijing reads the cheque rather than the speeches.

"Hence, to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting." — Sun Tzu, The Art of War, III. The Taiwan deterrent has always been an instrument of the supreme-excellence reading rather than the battlefield-victory one. The point of the HIMARS-and-Stinger-and-Harpoon procurement is not to win an amphibious war; it is to make the war structurally too costly for Beijing to choose. A $25 billion package landed under sponsorship of the parties most accommodating to Beijing is, perversely, more strategically credible than a $40 billion package landed under Lai's sponsorship alone — the former survives a change of government, the latter is contestable on day one of the next election.

The opposition seam.

Mearsheimer on the structural logic of small-power survival under a great-power neighbour is the cleanest first overlay. A small state on the seam of a continental power's geographic ambition has two postures available — accommodation, which trades sovereignty for time, or balancing, which trades autonomy through alliance for the cost of being the alliance's forward edge. Taiwan's domestic politics has carried both postures inside the same parliament since 2000: DPP balancing through US arms and identity politics, KMT accommodation through cross-strait economics and the 1992 Consensus. What Friday's vote does is collapse the binary. The KMT-TPP majority has just signed a balancing-instrument cheque — the first time in the parliamentary record that the party of accommodation has authored the procurement of weapons the doctrine of accommodation does not require. The reading is not that the KMT has converted to balancing. The reading is that the cost of being seen to refuse the package — to the United States, to the Taiwanese median voter, to the post-Pelosi-visit deterrent calendar — has exceeded the cost of being seen to write it.

Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of small-state defence procurement is the practitioner overlay. Singapore under LKY built the SAF on the Israeli model — total defence budget at five percent of GDP, conscription, structural deterrence priced as the necessary tax on sovereignty rather than as a political luxury. Taiwan under Lai had asked for the equivalent commitment as a single supplementary, on the calculation that an emergency posture against the 2027 PLA threshold required the political compression of multiple budget cycles into one. The opposition's counter is the slower discipline: regular procurement, US-arms-only, no domestic-industry pork, no executive-branded headline number. Both are recognisable defence postures. The compromise is structurally closer to the LKY discipline than to the Lai emergency. What it sacrifices is the political-narrative effect that an emergency package would have produced for Lai's domestic standing.

The Beijing question.

Confucius on the rectification of names is the institutional read on what Beijing receives. The KMT had spent the cycle naming Lai's package "war provocation" and "fiscal irresponsibility"; on Friday afternoon the same caucus voted through a sixty-two-percent version of the package and named it "responsible national security." The names have been rectified. The substance has not. Beijing now confronts a Taiwan whose accommodation-leaning parliamentary majority has just procured the asymmetric-defence inventory the accommodation framework was supposed to make unnecessary, and the political signal is that the cross-strait middle ground in Taiwanese domestic politics has been narrowed by the cost of refusing US pressure. La Rochefoucauld: hypocrisy is the homage that vice pays to virtue. The KMT-TPP authorship of the bill is the homage that the accommodation posture pays to the deterrent posture. Greene's Twenty-First Law — play a sucker to catch a sucker — runs in reverse here: Lai's apparent loss (sixty-two percent of the ask) converts into the structural win (a balancing posture written into law by the parties who would have repealed an executive-only version on the next election day).

Dugin on the Eurasian land-power's reading of the maritime-power's forward edge is the structural overlay on Beijing's options. Xi's Politburo cannot read the bill as anything other than confirmation that the political space inside Taiwan for accommodation has narrowed under sustained US pressure. The PLA reform purges of 2024-2025 and the institutional pre-Party-Congress signalling cycle have already constrained Beijing's near-term escalation envelope; the bill does not raise the probability of a 2026 invasion or blockade — those probabilities are governed by Beijing's own readiness curve, not by Taipei's. What the bill raises is the cost of the eventual confrontation when the readiness curve allows it. Mearsheimer is correct that the structural pressure for Chinese reunification continues to compound; the bill is correct that the cost of acting on that pressure has just been raised by another increment.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: a small power on the seam of a continental neighbour's geographic ambition has converted its parliamentary majority — the majority structurally inclined to accommodation — into the author of a balancing-instrument procurement, on the political calculation that the cost of refusing the package now exceeds the cost of writing it. Coordination: the United States, the AIT, and the Pentagon's FMS pipeline have together compressed the Taiwanese parliamentary timeline through the spring, and the bill is the institutional output of that compression as filtered through the KMT's electoral incentive to author the package rather than to be seen blocking it. What is not driving this is a Lai administration political triumph; the bill is sixty-two percent of the ask, the headline number is the opposition's, the political dividend is the opposition's, and the executive branch is the actor whose preferred frame the chamber has just disposed of. The Stratagem reads the next twelve months as the validation window — whether the procurement actually clears the FMS pipeline, whether the systems actually arrive on the asymmetric-defence timetable the doctrine assumes, and whether the cross-strait posture absorbs the bill without acceleration. The cheque has cleared the chamber. The deterrent is now an institutional fact rather than a presidential aspiration.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: A small power on the geographic seam of a continental neighbour's ambition has just converted its accommodation-leaning parliamentary majority into the author of a balancing-instrument procurement, on the political calculation that the cost of refusing the package — to the United States, to the Taiwanese median voter, to the post-Pelosi-visit deterrent calendar — has exceeded the cost of writing it. The structural mechanism is that the cross-strait middle ground in Taiwanese domestic politics has been narrowed under sustained US pressure to the point that even the parties electorally invested in accommodation must publicly own a US-arms package or be seen blocking it. The coordination layer is the FMS pipeline, AIT communications and Senate-level US public pressure compressing the Taiwanese parliamentary timeline through the spring. The substantive cost of the bill — sixty-two percent of the ask, no domestic-industry envelope, KMT authorship — is the political price the executive paid for the procurement landing on a timetable Beijing reads as binding rather than aspirational.

What's not driving this: A near-term shift in the cross-strait kinetic probability. PLA reform purges, the pre-Party-Congress signalling cycle, and Beijing's economic constraints govern the near-term escalation envelope; the bill raises the cost of the eventual confrontation rather than altering the near-term probability of it. Reading the appropriation as a 2026-invasion-trigger or as a Lai political triumph misses the cleaner read: it is a structural cost-imposition by Taipei on a Beijing readiness curve that runs on its own clock, written by the parties least invested in writing it.

What would break this reading:

  • The KMT-TPP caucus moves in the next 90 days to delay the appropriation's contracting timeline through Yuan oversight procedures — the political-credit-claim was the move the bill executed and the implementation discipline does not in fact match the authorship discipline.
  • Beijing announces a major PLA exercise or Taiwan Strait closure in the next 60 days specifically referencing the bill — the readiness-curve-versus-cost-imposition framing is wrong, the bill did move the kinetic-probability needle, and the assumption that Beijing's calendar is governed by its own readiness rather than by Taipei's procurement has misread the move.
  • The US Pentagon FMS pipeline cannot deliver the prioritised inventory before the 2027 PLA-readiness threshold the doctrine assumes — the appropriation produces the deterrent posture without producing the deterrent capability, and the bill's structural value is reduced to the political-signal value alone.
  • Lai is impeached or otherwise removed in the post-bill cycle on the back of the headline-loss reading inside the DPP — the executive-strength assumption was wrong and the structural-survival-of-bipartisan-procurement framing carried a hidden cost the article did not price.

Sources used: Mearsheimer on small-power survival under a great-power neighbour for the structural posture-choice between accommodation and balancing, read on the inversion of the cross-strait reading the KMT had carried for two decades; Sun Tzu on supreme excellence as breaking resistance without fighting, for the deterrent-not-victory function of the procurement; Lee Kuan Yew on small-state defence procurement discipline for the regular-budget-versus-emergency-supplement reading the compromise structurally lands on; Confucius on rectification of names for the KMT-TPP authorship contradiction the bill institutionalises; La Rochefoucauld on hypocrisy as homage paid by vice to virtue, for the accommodation party's authorship of the balancing instrument; Greene's Twenty-First Law on play-a-sucker-to-catch-a-sucker, read in inverted form against Lai's apparent loss; Dugin on the Eurasian land-power's reading of the maritime-power's forward edge for Beijing's institutional reception of the bill.

The Cloudflare Cull · A Record-Revenue Quarter Is Paired With a Twenty-Percent Workforce Cut, and the Frame the CEO Reaches For Is the One Every CEO Will Soon Borrow

The Cloudflare Cull

Cloudflare's first-quarter print on Thursday afternoon was a beat on every visible line — revenue of $639.8 million against $635 million consensus, up 34 percent year-on-year; non-GAAP EPS of $0.25 against $0.22 expected; record gross margin and free-cash-flow figures. The print was paired with a memo from CEO Matthew Prince and co-founder Michelle Zatlyn announcing the immediate termination of approximately 1,100 employees, roughly twenty percent of headcount and the first mass layoff in the company's sixteen-year history. The framing was not a cost-reduction framing. “Today's actions are not a cost-cutting exercise or an assessment of individuals' performance; they are about Cloudflare defining how a world-class, high-growth company operates and creates value in the agentic AI era.” Internal AI usage, the memo noted, had increased by more than six hundred percent in the prior three months. The stock fell eighteen to twenty-four percent on the news. The frame Prince reached for — record revenue paired with twenty-percent workforce reduction in the name of the “agentic AI era” — is the frame every CEO of a similar-sized public-company tech business will borrow inside the next two earnings cycles. The cull is the move. The frame is the contract.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed San Francisco & New York·9 May 2026

San Francisco —A layoff is a reading of the labour contract. The reading says: the work the worker did is no longer worth the wage at which the worker did it. What changes across cycles is the framing the company offers for why the wage no longer clears. The 2008 frame was demand collapse. The 2022-2023 frame was over-hiring and rate normalisation. The 2026 frame Prince has just put on the page is qualitatively new: the work itself no longer requires a worker. Cloudflare did not lay off 1,100 people because revenue fell. Revenue rose 34 percent. The company laid off 1,100 people because revenue rose without the corresponding labour input the prior production function priced. Internal AI agent sessions ran across engineering, HR, finance and marketing at six-hundred-percent quarter-on-quarter growth, and the marginal product of the marginal worker fell inside the same quarter that the marginal product of the company rose. The labour contract has not been violated. The labour contract has been priced out.

Read the geometry of the cycle Cloudflare sits inside. Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, Oracle, Block, Snap, IBM, Salesforce, Upwork — each has run an "AI-driven restructuring" through 2025 and the first half of 2026, each one publicly attributing some share of the headcount reduction to AI-substitution rather than to demand. The share attributed to AI in tech-sector layoffs has crossed forty-eight percent in the year-to-date Layoffs.fyi tally. The aggregate is not subtle: Q1 2026 alone has produced ninety-five thousand US tech-sector layoffs across two hundred and forty-eight events, on pace to exceed the full-year 2025 figure inside half the calendar. Cloudflare's framing is therefore not an outlier. It is the consensus frame in cleaner language than the consensus had previously chosen to use. The Duke-Federal Reserve survey of seven hundred and fifty CFOs last quarter reported that more than eighty percent of those firms had measured zero productivity gain from AI deployment to date, and that the same firms projected approximately five hundred thousand AI-attributed US job cuts in 2026 — nine times the 2025 figure. Each link is independently sourced. The chain reads as a single coordinated framing.

"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome." — Charlie Munger. The incentive Cloudflare's framing reveals is the public-company CEO's incentive to package any twenty-percent headcount reduction as agentic-AI substitution rather than as cyclical demand softness, productivity overhang, or strategic misallocation. The first three framings would each cost the multiple. The fourth raises it — at Block and Oracle, the stock rallied on the announcement; at Cloudflare it fell, but the post-print consensus revisions ratcheted up the FY26 EPS curve. The frame is a tax shelter for the multiple. The eighty-percent of CFOs who report zero productivity gain are reporting the input. The frame is the output the input is being asked to fund.

The productivity seam.

Munger on incentive-caused bias is the first overlay. Every public-company CEO has now seen the same data: the labour line is the largest controllable cost in a software business, the multiple is determined by the operating-margin trajectory, and the public framing of an AI-driven cull preserves the multiple while the private framing of a demand-driven cull would compress it. The framing is not lying. AI tools are real, agent sessions are running, productivity gains exist somewhere on the distribution. What is being priced is not the gain — it is the frame the gain enables the CEO to use. La Rochefoucauld is the cleaner read: we confess our small faults to persuade others that we have no large ones. The small fault Cloudflare confesses is "twenty percent of the workforce was doing work that does not require humans"; the large fault the framing forecloses is "twenty percent of the workforce was hired during the cycle to deliver the revenue that has now matured into the cash flow the company would not survive without". The first reading is forward-looking and multiple-positive. The second reading is backward-looking and multiple-negative. Each public-company CEO with a similar headcount overhang now has the framing template to choose the first.

Dalio's big-cycle frame gives the macro overlay. The labour-share-of-output curve in the post-1980 US has compressed in every cycle in which capital has been able to substitute technology for labour at the marginal cost the cycle's interest-rate environment supports. The 2026 cycle is particular: the substitution technology is general-purpose, the substitution surface covers cognitive rather than manual labour, and the political-institutional response — taxation of AI productivity, retraining infrastructure, social-insurance reform — is structurally absent under the current US administration's deregulation posture. The labour share will compress under the substitution; the political question is whether the institutional response builds inside the cycle or after it. Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of building human capital ahead of the substitution curve is the practitioner overlay: a society that does not retrain through the substitution wave produces the political coalition the next cycle's politics is built on. The Hartlepool Sweep is one form of that coalition; the AfD polling rebalance in the German East is another. The substitution is not abstract. The political consequence is not abstract either.

The recession question.

Voss on tactical empathy is the read on what the labour market currently registers. April US unemployment held at 4.3 percent. Initial jobless claims for the week ending 2 May printed at two hundred thousand — historically benign. Nonfarm payrolls expanded by 115,000. The aggregate labour market has not yet absorbed the AI-substitution wave the corporate framing is announcing. The gap is the gap between announced cuts (forward-looking, attributed to the framing) and absorbed cuts (lagging, distributed across severance windows, contractor reclassifications, and the cohorts that absorb the displacement first — entry-level, recent graduates, mid-career white-collar). The macroeconomic instrument that is most exposed to this gap is the Federal Reserve. Powell's hold at 3.50-3.75 percent through the April 30 meeting was justified on inflation. The same hold under a labour market that is being structurally compressed by AI substitution becomes harder to defend each quarter the substitution accelerates without the unemployment rate registering it. The Fed's reaction function is calibrated for cyclical labour-market signal; the AI-substitution wave runs underneath the cyclical signal. The mismatch is the institutional risk the next four quarters carry.

Greene's Sixth Law — court attention at all cost — is the doctrine Cloudflare's framing materially executes. The 1,100-person cull is small in aggregate dollar terms next to the company's revenue base; what the cull buys is the position of being the framing that other CEOs reference when they reach for their own. Prince's memo will be quoted in twenty earnings calls between now and August. Each subsequent CEO who borrows the “agentic AI era” frame extends the legitimacy of the frame and lowers the political cost of the next round of cuts in the same form. The dynamic is the dynamic Munger named in 2010 on the corporate-governance mimicry of stock-buyback patterns: the framing template is adopted because it has been adopted, the adoption itself is the evidence the adopter cites, and the marginal CEO who would have framed the cull differently absorbs the political cost of the deviation. The Cloudflare cull is the move that lands the framing as consensus. The framing is the structural fact the cycle now compounds against.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: a substitution technology of general-purpose scope is compressing the labour-share-of-output curve inside the same operating-margin cycle the equity multiple is priced on, and a public-company CEO can now publicly attribute any twenty-percent headcount reduction to that substitution at zero multiple cost rather than the multiple penalty the prior cyclical framings would have imposed. Coordination: the framing is being adopted across a coordinated set of public-company CEOs in the same form within the same quarter, the eighty-percent-zero-productivity-gain CFO finding is not displacing the framing, and the political-institutional response that would normally tax or redistribute the gains is absent under the current administration. What is not driving this is a Cloudflare-specific demand collapse; the print is the strongest in the company's sixteen-year history. The Stratagem reads the next four quarters as the institutional-coordination window — whether the framing holds across enough public-company cycles to land as consensus, whether the labour market registers the cumulative cuts in the unemployment rate, and whether the Federal Reserve's reaction function is recalibrated to the AI-substitution wave rather than the cyclical labour signal it currently runs on. The cull has been booked. The political cost the framing makes invisible is what the cycle compounds against next.

The Bundeswehr Census · Twenty-Eight Percent of German Eighteen-Year-Old Men Have Failed to Return the Mandatory Military-Service Questionnaire and the Fines Have Begun

The Bundeswehr Census

The Wehrdienstreform took effect on 1 January 2026: every German man turning eighteen in the calendar year must complete and return a mandatory questionnaire on health, education, fitness and motivation to serve in the Bundeswehr. The first compliance window closed at the end of April. The numbers released this week are the first the system has produced. Twenty-eight percent of the eligible cohort — roughly one in four young men of conscription age — failed to return the questionnaire. The Defence Ministry has now begun issuing administrative fines to non-respondents. Of the responding seventy-two percent, roughly half indicated willingness to serve voluntarily for six months or longer. Total Bundeswehr applications through Q1 2026 stood at sixteen thousand, twenty percent above the same period of 2025; first-quarter inductions reached five thousand-plus, fourteen percent up year-on-year. Defence Minister Boris Pistorius's public benchmark, restated in Düsseldorf this week, remains 260,000 active and 460,000 combined active-and-reserve by 2031. The conscription-as-fallback clause in the same law triggers automatically if the voluntary route fails the benchmark. The 28 percent refusal is not yet a constitutional crisis. It is the first quantitative reading of the political price the Zeitenwende has accumulated since 2022, and the price has just become countable.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Bonn·9 May 2026

Berlin —A conscription census is the cleanest instrument an industrial state has ever built for measuring its civic contract. The questionnaire is not a draft; it is a piece of administrative data — name, address, fitness, education, willingness to serve. The act of completing it carries no obligation beyond the data itself. The act of refusing it carries a fine and a small but rising legal cost. The 28 percent refusal rate is therefore not a measurement of the German military's recruiting position. It is a measurement of the proportion of eighteen-year-old German men who, when asked to commit even the minimal civic act of returning a form to the federal Defence Ministry, would prefer to pay the fine. The number is the political price of the Zeitenwende, paid in the cohort that is being asked to fight the war the Zeitenwende was declared to deter.

Read the geometry of the cohort. The post-Cold-War German social contract has run on the assumption that the Bundeswehr is a managed institution rather than a binding obligation — voluntary since 2011, structurally underfunded through the Merkel cycles, framed as one career path among many rather than as a duty owed. The 2022 Zeitenwende speech, the €100 billion special fund, the NATO 2-percent threshold-crossing, and the post-2024 Pistorius reform programme have collectively raised the institutional capability the Bundeswehr is being asked to deliver — 260,000 active by 2031 against the present 181,000, an additional 200,000 reservists, the new heavy-brigade-in-Lithuania commitment, the Czech-Slovak-Baltic integration. None of those institutional moves alters the underlying social-contract assumption that the cohort answering the questionnaire was raised inside. The 28 percent figure is the cohort registering the gap.

"What is best for the city is not what is most stable, but what makes the city most able to defend itself; and the city most able to defend itself is the city in which the citizens themselves are willing to bear arms." — Aristotle, Politics, VII. The Aristotelian reading runs through the gap. A city — a polity — whose citizens cannot be expected to bear arms in their own name has the form of a city without the substance. Germany has spent four years rebuilding the form of the post-war military-industrial capacity (the budget, the procurement, the integrated NATO command structure). The substance — the citizens' willingness to be the bodies inside the form — is the variable the questionnaire has just measured, and the measurement is the first piece of structurally credible data the Federal Republic has produced on its own civic-defence position since reunification.

The generational seam.

Helen Thompson on the post-2022 European political economy is the structural overlay on what the cohort is registering. The Zeitenwende was sold as a five-year discontinuity in German strategic posture. The cohort being asked to underwrite it has lived inside three intersecting compressions across the same five years: the 2022 inflation shock and the unwinding of the Russia-gas industrial base; the post-Iran-war Hormuz oil shock that has carried German diesel prices through multiyear highs; the post-2024 Trump-administration tariff cycle that has compressed the German automotive export envelope. The cohort is being asked, at age eighteen, to underwrite the institutional rearmament against a Russian threat the same cohort has watched the previous administration's diplomatic architecture fail to contain, in an economy whose growth trajectory has not cleared zero in three years. The refusal is not anti-state, in the historical sense the Federal Republic worried about under Adenauer. It is a price quotation. The state has not yet met it.

Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of national service is the practitioner overlay. Singapore built the SAF on the Israeli model and on the explicit doctrine that universal national service is the single most important institution-building instrument a small state has — not for the military capability it produces, but for the cross-class, cross-ethnic civic identity it manufactures over the cohort's two-year compulsory term. The German Wehrpflicht, suspended in 2011, ran on a similar logic for sixty years. Pistorius's reform is structurally a partial restoration: the questionnaire is universal, the service is voluntary, the conscription clause is the fallback if the voluntary path fails the 260,000 benchmark. The 28 percent refusal does not yet trigger the conscription clause. It does begin the political clock on it. La Rochefoucauld: we promise according to our hopes, and perform according to our fears. The political class promised the Zeitenwende according to the hope that the cohort would absorb the rearmament without the formal compulsion the law's fallback envisions. The performance is the questionnaire return rate. The fear is the conscription clause.

The Merz question.

The institutional question is the CDU-SPD coalition's reading of the figure. Friedrich Merz enters the second year of his chancellorship with approval ratings near nineteen percent, a coalition partner (the SPD under Lars Klingbeil) whose own polling is below the AfD line, and an institutional crisis that on 6 May produced his explicit public ruling-out of both a minority government and a snap election. The Wehrdienst figure lands inside that crisis as a structural problem the coalition is institutionally weakest at addressing. The CDU's traditional answer would be to escalate to the conscription clause; the SPD's traditional answer would be to expand the social-contract incentives (housing, education, training) that make voluntary service materially attractive. The coalition's incentive is to do neither — escalating fractures the partnership, expanding the incentives requires fiscal envelope the German debt brake structurally restricts. Confucius on rectification of names is the institutional read: the Wehrdienstreform was passed in December 2025 as a "voluntary military service law with conscription as fallback"; the figure produced in May 2026 names the law as one whose voluntary path is structurally insufficient and whose fallback the political class will not yet trigger.

Mearsheimer on the structural logic of NATO under conditions of asymmetric burden-sharing is the strategic overlay on what the figure means for the alliance. The Trump-administration posture on European defence — the 5,000-troop drawdown announced in March, the conditional Article 5 framing, the explicit demand that European NATO members underwrite the bulk of the conventional defence of Europe — has placed Germany in the position of being the load-bearing continental NATO member at the moment its own demographic and civic capacity to fill that role is registering at 72 percent of the eligible cohort. The figure is not a rejection of NATO. It is the cost of the burden-shift becoming visible at the level the burden actually has to be paid: the bodies. The structural problem the cohort registers is the structural problem the alliance has not yet priced.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: a civic cohort raised inside a sixty-year voluntary-military-service framework is being asked, inside a four-year-old strategic discontinuity, to underwrite a rearmament programme whose institutional form has been built faster than the social-contract substance the form depends on, and the gap is now measurable at twenty-eight percent of the cohort. Coordination: the CDU-SPD coalition's incentive to do neither of the available institutional responses (conscription escalation; social-contract expansion) preserves the surface stability of the coalition but compounds the underlying problem the figure has just made countable. What is not driving this is a generational rejection of NATO or of national defence in principle; the seventy-two-percent compliance rate and the sixteen-thousand voluntary applications already exceed the 2025 baseline. What is driving it is the price the cohort is quoting for the discontinuity, and the price the German political class has not yet decided how to pay. The Stratagem reads the next twelve months as the institutional-response window — whether Pistorius's reform produces the 260,000 active number on the voluntary path the law privileges, whether the coalition holds together long enough to make the institutional decision the figure forces, and whether the conscription clause is treated as a structural fallback or as the political cost the partnership will not absorb. The questionnaire has been returned. The price has been quoted. The state has not yet replied.

Law of the Day · No. 13

Appeal to people's self-interest, never to their mercy.

Greene's Thirteenth Law: when asking for help, do not remind people of past favours or appeal to their generosity. Find the part of your request that materially serves them, and put that part in the foreground. The Law is not cynical; it is a discipline about how durable contracts are written. A contract written on mercy is rewritten the moment the moral mood turns. A contract written on self-interest survives the moral mood because it does not depend on it.

Today's reading: the appropriation, the cull, the census. Each story is a contract being written or revealed, and each is a different reading of which side the contract appeals to. The Practitioner's Note

Contracts reveal which side has the weaker reading.

The three stories on Saturday's table each turn on a contract being written, rewritten, or quoted — the Taiwanese parliament's authorship of the US-arms package, the Cloudflare-cohort's rewriting of the labour contract under the agentic-AI framing, the German cohort's quotation of the price of the Zeitenwende civic contract. In each case, the side that is publicly the principal — president, CEO, defence minister — is the side whose reading of the contract the headline records, but the side whose reading actually carries the contract is the side whose alternative was structurally weaker. The KMT signs because the alternative is the zero outcome. The displaced workers absorb the framing because the alternative response is structurally absent. The German cohort returns 72 percent compliance because the alternative civic stance is structurally undefined. The discipline of reading a contract is to identify which side's alternative was the binding constraint, and to price the contract on that alternative rather than on the principal's preferred frame.

In your own affairs: every contract you sign — employment, partnership, family, health, financial — is priced on the alternative the weaker side would have to accept. Your own bargaining position in any specific contract is a function of how clearly you have priced your own alternative. Most material errors of judgement in negotiation come from misreading either your own alternative or the other side's; almost no error comes from misreading the contract itself, which is usually written down. The discipline is to ask, before signing or accepting any new contract: what is the alternative I would have to accept if this fell through, and what is the alternative the other side would have to accept? The side whose alternative is worse is the side underwriting the contract.

The hard question: in the contracts you currently hold open — the job, the relationship, the partnership, the credit line — which side's alternative is binding, and which side has been pricing the contract on the wrong reading? And what would change if you priced your next move on the alternative the other side would have to accept rather than on the alternative you have been quoting yourself?
