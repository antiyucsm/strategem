The Hormuz Blockade

A US Navy F/A-18 launched from the USS Gerald R. Ford put a Hellfire missile into the smokestack of the Curacao-flagged tanker M/T Belma on Wednesday afternoon 15 July as the vessel moved toward Kharg Island. The strike is the first US kinetic action against a commercial ship since President Trump reimposed the naval blockade of Iran on Monday 13 July, after the June 14 diplomatic MoU collapsed following a July 12 IRGC strike on two tankers inside the Strait. Brent settled at USD 94.20; the Baltic Dirty Tanker index moved 8 per cent higher; the White House framing is that Washington is “restoring the pressure envelope on Iran’s ability to move oil to the market.” Read as tanker-war enforcement the Belma strike is a discrete incident. Read as coordinated coercion it is the specific move by which Washington converts Hormuz into the exclusive channel through which Tehran’s nuclear-programme concessions will be priced, and by which the ceasefire the June MoU tried to build is replaced by a naval choke-hold whose only exit route is a written Iranian concession the June architecture could not extract.

The sequence, first.

Read the four-week arithmetic carefully; the tabloid framing of “another Iran strike” loses the mechanism. On 14 June the United States and Iran signed a written diplomatic MoU opening a sixty-day extendable window in which Tehran committed to allow IAEA re-entry and reopening of the Strait in exchange for Washington lifting the naval blockade and specific sanctions on the Central Bank of Iran. IAEA inspectors were readmitted on 22 June; the Strait’s Portwatch transit index recovered to 55 per cent of pre-conflict baseline by 28 June; a specific Iranian concession on the 60-per-cent-enriched uranium stockpile was still under discussion. On 8 July Iran’s IRGC seized the Marshall Islands-flagged product tanker MV Cygnus; on 12 July an IRGC fast-attack craft struck two commercial tankers inside the Strait with Iranian-manufactured Ghadir-class rockets. The Trump National Security Council convened Sunday 12 July; the blockade was reimposed at 06:00 EDT Monday 13 July by CENTCOM order; the Vance Oval briefing landed Wednesday; the Belma strike was executed inside forty-eight hours to establish that reimposition was not the June-2019 rhetoric-only tanker-war. Tehran’s response — Araghchi’s UNSC petition, the IRGC-Aerospace threat to Gulf shipping, and Ayatollah Khamenei’s Friday sermon language of “final option” — is the pattern the previous three months of hostilities produced. The specific new fact is that the diplomatic architecture the June MoU tried to build is now the specifically absent instrument.

Greene’s Law 33 on the thumbscrew.

Robert Greene’s thirty-third Law — discover each man’s thumbscrew — reads the specific coercion architecture the Trump-Vance team have organised the Iran question around since the February 28 strikes. Iran’s political-economy dependence on oil-export revenue is not general; it is specifically Hormuz-routed, and specifically routed through the Kharg Island loading terminal that handles more than 95 per cent of Iran’s crude exports. Iranian domestic industrial capacity to store, refine, or route around a Hormuz interdiction is limited; the November 2024 completion of the Goreh-Jask pipeline extension, which was supposed to give Tehran a Gulf-of-Oman bypass, added only 300,000 barrels-per-day of alternate export capacity against a pre-conflict export baseline of 1.6 million. The thumbscrew is not the sanctions envelope; it is the specific Kharg-to-market shipping route, and Greene’s discipline is that the operator who identifies the single asymmetric dependence of a counterparty and applies pressure at exactly that point can extract concessions the general-purpose coercion instruments cannot. The June MoU tried to convert the thumbscrew into a diplomatic transaction; the reimposition converts it back into a naval one. The Belma strike is the specific proof-of-application that Washington intends to run the pressure until the concession is written down, not merely discussed.

Sun Tzu on breaking the alliances.

Sun Tzu’s third chapter instructs the general to break the enemy’s alliances before the enemy’s army. The specific 2026 execution of the discipline is not against Tehran’s formal alliances — Iran has no formal treaty allies inside the Gulf — but against Tehran’s informal shadow-fleet architecture. The Belma is Curacao-flagged, insured through an obscure Panama-domiciled hull-and-machinery underwriter, and beneficially owned through a Marshall-Islands corporate stack whose ultimate owner is a Dubai-based Iranian expatriate. That shadow-fleet architecture — roughly 300 tankers according to a July 2026 UANI count, up from 170 in 2023 — is the specific instrument by which Iran has continued to move oil against the sanctions envelope for three years. The Trump blockade reimposition does not target the Iranian navy; it targets the shadow fleet. Every Belma-style strike raises the underwriting cost of the next ship-owner willing to be the shadow-fleet counterparty, and every dollar of underwriting cost widens the discount at which shadow-fleet operators require the barrel to be sold, and every widening of the discount reduces the specific revenue on which Tehran’s domestic political economy depends. The Sun Tzu discipline is that breaking the informal alliance-of-convenience shadow-fleet architecture is cheaper than fighting the Iranian navy, and produces the same coercive result at a fraction of the escalation risk.

Mearsheimer on choke-point coercion.

John Mearsheimer’s offensive-realist reading of maritime choke points is that they are the specific instruments through which the global maritime hegemon converts geographic accident into political leverage. Hormuz carries roughly a fifth of world seaborne oil and a third of world seaborne LNG; the specific structural fact is that the maritime hegemon — the United States, through the Fifth Fleet at Manama and the strike-group presence in the northern Arabian Sea — can close the Strait to a specific state’s commerce while leaving the rest of the choke-point traffic flowing. The Chinese economy imports 41 per cent of its crude through Hormuz; the Trump administration’s specific Wednesday briefing signalled that Chinese-flagged tanker traffic would not be interdicted at present, and the Xi-Trump phone call reported by Xinhua on Thursday morning was Beijing’s acknowledgement that the choke-point discipline can be exercised against Iran without being exercised against China. That is the specific realist arithmetic Mearsheimer names: the choke-point is not a general instrument but a targeted one, and its coercive power is exactly proportional to the ability of the hegemon to discriminate between counterparties. The June MoU tried to relinquish that instrument in exchange for nuclear concessions; the reimposition takes it back, and the Belma strike is the operative demonstration that the discrimination discipline remains intact.

The 1986-88 tanker war rhyme.

The immediate operational rhyme is not the 2019 Abqaiq strike and not the 2024 Red Sea Houthi campaign; it is the specific December 1986 to August 1988 US Navy tanker-reflag operation known as Operation Earnest Will. During the final phase of the Iran-Iraq War, Kuwaiti tankers were reflagged as US-flagged vessels and escorted through the Strait by US Navy warships; Iranian mines and IRGC boghammar-boat attacks continued through 1987; the July 1988 USS Vincennes shootdown of Iran Air 655 was the specific escalation the Reagan administration used as the pretext to force the Iranian acceptance of UNSC Resolution 598, ending the war on Iraqi terms. The three-phase arc — naval-escort establishment, tit-for-tat commercial-vessel attrition, catalytic escalation, concession — is the pattern the 2026 reimposition telegraphs. The specific differences with 1987-88 are three: the shadow-fleet architecture did not exist in 1987, and the discrimination discipline is therefore sharper today; the Chinese economy’s Hormuz dependence in 1987 was structurally zero, and Beijing’s bilateral leverage today is a specific factor Washington must price; and the Iranian domestic-political situation today is materially weaker than in the Khomeini era, with the July 2026 IMF Article IV estimating headline inflation at 47 per cent and youth unemployment at 26 per cent. The rhyme is not the outcome; it is the mechanism.

The base case is materially more probable; the alternative case is the higher-magnitude one; the specific variable is the tempo of the next Iranian retaliation and its choice of target.

What’s not driving this. Regime change is not driving this. Vance’s specific public commentary since April has been that a US strategy predicated on regime change would fail and that the object is a bounded nuclear settlement plus a normalised Strait regime, not a Tehran power transition. And popular anti-regime sentiment inside Iran is not driving this: the IMF headline inflation and youth unemployment numbers make a Green-Movement-scale mobilisation plausible but Washington is not organising the coercion around that theory of victory; the coercion is organised around producing a signed nuclear concession, not a regime collapse.

What would break this reading.

  • Tehran announces a unilateral Hormuz closure Monday or Tuesday, mines the Central Strait, and Portwatch registers weekly transit calls below 10 per cent of baseline for four consecutive weeks; the discrimination discipline collapses because there is nothing left to discriminate against; the choke-point coercion reading is falsified by the counterparty preferring the mutually-costly closure to the asymmetric squeeze.
  • Beijing publicly protests the discrimination discipline after a Chinese-flagged tanker is interdicted (accidentally or by escalation), and a specific US-China Gulf-shipping dispute opens; the discrimination arithmetic the Trump reading depends on is compromised; the coercion loses its targeted character and either escalates against China or is walked back against Iran.
  • A specific narrow-scope prisoner-exchange or tanker-release confidence-building measure emerges from a Doha or Muscat intermediation inside three weeks and produces a written return-to-the-June-MoU framework; the reading that the diplomatic architecture has been fundamentally replaced by naval coercion is falsified; the base case is upgraded to a slower version of the June path.
  • The IRGC executes a specific Bandar Abbas or Fujairah kinetic action that produces US casualties and forces a Trump decision on striking Iranian sovereign territory (as distinct from shadow-fleet vessels); the reading that the current coercion is calibrated below the escalation threshold is falsified by a counterparty willing to force the escalation.
