The Trilateral Frame

The United States, Israel, and Lebanon signed a Trilateral Framework in Washington on Friday after four days of closed talks at Foggy Bottom. The text declares the “shared goal of achieving lasting peace and security” and installs a sequenced process to restore Lebanese sovereign authority over all Lebanese territory pending the verified disarmament of non-state armed groups. Israel will withdraw from two pilot zones across the standing buffer architecture; the Lebanese Armed Forces will assume the security mandate inside the zones and demonstrate disarmament of Hezbollah inside the pilots before further handovers are calendared. A trilateral Military Coordination Group for Lebanon (MCG4L), facilitated by the United States, is installed as the implementation organ; an immediate one-hundred-million-dollar US humanitarian commitment is booked against the broader reconstruction file. Secretary of State Marco Rubio named the moment “the beginning of the beginning” at the signing. Hezbollah Secretary-General Naim Qassem condemned the linkage as “a very dangerous proposition that crosses all red lines.” The Stratagem reads the Trilateral Frame as the move at which Israel installs the Lebanese Army as the cohort that finishes the disarmament campaign Israel cannot finish without absorbing the political cost of permanent occupation. Greene’s seventh law on getting others to do the work while keeping the credit; Sun Tzu on subduing the enemy without fighting; Voss on the sequenced-process architecture that ties incentives across time; Mearsheimer on the offshore balancer outsourcing the operational burden to the regional principal whose own survival depends on completing it.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Washington, Beirut & Jerusalem·28 June 2026

Washington —The document signed at Foggy Bottom on Friday is not a peace deal and does not pretend to be one. Rubio’s phrase — “the beginning of the beginning” — was chosen with the precision of a Secretary of State who has read the room: the cohort that ratifies a final settlement in Lebanon does not yet exist, and the cohort that signed on Friday has agreed to a process inside which the future cohort might be assembled. What the Frame installs is not peace; it is the organisational chart inside which peace, if it arrives, will be processed. The two pilot zones are the operational unit. The Lebanese Armed Forces are the operational principal. Hezbollah’s disarmament inside the pilots is the operational deliverable. The IDF buffer-zone presence is the operational lever. The MCG4L is the operational secretariat. Each piece of the architecture is calibrated against a single proposition: Israel will not finish the war against Hezbollah from outside the Lebanese state, and Hezbollah cannot be disarmed except by the Lebanese state itself.

Begin with Greene’s seventh law. Get others to do the work for you, but always take the credit. The discipline is not the avoidance of labour but the architecture inside which the labour is performed by the cohort whose own self-interest aligns with the outcome the principal needs. Across the past forty years the standing Israeli posture against Hezbollah has been the kinetic one: 1982, 1996, 2006, 2024, the rolling drone-and-air campaign across 2025-and-2026. Each cycle has revealed the same structural constraint — Israel can degrade Hezbollah from the air and from limited ground incursions, but cannot disarm Hezbollah without absorbing the cost of permanent occupation of southern Lebanon, a cost the 1982-to-2000 occupation made operationally unaffordable. The Frame retires the kinetic-alone register and installs the agent-and-principal register. The Lebanese Armed Forces become the principal who, inside the two pilot zones, take operational responsibility for disarming the standing Hezbollah deployment. The Israeli buffer-zone presence becomes the lever inside which the Lebanese cohort has to demonstrate capacity if the IDF is to withdraw further. The political cost of the operation is borne by the LAF and the Lebanese state — not by the IDF and the Netanyahu coalition. The credit, when the operation succeeds, accrues to the cohort that wrote the Frame — the Trump administration as architect, the Israeli cohort as patron, the Lebanese cohort as implementer. Greene’s discipline cuts at the structural register: the principal who does the work himself absorbs the political cost himself; the principal who structures the architecture so the agent does the work distributes the cost to the cohort whose own survival depends on the outcome.

Then read Sun Tzu. The supreme excellence is not winning every battle; it is subduing the enemy without fighting reads the Frame at its strategic register. The Israeli cohort has spent eighteen months learning, at significant cost, that the kinetic register against Hezbollah operates under a constraint Clausewitz named explicitly: the political object bounds the military means, and the military means available to Israel are bounded by the political cost of occupation Lebanon and Lebanon alone can absorb. The Frame substitutes the indirect approach for the direct one. The Lebanese Armed Forces, not the IDF, will be the cohort that confronts Hezbollah units inside the pilots. The Lebanese state, not the Israeli state, will be the cohort that absorbs the domestic-political cost of the disarmament demand. The standing Lebanese sectarian-balance architecture — Shia-Sunni-Maronite-Druze under the 1989 Taif arrangement — will be the cohort inside which Hezbollah’s integration-or-defection plays out. Voss’s discipline that the negotiated process is the architecture inside which incentives are tied across time reads the sequenced-process structure: handover triggers withdrawal; withdrawal triggers humanitarian disbursement; disbursement triggers reconstruction-cohort engagement. Each phase is conditioned on the prior phase’s deliverable. The Frame is the operational manual for the indirect approach Sun Tzu reserved for the strategist who has understood that fighting is the second-best option.

What the Frame installs.

Mearsheimer’s discipline that the offshore balancer subsidises the regional principal whose survival depends on the outcome the balancer wants reads the US role at its structural register. The Trump administration does not have the political bandwidth to underwrite a permanent Israeli presence in southern Lebanon, nor the operational instrument to run the disarmament programme itself. What it does have is the convening power to assemble the cohort that signs, the diplomatic recognition to ratify the LAF as the cohort that disarms, and the financial instrument — the hundred-million-dollar disbursement, the reconstruction-fund engagement, the IMF-and-World-Bank cohort coordination — to underwrite the Lebanese state’s capacity to absorb the cost. The MCG4L is the institutional secretariat inside which the cohort meets, the deliverables are calendared, and the public-political accountability is staged. The architecture is recognisable from the playbook: the 1978 Camp David precedent, the 1995 Dayton precedent, the 2020 Abraham Accords precedent — the US assembles the cohort and underwrites the architecture, and the regional principals execute the substantive work. La Rochefoucauld’s observation that self-love can adapt to any role; it cannot adapt to no role at all reads the Lebanese cohort’s acceptance: the LAF’s standing institutional purpose has been the maintenance of internal order against the Hezbollah veto on its own deployment in the South; the Frame gives the LAF the role its self-interest has demanded for two decades. The Frame works because each cohort — the US, Israel, the LAF, the reconstruction-funder cohort — has been given the role its self-interest required.

What the calendar tests.

Three variables across the next six months will reveal whether the Frame holds. First, the pilot-zone deliverable: if the LAF demonstrates measurable disarmament progress inside the two zones — weapon caches surrendered, fighters demobilised, command structure visibly degraded — the architecture has been operationally ratified and the standing IDF cohort will calendar a third zone, then a fourth, and the sequencing instrument has been installed. If the LAF moves into the pilots without confronting the Hezbollah deployment — the historical pattern across the 2006-to-2024 cycle — the Frame has been operationally falsified and the IDF buffer-zone presence becomes permanent by default. Second, the Hezbollah refusal cycle: Qassem’s “crosses all red lines” condemnation is the standing posture, but the operational question is whether the cohort accepts an integration-into-LAF transition for its standing fighter base, refuses the disarmament demand and escalates kinetically against the LAF, or negotiates an internal partition under which selected units stand down and selected units fold into the standing political-party architecture. The 1989 Taif precedent ran on the last; the Frame implicitly assumes a 1989 outcome. Third, the Israeli political bandwidth: if the standing Netanyahu coalition holds and Defence Minister Katz’s “indefinite buffer-zone” posture conditions the standing IDF cohort’s sequencing patience, the Frame survives at compressed cadence; if the standing Israeli political cycle replaces the coalition with one less invested in the sequenced-process architecture, the kinetic register returns. Talleyrand’s discipline that treaties are durable in proportion to the cost each signatory would absorb by breaking them reads the calendar: the Lebanese state cannot break the Frame without absorbing the cost of Israeli reoccupation; the Israeli state cannot break the Frame without absorbing the cost of becoming the occupying power again; the US cannot break the Frame without absorbing the cost of the alternative it has no instrument to underwrite. The cohort that signed has been given a stake each will protect against the cost of breaking; the cohort that did not sign — Hezbollah — is the cohort against which the architecture has been written.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What’s driving this: The tactical layer carries the weight here. What is named “the Trilateral Framework” is operationally an agency architecture: the principal who needs the work done (Israel) installs the agent who can do it without the principal absorbing the political cost (the LAF), inside the secretariat that distributes accountability (the MCG4L), with the financial underwriter (the US humanitarian disbursement) that gives the agent the capacity it lacks. The structural layer underneath is the Clausewitzian constraint Israel learned across the 1982-to-2024 cycle: the political cost of permanent occupation of southern Lebanon exceeds the kinetic gains, and the disarmament of Hezbollah cannot be completed by a cohort that is itself the occupying power. The Frame does not solve the constraint; it routes around it. The Lebanese state becomes the cohort that absorbs the political cost; the IDF buffer-zone presence becomes the lever the LAF must clear; the US convening role becomes the institutional cover under which the architecture survives the political-cycle volatility of any one signatory.

What’s not driving this: A reading that frames the document as “the beginning of peace” misses the operational structure. Rubio chose his phrase carefully: this is the beginning of the architecture inside which a peace deal might be processed, not a peace deal. Hezbollah, the cohort against which the architecture has been written, has not signed and will not. The Frame works whether or not Hezbollah cooperates — cooperation produces the disarmament outcome; refusal produces the political case for permanent buffer-zone presence and Lebanese-state mobilisation against the cohort that refuses. This is not a peace document; it is a routing instrument.

What would break this reading:

  • The Lebanese Armed Forces fail to enter either pilot zone in operational strength inside ninety days — the “agent-and-principal architecture” reading was wrong; the LAF cohort cannot execute the role even when given the institutional cover, and the Frame collapses back into the IDF-buffer-zone equilibrium.

Baghdad —The Iraq Threshold is the second pressure-point test the standing cartel has had to absorb inside fifty-eight days. The first was the United Arab Emirates’ May 1 exit, executed cleanly inside the standing OPEC+ architecture, with a notification cadence and a successor-state production trajectory the cohort had been signalling for eighteen months. But the instrument has a property the standing cohort has not had to absorb since the 2014-to-2016 cycle: the cohort that holds the threat is the second-largest producer, the cohort that would absorb it — Saudi Arabia — cannot raise its own quota without absorbing the standing fiscal cost of a lower price, and the cohort whose departure the cartel could absorb — the smaller producers — does not include Iraq. The Iraqi cohort has discovered the cartel’s thumbscrew. The standing question is whether the architecture survives the discovery.

Begin with Mearsheimer. The realist tradition treats cartels as instruments of regional hegemony — the cohort that holds the marginal barrel sets the global price, and the cohort that holds the cartel-coordination instrument sets the cohort’s discipline. OPEC has functioned since 1973 as the Saudi cohort’s instrument: a price-coordination architecture inside which the standing cohort accepts quota discipline in exchange for the price-floor the Saudi swing-producer capacity defends. The instrument’s legitimacy rests on a single load-bearing proposition — that the Saudi cohort, when forced to choose between defending the cartel and defending its own market share, will defend the cartel. The proposition was tested in 2014 and the cohort defended its own market share. It was tested in 2020 and the cohort defended its own market share inside thirty days of a price-war detour. The Iraq Threshold is the third test inside twelve years. The cohort that has been trained on three cycles of Saudi behaviour reads the standing posture differently from the cohort that read it inside the 1980-to-2008 cycle: the cartel is a Saudi instrument that the Saudi cohort uses when it serves Saudi interests, and the discipline the cohort imposes on the smaller producers does not extend to the cohort itself. Iraq has read the lesson. The threat to leave is the application: if the discipline does not apply equally, the discipline does not apply at all.

Then read Greene’s thirty-third law. Discover each man’s thumbscrew — the weak point, the unconfessed need, the constraint he cannot afford to make public — and you have the instrument. The discipline reads the Iraqi posture at its tactical register. The Saudi cohort cannot afford to raise its own quota without absorbing a lower price the standing fiscal architecture requires it not to absorb — the breakeven-fiscal-price profile is in the eighty-five-to-ninety-dollar band, against the standing seventy-two-dollar Brent print. The Saudi cohort cannot afford to lose Iraq from the cartel without forfeiting the coordination instrument it has built since 1973. The Saudi cohort cannot afford to acknowledge the trade-off in public because acknowledgement converts the standing cartel architecture from a price-coordination instrument into a quota-bidding architecture inside which every member can extract a higher number by replicating the Iraqi threat. The thumbscrew is the unresolved gap between “coordination architecture” and “Saudi quota-defence instrument.” Iraq has placed its grip on the screw. The standing question is whether Saudi grants the higher quota and pays the price-collapse cost, refuses and pays the Iraq-exit cost, or invents a third option — a phased-in compensation programme tied to the reconstruction file, an explicit Kurdistan-pipeline carve-out, a parallel sanction-evasion architecture the cohort can absorb without ratifying it — that defers the choice without resolving it. La Rochefoucauld’s observation that those who claim to be negotiating in good faith are usually negotiating from positions they cannot defend reads the Iraqi posture: the cohort claims it does not want to leave; the cohort wants its number raised. The framing is the second instrument; the threat is the first.

Munger’s inversion — ask what the cartel architecture has become and whether the cohort that operates it has reason to keep operating it — reads the architectural register: the cartel still exists, the cohort still meets, the quotas are still set, but the operational consequence of the coordination instrument has been compressed below the threshold at which the Saudi cohort absorbs the cost of defending it. What the calendar tests.
