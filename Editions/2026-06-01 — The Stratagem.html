The Crusader Climb

Israeli forces raised the IDF flag over Beaufort Castle on Saturday afternoon, the deepest incursion into Lebanon since the 2000 withdrawal and the third major position taken since the April 17 ceasefire. France has called an emergency meeting of the UN Security Council for Sunday; Foreign Minister Jean-Noel Barrot said that “nothing can justify the continuation of Israeli military operations in Lebanon and its ever-deeper occupation of Lebanese territory.” The Stratagem reads the geography, not the rhetoric. Five days ago this paper read the Pentagon’s direct Israel-Lebanon talks as the “Litani Line” — a negotiated frontier traced on a map. The Beaufort climb pre-empts that map with stone. The April ceasefire is being voided by position rather than by statement, and Paris’s emergency call is the Western balancer arriving after the ridge has already been taken.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Beaufort & Paris·1 June 2026

Beaufort —The twelfth-century Crusader fortress sits on a limestone ridge five hundred metres above the Litani valley, commanding the southern Bekaa, the Hula plain in northern Israel, and the road to Sidon. Its tactical value has been understood by every army that has reached it since the County of Tripoli: whoever holds Beaufort sees what moves on the plain. The PLO held it through the 1970s and shelled Israeli farms from its walls. Israel took it in 1982 in a battle whose name still reads in the IDF’s training cycle. The withdrawal of May 2000 returned it to Lebanese sovereignty and to UNESCO custody. On Saturday afternoon Israeli infantry climbed it again, and the prime minister’s office released the photograph of the flag at sunset before the ridge cooled. Twenty-six years of return ended in a single afternoon.

Begin with the structural reading, because it is the one most easily missed under the headline. Israel did not take Beaufort to fight an enemy presently on it. The Hezbollah units that engaged the climb were a screening force. The castle is taken because the castle is the ridge, and the ridge is the line that any future negotiated frontier will be drawn around. The April 17 ceasefire and its May 15 extension by forty-five days were diplomatic instruments resting on a presumed return to status quo ante. The Beaufort climb removes the status quo ante from the table. The talks that the Pentagon hosted through April and May — the “Litani Line” this paper read on the 28th — were negotiations over where the line would be drawn. The line is now being drawn by occupation rather than by mediation. Mearsheimer’s offensive realism reads this precisely: a regional power whose adversary is structurally weakened does not split the difference at the bargaining table when it can fix the difference on the ground. The talks were the wrapping. The ridge is the gift.

Sun Tzu is the older voice on commanding heights: “the army that arrives first at the disputed ground and waits for the enemy is at ease; the army that arrives later and rushes into action is weary.” The corollary, on terrain, is that some positions render the negotiation that would otherwise have decided them moot. Greene’s twenty-third law — concentrate your forces — describes the Israeli operational discipline of the past six weeks: not a broad-front offensive but a precise climb up specific high ground, each piece taken before the diplomatic machinery responds. La Rochefoucauld’s line on apologies, that one rarely owes them to those one has dispossessed, is the moral grammar of what has been done; the demand that Israel justify the climb in the language of self-defence is a demand its government will meet at the podium and disregard in the field. The French request for an emergency Security Council session is the diplomatic move; the Russian and Chinese votes will block any binding resolution, the United States will abstain on language and veto on enforcement, and the ridge will still be Israeli on Tuesday.

What Paris is doing.

Read the French move on its own terms. Barrot’s statement — that France recognises Israel’s right to self-defence but that nothing justifies an “ever-deeper occupation of Lebanese territory” — is the careful diplomatic formulation of a country that has had Lebanon in its sphere of concern since the 1860 intervention and the mandate of 1923, and that is now watching the architecture it underwrote in the 2024 ceasefire mechanism collapse. France cannot stop the climb. It can put on the record that the climb has happened. The Security Council session will produce a statement that the United States will dilute and Russia will weaponise; the meaningful French move sits one level down, in the bilateral pressure Paris can apply on Beirut to keep the Lebanese army from being used as cover for Hezbollah’s reconstitution, and on Cairo and Amman to keep their own quiet channels open. Talleyrand’s discipline applies: the diplomat’s task is not to prevent what cannot be prevented but to ensure that the record reflects who did what. Paris is updating the record. The record is what survives the cabinet that took the ridge.

What the climb tests.

Three indicators in the coming weeks. The first is whether the Israeli advance halts at the Zaharani — the river ten kilometres north of the Litani that Netanyahu named as Saturday’s objective — or whether the climb continues toward Sidon. A halt at the Zaharani argues that the operation has the territorial objective it stated and that the climb is now a fixed-position bargaining chip; a continuation argues that the diplomatic ceiling has been removed and the operation will run to whatever line the IDF’s logistics support. The second is the Lebanese army. If Beirut moves the army south of the Litani in any visible way, the institutional commitment to the 1701 framework survives; if the army holds its line and lets the climb stand, the framework is over and Lebanon’s post-Hezbollah architecture begins to be negotiated under Israeli occupation rather than over it. The third is Tehran. Iran is sixty days into a US-Iran ceasefire memorandum and the Hormuz reopening this paper read on Friday; a visible Iranian rearm of Hezbollah this week would burn the memorandum, and Iran has every short-term incentive not to. Lee Kuan Yew’s rule on small-state survival belongs here: Lebanon’s only durable defence is the architecture of international guarantees, and the architecture has been climbed over. The map is being redrawn. The negotiation will catch up.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Two layers carry the climb. The structural one is the asymmetric collapse of Hezbollah’s deterrent capability since autumn 2024 and the US absorption in the Iran file: Israel faces no power that can impose a cost on continued occupation of southern Lebanon at the scale the operation requires. Inside that field, a state with the option to fix its northern border by force rather than by negotiation will, on offensive-realist terms, do so. The second is tactical: the specific choice of Beaufort — not a strategic crossroad, not an industrial target, but a commanding ridge that any future line will be drawn around — is the operational signature of a campaign whose architecture is permanent occupation dressed as security buffer. The mechanism that decides outcomes is whether the IDF halts at the Zaharani or continues to Sidon, because the halt-line is the new Israeli border by tomorrow morning whatever the Security Council says on Sunday.

What's not driving this: A retaliatory cycle from a specific Hezbollah provocation. Reading the climb as a response to drone strikes on Israeli troops misses that the operation has been prepared for weeks under cover of a nominal ceasefire and that the Hezbollah engagement was a screen, not a trigger. The drone fire is the pretext; the ridge is the project.

What would break this reading:

  • The IDF announces a Beaufort withdrawal within thirty days conditional on a Lebanese army deployment — the “permanent buffer by occupation” reading was wrong, and the climb was a bargaining tool inside the existing ceasefire architecture rather than a replacement for it.
  • The United States moves from a “surgical” caveat to a visible public restraint on the operation, conditioning aid or arms on a halt — the “Trump tolerates because Iran is the file” reading was wrong, and the Iran deal is more brittle than the Lebanon climb requires.
  • The Lebanese army moves visibly south of the Litani in coordination with Hezbollah remnants — the “Beirut cannot convert this” reading was wrong, and the climb produces the national mobilisation that the war of attrition did not.
  • Iran resupplies Hezbollah at scale and the Hormuz memorandum collapses inside the quarter — the “Tehran cannot afford to escalate” reading was wrong, and Lebanon is the file on which Iran chooses to test what the deal will tolerate.

Sources used: Mearsheimer’s offensive realism supplies the structural spine — states fix borders by force when the structural field permits, and the post-2024 Hezbollah collapse permits it. Sun Tzu on commanding heights and Greene’s twenty-third law on concentrated force describe the operational discipline that picked Beaufort rather than a broader front. Talleyrand on the diplomat’s task of updating the record names what Barrot is doing at the Council. La Rochefoucauld on the moral grammar of dispossession reads the gap between Israeli justification at the podium and Israeli posture in the field. Lee Kuan Yew’s rule on small-state survival names what Beirut is no longer able to do.

The Vacant Quota · The 41st OPEC and Non-OPEC Ministerial Meeting Convenes in Vienna on Saturday, June 7, the First Such Meeting Since the United Arab Emirates Withdrew From the Cartel on May 1 After Fifty-Eight Years of Membership — the Seven Remaining OPEC+ Producers Already Agreed on May 3 to Raise Output by 188,000 Barrels per Day in June, the First Hike of the New Architecture, With Saudi Arabia and Russia Each Taking 62,000 Barrels of the Increase — Brent Has Held Around $108 a Barrel Even After the May 30 US-Iran Memorandum Reopened the Strait of Hormuz to Iranian Shipping, Because the UAE Sits Outside the Cartel With 1.6 Million Barrels of Idle Capacity and a Public Plan to Ramp From 3.4 to 5.0 Million Barrels per Day by 2027 — the June 7 Meeting Is the First Test of Whether the Saudi-Russian Axis Can Defend Quota Discipline Against a Shadow Producer Now Free to Flood, and Whether the Cartel That Set the World Oil Price Through Sixty Years of Geopolitics Still Sets the Cost of an Industrial Marginal Barrel After Losing Its Third-Largest Member to the Same Iran War That Was Meant to Bind Them All Together

The Vacant Quota

The 41st OPEC and non-OPEC Ministerial Meeting convenes in Vienna on Saturday, June 7 — the first since the United Arab Emirates walked out of the cartel on May 1 after fifty-eight years of membership. The seven remaining producers have already agreed to raise output by 188,000 barrels per day in June, with Saudi Arabia and Russia each taking 62,000. Brent has held around $108 a barrel even after the May 30 US-Iran memorandum reopened the Strait of Hormuz, because the UAE now sits outside the room with 1.6 million barrels of idle capacity and a plan to flood. The Stratagem reads the geometry, not the headline number. OPEC was an architecture for absorbing internal dissent into joint discipline. The architecture has lost its third-largest member to the war that was meant to bind them all together, and the meeting on Saturday is the first test of whether Saudi-Russian quota discipline can be defended against a shadow producer that is now free to undercut it.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Vienna & Riyadh·1 June 2026

Vienna —OPEC was founded in this city in September 1960 by five producers seeking to extract a coordination premium from the seven majors who priced their crude for them. Within a generation the cartel was the price-maker for a third of global oil; within two, it had taught the world that producers acting as a coalition collect rents that producers acting alone cannot. The institution’s history is the history of how that coalition has held together through internal dissent — through the 1986 Saudi market-share war that broke the first cartel and rebuilt it, through the 1998 Riyadh-Caracas-Mexico accord that ended a second collapse, through the 2016 OPEC+ enlargement that brought Russia into the joint quota discipline. Each crisis was absorbed because the architecture was worth more inside than outside. On May 1, for the first time in fifty-eight years, a senior producer concluded the opposite, and OPEC is now the institution that has to write the next chapter without its third-largest member in the room.

Begin with the structural reading, because it sets every other variable on the table. The UAE’s exit was not a sudden grievance. Abu Dhabi had pressed for a higher quota baseline since 2021, settled for a partial revision in 2022, and quietly built capacity on the assumption that the cartel could not contain it indefinitely. The Iran war of February 2026 closed the Strait of Hormuz and physically prevented the UAE from exporting its lifted crude; the cartel meanwhile asked it to absorb its share of voluntary cuts as if the strait were open. Abu Dhabi waited for the political moment — the US-Iran ceasefire track reopening, the Saudis distracted with the war — and walked out. The May 30 Hormuz memorandum is the variable everything else now turns on. If the strait reopens to pre-war flow levels through Q3, the UAE’s 1.6 million barrels per day of idle capacity becomes a physical instrument of market share that no one in Vienna on Saturday will be able to discipline. Brent at $108 reflects traders pricing both the war premium that has not yet drained and the shadow-producer overhang that has not yet arrived. The cartel is bargaining over a quota baseline whose denominator is being redrawn around it.

Dalio’s big cycle on reserve and commodity power names the deeper pattern: institutional cartels accumulate authority during the rising phase of the system that produced them and shed it during the fragmenting phase. OPEC is fifty-eight years into a cycle whose first three decades it dominated; the past fifteen have been the slow concession that the cartel sets price only at the margin and that the marginal barrel is increasingly determined by US shale, Russian crude trading discounted into Asia, and Iranian and Venezuelan supply moving through sanctions channels. The UAE exit makes the concession explicit. Sun Tzu’s rule on commanders is the operational discipline that Saudi Arabia and Russia will bring to Vienna: the general who knows when to fight and when to wait understands that the contested ground on Saturday is not the next 188,000 barrels but the credibility of joint discipline once the room knows a member can leave and prosper. Greene’s twenty-third law on concentrated forces is the temptation Riyadh will resist: a market-share war against the UAE would crush the cartel faster than any quota dispute, and the Saudi memory of 1986 and 2014 still works. The harder discipline is Lee Kuan Yew’s on coalitions of producers: the rents survive only as long as each member believes the inside-the-tent return exceeds the outside-the-tent option, and the UAE has now publicly demonstrated that for at least one member, it does not.

What the meeting actually decides.

The headline question for Saturday is whether OPEC+ confirms the May 3 hike trajectory, slows it, accelerates it, or reverses it. The structural question underneath is which architecture the cartel is rebuilding for the post-UAE era. Three options are visible. The first is to defend the existing quota with a token concession, hike modestly through Q3, and bet that the UAE’s production ramp is slower than its public plan; this is the Saudi-preferred path because it preserves the institutional fiction. The second is to absorb the UAE’s departure by formally rebasing the baselines — conceding that the cartel is now smaller and the quota smaller with it, and trying to negotiate the residual at a lower production level the smaller cartel can actually defend. The third is the option no one will name in the communique but which haunts every Saudi-Russian conversation on the side: a controlled market-share contest to discipline the UAE before its ramp is complete, accepting a quarter or two of price compression as the cost of teaching Abu Dhabi that exit is more expensive than membership. Each has a different price implication. The first keeps Brent near $100 through the summer if the Hormuz reopening is gradual; the second compresses prices to $85-90 as the cartel cedes ground; the third risks a 1986-style collapse to $60 territory that the Saudi fiscal position cannot now absorb. The path Vienna chooses on Saturday tells us which of these readings the cartel believes about itself.

What the vacant quota tests.

Three indicators in the coming month. The first is Saturday’s communique itself. A hike at 188,000 barrels per day with no rebasing language is path one; a baseline revision is path two; an unusually large hike or a public lecture aimed at Abu Dhabi is path three. The second is UAE behaviour. If Abu Dhabi follows its public ramp plan and starts adding production publicly in June and July, the cartel’s discipline is already broken; if it ramps quietly through Asian trading houses without official announcements, it is signalling that it would rather be welcomed back than free, and the door is open. The third is the Iran track. The Hormuz memorandum has a sixty-day clock; if the US-Iran ceasefire holds and the strait reopens at pre-war flow, the physical constraint that has masked the cartel’s loss of price-setting power lifts and the structural reality becomes visible inside one quarter. Confucius’s rectification of names belongs here: a cartel that has lost the institutional capacity to enforce its quotas is not a cartel, it is a meeting; and a meeting that calls itself a cartel for long enough becomes the thing it pretends to be only as long as the participants agree to keep pretending. Vienna on Saturday will reveal whether the participants are still willing to pretend. The market will do the rest.

The Constitution War

President Karol Nawrocki used the anniversary of the 1791 Polish Constitution on May 3 to appoint a Council for a New Constitution, tasked with drafting a replacement for the 1997 Basic Law by the 2030 presidential election. The draft would push Poland from a parliamentary-cabinet system toward a semi-presidential architecture and limit the scope of EU law in Polish territory. Prime Minister Donald Tusk has dismissed the initiative as a “political game,” noting that the two-thirds Sejm majority required for any change does not exist and cannot be assembled in 2027. The Stratagem reads the project, not the math. Yesterday this paper read Italy’s bonus seat as an engineered majority bought against proportional fragmentation. Today the move is Polish and the architecture in question is the regime itself. Nawrocki cannot pass a constitution. He can frame the 2027 parliamentary contest as a vote on the constitution. The math is the wrong question.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Warsaw & Brussels·1 June 2026

Warsaw —The 1997 Polish Constitution was the architectural product of a particular cohort. Drafted between 1989 and 1997 under the long shadow of the Round Table negotiations, ratified by a 53 percent referendum, it was engineered to prevent the Polish state from being captured again by any single force — the parliamentary cabinet that wins the Sejm, the president who holds the limited veto, the Constitutional Tribunal that polices the line between them. The architecture has been visibly under strain since the 2015 PiS victory began the Constitutional Tribunal contest that the 2023 Tusk return inherited; Karol Nawrocki’s May 3 announcement of a Council for a New Constitution, chaired by his adviser Dariusz Dudek, is the open declaration that the architecture itself is now the contested ground. The Council’s charge is to deliver a draft by 2030. The political project is to convert the 2027 parliamentary election into a referendum on what the Polish regime is.

Begin with the institutional reading, because it is the one Tusk’s dismissal misses. The two-thirds Sejm majority required to change the constitution is unreachable on any current polling — PiS and Civic Coalition sit at 30 and 32 percent respectively, Confederation at 15, the remaining parties below threshold. Tusk’s public response — “the president knows full well that there will not be a constitutional majority in favour of his ideas” — is correct on the arithmetic and wrong on the project. Nawrocki is not running for a two-thirds Sejm; he is running for the framing of every Polish vote between now and 2030. The Council operates on the calendar of presidential politics, not parliamentary arithmetic. By the time the draft is published, the 2027 parliamentary contest will be six months out and the question on the ballot will have been reframed by years of constitutional debate. Aristotle’s Politics is the older reading: the deepest fights in a political community are not over which laws to pass but over which constitution to live under, and the regime that controls the question on the ballot has already won half the contest.

The semi-presidential push is the specific architecture under negotiation. The 1997 system gives Poland a directly-elected president with a veto and a few residual prerogatives but parliamentary supremacy in policy and government formation; the semi-presidential proposal would shift policy-making competence into a presidential office structurally insulated from the Sejm cycle, on the model that France has used since 1958 and that Poland’s neighbour Hungary has pursued through other means. The move serves a specific reading of the past decade: when the president and government are from opposing camps, as they are now and were from 2015 to 2023, the architecture produces paralysis; when they are aligned, the system is the most effective parliamentary regime in Central Europe. Nawrocki’s implicit case is that the architecture was built for governments that no longer exist, and the regime needs to be rewritten before the next decade of governance can be coherent. Lee Kuan Yew’s discipline is the cross-current: institutions earn legitimacy by producing outcomes citizens recognise as effective, and Polish citizens have lived through eight years of cohabitation paralysis. The case for redesign has a constituency. The case for keeping the 1997 settlement is being made by a Prime Minister whose own majority depends on it.

The EU dimension and the sovereignty framing.

Read the second plank of Nawrocki’s charter explicitly: limiting the scope of EU law inside Polish territory. The framing is national-sovereignty conservatism, the same vocabulary that has run through PiS politics since 2015 and that the Polish-German rule-of-law dispute under the von der Leyen Commission left unresolved. Brussels has spent ten years calibrating its instruments against this case: the Article 7 procedure that never quite triggered, the Recovery Fund conditionality that did, the Court of Justice rulings on Polish judicial reform that produced the political ground Tusk inherited. A formal constitutional limit on EU law primacy would be a direct challenge to the supremacy doctrine that has bound the Union since Costa v. ENEL in 1964, and would force the Commission to choose between a slow institutional response and a confrontation it has consistently preferred to defer. Greene’s twenty-third law on concentrated force is the operational discipline: Nawrocki has chosen a single, narrow contested point — the constitution — on which to make his presidency about something larger than its formal powers. Talleyrand’s rule on regimes is the deeper grammar: it is the surviving institution, not the founding one, that defines the legitimate order, and a president who keeps the redesign question alive through five years can convert the parliamentary politics his party cannot dominate into the constitutional politics his office can.

What the Constitution war tests.

Three indicators in the coming year. The first is whether the Council produces visible drafting work or remains a rhetorical body. A working group that publishes interim text by autumn is a serious project; a council that meets quarterly and produces communiques is a campaign tool, which is also useful but in a different register. The second is the Tusk coalition’s response. If Tusk continues to dismiss the project, the Council writes its own framing; if Civic Coalition produces a counter-architecture — a reform proposal of its own, perhaps within the 1997 framework — the constitutional question becomes a two-sided contest, which is the trap PiS would prefer to set. The third is the EU. The Commission has every reason not to escalate while the project is preparatory and every reason to publicly engage if the Council produces a draft that touches EU primacy. Confucius’s rectification of names belongs to the closing question: a presidency that cannot pass a constitution but can keep the question of which constitution to live under at the centre of every vote is not a constraint-following office — it is a campaign for the future regime running on the formal architecture of the current one. The math is unfavourable. The framing is not. The 2027 election is the first verdict.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Two layers carry the Council. The institutional one is the eight-year wear of cohabitation politics on the 1997 Polish constitution: presidents and governments from opposing camps have produced the paralysis the Nawrocki project diagnoses, and the case for a redesigned architecture has a constituency built from lived experience, not only ideology. The second is coordination on the right: Nawrocki, PiS, the Catholic-conservative legal academy, and Confederation share a sovereignty-against-Brussels framing that the Council operationalises in constitutional form, and the alignment lets the presidency act as the centre of a movement whose formal powers it could not generate alone. The mechanism that decides outcomes is whether Civic Coalition responds with substantive counter-architecture or continues to dismiss the project, because the constitutional question is decided by which side sets the framing of the 2027 parliamentary ballot.

What's not driving this: The two-thirds Sejm majority. Reading the project as a legislative bid misses that Nawrocki is not running for the Sejm; he is running for the framing of every vote between now and 2030. The math is unreachable and that is exactly why the politics are not about the math.

What would break this reading:

  • The Council fails to produce any working draft by year-end and dissolves into rhetorical communiques — the “serious constitutional project” reading was wrong, and the Council was a presidential platform with a shorter half-life than expected.
  • Civic Coalition publishes a substantive constitutional-reform counter-proposal of its own — the “Tusk dismisses while Nawrocki frames” reading was wrong, and the constitutional question becomes a two-sided debate that both camps prefer to a Nawrocki monologue.
  • The Commission engages publicly on EU primacy before the Council has produced a draft — the “Brussels calibrates engagement” reading was wrong, and the European institutions decide to set the terms of the constitutional dispute rather than wait.
  • Confederation explicitly endorses the Nawrocki framework and commits to vote with PiS on constitutional questions — the “swing variable” reading was wrong, and the right-wing parliamentary geometry consolidates faster than the Tusk coalition expected.

Law of the Day · No. 23

Concentrate your forces.

Greene’s twenty-third law is the thread through Monday’s three stories. Each is a contest in which one side, faced with limited resources and unlimited opportunities to dissipate them, chose instead a single narrow point of pressure and concentrated everything there. The architecture of the war, the cartel, the regime — large and diffuse — is contested by the actor who picks one ridge, one quota, one founding text, and stakes the contest on it. The worldview’s sharper claim sits underneath: coordinated power is the default, and the side that wins is usually not the side with more force but the side that chose the place where force can decide.

Today’s reading: the climb, the quota, the council. In Beaufort, the IDF did not open a broad front; it picked one ridge that any future negotiated frontier will be drawn around and climbed it, knowing that the diplomatic machinery cannot reverse a fixed position before Tuesday. In Vienna, the UAE chose the single moment when Hormuz was closed and the Saudis were distracted by the Iran war to walk out, converting an unwinnable internal-cartel argument into a market-share contest it can fight from outside. In Warsaw, Nawrocki chose the constitution — the founding text his office cannot rewrite alone — as the single contested point of his presidency, knowing that the math is unfavourable and the framing is decisive.

  • The Crusader Climb concentrates force on the one ridge that decides every future map — the diplomatic line is drawn around what was already taken.
  • The Vacant Quota concentrates pressure on a single contested moment — the exit happens when the cartel cannot afford to respond.
  • The Constitution War concentrates a presidency on one debate — the office cannot pass the text but it can decide what every vote is about.

The Practitioner’s Note

Pick the ridge, then climb.

Each of Monday’s stories turns on the same instrument read three ways: an actor with less force than the structure around it nevertheless decides the contest by choosing one specific place where its force is enough. In Beaufort, the Israeli operation did not need to defeat Hezbollah broadly; it needed to take the one ridge that any future map would be drawn around. In Vienna, the UAE did not need to win the quota argument inside OPEC; it needed to leave at the one moment the cartel could not afford to fight back. In Warsaw, Nawrocki cannot pass a constitution; he can choose the constitution as the topic of every Polish political argument for the next five years. Greene’s twenty-third law is the discipline: identify the one point where your concentrated force decides the contest, and accept that you cannot win at every point. The diffuse opponent has more resources and less direction; the concentrated actor has less force and more focus, and focus is what decides the contests that matter.

In your own affairs: the law cuts three ways. When you face an architecture larger than you, do not contest it at every point — identify the single ridge, quota, or framing whose capture decides the whole and concentrate there. When you have a position you cannot defend everywhere, choose the place you can hold and let the rest go; the structure that demands you defend every front is the structure that grinds you down by attrition. And when you are tempted to spread effort across many initiatives in your work or your life, ask which single one of them, if it succeeded, would make most of the others unnecessary — that is the ridge to climb. The Beaufort discipline is the inverse of the goodwill discipline that ran through yesterday’s edition: where Greene’s thirteenth law builds the architecture that lasts, his twenty-third law decides which ridge inside the architecture is actually worth taking.

The hard question: in your own life, where are you fighting on multiple fronts because you have not yet identified the one ridge that would decide the whole contest? Which arrangement of yours is being lost at every point because you have not chosen the point where your force can win? Greene’s twenty-third law is the standing discipline — pick the ridge, then climb; let go of the perimeter you cannot hold; and accept that the contest is decided not at the front you defend but at the front you choose to attack.
