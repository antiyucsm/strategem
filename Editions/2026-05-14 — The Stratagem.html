Lead Analysis · Trump Lands in Beijing With a CEO Column for the First US Presidential Visit in Nearly a Decade; Xi Opens With the "New Crossroads" Framing and Keeps the Calendar

The Beijing Audience

Donald Trump landed in Beijing on Wednesday evening and walked into the Great Hall of the People on Thursday morning for the first US presidential bilateral with Xi Jinping on Chinese soil in nearly a decade. The delegation behind him was the tell: Treasury Secretary Bessent, State Secretary Rubio, Defence Secretary Hegseth, and behind them a CEO column — Jensen Huang of Nvidia, Tim Cook of Apple, Elon Musk of Tesla and SpaceX, and the Boeing and BlackRock principals. Xi opened with the framing the Chinese side had prepared for weeks: the world had "arrived at a new crossroads," the two states "should be partners rather than opponents," and the global system should be "stabilised" through joint work. Trump's response — "fantastic relationship," "great leader," "an honour to be your friend" — accepted the framing. The summit will run through Friday, May 15, with trade, tariffs, rare-earth export licences, AI-chip controls, Taiwan arms sales and the Iran file all on the agenda. The audience is the move. The question is which side controls the calendar the audience produces.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Beijing & Washington·14 May 2026

Beijing —A state visit is a piece of choreography that names which side asked for the audience. Trump went to Beijing — not Anchorage, not Honolulu, not a third capital, not Mar-a-Lago. The CEO column behind him went on Trump's manifest, not Xi's invitation list. The opening tableau in the Great Hall — Xi delivering the "new crossroads" frame in steady cadence, Trump returning the warmth in a register pitched for the cameras at home — is the choreography of a host receiving a petitioner who has dressed the petition in the language of partnership. It is not a humiliation; it is a hierarchy. The question across the next thirty-six hours is what the petitioner secures and at what price, and on every line on the agenda Beijing has more time on the clock than Washington has.

Read the geometry of who came to whom. The April 2025 reciprocal-tariff exchange settled into the autumn truce on terms both sides framed as their own win; the spring 2026 calendar then ran against Washington faster than against Beijing. Inflation has reaccelerated in the United States — Wednesday's PPI shock above. The Iran war has stretched the Pentagon's inventory across Hormuz, the Gulf, and the Patriot pipeline now committed to Ukraine. Congressional control through November is contested. The Trump White House came to Beijing carrying the Boeing book of orders, the soybean-and-LNG commitments the trade deficit theatre requires, and an explicit reading of Beijing as the swing vote on the Iran question — China still takes nine in ten barrels of Iranian crude. Beijing came to the table with rare-earth export licences, the AI-chip-buyer position Jensen Huang has personally flown across the Pacific to defend, and the patient knowledge that the American electoral cycle has eighteen months on it and the Chinese institutional cycle does not.

"Therefore the skillful leader of armies subdues the enemy's troops without any fighting; he captures their cities without laying siege to them; he overthrows their kingdom without lengthy operations in the field." — Sun Tzu, The Art of War, III. The summit is the city captured without siege. Xi has not had to deploy a single carrier or pass a single export-control statute to secure the choreography in which the American president flies to Beijing with the chief executives of Nvidia, Apple, Tesla, and Boeing in the delegation. The substantive concessions across the next thirty-six hours will be framed in both directions as mutual; the choreography has already been settled on Beijing's terms, and the framing of "partners rather than opponents" is the line on which every subsequent concession will be measured.

The leverage seam.

Mearsheimer on the structural logic of bipolar competition under managed rivalry is the cleanest first overlay. Two great powers in a contested order do not reconcile; they manage. Each summit is a piece of that management — a re-pricing of the leverage stack each side has accumulated since the last meeting, signalled through the calibration of public concessions inside choreographed reciprocity. The leverage Beijing now holds is concentrated: rare-earth processing share above seventy percent of global supply, the buyer-of-last-resort position on Iranian crude, the AI-compute consumer base Nvidia's revenue trajectory has come to depend on, and the structural patience of a regime whose succession horizon is measured in decades rather than electoral cycles. The leverage Washington holds is broader but spent faster: the dollar invoicing system, the semiconductor design stack, and the security architecture across the Indo-Pacific. The asymmetry is in the time-discounting. Washington needs deliverables this week. Beijing needs nothing on this calendar.

Talleyrand on the diplomat's task is the practitioner overlay. Above all, not too much zeal: the discipline of the cooler party at any negotiation is to let the warmer party fill the silence with concessions. The American CEO column that landed in Beijing on Trump's plane is the warmer party made institutional — Cook needs the Chinese consumer market, Huang needs the export licence, Musk needs the Shanghai Gigafactory clearance and the Starlink-adjacent regulatory posture. Each is a private-sector principal whose presence on the manifest signals the executive branch's willingness to underwrite their commercial position with the summit's diplomatic capital. The Chinese side reads each principal accurately. The concession on rare earths, when it lands, will be framed as a gesture; the concession Beijing receives on AI-chip exports will be priced against the cumulative book of Cook-Huang-Musk dependencies.

The Taiwan question.

Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of small states between giants is the institutional read on what Taipei is watching. The Taipei Appropriation cleared the Legislative Yuan a week ago — a $25 billion balancing-instrument cheque written by the KMT-TPP majority. The summit's Taiwan file is not whether Trump will sell Taipei out — the FMS pipeline is contracted, the systems are committed, and the political cost of an explicit reversal exceeds any plausible Chinese counter-offer. The file is the framing: whether the summit communiqué names "one China" in the strong form Beijing has insisted on since 1972 or in the weaker "acknowledge" register Washington has held since the Shanghai Communiqué, and whether arms-sale notifications are slowed, paused, or merely choreographed as routine. LKY's discipline applies in inverse here: Taipei is the small state whose room for manoeuvre is determined not by what it does but by what its giant patron declines to surrender. The summit's outputs on Taiwan will not be the headlines. They will be the small calendar shifts — a notification delayed, an exercise rescheduled, a transit silenced — that Beijing reads as the substantive concession and that Washington can deny it has made.

Dugin on the Eurasian land-power's reading of the maritime-power's forward edge is the structural overlay on what Beijing wants. The maritime power has come to the continental capital with its industrial principals in the train. The continental power's task is not to extract a single decisive concession; it is to extract a stream of small ones each of which reframes the next as routine. Iran sanctions relief in exchange for a Taiwan posture moderation; rare-earth licence ease in exchange for AI-chip access; soybean-and-LNG order books in exchange for currency-management commitments. Each trade is individually defensible to a domestic American audience. The cumulative effect, read from the Eurasian capital, is the recalibration of the western Pacific security architecture toward a Chinese-centred order — not by force but by the cadence of summit choreography. The land power does not need the maritime power's defeat. It needs the maritime power's exhaustion.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: a bipolar competition between an incumbent maritime hegemon and a rising continental peer is being managed through summit choreography in which the asymmetry of time-discounting favours the side with the longer institutional horizon, and Washington's spring-2026 economic and Hormuz inventories have shortened its time on the clock relative to Beijing's. Tactical: the choreography of the audience — host capital, CEO column, opening framing — has been settled on Beijing's terms before substantive negotiation has begun, and the framing now sets the register against which every subsequent concession will be measured. What is not driving this is the public theatre of the trade deficit or the tariff arithmetic; the underlying file is the leverage transfer the cumulative concessions across the next thirty-six hours encode. The Stratagem reads the deliverables window as the framework-not-substance one — the Friday communiqué will name a Board of Trade, a soybean purchase, perhaps a rare-earth working group, and will defer the load-bearing files (Taiwan, AI chips, Iran) to follow-on tracks Beijing controls the calendar of. The audience has been granted. The terms of the next audience are what the summit is actually negotiating.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: A bipolar managed-rivalry summit is being negotiated under an asymmetry of time-discounting that favours the side with the longer institutional horizon. The structural mechanism is concrete: Washington came to Beijing because its spring-2026 inventories — economic, military, political — have shortened the calendar it can wait on, while Beijing's leverage stack (rare-earth processing, Iranian-crude buyer position, AI-chip consumer base) sits as unspent reserve a regime with no electoral exit can hold indefinitely. The tactical layer is the choreography: the audience is in Beijing, the CEO column travels on Trump's manifest, and Xi opened with the framing the entire negotiation will be measured against. Each substantive concession over the next thirty-six hours is being priced into a register Beijing has already set. The framework deliverables (Board of Trade, soybean order book, rare-earth working group) are likely; the load-bearing files (Taiwan posture, AI-chip controls, Iran-sanctions reciprocity) get deferred to follow-on tracks whose calendar Beijing controls.

What's not driving this: The trade-deficit theatre or the tariff arithmetic. The $202 billion deficit framing is the public surface the White House communications cycle requires for the Republican base; the underlying file is the leverage-transfer pattern the cumulative concessions across the summit encode. Reading the summit as a tariff negotiation misses the cleaner read: it is a recalibration of the western Pacific security-and-supply-chain architecture, transacted through commercial deliverables and small-calendar shifts.

What would break this reading:

  • The Friday communiqué names a specific, dated US arms-sale pause to Taiwan or downgrades the routine-language register the post-1972 framework has held — the small-calendar reading was wrong and the substantive concession was made at the headline level rather than the notification-calendar one.
  • Beijing announces an immediate, unconditional rare-earth export-licence ease before the communiqué signature — the leverage-stack-as-unspent-reserve reading was wrong, Beijing was prepared to spend the reserve at the headline level rather than across the follow-on track.
  • Trump publicly walks out or shortens the visit citing Chinese non-cooperation on Iran sanctions — the warmer-party reading was wrong and Washington had more confidence in its bargaining position than the choreography suggested.
  • The Indo-Pacific allied cohort (Tokyo, Seoul, Manila) issues coordinated public statements within 72 hours of the communiqué expressing concern over specific Taiwan or alliance commitments — the alliance-management line in the Rubio file was inadequate and the burden-shift framing extended to East Asia faster than the article reads.

The Warsh Inheritance · April PPI Rises 1.4 Percent Against 0.4 Expected, the Ten-Year Yield Touches a 2026 High, and the Senate Confirms Kevin Warsh on the Wednesday Before Powell's Friday Exit

The Warsh Inheritance

Wednesday delivered two pieces of news on the same calendar. The Bureau of Labour Statistics released April producer prices: a 1.4 percent month-on-month jump, against a 0.4 percent consensus, the sharpest single-month PPI print since 2022, with core PPI rising 1.0 percent against a 0.3 percent expectation. The Senate, on the same afternoon, voted to confirm Kevin Warsh as the next Chair of the Federal Reserve. The ten-year Treasury yield closed at 4.48 percent — the highest of the year — and the thirty-year traded above 5 percent for the first time since the 2023 regional-bank stress window. Jerome Powell's term as Chair expires on Friday, May 15; he stays on the Board of Governors but the gavel passes. Warsh — the Fed's prior dove-with-credibility, the AI-as-disinflationary-force voice, the man on record for a target range rather than a fixed two percent — inherits the chair on a week in which the inflation data has just delivered the worst possible welcome and the bond market has registered its vote before the man has unpacked his office. The inheritance is the hot print, not the soft handover.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington & New York·14 May 2026

Washington —A central-bank handover is supposed to be the calmest piece of choreography on the institutional calendar. Two men in suits stand for a photograph, the outgoing chair hands a ceremonial gavel that no chair has ever literally used, the new chair gives a statement so deliberately blank that the bond market is supposed to register it as continuity. Wednesday produced something else. The PPI print landed at 0830 — 1.4 percent against 0.4 percent expected, the worst headline beat of the post-pandemic series — and within ninety minutes the ten-year Treasury was through 4.45, the thirty-year through 5, and the futures curve had erased the modest summer-cut probability the Powell-era guidance had let drift in. By the time the Senate gavelled Warsh's confirmation in the late afternoon, the new chair had inherited a market that had already priced his first decision. The inheritance is not a soft transition; it is a bond-market vote against the easing path Warsh was nominated to deliver.

Read the geometry of the data Warsh inherits. CPI on Tuesday printed at 3.8 percent year-on-year — the highest since the 2023 reacceleration scare. April PPI on Wednesday delivered the supply-side companion. Energy costs carried the bulk of the headline jump — Hormuz residuals in the diesel and jet-fuel cuts of the index — but the core measure, ex-energy and ex-food, also surprised by three quarters of a percentage point. The implication is not a one-month outlier explainable by oil. The implication is that the disinflationary path the Powell-era forecasts had built around — services inflation rolling over into 2026, goods inflation already at trend — is not the path the data is actually walking. The bond market has read the gap and re-priced the curve before the new chair has any institutional capacity to deny the read.

"Know yourself and know your enemy; in a hundred battles you will not be in peril. Know yourself but not your enemy; for every victory you will suffer a defeat. Know neither yourself nor your enemy; in every battle you will be defeated." — Sun Tzu, The Art of War, III. The bond market is the enemy Warsh inherits without having yet identified. The market knows what Warsh thinks — the prior speeches, the AI-as-structurally-disinflationary thesis, the range-rather-than-point inflation target, the dovish posture against a Powell hawk-pivot. Warsh's first task is to know what the market thinks of him: not as a thinker but as a chair, which is a different institutional position the market reads through actions rather than through speeches. The PPI shock is the market's opening proposition. Warsh's response in the next four weeks will name whether the inheritance is being honoured or contested.

The credibility seam.

Munger on incentive-caused bias is the first overlay. Every incoming Fed chair faces the same incentive: to establish hawkish bona fides early so that any subsequent easing reads as data-driven rather than as ideological. The Warsh prior — AI-as-disinflationary, target-range-not-point, dovish posture — is structurally vulnerable to the early-tenure data and must be defended before it can be deployed. The PPI shock is the data that forecloses the easy version of the defence. Warsh cannot cut at the June 17 meeting on a hot PPI print without producing the inflation-credibility cost that would unmoor the entire dovish thesis. He cannot hike either — hiking would repudiate the appointment's framing and produce the political cost the White House communications cycle was not prepared for. The hold-and-wait position is the only defensible one, and the hold is the inheritance the market has already priced.

Dalio's big-cycle framework is the macro overlay. The post-2020 monetary cycle is the long-cycle reflation pattern: large fiscal expansion, monetisation of the resulting debt, currency-management negotiations through reserve-currency competitors, and the slow re-anchoring of inflation expectations at levels above the prior regime. The thirty-year yield through five percent is not a one-week trade; it is the market's repricing of the long-end inflation expectation upward as the disinflationary forces (AI productivity, China deflation export, post-pandemic normalisation) compete with the inflationary ones (Hormuz energy, deglobalisation, fiscal dominance, tariff cycle). Warsh's institutional task is to hold the credibility of the inflation target while the cycle that target was calibrated against is materially re-anchoring. The Powell inheritance is not the rate level — that is set by the FOMC — it is the credibility infrastructure the Powell chair built around the 2 percent commitment. Warsh now has to either honour that infrastructure or rebuild it on his own framing. The bond market's read is that the rebuild is the harder task.

The handover question.

Talleyrand on succession is the institutional read. Begin by listening; do not speak until you have heard the room. The Senate-confirmed chair who arrives in the middle of an inflation upside surprise has the choice between an early hawkish signal and a deliberate silence; either reads as a commitment to the prior framework rather than as an independent posture. Powell remains on the Board of Governors. The institutional weight of the prior chair on the new chair's first three meetings is structurally heavier than the announcements have framed. The Warsh team's first task is the choice between treating Powell's continued presence as a stabiliser (which preserves the framework's credibility at the cost of the appointment's distinctiveness) and treating it as a competing institutional pole (which establishes the new chair's authority at the cost of public Fed disagreement during a fragile data window). The institutional incentive is the first. The political incentive — the White House's preference for visible easing — is the second. The bond market's vote on Wednesday is that the institutional read holds.

La Rochefoucauld is the cleaner read on what the political class is privately registering. Self-love is the greatest of all flatterers. The White House had nominated Warsh on the explicit framing that the next chair would deliver the easing Powell would not. The PPI shock is the data that names that framing as flattery rather than as analysis. Warsh is institutionally compelled to disappoint the appointment's premise in the first three meetings of his tenure or to spend his credibility prematurely and have nothing left for the second half of the cycle when the data may genuinely support the easing the appointment was meant to deliver. The discipline is the discipline of any new principal in a senior role: spend authority on the most important decisions, not on the first ones. The market's pricing of zero cuts through year-end is the market's vote that Warsh will be disciplined.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: the long-cycle reflation pattern is re-anchoring inflation expectations above the post-2008 regime, the disinflationary and inflationary forces are running close to balance, and the data flow no longer cleanly favours the easing thesis the Warsh appointment was framed around. Tactical: the bond market voted on Wednesday before the new chair had institutional capacity to respond, and the curve has re-priced through the rest of 2026 on a hold-not-cut assumption that the new chair will be institutionally compelled to honour. What is not driving this is a Warsh political ideology mismatch — the chair has the framework the appointment expected, but the data window has compressed the runway for deploying it. The Stratagem reads the next three FOMC meetings (June 17, July 30, September 17) as the recalibration window: whether the data delivers Warsh the cover for a September move, whether the bond curve absorbs a hold without further spillover into the long end, and whether the political pressure on the chair from the White House communications cycle produces the early signal the institutional discipline says he cannot afford. The handover has been completed. The inheritance is the data the new chair has not yet replied to.

The Kyiv Forge

Boris Pistorius and Mykhailo Fedorov signed a letter of intent in Kyiv on Monday establishing “Brave Germany” — a joint German-Ukrainian industrial defence programme run through the Brave1 innovation cluster, formally targeting short and medium-range drones under 100 kilometres, long-range strike systems out to 1,500 kilometres, AI-enabled weapons, contract-recruitment reform, and what the Ukrainian side has begun naming “cognitive warfare” capabilities. The Bundeswehr Census a week ago priced the civic contract Berlin had not yet underwritten. Brave Germany is the answer Berlin chose to write: not the bodies the Wehrdienst registered the refusal of, but the industrial substance the bodies were the constraint on. The forge has been opened. The terms on which it has been opened are the terms the next phase of the European war is now being fought on.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Kyiv & Berlin·14 May 2026

Kyiv —A joint production line is the most committing thing one state can sign with another short of an alliance treaty. The capital cost is split, the supply chain is integrated across two political jurisdictions, the workforce is co-located, and the off-take is contracted in advance against a war whose duration both signatories have just publicly priced as long. Pistorius did not fly to Kyiv to deliver a cheque; cheques can be reversed, paused, or quietly slowed by the receiving end's burden tolerance. He flew to Kyiv to sign capital infrastructure into Ukrainian soil. The Brave Germany letter of intent is the architecture of a two-country defence-industrial complex — drones under 100 km, strike systems out to 1,500 km, AI-enabled weapons, joint contract-recruitment reform, cognitive-warfare capabilities, six production lines confirmed, military training centres financed. The instrument is not aid. The instrument is a forge.

Read the geometry of the timing. The Trump administration has spent the spring telegraphing a 5,000-troop European drawdown, conditional Article 5 framing, and the explicit demand that European NATO members underwrite the conventional defence of Europe on their own balance sheets. The May 9 Moscow Victory Day pageant came and went; the three-day Trump-brokered ceasefire that bracketed it collapsed on the second day, both sides accusing the other of drone strikes through the truce window. The Kremlin's May 9 public posture — peace remains “a very long way off” — closed the negotiating space the administration had spent April trying to open. Berlin reads the signal. If Washington is exiting the visible underwriting role over the next eighteen months, the only viable European answer is the industrial substitution that lets Ukraine outlast the Russian war economy without a permanent American operational presence behind it. Pistorius's Kyiv signature is the answer in the form that survives a US policy reversal: not bilateral political will, which is contestable on the next election day, but contracted production capacity, which is contestable on the timeline of a factory.

"War is the continuation of policy by other means; therefore the highest form of the art is to fight in such a manner that the policy itself is the instrument carried into the field." — Clausewitz, Vom Kriege. The policy carried into the field by the Brave Germany letter is not the policy of the 2022 Zeitenwende speech, which was a budget commitment in Berlin. It is the policy of a forward European-industrial-base strategy in which Germany underwrites Ukrainian production rather than imports Ukrainian destruction. The forge is the policy. The drones produced inside it are the means by which the policy is fought.

The industrial seam.

Mearsheimer on offshore balancing under conditions of hegemon retrenchment is the cleanest first overlay. A continental power that has lost confidence in its offshore patron's underwriting must either bandwagon with the rising threat (Russia, in this geography) or build the conventional balancing instrument inside its own production envelope. Bandwagoning is what AfD-coded populism would prescribe; the Merz government has structurally foreclosed that route. Building the balancing instrument inside the production envelope is the alternative, and the German industrial-base capacity to deliver it — Rheinmetall, KMW-Nexter, Diehl, Hensoldt — is the most concentrated in continental Europe. The Brave Germany frame extends the German industrial base across the Polish-Ukrainian border at the manufacturing-cost-and-speed advantage the wartime Ukrainian production environment carries. The structural read is that a balancing instrument forged inside the threatened state's own economy is materially harder for the threat power to interdict than a balancing instrument shipped from the rear.

Helen Thompson on the European political economy of war is the macro overlay. The post-2022 European energy reorientation has built a German industrial base whose dependence on inexpensive Russian gas has been replaced by a structurally higher cost-of-energy operating environment. The choices available to that industrial base are managed contraction (the Mittelstand decline the AfD electoral surge has built around), services pivot (which the German workforce composition does not support at scale), or wartime industrial reorientation toward defence demand. The Brave Germany framework chooses the third. Ukrainian production environments offer Rheinmetall and the German Mittelstand the manufacturing-cost advantage that German wages and energy prices no longer offer, and German technology and capital offer Ukraine the production scale Ukrainian industry has not been able to build under sustained Russian missile pressure. The forge is the trade.

The Merz question.

Confucius on rectification of names runs through the political read. The Merz CDU campaigned on a posture of fiscal discipline, debt-brake preservation, and structural reform; the Brave Germany framework requires precisely the opposite — open-ended defence-industrial commitment, multi-year capital flow into Ukrainian production, and the institutional pre-commitment to a war whose end the German political class cannot calendar. The naming gap is the political problem. Merz's approval at 19 percent and the AfD polling lead at 28 percent reflect a cohort that has not been told the cost of the policy the chancellor is signing in Kyiv through his defence minister. La Rochefoucauld is the cleaner read: we promise according to our hopes and perform according to our fears. The Zeitenwende was promised as a five-year discontinuity. Brave Germany is the performance — open-ended, industrial, joint, and structured around the fear that Washington will not be the underwriter the promise priced. The constitutional weight of the commitment exceeds the political authorisation the chancellor has assembled for it. The next year of German politics is the period inside which the gap either narrows or breaks.

Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of small-state defence procurement runs in inverse here. LKY's doctrine was: a small state with a credible existential threat builds defence-industrial capacity ahead of political demand for it. Ukraine is the small state; Germany is the great power supplying the procurement architecture. The discipline is the Ukrainian discipline — building production capacity on a war calendar while absorbing daily missile and drone strikes against the infrastructure that capacity depends on. The German contribution is the capital and technology that lets that discipline scale beyond what Ukrainian state finances can underwrite alone. The Brave Germany framework is recognisable as the LKY architecture applied two-state: the technology and capital great-power partner underwrites the threatened state's defence-industrial scale-up, on the calculation that a small state which can produce its own deterrent inside the threat geography is materially more survivable than one whose deterrent must be shipped from outside.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the story. Structural: a continental power has read the offshore hegemon's retrenchment signal accurately and has committed industrial-base capacity to underwrite the threatened state's defence production on a calendar measured in years, on the calculation that contracted factory capacity inside the threat geography survives a US policy reversal that a political-will-only commitment would not. Coordination: the Berlin-Kyiv signature has run ahead of the political authorisation the Merz coalition has assembled at home, the Brave1 innovation-cluster architecture has been institutionally extended to Germany without parliamentary debate at the structural level, and the bilateral framework substantively pre-commits Germany to a war whose end the German political class cannot calendar. What is not driving this is European-cohort consensus; France's industrial-base posture, Poland's preference for sovereign procurement, and the Italian and Spanish positions are not yet aligned with the Berlin-Kyiv axis. The Stratagem reads the next eighteen months as the political-authorisation window — whether the Merz coalition can build a domestic political consensus for the commitment the defence ministry has already made, whether the AfD framing of Brave Germany as “German taxpayer underwriting of a foreign war” gains the electoral altitude the Sachsen-Anhalt September Landtag will measure, and whether the forge actually produces the volume the contracted capacity has been priced on. The letter of intent has been signed. The political contract that authorises it has not yet been written.

Always say less than necessary.

And what would change if you held the next silence longer than your anxiety naturally permits — long enough for the other party to fill it with the framing, concession, or reveal you have been speaking in order to extract?
