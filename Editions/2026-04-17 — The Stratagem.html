Law of the Day · No. 29

Plan All the Way to the End.

The ending is everything. Plan all the way to it, taking into account all the possible consequences, obstacles, and twists of fortune that might reverse your hard work. By planning to the end you will not be overwhelmed by circumstances and you will know when to stop.

Today's inversion: each of the three stories below involves an actor who made a bold opening move — ceasefire, forecast, fiscal commitment — without a visible endgame. The opening is not the hard part. The ending is.

  • Ask: does this plan have a defined end state — or only a beginning?
  • A ceasefire without terms is not peace. A forecast without a policy is not a plan. A fund without delivery is not reform.
  • The most dangerous move is the one that looks bold at the start and has no exit at the end.
From the Classics

Clausewitz & Machiavelli.

No one starts a war — or rather, no one in his senses ought to do so — without first being clear in his mind what he intends to achieve by that war and how he intends to conduct it.

— Carl von Clausewitz

The Romans never allowed a trouble to develop in order to avoid a war, because they knew that war is not avoided but only postponed to the other's advantage.

— Niccolò Machiavelli

The Practitioner's Note

On moves without endings.

A ceasefire without terms is a clock without hands. An economic forecast without a policy response is a diagnosis without treatment. A half-trillion-euro fund without delivery milestones is a promise that erodes with every passing month.

In your own affairs: identify one commitment you have made — to a project, a relationship, a strategy — where you planned the opening but not the close. What would "planning to the end" require you to decide today?

The hard question: are you avoiding the end because it is difficult, or because defining it would force you to confront what you actually want?

The Nested Truce

A ceasefire inside a ceasefire. Israel and Lebanon pause while the Iran clock still ticks. The precondition was met — on paper. The contradictions were not.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Beirut & Washington·17 April 2026

Beirut —At midnight on April 17, gunshots rang across Beirut — not in violence but in celebration. The 10-day ceasefire between Israel and Lebanon had taken effect. Families began moving toward southern Lebanon despite official warnings. Day 49 of the broader conflict. The Lebanon truce is not the peace. It is a precondition for the negotiation that is supposed to produce the peace. A truce nested inside a truce, each one contingent on the other.

The architecture is as follows. On April 7, the United States and Iran agreed to a two-week ceasefire expiring April 22. The Islamabad talks collapsed. Iran then conditioned any second round of negotiations on a ceasefire in Lebanon — the war Israel launched against Hezbollah on March 2, after Hezbollah opened a support front for Tehran. On April 16, Trump announced the 10-day Lebanon truce, brokered through direct Israeli-Lebanese talks in Washington — the first since 1993. Iran’s precondition was formally met. But three structural contradictions arrived with it.

”No one starts a war — or rather, no one in his senses ought to do so — without first being clear in his mind what he intends to achieve by that war and how he intends to conduct it.” — Clausewitz.

The occupation contradiction.

Netanyahu agreed to the ceasefire — and immediately announced that Israeli troops would remain in southern Lebanon, establishing a ten-kilometre “security zone” north of the border. Hezbollah’s response was precise: “Israeli occupation on our land grants Lebanon and its people the right to resist it.” A ceasefire in which one side retains military occupation and the other reserves the right to resist that occupation is not a ceasefire in any meaningful operational sense. It is a pause with a fuse attached.

The historical parallel is not the Korean armistice this time. It is the Dayton Accords of 1995. Dayton ended the Bosnian War by freezing the lines where they stood — creating a peace that was formally a peace and structurally a partition. The contradictions were not resolved; they were embedded in the architecture. Twenty years later, Bosnia’s constitutional structure still reflects the ceasefire lines. Netanyahu’s security zone risks the same pattern: a “temporary” presence that becomes permanent because removing it requires a political cost no Israeli leader will pay, and maintaining it requires a military cost that erodes the ceasefire from within.

The precondition paradox.

Iran demanded a Lebanon ceasefire before resuming negotiations with the United States. The ceasefire was delivered. But the substance of the ceasefire — Israeli troops staying, Hezbollah reserving resistance rights, no timeline for withdrawal — means Iran can claim the precondition was met or not met, depending on which interpretation serves its next move. This is Voss’s concept of “tactical empathy” turned inside out: Iran set a precondition it knew would be met ambiguously, preserving optionality at the exact moment Washington thought it was closing a loop.

Greene’s Law 29 describes the error precisely: plan all the way to the end. Washington planned the ceasefire as a means to the next round of talks. It did not plan the substance of the ceasefire as a self-contained settlement. The result is a move that looked like progress — Trump told reporters “something very positive” was happening — but that created a new set of leverage points for the other side.

The mediators ascend.

What has changed since yesterday’s edition is the role of intermediaries. The Lebanon ceasefire was brokered through direct talks in Washington, with Secretary of State Marco Rubio mediating between Israeli and Lebanese delegations. But the Iran track remains hosted by Pakistan, with Egypt and Turkey running parallel channels. Two truces, two mediator architectures, two separate diplomatic ecosystems — each with its own momentum and its own stakeholders.

Mearsheimer’s structural realism predicts exactly this: when the great powers are locked in negotiation, the medium powers do not wait. They build. Pakistan’s hosting of the Iran talks and Washington’s hosting of the Lebanon talks are not parallel by accident. They are competitive — each host accumulating diplomatic capital that will outlast whatever settlement emerges. The question is not who brokers the peace. It is who becomes indispensable to the process, and therefore to the architecture that follows.

Five days on the clock.

The Iran-US ceasefire expires April 22. The Lebanon ceasefire expires April 26. The nesting is deliberate: if the Iran truce extends, the Lebanon truce has room to breathe. If the Iran truce collapses, the Lebanon truce becomes meaningless — Hezbollah’s war was always a front of Iran’s conflict, not an independent campaign.

Trump says Tehran is close to a deal. Iran’s ten-point negotiation plan demands frozen-asset release, enrichment rights, Hormuz toll authority, and an end to Israeli operations in Lebanon. These are not opening positions; they are structural demands that define the regime’s minimum viable survival. The gap between “something very positive” and Iran’s ten points is the gap between Law 29 obeyed and Law 29 violated. Someone has planned to the end. The question is whether it is Washington or Tehran.

The lenses used.

What’s driving this: The Lebanon ceasefire exists because Iran demanded it as a precondition for nuclear talks — not because Israel and Lebanon independently wanted peace. That makes it structurally dependent on the Iran-US truce. If the larger truce collapses on April 22, the Lebanon ceasefire becomes meaningless overnight. The nested structure, not the diplomatic language, determines whether either truce survives.

What’s not driving this: Goodwill between Israel and Lebanon. The first direct talks since 1993 happened because Washington needed them to happen, not because the parties were ready.

What would break this reading:

  • Israel withdraws troops from southern Lebanon before April 26 — the “frozen occupation” reading was premature.
  • Iran opens second-round talks before April 22 without insisting on Lebanon withdrawal — the precondition was genuine, not instrumental.
  • Hezbollah attacks during the ceasefire — the nested-truce architecture collapses from inside.
  • The Lebanon truce outlasts the Iran truce — the dependency structure was wrong.

In the Shadow of War

The IMF has issued its first full economic accounting of the Iran conflict. Three scenarios, one chokepoint, and a world economy that may already be past the point of easy recovery.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington & Geneva·17 April 2026

Washington —The International Monetary Fund titled its April 2026 World Economic Outlook with unusual candour: Global Economy in the Shadow of War. Released on April 14, the report is the first comprehensive institutional accounting of the economic damage wrought by the US-Israeli strikes on Iran that began February 28. The numbers are precise. The implications are not.

Global growth is projected at 3.1% for 2026, down from the January forecast of 3.3%. Global headline inflation reverses its disinflationary trend and rises to 4.4%. The reference scenario assumes a short-lived conflict and a “moderate” 19% increase in energy commodity prices. That word — moderate — is doing more work than anything else in the document, because Brent crude has already touched $103 per barrel this month and the Strait of Hormuz remains gridlocked at fewer than 15 vessel transits per day, down from the pre-crisis average of 130.

”Invert, always invert.” — Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi, via Charlie Munger. Ask not what happens if the war ends. Ask what happens if it doesn't.

The three scenarios.

The IMF's analytical power lies not in its baseline but in its scenarios. The reference case — 3.1% growth, 4.4% inflation — assumes the conflict is “limited in duration and scope.” The adverse scenario — 2.5% growth, 5.4% inflation — assumes the war lingers, energy prices average $100 per barrel through the year, and financial conditions tighten further. The severe scenario — 2.0% growth, above 6% inflation — assumes Hormuz disruptions extend into 2027 and oil spikes 100-200% above January levels.

The distance between 3.1% and 2.0% is the distance between a soft landing and a global recession. The IMF defines a recession as growth below 2%, an event that has occurred only four times since 1980. What the scenarios reveal is that the variable determining which world we inhabit is not monetary policy, not fiscal stimulus, not trade architecture. It is the Strait of Hormuz. Twenty percent of global oil and liquefied natural gas passes through that channel. The chokepoint is the economy's hinge.

The map and the territory.

Munger's latticework insists on distinguishing the map from the territory. The IMF's three scenarios are maps. The territory is a strait where fewer than 15 ships per day are passing, where Iran has imposed a $2 million toll on transiting vessels, where sea mines have been laid and not fully cleared, and where the US Navy is simultaneously enforcing a blockade of Iranian ports. The reference scenario assumes this resolves quickly. The territory suggests otherwise.

The structural pattern is what Dalio calls a “conflict-driven phase” of the Big Cycle: geopolitical confrontation produces an energy shock that cascades into inflation, tighter financial conditions, slower growth, and political instability in commodity-importing nations. The 1973 oil crisis is the obvious historical parallel, but the analogy is imprecise in one critical dimension. In 1973, the producer (OPEC) weaponised supply. In 2026, both the producer (Iran, closing the strait) and the consumer (the US, enforcing a blockade) are simultaneously restricting the same chokepoint. The strait is being squeezed from both ends.

Who absorbs the shock.

The IMF is explicit: pressures are concentrated in “emerging market and developing economies, especially commodity importers with preexisting vulnerabilities.” Iran's own economy is projected to contract by 6.1% — a 7.2-point downward revision. But the second-order casualties are not the combatants. They are the bystanders. Countries that import both oil and food — Pakistan, Egypt, Bangladesh, sub-Saharan Africa — face the compound pressure of higher energy costs, higher food prices (fertiliser prices track energy), and tighter dollar-denominated borrowing conditions.

This is Munger's Lollapalooza effect at the macroeconomic scale. No single pressure — oil, inflation, financial tightening — would be sufficient to tip the global economy into recession. But stacked together, each amplifying the next, the combined force exceeds what any individual variable would predict. The IMF's severe scenario is not a stress test for an unlikely event. It is a description of what happens when the Lollapalooza stack reaches its third or fourth layer.

The forecast as instrument.

There is a deeper read. The IMF report is not merely descriptive. It is performative. When the Fund publishes a scenario in which global growth falls to 2%, it is not predicting that outcome. It is creating the institutional permission for central banks to cut rates, for governments to expand fiscal buffers, and for international lenders to restructure debt — all in the name of preventing the downside scenario. The forecast is itself a policy instrument, as surely as any interest rate decision.

Sun Tzu understood this: the supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting. The IMF's report is an attempt to subdue the recession without experiencing it — to use the credibility of the downside forecast to catalyse the policy responses that prevent it from materialising. Whether it works depends on whether governments act on the map or wait for the territory to arrive.

The Crossing

For the first time, the AfD leads the CDU in nationwide polling. Four out of five Germans are dissatisfied with the government. The firewall holds — but what it protects is shrinking.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Erfurt·17 April 2026

Berlin —The number arrived on April 13, in a YouGov survey that would have been unthinkable eighteen months ago: AfD 27%, CDU/CSU 23%. For the first time in the current polling cycle, the party that every other party has sworn never to govern with leads the one that governs. The INSA poll published the same week confirms the trend: AfD and CDU/CSU are essentially tied at 25-26%, with the AfD ahead or level in five consecutive surveys. Seventy-nine percent of Germans say they are dissatisfied with the federal government's performance — up from 55% last June.

The crossing is not yet an election result. But it is a structural signal, and structural signals are what this paper reads.

"The prince who relies entirely upon fortunes is lost when they change." — Niccolò Machiavelli.

What the firewall cannot contain.

Germany's political establishment operates on a formal principle: no major party will govern with the AfD or accept its parliamentary support. This Brandmauer �� firewall — has held since 2017. It held after the AfD won the most votes in Thuringia and Saxony in 2024. It held after the February 2025 federal election, where the AfD finished second with 20.8% of the vote. It holds still, formally.

But a firewall protects a structure. The question is the condition of the structure behind it. When the AfD was polling at 20%, the firewall contained a manageable force. At 27%, with the governing party four points behind and shrinking, the firewall is being asked to contain something it was not designed for: not a fringe party, but the most popular political movement in the country.

Machiavelli understood the distinction: a prince's fortress is useful only so long as the people do not hate him. Once hatred arrives, the fortress becomes a prison. The firewall's strength was always derived not from its own logic but from the CDU's ability to govern competently enough that voters did not need the AfD. At 79% dissatisfaction, that condition no longer holds.

The compound failure.

The previous edition analysed the €500 billion infrastructure fund's execution gap — €24 billion disbursed of €500 billion allocated. That gap provides the AfD's narrative without requiring explanation: they promised half a trillion and delivered five percent. But the infrastructure gap is only one layer. The Hormuz oil shock has pushed German energy costs higher. The IMF report published this week projects slower growth across the eurozone. The Bundeswehr recruitment is up 20%, signalling a population that senses danger — but defence spending competes with the same fiscal envelope as infrastructure.

Munger's Lollapalooza operates here as it did in the economics section: infrastructure delay, energy shock, defence burden, and electoral clock, each amplifying the others. No single failure would produce the AfD's polling surge. The compound stack produces what no individual variable would explain.

Merz himself acknowledged the compound pressure this week, telling lawmakers the outlook remains "very critical" and that "radical" steps would be needed. Among CDU voters specifically, only 34% say they are satisfied with his performance — down from 48% in March. When a chancellor's own base defects, the structural reading is not about policy. It is about legitimacy.

The AfD's method.

The AfD under Alice Weidel does not need to propose an alternative infrastructure plan, an alternative energy policy, or an alternative foreign policy. Its method is structural, not programmatic. It names failures. It points at the gap between promise and delivery. It absorbs the dissatisfaction that has nowhere else to go, because the firewall ensures that dissatisfaction cannot express itself through a change of government — it can only accumulate.

This is Greene's Law 5 — so much depends on reputation — applied in reverse. The AfD's power does not come from its own reputation for competence. It comes from the destruction of everyone else's. Each failed promise, each delayed project, each poll showing rising dissatisfaction erodes the governing coalition's reputation further, and the AfD benefits without having to build anything of its own.

The autumn state elections in Sachsen-Anhalt and Mecklenburg-Vorpommern are the test. Polymarket prices the AfD to win the most seats in both — 92% in Sachsen-Anhalt, 84% in Mecklenburg-Vorpommern. If those results materialise, the firewall will face its first genuine structural crisis: a party that leads nationwide and governs in multiple states can no longer be treated as a fringe to be excluded. The firewall does not fall. It becomes irrelevant.

What Merz must decide.

The CDU's strategic dilemma is the one Machiavelli diagnosed five centuries ago: the prince who tries to please everyone ends by pleasing no one. If Merz moves rightward to recapture AfD voters, he loses centrist urban voters to the Greens and SPD. If he stays course, the AfD consolidates its lead and the "dissatisfaction without alternative" pattern deepens.

Law 29 — plan all the way to the end — applies with particular force here. Merz planned the €500 billion fund as his signature achievement. He did not plan the institutional machinery to deliver it at the speed the electoral timeline requires. He did not plan for an external energy shock. He did not plan for the compound failure that has now arrived. The opening move was bold. The end state was never defined.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: The AfD's polling surge is not driven by the AfD's own programme. It is driven by compound government failure — infrastructure delay, energy shock, defence burden — that has pushed dissatisfaction to 79%. The firewall prevents that dissatisfaction from producing a change of government, which means it accumulates rather than dissipates. The AfD is the container for a pressure that has nowhere else to go. This is structural, not ideological.

What would break this reading:

  • Merz delivers 2-3 visible infrastructure projects before autumn — the compound-failure narrative loses its core exhibit.
  • AfD underperforms its polling in Sachsen-Anhalt and M-V — the protest does not convert to votes, and the structural thesis was overstated.
  • Energy costs normalise and the Lollapalooza stack decompresses — government performance improves before voters lock in their judgement.
  • A CDU-AfD cooperation deal in a state parliament — the firewall falls, transforming the entire party system.
