The Bundibugyo Line · Sunday, July 12, the DRC Ministry of Health and WHO joint bulletin passed 1,792 confirmed cases and 625 deaths from the Bundibugyo strain of Ebola virus disease — the third-largest Ebola outbreak on record, having crossed 1,000 confirmed cases in 40 days from response activation against 235 days for the 2018 North Kivu outbreak. Cases now sit in Ituri, North Kivu, and South Kivu on the DRC side; twenty confirmed cases and two deaths sit inside Uganda, seeded through Kampala rather than the rural border corridor the 2000 Gulu outbreak followed. Bundibugyo has no licensed vaccine and no approved therapeutic. A medical evacuation to Germany in May and a French imported case on 24 June have already extended the perimeter of exposed jurisdictions. The Stratagem reads the outbreak not as a public-health incident but as the moment the coordinated architecture that was supposed to hold zoonotic spillover inside eastern Congo — ArtM23-adjacent security vacuum, mineral-corridor labour flows, WHO surveillance grid, ERVEBO Zaire-strain vaccine stockpile — is visibly outrun by a strain the stockpile does not cover. The reading draws on Munger on second-order effects at the interface of insurgency and epidemic, Dalio on the structural cost of a state whose administrative capacity has been outsourced to the outbreak-response NGO complex, Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of tight quarantine perimeters that the West cannot politically execute, Ripperger on the mechanics of a disease whose symptoms travel with the pilgrim and the miner, and the 2014 West Africa outbreak as the historical rhyme in which the WHO’s late escalation converted a regional emergency into a Dallas-and-Madrid one before the vaccine campaign caught up.

The Bundibugyo Line

1,792 confirmed cases; 625 dead; the third-largest Ebola outbreak on record. Bundibugyo, not Zaire — the strain the stockpile does not cover. Cases in Kampala, not the rural border corridor. A medical evacuation to Germany, an imported case in France. The line the international system built after 2014 to contain zoonotic spillover to a rural DRC quadrant has broken twice at once: at the strain the vaccine cannot address and at the perimeter the surveillance grid assumed would hold.

The bulletin, first.

The Sunday joint bulletin from the DRC Ministry of Health and the WHO country office confirms 1,792 cases and 625 deaths from the Bundibugyo strain of Ebola virus disease, with confirmed cases now sitting in Ituri, North Kivu, and South Kivu provinces. Uganda reports 20 confirmed cases and 2 deaths, seeded not through the rural border villages of the 2000 Gulu outbreak but through Kampala, the capital — the outbreak is urban on the Ugandan side from case one. Two events already extend the perimeter outside East Africa: a US citizen medically evacuated to Germany in May and a confirmed imported case in France on 24 June. The response has now been active roughly 55 days from the WHO’s 17 May Public Health Emergency of International Concern declaration; the outbreak crossed 1,000 confirmed cases in 40 days, against 235 days for the 2018 North Kivu outbreak — the fastest doubling in the surveillance record. The scale, the pace, and the geography are each individually significant. Their coincidence is what the bulletin reports.

Munger, patiently, on the wrong strain.

Munger’s discipline of inversion asks the useful question first: what makes this outbreak dangerous in a way the 2018 one was not? The answer is a single sentence. Bundibugyo is not Zaire. The ERVEBO ring-vaccination programme that broke the 2018 outbreak covers the Zaire strain of ebolavirus and does not cross-protect against Bundibugyo. There is no licensed vaccine and no approved therapeutic for the strain now circulating in Ituri and Kampala. The stockpile the international response was built around — two million doses of ERVEBO warehoused in Geneva and pre-positioned in East Africa — is not the response. The response is contact-tracing, isolation, safe burial, and the older discipline of quarantine that the Zaire vaccine had gradually replaced as the primary instrument. Every previous outbreak since 2018 has been broken by a ring campaign inside three weeks. The current one cannot be. The compounding effect Munger reads at the second order is the erosion of the surveillance-and-response muscle that the vaccine substituted for — the field teams who once did the tracing have been running vaccine campaigns for six years, and the strain-agnostic muscle has atrophied where the vaccine-specific muscle was cultivated.

Dalio on the outsourced state.

The register on which the outbreak sits at the structural layer is the one Dalio has been describing for a decade: the phase at which a state whose administrative capacity has been outsourced to the international NGO complex meets a shock the outsourced complex was not designed for. The DRC has run its public-health function through WHO, MSF, and the Gates Foundation-adjacent research pipeline since the 2018 North Kivu outbreak. The domestic health ministry executes; it does not design. The design authority for the surveillance grid, the vaccine stockpile, the diagnostic supply chain, and the field-team rotation sits in Geneva, Atlanta, and Seattle. When the strain circulating is the one the design was built around, the outsourcing produces a fast, well-calibrated response. When the strain is Bundibugyo — a strain the vaccine does not cover and the diagnostic pipeline has not been rehearsed for — the outsourcing produces a two-week delay while Geneva re-authorises the diagnostic algorithm and re-tasks the field teams. Cases doubled inside that fortnight. The 40-day-to-1,000 marker is what the outsourced design produced when the shock was outside its calibration.

The Ituri corridor.

The geographical fact underneath the case count is that Ituri is the mining corridor into which the M23 conflict has been displacing populations for eighteen months. Artisanal cobalt, coltan, and gold flow south along the same roads on which the exposed populations are moving; the truckers who carry the ore are also the vector on which the virus reaches Bunia and, on connecting flights out of Bunia, Kampala. The 2000 Gulu outbreak reached Uganda through rural border crossings; the 2026 outbreak reached Kampala through the mineral-supply chain that connects the DRC hinterland to the Ugandan capital through Entebbe. Lee Kuan Yew on quarantine discipline reads the arithmetic Western public-health systems cannot politically execute: a two-week hard cordon on the mineral corridor between Bunia and Entebbe stops the aggregate outbreak inside a month. The cordon is not imposed. The cost of imposing it — on Kampala’s cobalt export receipts, on the artisanal miners whose day-labour cannot survive a fortnight of confinement — is priced higher than the marginal cost of the international response absorbing another two thousand cases while a Bundibugyo therapeutic is stood up. The reading is that the cordon is not politically available and the outbreak will run its course inside the response architecture that exists.

The 2014 rhyme.

The historical rhyme is the West Africa outbreak of 2014, in which the WHO’s late escalation converted a regional emergency into an intercontinental one before the vaccine campaign caught up. The 2014 arc ran from Meliandou in Guinea in December 2013 through the imported Dallas case in September 2014 — ten months from spillover to the moment the outbreak was on American television. The 2026 arc has already delivered the medical evacuation to Germany and the French imported case by day 55 of the response. The rhyme is not the geography and it is not the strain; it is the interval between the moment the outbreak is regional and the moment it is exported. In 2014, the interval was six months. In 2026, the interval was five weeks. The compression is what the surveillance grid was supposed to catch. The strain the stockpile does not cover is what the compression means for the next six months.

What the reading expects.

Base case: the outbreak continues to double at a slower cadence as the older contact-tracing muscle rebuilds; case count crosses 3,000 by end September and 5,000 by end November; Uganda reports a second cluster outside Kampala inside a fortnight; one further imported case reaches Europe (most likely Belgium or the UK) before end October, and probably a US case follows before end November. The WHO does not upgrade the PHEIC to a pandemic characterisation before end 2026 because the international definition of pandemic is not applied to a geographically bounded zoonotic outbreak even at this scale. A Bundibugyo therapeutic reaches emergency-use authorisation inside six months but not inside three. The alternative case, which repositions the reading, is a second seeded cluster outside East Africa — a Kampala-to-Dubai-to-elsewhere transmission chain confirmed with genomic sequencing — before end September. That case forces a WHO pandemic characterisation and puts the response inside the same politically-loaded discussion the 2020 SARS-CoV-2 emergency lived through. The base case does not require it; the base case does not preclude it.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What’s driving this. The reading is load-bearing at two layers. Structural: the outbreak-response architecture assembled since 2018 was calibrated around the Zaire-strain vaccine and around a rural surveillance grid; the current outbreak runs on a strain the stockpile does not cover and inside a capital city the grid was not designed for. That mismatch is not accidental — it is what happens when a state’s public-health capacity has been outsourced to a design authority abroad whose calibration is one specific shock. Coordination: the artisanal-mining corridor between the M23-displaced Ituri populations and Kampala’s Entebbe airport is the transmission chain the surveillance grid does not price, because the cordon that would break it is politically unavailable to both governments.

What’s not driving this. The consciousness-state layer is not load-bearing — different health ministers with different personal dispositions would produce the same case curve because the constraints are architectural. The metaphysical layer — the recurrent shape of civilisational disease events — is background rather than foreground; it names the pattern but not this quarter’s trajectory.

What would break this reading.

  • A Kampala-to-Dubai (or Kampala-to-Nairobi) transmission chain is genomically confirmed before end September; the outbreak becomes visibly intercontinental and the WHO is forced into a pandemic characterisation the base case did not price.
  • Uganda imposes a hard cordon on the mineral corridor between Bunia and Entebbe with Rwandan and DRC operational cooperation; the cordon holds; the Kampala case count stops rising inside a fortnight, and the structural claim that the cordon is politically unavailable fails.
  • Merck or Sabin secures WHO emergency-use authorisation for a Bundibugyo vaccine candidate before end September on the basis of the current Phase II data; ring-vaccination replaces contact-tracing as the primary instrument, and the outbreak-arc looks like 2018 in a truncated timeline.
  • The M23 offensive resumes into Ituri and displaces the response teams; the surveillance grid collapses on the DRC side and the case count doubling recovers to the 40-day cadence of the first six weeks.

Sources used. Filed Washington / Brussels / Sao Paulo
The Tariff Letters · Between Wednesday and Saturday, the White House issued a synchronised sequence of tariff letters over Truth Social: 50 per cent on refined copper effective August 1 (Tuesday), 25 per cent on Japan and South Korea (Monday), 50 per cent on Brazil citing “grave injustices” against former President Bolsonaro (Wednesday), 30 per cent on Mexico for “insufficient cooperation on fentanyl” (Friday), 35 per cent on Canada (Friday), and 30 per cent on the European Union (Saturday). The August 1 date is common across all letters. Wall Street futures reopen Sunday evening into a rerating the CBOE VIX-forward curve has not yet priced. The Stratagem reads the letter blitz not as a negotiation instrument but as the moment the “TACO” framing — Trump Always Chickens Out — the sell side has been running since April is retired, and the market is forced to price the possibility that the letters are the policy rather than the opening bid. The reading draws on Munger on the discipline of second-order effects at the moment a well-priced narrative is punished, Dalio on the phase of the Big Cycle at which a debt-heavy hegemon reaches for the tariff as the substitute for a fiscal instrument it cannot get through Congress, Sun Tzu on the shape of the multi-front campaign in which the timing of the letters is the strategy, La Rochefoucauld on the political class whose Truth-Social calm masks a treasury bind, and the Smoot-Hawley cascade of June 1930 as the historical rhyme in which a scheduled tariff calendar collided with a leveraged equity complex that had run to a valuation the tariff could not accommodate.

The Tariff Letters

Six letters in four days. Copper 50 per cent. Japan and Korea 25. Brazil 50. Mexico 30. Canada 35. The European Union 30. All effective August 1. The uniform date is the point. This is not a negotiation calendar — it is a scheduled market shock, and Wall Street reopens Sunday evening into a rerating the sell side has spent April through June writing off as another round of Truth Social bluster. The TACO trade retires on Monday.

The schedule, first.

Between Tuesday, July 7, and Saturday, July 11, the President issued six tariff letters on Truth Social with a common effective date of August 1, 2026. In order: 50 per cent on refined copper on Tuesday; 25 per cent on Japan and South Korea on Monday-then-reissued-Tuesday; 50 per cent on Brazil on Wednesday, framed as retaliation for the ongoing prosecution of former President Jair Bolsonaro and the “grave injustices” the administration reads into it; 30 per cent on Mexico on Friday, cited to “insufficient cooperation on fentanyl”; 35 per cent on Canada on the same Friday, framed as USMCA-compliance failure; 30 per cent on the European Union on Saturday, framed as reciprocity for “structural surpluses.” The letters are not a negotiation opening. They set a scheduled cost function that the market has three weeks to price and that no counterparty can plausibly close before August 1 because the terms in each letter are individually negotiable but the aggregate is not. Wall Street reopens Sunday evening into the rerating. Futures are already down; the Sunday-close VIX curve did not price the aggregate.

The TACO trade retires.

The sell-side framing since April has been that the tariff letters are opening bids, the counterparties will negotiate to a soft landing, and the administration will “chicken out” before the effective date because the marginal cost of a domestic price shock in an election-adjacent year is too high. The framing has been correct twice — the April tariffs on Japan and Korea were negotiated to zero at USMCA-qualifying rates, and the June threat against Canada was suspended within seventy-two hours. It has become the modal expectation. Munger’s discipline of second-order effects reads the pattern from the other side: the moment a well-priced narrative is punished, the punishment is at the scale of the priced-in expectation, not at the scale of the underlying event. The market is not positioned for the aggregate to be delivered. Every risk-parity book, every long-vol substitute, every hedge that priced August-1 as a negotiation date rather than a cost function is positioned inside the TACO framing. When the framing breaks, it breaks the positioning, not the tariffs. The tariffs are already the policy. The framing is what has to reprice.

Dalio on the tariff-as-fiscal-substitute.

The register at which the letter blitz sits at the structural layer is the Dalio Big Cycle’s late-imperial phase, in which a debt-heavy hegemon reaches for tariffs as the fiscal instrument it cannot get through the legislature. US public debt sits above 130 per cent of GDP; the 2027 budget resolution has not cleared the House Rules Committee; the Cato-cited scoring on the letter blitz projects $260-$300 billion in gross tariff revenue if the schedule is delivered at the announced rates and $180-$220 billion in net after growth-drag adjustment. The administration’s domestic fiscal instruments — income tax, corporate tax, capital gains — are politically bounded by the 51-49 Senate math and the Freedom Caucus arithmetic in the House. The tariff is the instrument that clears the political constraint because it is executive rather than legislative and because its incidence is broadly distributed rather than concentrated in the voter groups the administration cannot afford to lose. The reading is not that the tariffs are ideologically motivated; the reading is that the fiscal instrument the debt cycle now requires is unavailable through Congress and available through Truth Social, and the administration has chosen the available instrument.

Sun Tzu on the multi-front calendar.

Sun Tzu on the shape of a campaign whose success is measured by the enemy’s inability to concentrate his force reads the letter schedule at the tactical register. The six letters are issued in four days to five distinct counterparties (Japan-Korea, Brazil, Mexico, Canada, EU) plus a commodity (copper). Each counterparty must respond inside three weeks; each response is a domestic-political event with its own calendar, its own veto architecture, and its own currency implications. Brasilia, Mexico City, Ottawa, Tokyo, Seoul, and Brussels cannot coordinate a joint response inside three weeks because their electoral cycles are misaligned, their trade portfolios are asymmetric, and their central bank postures are heterogeneous. The Sun Tzu reading is that the compressed calendar is not sloppy sequencing; it is the strategy. Every counterparty that responds bilaterally forecloses a coordinated retaliation the aggregate would have supported. The market repricing does not have the equivalent structural fragmentation — every allocator faces the same aggregate cost function on the same August-1 date. That is the asymmetry the letters’ author is trading against.

The Smoot-Hawley rhyme.

The historical rhyme is Smoot-Hawley, June 1930: a scheduled tariff calendar collides with a leveraged equity complex that had run to a valuation the tariff could not accommodate. The rhyme is not the ideology, the depth, or the recovery arc — it is the timing. Smoot-Hawley was signed on June 17, 1930, seven months into a decline that consensus had already read as the correction the market needed. The tariff did not cause the depression; the tariff accelerated the second-derivative deterioration in trade volumes that made the equity complex unable to recover through the earnings channel. The 2026 rhyme runs at half the leverage and at twice the pace — the S&P 500 sits at 30-year forward P/E highs on a 20-year forward earnings-growth schedule that the tariffs mechanically reprice. Neither the S&P nor the Nasdaq has yet delivered the second-derivative shock the equivalent tariff schedule would deliver. Whether the shock arrives Monday, or is stretched across three weeks of daily reratings, depends on how many counterparties break the calendar with sub-August-1 concessions the letters were engineered to preclude.

What the reading expects.

Base case: Sunday-evening futures open down two to four per cent on the S&P and three to five on the Nasdaq; the VIX gaps into the mid-30s and settles in a 24-28 range for the week; the DXY rallies then reverses as the tariff-revenue arithmetic collides with the growth-drag arithmetic on the Fed’s September call; the 10-year Treasury sees a curve-inversion move as the growth expectation and inflation expectation move in opposite directions. On the political side, at least three of the five country counterparties (probably Mexico, Japan, and one of Korea or Canada) reach a bilateral concession before August 1 that trims the notional rate by five to ten points but does not remove the schedule; Brazil and the EU do not, and the letter rates apply at August 1 to those two counterparties. The copper tariff sticks for at least four weeks — the COMEX-LME spread widens further before the White House introduces the refined-copper exemption Cato-cited scoring says the arithmetic requires. The alternative case is a comprehensive weekend deal announced Monday morning that trims the aggregate to a 10-15 per cent baseline; that scenario is the TACO framing succeeding one more time; the market pops five per cent inside 48 hours and the framing survives another quarter. The base case does not require it; the base case does not preclude it.

Filed Berlin / Brussels / Paris
The Merz Scramble · Chancellor Friedrich Merz on Sunday told ARD, in the wake of Saturday’s Truth Social letter setting a 30 per cent tariff on European Union exports effective August 1, that he had spent the weekend on urgent calls with Emmanuel Macron, Ursula von der Leyen, and Donald Trump directly — and that “the German economy would be hit hard by the tariffs.” The 30 per cent rate is triple the 10 per cent baseline the EU had been preparing for as recently as July 7. Germany runs a $60 billion goods trade surplus with the United States; roughly one in eight German manufacturing jobs is tied to US demand; the automotive complex — the sector already reeling from the electric transition — is the sector at which the letter is loudest. The DAX-40 closed Friday at 24,120 into the Saturday letter that markets had not yet seen. Brussels has a $100 billion retaliatory list already drafted from the April cycle. Merz’s pan-European offensive is now the only political instrument between the Saturday letter and August 1. The Stratagem reads the scramble not as diplomatic theatre but as the moment the post-2017 European economic architecture — export-led growth priced against US demand, single-currency architecture whose fiscal ceiling assumes that demand, security architecture that Washington guarantees — is being tested against a hegemon that has decided the terms of the guarantee. The reading draws on Dalio’s Big Cycle on the phase at which a rising challenger uses tariff and currency instruments against a mature exporter, Helen Thompson’s political economy of a Germany whose surplus is now the specific counterparty grievance, Mearsheimer on the moment a security-dependency is priced into a trade negotiation, Voss on the negotiation posture that a smaller counterparty runs against a larger one that controls the calendar, and the 1971 Nixon shock as the imperfect historical rhyme — a unilateral US move that fractured the currency architecture the exporter had assumed as background.

The Merz Scramble

The letter, first.

The Saturday letter posted on Truth Social sets a 30 per cent tariff on European Union exports to the United States effective August 1, 2026, framed as reciprocity for “structural surpluses” and non-tariff barriers the White House reads into the EU regulatory architecture. The rate is triple the 10 per cent baseline Brussels had been preparing to accept as recently as July 7. Sunday morning Merz was on ARD confirming that he had spent the weekend on urgent calls with Macron and von der Leyen, and on a direct call with Trump — the third since the letter was posted. His formulation was blunt: “the German economy would be hit hard by the tariffs” and Berlin is “working intensively” to prevent the aggregate outcome the letter would deliver. On the European side the retaliatory framework is already published. The Commission has a $100 billion (roughly €92bn) list from the April cycle covering Boeing aircraft, US vehicles, Kentucky bourbon, and Louisiana soybeans, ready to activate by qualified majority in the Council on 24 hours’ notice. The DAX-40 closed Friday at 24,120 into a letter markets had not yet seen; the German equity index sits at record highs, priced against a US-demand assumption the Sunday call schedule is now trying to preserve.

Dalio on the challenger and the mature exporter.

Dalio’s Big Cycle reads the specific mechanic at work: a hegemon whose debt-to-GDP has entered the phase of fiscal exhaustion uses tariff and currency instruments against the mature exporter whose surplus is the counterparty of the hegemon’s deficit. Germany is not a peripheral counterparty in this frame; it is the archetypal one. Every German budget since 1949 has been built on an export-led growth model whose external demand backdrop was underwritten by the same US security-and-trade architecture that produced the Marshall Plan, the D-Mark stabilisation, the EMU accession, and the post-1990 reunification arithmetic. The architecture was priced as background. Saturday’s letter is Washington explicitly repricing the background. The DAX-40 that closed at 24,120 on Friday is priced against a US-demand assumption that the Saturday letter now converts into a variable. The reading is not that Germany is uniquely exposed — the euro area as a whole runs a $200 billion goods surplus with the US. The reading is that Germany is the counterparty whose surplus is the specific framing pretext in the letter, and whose Bundesbank orthodoxy has the least room to accommodate a currency devaluation as a substitute for the trade shock the tariff imposes.

Mearsheimer on the trade-in-security.

The specific bilateral leverage the White House holds against Berlin runs at a register the trade-desk press is under-pricing. Mearsheimer’s offensive realism reads a security-dependency as fungible with a trade concession the moment the dependent counterparty is asked. Germany is inside NATO, hosts 40,000 US personnel at Ramstein, Kaiserslautern, Grafenwöhr, and Wiesbaden, and has spent the entire Zeitenwende re-arming through a US supply chain that runs from Lockheed F-35s to Patriot missile replenishment to the intelligence-sharing architecture on the Baltic front. The trade letter can be answered with a $100 billion retaliatory tariff list; the security architecture cannot. Merz’s pan-European diplomatic offensive is running against precisely this asymmetry — Macron and von der Leyen can co-sign a retaliation the ARD interview named as the fallback, but they cannot re-arm the Baltic front on 90-day notice. The reading is that the specific concession the White House is extracting is not a trade concession alone; it is the price at which Berlin re-underwrites the security relationship it had assumed as background. Voss’s negotiation register — the calibrated question — is the operational instrument: Merz has to ask Trump how the counterparty makes the letter go away without conceding that the letter is the price of the security architecture, because conceding that resets every future letter’s baseline.

Helen Thompson on the surplus as grievance.

Helen Thompson’s political economy of Europe reads the letter’s framing at the domestic-political register. The White House is not treating the $60 billion German goods surplus as an economic fact; it is treating it as a political grievance whose satisfaction is denominated in specific concessions the letter does not enumerate. Thompson has been describing this transition for four years — the moment the surplus-and-deficit arithmetic that has organised German-US relations since Bretton Woods is repriced as a claim rather than a fact. The transition is not reversible by DAX-earnings calls. It is reversible by an explicit concession package that Merz’s coalition can carry through the Bundestag and that the SPD-analogue moderate wing can co-sign. That package is the specific test Merz’s scramble is now running against. The reading is that a comprehensive July-negotiation win at a 10-15 per cent baseline is available but requires Merz to accept the framing that the surplus is the grievance — a framing every previous Chancellor since Kohl was willing to fight, and that Merz’s post-Zeitenwende coalition is politically forced to accept.

The Nixon shock rhyme.

The imperfect historical rhyme is the Nixon shock of August 1971 — the unilateral US move that broke the Bretton Woods dollar-gold convertibility on which the German export architecture had been built since the D-Mark stabilisation of 1948. The rhyme is not the specific instrument (Nixon closed a window; Trump opened a tariff); the rhyme is the direction. In both cases, an American executive concluded that the currency and trade architecture underwriting the European exporter’s growth model was more expensive to the hegemon than the counterparties had realised, and repriced it unilaterally on a compressed calendar. The 1971 shock produced the D-Mark revaluation the Bundesbank had been resisting for a decade, drove German inflation from 5 to 7 per cent inside 18 months, and forced the West German current account into a decade of adjustment. The 2026 rhyme runs at a different instrument and at a compressed calendar — three weeks rather than three months — but the mechanism is the same. The exporter’s growth model is being repriced from outside because the hegemon has decided the exporter has been running the model on terms the hegemon can no longer afford.

Neither scenario currently reads as the modal case at the sell side; the sell side is priced for a soft climb-down at 10 per cent baseline that neither Trump’s Truth Social nor Merz’s Sunday interview is signalling as the likely landing point.
