From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Bogotá & Washington·01 July 2026

Bogotá —The useful frame for reading Monday’s handoff meetings is not the domestic Colombian one; it is the hemispheric one. In 2022 Gustavo Petro was elected the first left-wing president in Colombia’s history on a platform of a negotiated peace with dissident FARC formations, a phased exit from hydrocarbons, and a foreign policy that put visible distance between Bogotá and Washington on the security file. Four years later a Cartagena criminal-defence lawyer who had never stood for office — a man who spent Q1 polling in fifth place behind two Uribistas and two independents — took the Casa de Nariño on a platform that inverts every one of Petro’s commitments. The gap between the two elections is the political content of the morning.

Begin with Mearsheimer. The regional hegemon does not lose the sphere for long. When a peripheral state at the second rank of a regional distribution elects a government pursuing a foreign policy incompatible with the hegemon’s structural interests, the hegemon’s toolkit is well understood: financial pressure through sanctions and secondary sanctions; law-enforcement pressure through indictments and asset seizures; framing pressure through coordinated designation of the incumbent as illegitimate; and, in the terminal case, direct kinetic action. The Trump administration deployed the first three tools in sequence across nine months: the September 2025 major-drug-transit designation of Colombia; the October 2025 SDN listing of Petro and his inner circle under Executive Order 14059; the January and March 2026 disclosures of ongoing federal criminal investigations in the Manhattan and Brooklyn US Attorneys’ offices; the March Latin American summit at which Trump publicly encouraged partner leaders to use military force against cartels. The kinetic tool was held in reserve. It did not need to be deployed; the election delivered the political outcome the kinetic tool would have been reserved to produce.

Then read Sun Tzu. The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting. De la Espriella’s campaign was not organised against Petro; it was organised inside the political space Washington’s sequence of pressure had produced. Every SDN designation compressed the bandwidth Petro could deploy in defence of his platform. Every federal-investigation cycle produced a media rhythm in which his foreign-policy independence read as evidence of the corruption the investigations were being reported on. The Fedegán ranchers, the Uribista establishment, the evangelical cohort in Antioquia and Santander, and the transactional coalition around the Ocampo Liberals consolidated behind De la Espriella not because he was the strongest right-wing candidate on offer — Paloma Valencia and María Fernanda Cabal both polled better in Q1 — but because he was the candidate the hemispheric hegemon endorsed. Trump’s Truth Social endorsement in the days before the runoff was not a courtesy; it was the cohort signal the transactional coalition needed to consolidate. The 251,000-vote margin is the political content of that signal.

What the Handoff installs.

Greene’s thirteenth law — when asking for help, appeal to people’s self-interest, never to their mercy or gratitude — reads the ninety-day US-backed air campaign at its tactical register. De la Espriella’s most extraordinary campaign promise was not a Colombian security policy; it was an American security-policy offer, priced in Colombian sovereignty, structured as a mutual-defence exchange. Washington gets a staging platform for anti-cartel operations against the Segunda Marquetalia, the Gulf Clan, and the ELN’s eastern front. Bogotá gets the security instrument Petro’s negotiated demobilisation could not deliver against a dissident-FARC cohort that has doubled its territorial reach since 2023. The exchange is transactional in the register Talleyrand would recognise: two principals each bring what the other cannot manufacture domestically, and each pays in a currency the other values above the price at which he issues it. La Rochefoucauld’s observation that self-interest speaks all sorts of tongues and plays all sorts of parts, even that of disinterestedness reads the campaign framing: the sovereign-choice language is the tongue self-interest speaks when the underlying transaction cannot be publicly named.

The handoff belongs to a series. Since March 2025 the Trump administration has executed six coordinated Latin American moves: the Venezuelan-oil sanctions cycle that culminated in the Maduro-containment sequence; the Ecuadorian base-access agreement ratified in Quito in November; the Argentine IMF standby that Milei negotiated inside the Treasury’s framing; the El Salvador infrastructure exchange with Bukele; the Panama Canal renegotiation cycle that reopened after the November 2025 Supreme Court ruling; and, now, Colombia. Read one at a time, each is a discrete regional file. Read as a series, they are a coordinated recovery of the sphere Washington ceded across the 2000s and 2010s to a Pink Tide the structural cycle has now retired. The Dugin discipline — a mirror the Western analyst borrows only for the frame it offers — would read the sequence as the reassertion of a maritime hegemon’s continental margin; the Zeihan discipline reads it as demography and the reserve currency doing what demography and the reserve currency were always going to do; both readings arrive at the same instrument.

What the calendar tests.

Three variables across the next 120 days will reveal whether the Handoff holds at the cohort signal Washington has intended. First, the Petro SDN cycle: if the October 2025 designation is quietly lifted between August 7 and year-end as the face-saving instrument the outgoing administration extracts in exchange for a peaceful handover, the reading that the sanctions were leverage rather than a case will have been ratified. If the designation is held past year-end and the federal criminal case is filed inside the standing Brooklyn cycle, Washington has signalled that it intends to prosecute rather than pension, and the durability of the Handoff will rest on De la Espriella’s willingness to publicly ratify a case against his predecessor. Second, the kinetic cycle: if the first US anti-cartel kinetic action on Colombian soil occurs before year-end and is framed as joint rather than unilateral — the joint framing is the political durability De la Espriella needs against a domestic left that has not disappeared — the campaign promise has been converted into policy. Third, the hydrocarbon cycle: if the Ecopetrol board reverses the Petro-era fracking moratorium at the September AGM and a Chevron-Occidental joint-venture cohort files for exploration acreage inside Q4, the domestic-economic durability of the alignment is installed. Confucius on the rectification of names cuts at the test: the alignment was named “sovereign choice” in the campaign, and the operational content of the naming will be measured against how loudly the Casa de Nariño has to insist on the name once the joint operations begin. The morning’s news is that the handoff meetings went smoothly. Talleyrand would read the smoothness as the tell.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What’s driving this: The structural layer is doing the primary work here, with the coordination layer immediately underneath. The structural fact is that Colombia sits on the second-tier of a regional distribution in which the United States is the only pole with the capital, the sanctions instrument, the federal criminal architecture, and the media reach to compress any government that pursues a foreign policy incompatible with Washington’s security priorities. Petro’s 2022 mandate ran directly against that structure; the SDN-and-indictment sequence across September 2025 to March 2026 was the structural pressure being converted into political cost; the June 21 runoff was the peripheral polity ratifying the compression. The coordination layer sits inside the sequence — the Trump endorsement, the Rubio-and-Waltz-and-Landau cell operating the Latin American file, the coordination with Milei, Bukele, and the Ecuadorian and Panamanian executive cycles — and it is what converted the structural pressure into the specific timing and framing of the outcome. The transactional coalition around De la Espriella did not consolidate on the merits of the candidate; it consolidated on the cohort signal Washington issued.

What’s not driving this: A reading that treats the runoff as a domestic Colombian rejection of the Petro programme — a security failure, a fiscal failure, a corruption cycle inside the outgoing cabinet — understates the hemispheric architecture that framed the choice. Petro was compressed by the SDN designation before he was defeated at the ballot box. This is not a discrete Andean file; it is the sixth completed move in a coordinated series.

What would break this reading:

  • De la Espriella publicly refuses the ninety-day US air campaign inside his first sixty days, framing it as a red line against sovereignty — the “transactional exchange” reading was wrong; the campaign promise was rhetorical and the hemispheric architecture does not extend to kinetic authority inside Colombian territory.
  • The Manhattan or Brooklyn US Attorney’s office files a formal indictment against Petro inside 2026 — the “SDN as leverage rather than case” reading was wrong; Washington intends to prosecute, and the incoming Colombian administration will have to publicly ratify or refuse the case.
  • The Constitutional Court blocks the Ecopetrol fracking-moratorium reversal on standing environmental jurisprudence — the “hydrocarbon-cycle durability” reading was wrong; the domestic-economic substrate of the alignment fails inside the first year and De la Espriella’s coalition begins to split on the fiscal question.
  • A Venezuelan-cohort border incident escalates into a discrete kinetic exchange inside Q3 that the incoming Colombian administration is unable to absorb — the “coordinated regional recovery” reading was overstated; the sequence contains a Venezuelan wildcard the hemispheric architecture has not yet resolved.

From the Desk of Continental Affairs·Filed Berlin & Nürnberg·01 July 2026

Berlin —The useful thing to notice about the reform that takes effect this morning is that its most substantive change is not the change the political cohort has been debating. The €563 single-adult transfer is unchanged. The bedarfsgemeinschaft calculation is unchanged. The Wohnkosten reimbursement architecture is unchanged. The Erwerbsobliegenheit obligation to accept reasonable employment is unchanged from Hartz IV forward. What has changed is a name and a sanctions schedule — and the political proposition that a governing coalition has decided to make the sanctions schedule the visible sign of who it is.

Begin with Aristotle. In the Politics the constitution is not what the constitutional document names it as; the constitution is what the constitution actually practises. The 2019 Bundesverfassungsgericht ruling on the human-dignity floor established that a benefit sanctions architecture that compresses transfers below the level required for a dignified existence violates Article 1 of the Basic Law, and the Karlsruhe court set the maximum permissible sanction at 30 per cent for a limited duration on a single-instance non-compliance. The Merz reform sits precisely inside that envelope: 30 per cent for three months on a single refused training measure, escalating to full suspension only on cumulative non-compliance. The reform has been engineered by the Justizministerium and the Bundesagentur to survive the Karlsruhe review the Green and Linke cohorts are already petitioning for. Aristotle would read the engineering as the answer to his own question: the constitutional cohort now practising German social policy is the one that has accepted the 2019 floor as binding and has decided that the political value of moving to the maximum permissible sanction exceeds the political value of moving beneath the floor. That is a constitutional choice about who the state is when it is disciplining a beneficiary. The Merz cabinet has said, in the operational language of a sanctions schedule, that the state is the one that will use every basis point the Karlsruhe ruling still allows it.

Then read Lee Kuan Yew. The state whose demographic pyramid has inverted cannot afford a benefit architecture that has been calibrated to the demographic pyramid of its parents. Germany’s working-age population peaked in 2020 and is compressing by roughly 350,000 net per year. The old-age dependency ratio has moved from 32 to 38 across the past decade and is projected past 46 by 2035. The Renten pay-as-you-go architecture, the sickness-fund architecture, and the long-term-care insurance architecture all depend on a labour-market participation cycle that pulls every marginal worker into contribution. The Bürgergeld regime of 2023, as the Scholz cabinet had designed it, prioritised a humane transfer over an aggressive activation cycle; the Grundsicherung regime that takes effect today reverses the ordering. Whether the reversal is correct is a political judgment; whether it is structurally intelligible is not. A demographically compressing state that continues to pay non-participating adults on a schedule designed for a demographically expanding one will discover, in Lee Kuan Yew’s frame, that its fiscal envelope has already retired before its political cohort has ratified the retirement. Merz’s cabinet has decided to ratify the retirement in advance.

What the Turn installs.

Confucius on the rectification of names cuts at the political register. The Bürgergeld — “citizens’ income” — was a name that carried a political proposition: that the recipient was a citizen first and a claimant second, that the transfer was owed rather than conditional, and that the state’s posture toward the recipient was the posture of an equal. The Neue Grundsicherung — “new basic security” — is a name that carries a different political proposition: that the recipient is a claimant whose claim is being underwritten by a conditional cohort, that the transfer is a floor rather than a right, and that the state’s posture toward the recipient is the posture of a fiduciary against contingency. The change of name is the political content of the reform. Greene’s sixth law — court attention at all cost — reads the Merz cabinet at its tactical register: the reform will produce approximately €1.6 billion in fiscal savings across the eighteen-month cycle, a rounding error inside the Bundeshaushalt’s €480 billion frame, and delivers a political dividend the fiscal number cannot capture. The BILD-cohort front pages have run five days of Merz-as-disciplinarian framing; the FAZ business section has ratified the coalition’s labour-market posture; the AfD cohort has been forced into the position of arguing the sanctions do not go far enough, which is the position the coalition wanted the AfD in.

Munger’s discipline on incentive-caused bias reads the sanctions architecture at its behavioural register. The reform assumes that the marginal Grundsicherung recipient has been declining the marginal training measure because the transfer floor removes the incentive to accept it. That reading is right for some fraction of the caseload and wrong for another fraction. The fraction for which it is wrong — the recipient whose labour-market participation is compressed by a structural rather than a motivational constraint (a chronic-illness cohort, a caregiving cohort, a linguistic cohort inside the immigration pool, a region whose training providers are undersupplied) — will be compressed by the sanctions without being converted into employment. The reform’s political durability rests on the empirical question of how large that fraction is. The Bundesagentur’s internal modelling suggests 30-40 per cent of the caseload; the Deutsches Institut für Wirtschaftsforschung has published estimates closer to 55 per cent. The gap between the two numbers is the fiscal-and-political room the reform has to succeed or fail.

Law of the Day · No. 13

Appeal to self-interest, never to mercy or gratitude.

Greene’s thirteenth law is the discipline of the principal who has understood that a counterparty who is being asked to move will move for the reason it always moves — because the move serves an interest of its own that the request has been engineered to name — and will not move, durably, for any other reason. The unschooled principal appeals to friendship, obligation, or a shared past; the counterparty accepts the appeal in the register in which it is offered and defects, on schedule, in the register in which the exchange was never priced. The schooled principal locates the counterparty’s durable interest, structures the ask so that the ask is inside the interest, and waits.

Today’s reading: the Bogotá Handoff, the Wall Street Shrug, the Grundsicherung Turn. In Colombia, Washington has secured a foreign-policy alignment by offering the incoming administration an American security instrument — ninety days of US-backed operations against dissident FARC and ELN — that the incoming administration cannot manufacture domestically at any price it can afford. In the equity market, the passive-flow cohort has not been asked to price the Iran cycle, the copper cycle, or the constitutional cloud on the tariff regime; the cohort has been paid, by every incentive it operates under, not to price them, and it has done what the incentive told it to do. In Berlin, Merz has installed a coalition-discipline instrument the SPD junior partner has publicly ratified because refusing to ratify would collapse a coalition the SPD has more to lose from than to gain by ending. Each of the three moves is a Greene-13 instrument at a different register.

  • The Bogotá Handoff is the law at the geopolitical register — the ninety-day air-campaign offer is priced in Colombian sovereignty and structured so that the incoming administration’s durable interest (security capacity Petro’s ceasefire could not deliver) is served by accepting the American interest (a staging platform against cartel cohorts).
  • The Wall Street Shrug is the law at the market register — the equity cohort has not been asked to price the risk; every participant is paid to hold the trade, and no participant is paid to invert it. Greene-13 read: the cohort will do what the cohort is being paid to do.
  • The Grundsicherung Turn is the law at the coalition register — the SPD junior partner has publicly ratified the retirement of its own 2023 policy because the SPD’s durable interest (holding cabinet portfolios inside a Merz coalition) exceeds the SPD’s doctrinal interest (defending the Bürgergeld architecture).

If you need to turn to an ally for help, do not bother to remind him of your past assistance and good deeds. He will find a way to ignore you. The Practitioner’s Note

Locate the durable interest.

Each of Wednesday’s stories rewards the principal who located the counterparty’s durable interest and structured the ask inside it. Washington asked De la Espriella not for gratitude but for a security-cooperation exchange that serves an interest the Bogotá administration cannot serve any other way. The equity-cohort has been asked, tacitly, to keep holding the trade — and every participant’s fee income, benchmarked position, and career risk make the ask self-executing. Merz asked the SPD not for solidarity on labour-market reform but for a coalition partnership the SPD cannot afford to leave. The schooled practitioner in each case did not ask the counterparty to be someone the counterparty was not. The practitioner named what the counterparty already was, and priced the exchange inside it.

In your own affairs: the law cuts at three registers. At the professional register, the collaboration you are asking a colleague for will hold only inside the interest the colleague can publicly defend to their own principal; ask for the collaboration in that language and the collaboration is durable, ask for it in the language of favour and the collaboration will collapse at the first pressure point. At the personal register, a change of behaviour you have been asking a partner or family member to make will be sustained only inside a frame in which the change serves an interest the counterparty already recognises as their own; ask for the change on grounds that require the counterparty to become someone they are not, and the change reverts. At the institutional register, a policy you have been trying to move inside a large organisation will move only when the ask has been coupled to an interest a senior principal can publicly defend on their own terms; the organisational change that has been framed as ethically or intellectually correct will die inside the budget cycle.

The hard question: the thirteenth law has a second edge that is easier to miss than its first. Locating the durable interest is not cynical — it is precise. It is not the same as manipulating a counterparty against their own good; it is the discipline of naming, honestly, what the counterparty has come for. Before you place the next consequential ask, ask yourself: what is the durable interest my counterparty is defending, is my ask structured so that the counterparty’s durable interest is served by acceptance, and if not, what would I have to change about the ask so that acceptance becomes the counterparty’s own move rather than mine? Greene’s thirteenth law is the lever; the willingness to see the counterparty as they actually are is the foundation.
