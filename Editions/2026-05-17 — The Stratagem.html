The Hormuz Memo

Donald Trump on Saturday evening transmitted to Tehran through Pakistani and Omani mediators a one-page framework memorandum proposing the terms on which the United States would lift the naval blockade of Iranian ports imposed April 13 and reopen the Strait of Hormuz to commercial traffic. The memo is the latest move in a negotiation that has run since the February 28 joint US-Israeli air strikes assassinated Ali Khamenei and triggered the Iranian closure of the strait. The Hormuz blockade is now in its eleventh week. Shipping traffic through the waterway — which carried roughly twenty percent of global oil and a third of global LNG before the war — has collapsed from 120 vessels a day to four. WTI cleared $109 on Friday; Brent settled at $114; the global rates curve repriced in unison. The substantive sticking points are publicly named. Tehran has offered a five-year moratorium on uranium enrichment; Washington is demanding twenty. Tehran has demanded full recognition of its control over the strait inside the framework; Washington has refused. Polymarket prices a near-term deal — a nuclear agreement by May 31 — at seven percent. The longer-horizon market — an agreement before the end of 2026 — sits above fifty. The memo is not a deal; it is the architecture of the leverage being publicly named, in a window in which both sides know the other can read the same arithmetic.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Washington & Tehran·17 May 2026

Washington —A one-page memorandum sent through a mediator into a war that has run eleven weeks is not the substance of a deal; it is the negotiator's discipline of putting his position on a single page so the other side cannot misread the leverage. Trump's Saturday transmission to Tehran — routed, like the prior six exchanges, through Islamabad and Muscat — is the public form of an indirect negotiation that has been priced into the oil market and the rates market for two months. The architecture of the document is more legible than its content. A one-pager is the format a principal uses when he wants to compress the deliberation timeline of the other side's institutional process; it is also the format a principal uses when the substance is not yet stable enough to bear elaboration. Both readings are present here. Washington has the leverage of the blockade and the post-strike Iranian institutional crisis; Tehran has the leverage of the strait itself and of the time the blockade is structurally costing the American consumer. Each side has now publicly named what it wants. The Stratagem reads the memo as the inflection — the architecture inside which the next thirty days will resolve.

The leverage geometry is the cleanest place to start. Iran's economy has been on a war footing since February 28: the rial trades at a quarter of its pre-war level, fuel rationing is in its eighth week inside the country, and the post-Khamenei succession remains formally unresolved with three factional candidates and an Assembly of Experts that has not yet been convened. Washington's leverage is structural and direct. America's cost is the cost of an oil shock that has lifted gasoline futures through $3.40, repriced the thirty-year Treasury to 5.121 percent, and re-opened the inflation argument inside the new Warsh Fed before the chair has held his first meeting. Washington's cost is mediated, but it is the cost the political-rates-and-equity cohort can read directly. Tehran's cost is direct, but it is borne by an economy that has spent forty years building institutional muscle for siege. Sun Tzu's distinction between the war that can be won by direct application and the war that must be won by waiting is the structural read on the asymmetry — the side whose costs are mediated is the side that runs out of patience first.

“Conceal your intentions.” Greene's Third Law. The one-page memorandum is the negotiator's instrument for concealing the architecture of the position while disclosing only the price. The five-year-versus-twenty-year enrichment moratorium is the visible disagreement; the structural disagreement is whether the framework recognises Iranian sovereignty over the strait or merely the commercial reopening of it. The visible disagreement is what the press conferences will discuss in the next four days. The structural disagreement is what the principals are actually negotiating. The discipline of the one-pager is that it lets each side maintain the public position on the visible disagreement while moving the substantive position on the structural one. The market that prices a near-term deal at seven percent and a longer-horizon deal above fifty is reading exactly this asymmetry — the visible window is closed, the structural window is open.

The strait as institution.

Dugin on Eurasian land-power, read carefully, is the structural overlay on the Iranian half of the negotiation. The Iranian position is not maximalist by accident — it is maximalist because the strait is the institutional asset that converts Iran from a regional power into a continental-scale node in the Eurasian energy architecture. The recognition Tehran demands is not nominal; it is the institutional pre-condition for any post-war reconstruction of Iranian sovereignty inside the post-Khamenei configuration. Washington's refusal to concede it is symmetrical. The American framework cannot recognise Iranian sovereignty over the strait without inheriting the legal commitment to police it on Iranian terms — and the institutional logic of the Project Freedom mission, paused on May 6 and not resumed, was precisely to establish the precedent that the United States, not the IRGC, is the residual security provider inside the Gulf. Each side has named the position the other cannot concede. The negotiation is now about the language that lets each side say it has not conceded it.

Mearsheimer on offensive realism is the structural overlay on the Washington half. The American position in the Gulf has been the load-bearing piece of the post-1979 regional order. The Iran war's strategic purpose, as the administration has framed it, was to convert the Iranian nuclear program from a long-dated capability into a no-capability under American verification — and to do so without producing the regime-change cascade that the prior generation of analysts had assumed would follow. The blockade is the instrument that converts the post-strike position into a negotiating position. The longer Washington holds it, the deeper the institutional commitment to a Gulf order anchored in American naval primacy; the shorter Washington holds it, the lower the durability of the verification regime the framework is being built to deliver. The political cost inside the United States is the variable on the American side. The institutional cost inside Iran is the variable on the Iranian side. The negotiation is the calibration of the two costs against each other.

The mediators.

Talleyrand's discipline on the role of the third party is the practitioner overlay on the Pakistani-Omani channel. The mediators in any consequential negotiation are not neutral; they are interested parties whose institutional stake is in the framework existing rather than in either side's terms prevailing. Pakistan has been the route because Islamabad has the only working diplomatic relationship with Tehran that can survive an American framework, and because the Pakistani military has its own institutional case for a Gulf de-escalation that releases the oil-and-remittance pressure on the country. Oman has been the venue because Muscat has been the venue for every consequential US-Iran channel since the 2013 Geneva framework, and the institutional muscle memory is genuine. The mediators carry the framework. The Stratagem reads the cadence of the next thirty days as the test of whether the mediators can hold the framework against the inevitable breakdown moments — an Iranian probe of the blockade, an American strike against a redeploying IRGC vessel, an Israeli intervention designed to collapse the negotiation. Each is structurally possible inside the window. The mediators' institutional task is to absorb each as a tactical event rather than letting it become the framework's breakdown event.

Voss on the discipline of the late-stage negotiation is the closing practitioner overlay. The discipline of how the position is named, not just what is named, is what determines whether the principal across the table can accept the terms without losing his institutional position. The five-year-versus-twenty-year enrichment moratorium is solvable inside a calibrated language — a ten-or-twelve-year framework with sunset clauses, or a renewable framework on a five-year cadence — that lets both principals frame the outcome to their domestic audiences as the position they entered with. The strait recognition is the harder problem because the public language is binary. The Stratagem reads the principal-level discipline as the variable on which the next thirty days resolve. If the one-pager is followed by a public principal-level exchange — a Trump letter, a Pezeshkian or post-Khamenei response — the architecture is being built. If the next move is a unilateral US action or a unilateral Iranian closure of the channel, the framework was a posture and the war is the structure.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry this story. Structural: a single global supply variable — Iranian oil and the Hormuz transit it gates — is the load-bearing asset both sides are negotiating against, and the asymmetry of mediated-versus-direct costs is what determines whose patience runs out first. Strategic: the one-page memorandum is the discipline of compressing the negotiation onto a single page so the other side cannot mis-price the leverage, and the visible disagreement on enrichment is the public language inside which the structural disagreement on strait recognition is being moved. What is not driving this is regime change inside Iran; the succession contest is real and the institutional crisis is structural, but the negotiation is being conducted with the institutional Iran that exists, not the Iran the maximalist case inside Washington had imagined as the war's outcome. The Stratagem reads the load-bearing tests of the next thirty days as: the principal-level public exchange (does Trump send a letter; does Tehran respond), the blockade-and-counter-blockade cadence (does the IRGC probe; does the Navy escalate the boarding regime), and the Israeli posture (does Jerusalem accept the framework or does it intervene to collapse it). The architecture is the memo. The substance is the leverage. The next move is the principal-level signal that the negotiation can survive the institutional breakdowns each side knows the other can manufacture.

The H200 License

The US Department of Commerce on Thursday formally cleared ten Chinese technology firms — Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, JD.com, Baidu, Huawei Cloud, Lenovo, Inspur, Hygon, and SMIC — to purchase Nvidia's H200 artificial-intelligence accelerator under the case-by-case licensing framework Trump first authorised in December and Commerce formally implemented on 6 January. Each approved buyer is capped at 75,000 units; the aggregate ceiling sits at no more than fifty percent of Nvidia's domestic US shipments; the United States Treasury collects a twenty-five percent surcharge on each chip sold; the H200's more advanced Blackwell-series successor remains under stricter controls. Jensen Huang, in Beijing on Wednesday, framed the licence as the institutional end of the ten-month freeze his Q4 fiscal-2026 print had absorbed as the fourteen-billion-dollar revenue hole in the China line. The market read it the same way: Nvidia opened up four percent on Thursday, closed up three on Friday after the broader oil-shock-driven equity selloff, and is now within striking distance of the $4.6 trillion market capitalisation that puts it above Alphabet and back at the top of the public-markets table. The licensing framework is not a normal export-control adjustment. It is the institutional pivot from a containment regime to a capture regime — the architecture inside which Chinese hyperscaler demand becomes Nvidia revenue and US Treasury cash, with the Beijing alternative-stack timeline structurally delayed each year the regime holds.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Santa Clara & Beijing·17 May 2026

Santa Clara —A licensing framework that takes a strategic-export prohibition and converts it into a managed-revenue stream with a federal surcharge is not a relaxation of the export-control regime. It is a regime change. The 6 January implementation of the H200 framework, formally extended this week to ten named buyers, replaces the binary “allowed-versus-prohibited” architecture of the 2022-and-2023 Biden-era controls with a calibrated “allowed-with-conditions-and-tax” architecture inside which the United States captures the rent its prior posture had ceded. The rent runs in two directions. The Treasury collects twenty-five percent on every chip — at fifty thousand H200s per buyer on average and twenty-five thousand dollars per chip, the surcharge writes a $3.1 billion cheque to the federal balance sheet at the first cap-out, and a $9.4 billion cheque across the full annual ceiling. Nvidia collects the rest of the cheque on the $50 billion addressable Chinese hyperscaler market Jensen Huang publicly named at GTC in March. The deeper rent is institutional. Each H200 sold into Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, JD, Baidu, and the rest extends the structural dependence of the Chinese AI stack on the CUDA architecture by another product-development cycle — the alternative-stack timeline at Huawei and Cambricon is structurally delayed by every quarter Chinese hyperscaler GPU budget continues to be priced through Santa Clara.

The strategic logic of the move is the inversion of containment. The 2022-and-2023 export-control regime was built on the theory that denying frontier compute to China would delay the Chinese AI capability by a number of years equal to the architectural-substitution time required by the Chinese alternative stack. The theory had two failure modes that the post-2024 architecture has now publicly conceded. The first failure mode was that the denial would accelerate, not decelerate, the alternative-stack development — that the Chinese state would prioritise the domestic substitute precisely because the import path was closed, and that the resulting Huawei Ascend roadmap would converge on Nvidia capability faster than the pure-market alternative. The second failure mode was that the denial would compress American revenue against an irreplaceable Chinese hyperscaler demand pool, and the resulting margin compression at Nvidia would slow the very capability the policy was nominally protecting. The H200 framework concedes both failure modes. It opens the import path to keep the Chinese stack dependent; it taxes the import path to capture the rent for the United States; and it preserves the Blackwell-and-frontier denial as the institutional asset the framework retains. Greene's Twentieth Law on never committing to anyone, read as policy doctrine, is the architecture: the United States is committed neither to closed-doors nor to open-trade, but to the calibrated leverage of the framework itself.

“Use your enemy's strength against him.” Sun Tzu, on the discipline of converting an opponent's structural asset into the architecture of one's own position. The Chinese AI buildout is the structural asset — the largest single addressable hyperscaler market outside the United States, and the demand pool whose institutional muscle memory was built on Nvidia silicon. The 2022 containment regime treated that asset as the asset to be denied. The H200 framework treats it as the asset to be captured. The conversion is not symmetric for the two sides. The United States captures direct fiscal revenue, institutional dependency, and frontier-denial optionality. China retains the capability gap and now also bears a twenty-five-percent surcharge on the remediation of it. The framework is a rare instance of the harder strategic case being made and won inside the same administration that imposed the prior regime.

The CUDA moat.

Munger on moats, read carefully, is the structural overlay on why the framework's institutional logic works. Nvidia's moat is not the silicon; it is the CUDA software stack and the institutional muscle memory that two decades of accelerated-computing tooling has built across every AI lab on the planet. The hyperscaler GPU cluster is the durable capital asset; the CUDA stack is the indispensable software complement; the ecosystem of compilers, libraries, optimisations, and developer-mindshare is the institutional capital that compounds with every quarter of Nvidia-anchored deployment. The Chinese alternative stack — Huawei's CANN, the various academic frameworks, the Cambricon and Hygon and Biren toolchains — exists, and is improving, but the institutional muscle is two to three product cycles behind. Each H200 cluster the framework licences into Alibaba and Tencent extends the CUDA moat by another product cycle the Chinese substitute has to catch up across. The framework's economic logic is a federal-tax revenue stream. The framework's strategic logic is the rebuilding of the moat at the customer level.

Lee Kuan Yew on the discipline of small-state industrial policy reads, in inverse, as the Chinese-side overlay. LKY's doctrine — never let your industrial capability become structurally dependent on a foreign supplier — is the doctrine Beijing has been institutionally committed to since the 2018 ZTE sanctions. The H200 framework is the architecture inside which the doctrine is being violated by the Chinese hyperscalers themselves, despite the formal state direction. Alibaba and Tencent and ByteDance and Baidu need the H200 to keep their model training competitive with OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google; the Chinese state's instruction to prefer domestic substitutes is in tension with the hyperscalers' commercial imperative to compete in the global model market. The framework's caps — 75,000 per buyer — are calibrated tightly enough to preserve the Chinese state's structural-independence rhetoric, and loose enough that the hyperscalers' immediate training pipelines remain Nvidia-anchored. The Stratagem reads the tension as the load-bearing institutional fault line on the Chinese side. Each licence consumed is a vote by a Chinese hyperscaler against the state's industrial-policy doctrine.

The capex-to-revenue conversion ratio inside the hyperscaler cohort is the variable on which the cycle either continues compounding or breaks; the H200 framework is the institutional move that extends the conversion window for Nvidia by adding the Chinese addressable market back into the supply-constrained orderbook. The Vilseck Withdrawal

The Pentagon's May 1 announcement that approximately 5,000 of the 37,000 American soldiers stationed in Germany would withdraw inside six to twelve months has now resolved its institutional ambiguity into a single named unit. NPR's Saturday reporting from Vilseck — a Bavarian town of 6,500 in the Upper Palatinate — confirmed what defense correspondents and Stars and Stripes have spent the fortnight bracketing: the unit departing is the 2nd Cavalry Regiment's Stryker Brigade, the Army's only permanently stationed Stryker formation in Europe, headquartered at the Rose Barracks since 1992. Mayor Thorsten Grädler put the local arithmetic into the public record. Five thousand soldiers with 12,000 to 13,000 dependents — roughly twice the town's population — generate $800 million in annual revenue across rents, retail, contracting, and the schools. The decision is the documentary inversion of the post-war Bavarian-American compact that began in 1945. The political backdrop is the wider posture: Trump on May 2 added that the cut would go “a lot further than 5,000”; the deployment of the 2nd Armored Brigade Combat Team from Fort Hood to Poland is on indefinite hold; NATO has been “working with the US” on the details but has been given no scope of the wider drawdown. Merz, who said in April that the United States had been “humiliated by Iran's leadership,” carries the political cost of the bilateral relationship at exactly the moment the institutional pillar of European deterrence is being publicly downgraded.

Law of the Day · No. 20

Do not commit to anyone — preserve optionality.

Greene's Twentieth Law: it is the fool who always rushes to take sides. Do not commit to any side or cause but yourself. By maintaining your independence, you become the master of others — playing people against one another, making them pursue you. The discipline is the refusal to convert leverage into commitment until the price of commitment compares favourably with the price of preserved optionality. The party that has not yet chosen is the party every other side must negotiate with; the party that has chosen has spent its leverage and inherits the cost of the chosen side's mistakes.

Today's reading: the memo, the licence, the withdrawal. Each story turns on a principal calibrating leverage rather than committing it, and the calibration is the architecture. The Hormuz memorandum is the one-page form of an indirect negotiation — Trump has not committed to a deal, Tehran has not committed to a moratorium, the mediators carry the framework, and the optionality on every side is preserved by the refusal to publish the binding architecture. The H200 licence is the framework Commerce has built to take a binary “allowed-versus-prohibited” export-control regime and convert it into a calibrated “allowed-with-conditions-and-tax” architecture that captures rent in both directions while committing to neither openness nor closure. The Vilseck withdrawal is the executive's refusal to honour the institutional commitment the prior eighty years of administrations had treated as load-bearing — the discipline of optionality applied at the level of the alliance architecture itself.

  • The Hormuz Memo is Washington and Tehran each refusing to commit ahead of the other — the optionality is the architecture, the mediators carry the calibration, the one-pager is the discipline.

The Practitioner's Note

Optionality is the asset. Commitment is the price.

The three stories on Sunday's table each turn on a moment in which a principal calibrated leverage rather than committing it. The Hormuz memo compresses the Iranian negotiation onto a single page so the other side cannot mis-price the leverage, but it does not publish the binding architecture — both sides retain the visible disagreement on enrichment years while moving the structural disagreement on strait recognition in the closed channel. The H200 licence converts the export-control regime from binary denial into calibrated capture, with a federal surcharge that monetises the relaxation and a Blackwell-tier denial that preserves the frontier-denial optionality the framework retains. The Vilseck withdrawal applies the optionality discipline at the level of the alliance itself — the institutional commitment that prior administrations had treated as load-bearing is now publicly named as renegotiable, and the cost is borne by the European-pillar architecture that built its own capability planning on the assumption of permanence.

In your own affairs: in any negotiation, deal, or relationship, distinguish between leverage that has been preserved and leverage that has been spent. Leverage preserved is the asset the next round can be priced against; leverage spent is the position the next round has to work around. The temptation in any negotiation is to spend leverage to close — to commit to the relationship, to publish the deal, to name the architecture — because closure feels like resolution and resolution feels like progress. The discipline is the opposite. The principal who has not yet committed is the principal every other side must negotiate with; the principal who has committed is the principal whose price has already been paid. Optionality is not indecisiveness; it is the institutional muscle that lets you accept the upside the next surprise produces without being locked into the position the prior commitment foreclosed.

The hard question: in the work, the partnership, the family, or the project you are inside this week — what commitments have you made out of the desire for closure rather than out of the assessment of the price? And, harder: which of those commitments has cost you the optionality the next round will require, and what would it take to recover the position before the round opens?
