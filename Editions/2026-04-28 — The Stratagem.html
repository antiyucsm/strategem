The Pyongyang Pact

Russia's defence minister Andrei Belousov landed in Pyongyang on Sunday and was received by Kim Jong Un. By the evening the two states had agreed to put their military cooperation on a "sustainable, long-term basis" and to sign a Russian-Korean Military Cooperation Plan for 2027–2031 inside this calendar year. The same weekend Pyongyang opened a memorial museum to its troops killed fighting for Russia in Ukraine. The North Korea question has stopped being a deployment question. It has become a treaty.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Pyongyang & Moscow·28 April 2026

Pyongyang —Russia's defence minister Andrei Belousov landed at Sunan with a delegation that included a deputy chief of the General Staff and the head of the Russian Defence Industry Department. Kim Jong Un received him on Sunday afternoon. By the evening Belousov had told the working session that "we have agreed with the DPRK Ministry of Defence to transition military cooperation to a sustainable, long-term basis. We are ready to sign a Russian-Korean Military Cooperation Plan for the period 2027–2031 this year." Earlier the same day, Kim cut the ribbon on the Memorial Hall of Combat Feats in Overseas Military Operations — a museum to the eleven thousand North Korean soldiers Pyongyang formally acknowledges have fought for Russia in Kursk and across the front. State Duma speaker Volodin used the language Russia normally reserves for its own war dead. Kim used the word "sacred."

Read the move. The deployment of North Korean infantry to Russian formations was, until this weekend, a reversible expedient. The Russian-Korean treaty of June 2024 was a mutual-defence shell; the troop pipeline was its first tactical application. What Belousov has just put on the table — a five-year written military cooperation plan with annual schedules, joint commissions, weapons transfers, and cadre exchanges baked in — is the same relationship in institutional form. Reversible expedients become irreversible architectures the moment the architecture is signed. The DPRK question has stopped being how many troops Pyongyang will send to Kursk this quarter. It has become how the alliance will be governed for half a decade.

"In war, the way is to avoid what is strong and to strike at what is weak." — Sun Tzu. Russia's structural weakness is manpower; the DPRK pipeline is the precise patch. The pact converts the patch into a plank.

The institutional turn.

Mearsheimer's reading on alliance formation under bipolar pressure applies cleanly. Two sanctioned, encircled, manpower-and-technology-constrained states facing a hostile coalition are doing what the structural logic predicts: pooling. Russia has the air defence, the ballistic-missile know-how, the nuclear engineering know-how, the satellite reconnaissance feed, and the front-line battlefield where North Korean cadres can be cycled through actual combat against NATO-trained Ukrainians. North Korea has the artillery shell stocks Western analysts thought were museum pieces, an army that has not been demobilised in seventy years, and a population from which manpower can be extracted on short notice. The trade is not symmetric in dignity. It is highly symmetric in usefulness. Pooling under pressure beats pooling for its own sake every time, and this is the pooling-under-pressure case.

Greene's Law 11 — learn to keep people dependent on you — describes the harder mechanic. Once the cooperation plan runs five years, with annual expenditure baked into both military budgets and joint procurement contracts already in flight, a future Russian government cannot exit the relationship without forfeiting the manpower pipeline that the long war has come to rely on. A future North Korean government cannot exit without forfeiting the technology and air-defence transfers that have already begun arriving in Pyongyang. Each side has built dependency in the other. This is what alliance institutionalisation does — it raises the cost of departure to the point where the parties stay because the leaving is more expensive than the staying.

What Seoul and Tokyo read.

South Korea's National Intelligence Service has briefed the National Assembly that what Pyongyang receives in exchange — and the briefing was unusually direct — runs in three lanes: tactical-ballistic-missile guidance refinement, advanced air defence (the S-400 component package, with possible S-500 follow-on), and submarine quietening. The third is the lane that matters most. A North Korean ballistic-missile submarine that can leave port without being tracked is a strategic-balance problem of a different category from one whose shaft signature is logged before it clears the breakwater. Tokyo reads the same brief. Both are now in the position of asking Washington whether the US extended-deterrence umbrella is still indexed to the threat the umbrella was designed for, or whether the threat has changed underneath it.

Lee Kuan Yew's instinct on small-state strategy applies in mirror to Seoul. The smaller, technologically advanced state surrounded by a hostile coordinated bloc has two options: accelerate its own indigenous deterrent (the South Korean nuclear-latency conversation, dormant for two years, has reopened in the conservative press over the past fortnight) or buy deeper into the alliance whose extended deterrence is its hedge. The pact in Pyongyang has shifted the cost matrix on both options. Both have become more expensive; the second option has also become more uncertain, because the umbrella's holder is governing in a fashion that allies can no longer assume is constant.

The Eurasian read.

Dugin's reading on Eurasian land-power, treated by Western chanceries as ideology, has a mechanical form here that has nothing to do with metaphysics. The land bloc Moscow envisions — Russia plus Iran plus North Korea plus, structurally if not declaratively, China — is the bloc the West's sanctions architecture has accidentally built. Each member has been pushed out of the dollar trade architecture, the SWIFT messaging architecture, and the Western technology architecture. Each has been forced to find inside the bloc what it cannot get from outside. North Korean shells in Russian guns; Iranian drones in Russian batteries; Russian anti-air over Iranian refineries; Chinese semiconductors inside Russian missile guidance. The Pyongyang pact is the visible institutional surface of an architecture whose deeper layers have been forming for three years. The cost of treating it as theatre is that the West reads each new visible event as the first, when it is the latest.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the analysis. Structural: two manpower-and-technology-constrained sanctioned states facing a coalition are pooling because pooling is what the structural pressure predicts; the only surprise is the speed at which the pooling has institutionalised. Coordination: the Pyongyang pact, the Petersburg meetings with Iran, the Belousov delegation, and the Volodin Duma session this week are pieces of a single switchboard, not independent flashes. What is not load-bearing today is tactical-virtuosity. Belousov's trip is competent diplomacy, not artistry. The interesting fact is what the trip institutionalises, not how it was conducted.

The lenses used.

The Hearing Begins

At 10:00 ET this morning the United States Trade Representative opens public hearings on sixty Section 301 investigations into other countries' failure to police forced-labour goods, with companion overcapacity probes already in motion. The hearings have no cameras, no livestream, and a written transcript will follow. They are also the legal architecture for the next round of sectoral tariffs against China — and the schedule has been timed to deliver determinations into the autumn, when the Busan truce expires on 10 November. Two hundred days separate the hearing from the renegotiation. The hearing is the renegotiation, in advance.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington & Geneva·28 April 2026

Washington —The US International Trade Commission's main hearing room at 500 E Street SW will fill with trade lawyers, supply-chain auditors, and a smaller number of foreign-government counsel this morning at 10:00. The hearing is the first formal public session on the sixty Section 301 investigations the USTR launched on 13 March into the failure of trading partners to ban goods produced with forced labour. Sixty economies sit inside the docket; the political weight of the docket sits on China. A parallel slate of Section 301 investigations into structural overcapacity — semiconductors, solar equipment, electric-vehicle components — is running on the same calendar. The two slates are designed to converge into determinations that arrive in late summer and early autumn, just before the Busan truce of October 2025 — which suspended the broad reciprocal tariffs and paused China's rare-earth export controls — expires on 10 November.

Read the schedule. The Trump administration is not building a case for a single tariff announcement this morning. It is building the legal architecture from which a sequence of sectoral tariffs can be issued without renewed public debate, on a timetable the trading partner cannot disrupt. Section 301 investigations are slow — six to nine months from initiation to determination — but the determinations, once issued, give the executive a procedural authority to act sectorally that the Liberation Day tariffs lacked when the Supreme Court invalidated them on 20 February. The 6-3 ruling is not the end of the tariff project. It is the redesign of the legal pipeline. This morning is the redesign's first public deliverable.

"Avoid trial of strength on terrain favourable to the enemy; choose the terrain on which the enemy cannot fight as well as you can." — Sun Tzu, paraphrased. After SCOTUS, the IEEPA terrain was lost. Section 301 is the terrain SCOTUS has not yet ruled on, and the procedure was the architecture's choice on which file to fight.

What the hearing actually does.

The hearings are a procedural floor, not a substantive ceiling. Witnesses will testify on whether each foreign country's enforcement of forced-labour bans is "unreasonable or discriminatory and burden or restrict US commerce" — the statutory phrase that triggers Section 301. Most of the testimony will be theatre; the substance has been written into the staff record over the preceding six weeks. What matters is the political-legal record the hearing creates: a documented basis on which the USTR can issue determinations the Court of International Trade and the Federal Circuit will review under deferential standards rather than the strict-scrutiny terms applied to IEEPA. The architecture is what Munger calls a "lollapalooza" of multiple compounding factors — statutory authority, procedural record, deferential review, sectoral specificity — none of which alone wins, all of which together do.

Michael Pettis's framework on Chinese imbalances reads the overcapacity slate cleanly. The structural distortion in Chinese saving-and-consumption is what produces the export surplus the rest of the world is now organising to push back against; the pushback is what Pettis has long argued was the inevitable late-stage correction. The Trump administration has not adopted Pettis's vocabulary, but it has adopted his diagnosis. The Section 301 overcapacity probes name the same mechanism — that the Chinese state's industrial subsidies and demand suppression produce a price level that bankrupts foreign competitors — and propose the tariff as the corrective. The argument is no longer fringe; it is the operational basis of the world's largest trade enforcement apparatus.

The truce arithmetic.

The Busan truce of October 2025 suspended the reciprocal-tariff rate, paused China's rare-earth export controls, and bought both sides 13 months of breathing room. The truce expires 10 November 2026. Between today and that date, three calendars run in parallel. The Section 301 forced-labour determinations are scheduled for July through October. The Section 301 overcapacity determinations follow a similar timetable. The political calendar — Trump's authority to act, Xi's tolerance for rate increases the Chinese economy is barely absorbing at current levels — runs underneath both. The structural fact is that neither side is preparing to extend the truce on the original terms. Both sides are preparing the legal and political architecture for what comes after the truce. The hearing this morning is the most visible of those preparations.

Lee Kuan Yew's instinct on regulation in small advanced economies generalises here against the United States. The country with the larger structural position can afford to take the time the slower procedure requires; the country with the weaker position cannot. China's response to the Section 301 docket has been quieter than the response to Liberation Day was a year ago — not because the docket matters less, but because the response is being prepared on the same timetable. Beijing tightened solar-equipment exports on 15 April. The rare-earth controls suspended at Busan can be reactivated on a week's notice. The countermeasure-decree authority issued at the National People's Congress in March extends extraterritorial jurisdiction to penalise foreign firms that comply with US sanctions. Each instrument is a piece of architecture being held in reserve for after 10 November. Both sides are running the same play, in mirror.

The court question.

The Supreme Court's February ruling did not kill the project; it forced the architecture to migrate. The Court of International Trade is now considering whether refunds of $130 billion in collected IEEPA tariffs must be issued, and the case is scheduled for argument in the second quarter. A "yes" forces the administration to either appeal — extending the legal life of the case past the truce expiry — or refund and replace, which is the architecture the Section 301 docket has been built to provide. The legal architecture is converging on the political schedule. The administration has placed itself in a position where, by November, the IEEPA tariff is a sunk cost, the Section 301 sectoral tariff is the live instrument, and the truce has either been extended on terms favourable to Washington or has lapsed into the new sectoral regime.

The reading.

Two layers carry it. Structural: a trade dispute that has run for eight years is now bound by the Chinese export surplus, the US legal review pipeline, and the November truce expiry; the timing of the Section 301 docket is dictated by the structure, not by the politics, and the politics has been retrofitted to fit. Strategy: the move from IEEPA to Section 301 is the execution of Sun Tzu's discipline of choosing the terrain — IEEPA was lost at SCOTUS; Section 301 has not yet been tested, and is harder to challenge under the deferential standards. What is not load-bearing today is consciousness-state. The hearing room will be calm. The action that matters is the procedural record being built, and the procedural record is built whether the witnesses are persuasive or not.

The Iberian Year

A year ago today, at 12:33 CEST, the Iberian peninsula went dark for ten hours. The investigations are now in. The ENTSO-E expert panel and the Spanish Senate inquiry both name the same cause: not renewables themselves, but voltage and reactive-power control architectures that did not keep pace with what the renewable share of the grid had become. The grid operator, the regulator, and the government share the verdict. The deeper finding is the one the policy debate keeps refusing to read: institutional drift outran the physics of the transition the institutions had committed to.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Madrid & Lisbon·28 April 2026

Madrid —One year ago today, at 12:33 CEST, the Iberian Peninsula lost grid frequency. Within four seconds, fifteen gigawatts of generation tripped offline. Mainland Spain and Portugal went dark for between ten and sixteen hours; six people died, most from oxygen-machine failure or chimney fires from improvised heating. The cause was not what the first wave of commentary said it was. The ENTSO-E final report, published 20 March, and the Spanish Senate inquiry, published 17 April, both rule out a cyber attack and rule out renewable share as the proximate cause. They name the same causal stack: voltage oscillations, gaps in reactive-power control, generators tripping faster than the system could rebalance, and uneven stabilisation capabilities across the synchronous Iberian island. The renewable energy share of generation that day was not the problem. The control architecture that was supposed to govern the renewable share was the problem. Twenty-four gigawatts of certified renewable voltage capacity was unused on the day, the panel found, because regulatory barriers prevented its dispatch.

Read the verdict carefully. ENTSO-E's chair has stated the conclusion in a sentence: "the problem is not renewable energy, but voltage control, regardless of the type of generation." The Spanish Senate goes further. Its nine-month inquiry holds the grid operator REE, the regulator CNMC, and the Sánchez government jointly responsible for what it calls "long-standing structural weaknesses already known." The structural weaknesses were known. The dispatchable voltage support was certified. The dispatch did not happen because the institutional layer that had to authorise the dispatch had not kept pace with the physical layer it was meant to govern.

"When the moral law and the institution are misaligned, the institution decays before the law fails." — paraphrase of Confucius. Spain's renewable transition was a stated commitment; the institutional architecture supporting it was the part that drifted.

What the physics required.

A grid running 70% inverter-based generation — the share Iberia frequently hit in 2025 — has no native inertia. Synchronous machines spinning iron cores are the traditional source of frequency stability; inverter-based generation has no rotating mass and no frequency response unless one is engineered into the inverter and dispatched into the grid. Engineered grid-forming inverters exist; battery-coupled synchronous condensers exist; the technology is mature. What did not exist on 28 April 2025 was the regulatory mechanism to call that capability into the operating reserve at the moment it was needed. The blackout was a four-second cascade between physical states the institution did not know how to ask the grid to occupy.

Helen Thompson's framework on energy as continuous political economy fits the verdict exactly. Spain's renewable build-out was a political-electoral commitment that ran ahead of the regulatory and institutional architecture required to operate the system the build-out produced. The political layer mobilised; the institutional layer did not. The two ran in parallel until the gap between them was too large for the physics to absorb. The blackout closed the gap. The cost of the closure was a country in the dark.

The institutional finding.

The Spanish Senate's report is the more interesting of the two. ENTSO-E's panel was technical; the Senate inquiry is political-institutional. Its first finding: the Sánchez government's energy minister, Sara Aagesen, in office since November 2024, inherited a regulatory architecture her ministry knew was inadequate and continued the operating posture her predecessor had set. Its second finding: REE, the part-private grid operator, was operating on staffing levels Adam Tooze would call the "absorption gap" — engineering capacity to monitor a system materially smaller than the system that had been built around it. Its third finding: CNMC, the regulator, was running an authorisation process whose timetable presupposed a transition pace materially slower than the actual transition.

This is what institutional decay looks like in real time, in a system that was nominally healthy. No actor was corrupt. No actor was incompetent on its own terms. Each actor was running on assumptions inherited from a previous decade. Confucius's reading of regimes that fail by drift rather than by error applies almost word for word: the form of government that disintegrates is rarely the one whose officials are bad, but the one whose officials cannot keep pace with the conditions they are meant to govern. Lee Kuan Yew's variation on the same theme is sharper: institutions that do not refresh their operating envelope at the speed of the underlying technology become hostages to the next failure event the technology produces.

What Spain has done since.

Operationally, much. Updated voltage-control procedures for renewables rolled out in March 2026. Portugal allocated €400 million to grid reinforcement and grid-battery deployment. Cross-border interconnection with France was upgraded. ENTSO-E published a defence-plan recommendation that all synchronous islands now reference. The technical layer has moved.

Politically, less. The June 2025 omnibus energy bill — proposed in the immediate aftermath of the blackout, focused on storage and grid reinforcement — was blocked in the Spanish Parliament in July 2025 and has not returned. The Sánchez minority government did not have the votes; the PP-led opposition did not have the political incentive to deliver them. Aragón fell to the PP-Vox in February. Extremadura had fallen in December. The Iran shock has reorganised the politics around energy prices in ways that benefit the government domestically — Sánchez has become a vocal opponent of the US-led war, polling at 36% — but it has not delivered a parliamentary majority for the structural reform the blackout exposed. The political fact a year on is that the legislative architecture that the blackout argued for is not in place. The technical fact a year on is that, if a similar contingency arrives, the system is closer to ready than it was.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry it. Structural: a power system whose physical envelope has been transformed faster than the regulatory and institutional envelope governing it; the gap, when stressed, produced the failure. Coordination: a regulator, an operator, and a government that all knew the gap existed and none of which closed it on the schedule the physics required. What is not load-bearing is consciousness-state. The actors involved were not in unusual conditions; they were in the routine condition of officials operating on yesterday's assumptions. That is the dangerous condition, in any system whose physical reality is moving faster than the institutional clock.

The lenses used.

What's driving this: A power system whose physical envelope was transformed faster than the regulatory and institutional envelope around it. Law of the Day · No. 11

Learn to keep people dependent on you.

To maintain your independence you must always be needed and wanted. The more you are relied on, the more freedom you have. Make people depend on you for their happiness and prosperity and you have nothing to fear. Never teach them enough so that they can do without you.

Today's reading: three theatres, three faces of the same dependency mechanic. The Pyongyang pact converts a reversible expedient — North Korean infantry deployed to Russian formations — into a five-year written architecture in which each side has built dependency in the other; departure becomes more expensive than continuation. The USTR's Section 301 architecture is the United States rebuilding the procedural dependency a sectoral tariff regime requires after the Supreme Court invalidated the broader IEEPA instrument. The Iberian blackout verdict is the inverse case: a system whose institutional architecture failed to keep pace with the technology it had become dependent on, and discovered the gap only when a four-second cascade closed it. In each case, the question is who is dependent on whom — and at what cost the dependency can be exited.

  • An alliance written down in five-year increments, with annual schedules and joint procurement contracts, is harder to leave than an ad-hoc deployment. Institutionalise during years of relative strength, not weakness.
  • A trade architecture migrated from a struck-down statute to a procedurally robust one delivers determinations on the calendar the political adversary cannot disrupt. Choose the terrain on which the next courtroom has not yet ruled.
  • An institutional layer that drifts behind the physical or technological layer it is meant to govern becomes hostage to the next failure event. Refresh the operating envelope at the speed of the underlying change, or the failure event will refresh it for you.

The Practitioner's Note

Build the dependency before you need it.

The three stories share a discipline at the layer below the headlines. Russia and North Korea are signing the architecture of their alliance during the war in which they need it most, not after. The USTR is building Section 301 procedural authority before the Supreme Court has had occasion to test it. The Spanish blackout is the cautionary case — the architecture that should have been refreshed before the test arrived was refreshed, in part, by the test itself. The first two are practitioners executing the discipline; the third is the practitioner who did not.

In your own affairs: the institutionalisation moment is when the conditions favour the move, not when the conditions force it. The five-year plan is signed during the war, not after the ceasefire. The legal architecture is built before the courtroom test, not in response to it. The technical envelope of your operation is refreshed before the contingency, not by the contingency. The pattern is constant across the three stories — and so is the cost when the pattern is broken.

The hard question: in your own work, what dependency are you building right now that will be expensive to exit later — and is it being built on a schedule you control or one that will be controlled for you? Whose institutional clock is running faster than yours, and at what point does the gap stop being recoverable?
