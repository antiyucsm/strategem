The Scarborough Decade

Saturday marks ten years since the Permanent Court of Arbitration voided Beijing’s South China Sea claim. Ten years later Beijing controls the shoal without contest, a 352-metre floating barrier walls the lagoon, and Filipino fishermen no longer sail east. The ruling was a legal instrument; the shoal is an occupation. The anniversary is where the frame ends its useful life and the fact stands alone.

The anniversary, first.

On July 12, 2016, the Permanent Court of Arbitration at The Hague ruled in Philippines v. China that Beijing’s nine-dash line had no basis in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea and that Scarborough Shoal — a fishing atoll ninety miles from the Philippine coast — was a traditional fishing ground of the two states. Beijing refused to participate and refuses to recognise the ruling. Ten years on, three facts stand on the tape. First: China Coast Guard patrols at Scarborough Shoal reached 933 ship-days in the first half of 2026, per the Asia Maritime Transparency Initiative — 156 ship-days a month against 90 in the same period of 2025. Second: in April, satellite imagery documented a 352-metre floating barrier at the entrance to the shoal’s lagoon, and in May the Armed Forces of the Philippines confirmed a six-by-six manned structure inside it. Third: the Philippine fishermen who worked Scarborough for generations have withdrawn from it. Rony Drio last sailed there in 2024; Henrilito Empoc in 2022. The anniversary the Marcos government marked in Manila on Saturday is a legal one. The operational one has been in Beijing’s hands for a decade.

Mearsheimer, patiently, on arbitration.

Offensive realism has one prediction about the 2016 ruling and it has been correct in every year of the decade since. In an anarchic international system, no third party has the coercive capacity to enforce an award against a great power that refuses the award. The Permanent Court of Arbitration was a mid-twentieth-century institution built to arbitrate between medium powers who preferred the arbitration to the war. Applied against a rising hegemon in its own near seas, it produces exactly the outcome Mearsheimer predicts: a paper judgment, a shrug from the defendant, and a decade of operational consolidation that gradually converts contested waters into settled control. The Philippines is not the party that lost at The Hague. The party that lost was the assumption — carefully cultivated in the post-1945 order — that legal instruments constrain great-power behaviour in their spheres of primary interest. The ruling did not constrain Beijing. What constrains Beijing at Scarborough now is the Mutual Defence Treaty and the calculation of the marginal cost of a Philippine casualty.

Dugin on the coastal salient.

Read from Moscow’s continental register — Alexander Dugin’s geopolitics of Eurasian land-power against the Anglo-maritime rim — the shoal reads differently. Scarborough is not a Chinese island; it is a Chinese salient into a maritime Rimland that Washington has held since 1945. Every additional day the barrier stands, every additional ship-day the Coast Guard adds, is a tactical increment toward a strategic reversal: the conversion of the near sea from an American commons into a Chinese lake. Beijing does not need Scarborough to build a base at. It needs Scarborough to be the salient on which the Rimland’s freedom-of-navigation doctrine has to be exercised or abandoned. The exercise costs the United States a carrier group and a casualty risk. The abandonment costs the credibility that underwrites Manila’s alliance and, by extension, Taipei’s posture. This is the pincer inside which the Marcos government has been operating for two years.

The grey-zone concealment.

Sun Tzu on the shape of an occupation whose success is measured by the adversary’s decision not to contest, and Greene’s third law — concealment of intentions — read the Coast Guard-and-militia posture as the tactical layer that ratifies the structural claim. Beijing does not deploy a naval combatant to Scarborough. It deploys a Coast Guard cutter and a fleet of maritime militia trawlers, and it walls the lagoon with a fabric barrier. Each act is individually below the threshold of casus belli under the Mutual Defence Treaty; the aggregate is the fait accompli. The 2016 ruling anticipated the aggregate; the treaty language did not. Ten years later the gap between the two — between what is legally impermissible and what is treaty-actionable — is the operational space Beijing has occupied one ship-day at a time.

The Ottoman rhyme.

The historical rhyme is the Ottoman consolidation of Constantinople after 1453. The Byzantine cause was legally intact in every European chancellery for a generation after the walls fell; the emperors-in-exile petitioned; the papacy issued instruments; the Venetians drafted memoranda. None of it moved a stone. What moved was the demographic and administrative fact of Ottoman rule, which by 1500 had converted the legal aberration into the operational status quo, and the Christian courts adjusted their treaty language accordingly. Scarborough will not follow the same arc — the shoal is not a city and the Coast Guard cutter is not a Janissary — but the mechanism is the same: a defeated legal instrument, an unbroken operational posture, and a decade during which the counterparties gradually stop invoking the ruling because the invocation begins to embarrass them.

What the reading expects.

Base case: the barrier stays; the ship-day count rises again in H2 2026; Manila performs the anniversary as a diplomatic instrument and continues to file diplomatic protests that neither Washington nor the ASEAN chair converts into binding action; the joint patrols with Japan and Australia expand under the reciprocal access agreements but remain outside the Mutual Defence Treaty envelope. A confirmed Filipino casualty at the shoal is the only event that repositions the reading — it forces the treaty question that Beijing has been careful never to force. Absent that, the decade’s pattern continues: incremental consolidation, credible legal defeat, and the quiet re-normalisation of what a great power in its near seas can do while the ruling on the shelf is invoked by the aggrieved as a moral instrument rather than by the guarantors as an operational one.

The Stratagem reads the debut not as a listing story but as the moment the AI-capex complex’s circular-capital architecture publicly extends across the Pacific: SK Hynix uses the $26.5 billion to build the HBM capacity Nvidia’s next-generation Rubin platform requires, Nvidia uses SK Hynix HBM to sell to Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Meta, and the four hyperscalers price their AI capex on the expectation that SK Hynix will build to meet it. The reading draws on Munger on incentive-caused bias inside a supplier-customer capital stack, Dalio on the Big Cycle phase at which cross-border capital rotates toward the marginal producer of the scarce input, Michael Pettis on the imbalance mechanics under which a savings-heavy economy exports capital by exporting equity, Lee Kuan Yew on the coastal Asian economy that converts a strategic industry into a US-listed instrument, and the Alibaba-2014 debut as the imperfect historical rhyme — the same scale of foreign listing at the moment the previous cycle had priced its scarce input to the ceiling.

The Hynix Debut

$26.5 billion raised on the Nasdaq, a book seven times oversubscribed, an ADR opening fifteen per cent above the offer — the largest foreign IPO in US history, larger than Alibaba 2014. The debut is not a listing story. It is the moment the AI-capex complex’s circular-capital architecture publicly extends across the Pacific: Nvidia’s largest HBM supplier now trades on the exchange where Nvidia’s customers price their capex.

The tape, first.

On Thursday evening, SK Hynix priced its Nasdaq listing at $149 per American Depositary Receipt; the deal offered 177.9 million ADRs, raising $26.5 billion in gross proceeds. Trading opened Friday, July 10, under the temporary ticker SKHYV; regular trading under SKHY begins Monday, July 13. The ADRs opened up fifteen per cent, traded at $171.41 mid-session, and closed near $169 — a first-day pop of roughly thirteen per cent on a book that had been more than seven times oversubscribed. The proceeds are the largest raised by any foreign company in US listing history, larger than Alibaba’s $25 billion 2014 debut. The company’s Icheon HBM fab now sits inside the Nasdaq perimeter; Jensen Huang’s public description of SK Hynix as Nvidia’s largest memory partner is now a fact the DTCC clears against.

The circular stack.

Read at the Munger register — incentive-caused bias inside a supplier-customer capital stack — the debut extends across the Pacific the pattern the OpenAI $122 billion round installed inside the United States. Nvidia sells accelerators built around SK Hynix HBM; SK Hynix sells HBM whose demand is set almost entirely by Nvidia’s Rubin platform build-out; the four US hyperscalers (Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta) buy the accelerators on capex plans they pre-announce a year forward; and the equity that funds the SK Hynix HBM fab is now priced against the same US investor base that prices Nvidia. Each link is individually defensible: SK Hynix needs the fab capital, Nvidia needs the HBM, the hyperscalers need the accelerators, the allocators need the exposure. The chain read as a whole is a supplier funded by its customer’s customer — the circular pattern the AI complex has been building since 2023, now with a public Korean anchor tenant. The book’s seven-times oversubscription is what a circular-capital chain looks like when every allocator is paid to believe every other allocator will pay for the next round.

Pettis on the imbalance mechanic.

The register on which the debut sits at the structural layer is Michael Pettis’s savings-imbalance mechanic. Korea, like Japan and Taiwan and China before it, is a savings-heavy economy whose current-account surplus recycles into overseas equity because the domestic pool cannot absorb the returns the export sector generates. SK Hynix is not raising the $26.5 billion because Korean allocators would not have paid it; the Kospi could have supplied the ticket at the same coverage. The reason is the reverse: SK Hynix is raising the capital in New York because the US allocator base is the marginal buyer of the AI trade, and being listed where the trade is priced is worth more than being listed where the balance sheet was built. The imbalance mechanic converts a Korean capex programme into a US-dollar-denominated equity float; the Korean central bank buys the resulting dollar inflow and recycles it into US Treasuries; the Fed absorbs the recycling into the guidance instrument. The Big Cycle name for this sequence is late-cycle terminal savings recycling, and SK Hynix’s book size is the marker.

Lee Kuan Yew on the coastal Asian economy that converts a strategic industry into a US-listed instrument reads the trade at the state register. Korea’s administrative state authorised the ADR listing because it prefers the geopolitical guarantee that comes with US investor exposure to the marginal control cost the listing extracts. Every American pension fund that owns SKHY is a small piece of counter-pressure against a Chinese semiconductor push at Icheon. The listing is a national instrument that reads as a market one.

The Alibaba rhyme.

The imperfect historical rhyme is Alibaba, September 2014: a Chinese e-commerce operator raising $25 billion in the largest foreign listing to date, at the moment the previous cycle’s scarce input (cross-border consumer platforms) had priced to the ceiling. The rhyme runs at two registers. First, scale: SK Hynix at $26.5 billion is now the record, but the record is set against a market cap for the AI complex that has expanded more than tenfold since 2022. Second, timing: Alibaba priced within eleven months of the S&P 500’s May-2015 nominal peak, and the subsequent 18 months delivered a fifteen-per-cent drawdown that punished the reflexive marginal buyer. Neither register argues the Hynix debut is mis-priced on entry — the HBM order book is real, and the Rubin schedule is disclosed. Both registers argue that the record-scale foreign IPO is a late-cycle marker even when the underlying operator is fundamentally sound.

What the reading expects.

Base case: SKHY holds a premium in a $150-to-$200 band through the ASML and TSMC prints next week; the first-day allocators redistribute inside the first ninety days without a floor break; Micron trades sympathetically in the $900-to-$1,100 range and the memory sub-index widens against the semi-cap-equipment sub-index. Nvidia’s multiple compresses ten to twenty per cent as capital rotates within the AI complex from the accelerator to the HBM supplier — the multiple was carrying part of the HBM allocation the Hynix listing now provides its own instrument for. The Korean current-account surplus recycles as expected; the KRW-USD does not break the July range. The alternative case is a hyperscaler capex deceleration announced at Q3 earnings (August through October); the circular chain unwinds at the second link, Nvidia gaps down and SKHY halves inside a fortnight, and the Alibaba rhyme prints with a five-month lag. Neither the Amazon capex guide nor the Microsoft one currently signals the deceleration; both signal the opposite.

The Lecornu Vise · Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu on Friday told Bloomberg and the wire desks that without a new finance bill France’s 2027 deficit will widen to 6.5 per cent of GDP as the current spending plans automatically roll over — the highest projected print of any large advanced economy. The Franco-German ten-year spread, at 80 basis points, sits at a nine-month wide. Two prime ministers have already been consumed by the two-year budget sequence that followed Macron’s 2024 snap election. Lecornu’s February 2026 budget passed only after two Article 49.3 invocations and two failed motions of censure. The 2027 preparation now opens without a working majority, without an SPD-analogue on the moderate left, and with the RN and LFI positioning ahead of the April 2027 presidential election. The Stratagem reads the vise not as a fiscal accident but as the moment the post-2017 Macronist political architecture — a centrist executive delivered by a two-round electoral system that no longer produces a working majority — is being consumed by the debt cycle it postponed. The reading draws on Dalio’s Big Cycle on the phase at which a debt-heavy sovereign loses its fiscal option, Helen Thompson’s political economy of the euro-area periphery whose fiscal sovereignty is enforced by the spread rather than by the treaty, La Rochefoucauld on the elite whose maxims outlive the constitution that produced them, Aristotle on the mixed regime that fails when the centre is hollowed out by both extremes at once, and the 1958 Fourth Republic collapse as the imperfect historical rhyme — a parliamentary architecture whose fiscal instrument fractured before its political one.

The Lecornu Vise

6.5 per cent projected deficit if nothing passes. 80 basis points on the OAT-Bund spread, a nine-month wide. Two prime ministers already broken by the 2024 hung parliament, a third weighing whether the fourth is now inevitable. The 2027 budget preparation opens without a working majority and 273 days before the presidential first round. The vise is not fiscal accident; it is the debt-cycle bill the post-2017 Macronist architecture has been postponing for eight years.

The arithmetic, first.

Prime Minister Sébastien Lecornu’s Friday brief to Bloomberg named the number the National Assembly has been trying not to price. Without a new finance bill, France’s 2027 deficit widens to 6.5 per cent of GDP as the current spending plans automatically roll over — up from the 5.0 per cent target that Article 49.3 forced through in February. Public debt sits above 115 per cent of GDP, second only to Italy in the euro area. The Franco-German ten-year spread is 80 basis points, its highest print since October 2025 when Lecornu briefly resigned before Macron reinstated him. On the day the number was released, the spread widened another two basis points and the CAC-40 closed down forty. The market is beginning to price a repeat of the two-year cycle it has already lived through: a finance bill, a censure motion, an Article 49.3, another censure motion, and either a rescue coalition of the Socialist rump or a fall of the government whose calendar collides with the April 2027 presidential first round.

Dalio on the fiscal option.

Dalio’s Big Cycle names the phase France is now visibly in: the moment at which a debt-heavy sovereign loses its fiscal option and its policy choices narrow to what the bond market and the central bank will let it deliver. The proximate cause is not the debt stock alone. It is the political architecture that no longer produces the majority required to bend the fiscal trajectory. When the majority is intact, a debt-heavy sovereign can spread the correction over a decade at market rates it broadly controls. When the majority is fractured, the correction is imposed from outside by the spread. Italy discovered this in 2011. Greece discovered it in 2010. France, whose 2024 snap election broke the majority instrument the Fifth Republic’s two-round system had reliably produced since 1958, is discovering it inside a slower version of the same mechanism.

The mechanism has a distinct signature: the political cost of any specific fiscal measure rises faster than the fiscal cost of not passing it, until the government falls, the next government inherits a smaller option set, and the cycle repeats. Two prime ministers — Barnier in December 2024, Bayrou after a nine-month tenure through late 2025 — have already been consumed by the sequence. Lecornu’s survival through February and June owed to face-saving concessions to the Socialist wing that cost approximately 6 billion euros per year and moved the trajectory in the wrong direction. Each round of concession has bought political time at the price of fiscal option. Dalio’s phase-transition marker is precisely this asymmetric trade.

Helen Thompson on the peripheral mechanic.

Helen Thompson’s political economy of the euro-area names the register at which the spread does its work. Inside the single-currency architecture, fiscal sovereignty is not enforced by the treaty; it is enforced by the price the market charges the sovereign to fund the deficit. The treaty says one thing; the OAT-Bund spread enforces another. What the Fifth Republic could postpone with autonomous monetary policy in 1983 — the Mitterrand tournant — it cannot postpone in 2027, because the ECB will not underwrite an OAT spread that is compensating for a fiscal path incompatible with the currency. The Transmission Protection Instrument exists on paper; its use requires the sovereign to be inside a stability programme; and a stability programme requires a majority France does not have.

Thompson’s reading is that Paris has entered the peripheral position without any of the political instruments the periphery developed for handling it: no Berlusconi-Draghi transition figure available, no Syriza-style rupture legitimated by the pro-European centre, no willingness of the establishment left to co-sign an austerity package as the German SPD did with the Merz Programme covered in yesterday’s edition. The vise is not what the peripheral treatment normally looks like. It is what the treatment looks like when the political architecture cannot deliver even the rescue coalition the treatment requires.

Aristotle and the hollowed centre.

The constitutional register is Aristotle’s. Book V of the Politics reads a mixed regime as durable only where the centre is broad enough to co-opt the extremes at the flank. The Macronist architecture was the twenty-first century’s clearest attempt at exactly that co-optation — a technocratic centre importing centre-right economics and centre-left social policy, disciplined by the two-round electoral system that punished the extremes. The 2024 snap election broke the arithmetic: the Rassemblement National on the right and La France Insoumise on the left both cleared the second-round threshold in enough constituencies to hollow the centre’s parliamentary purchase, and the centre lost the majority it had held since 2017.

The Aristotelian failure mode is the one now playing out. When the centre is hollowed, the extremes discover they can each veto anything the centre proposes while refusing to co-sign the alternative. The RN votes down the finance bill on the sovereignty register; LFI votes it down on the redistribution register; the Socialist rump extracts serial concessions in exchange for abstentions that do not build to a majority. La Rochefoucauld’s Maxim 218 — hypocrisy is a tribute vice pays to virtue — reads the current French political class at the register it deserves: everyone claims to want fiscal responsibility, no one is willing to pay the local political cost, and the aggregate is a slow-motion refusal to legislate that produces exactly the peripheral treatment the class had spent a decade insisting France was too central to receive.

The Fourth Republic rhyme.

The imperfect historical rhyme is the Fourth Republic’s 1958 collapse. The parliamentary architecture of 1946-1958 produced twenty-one governments in twelve years, each broken on the Algerian and fiscal questions the political class could not aggregate a majority around. De Gaulle’s Fifth Republic was engineered specifically to prevent the pattern by inventing the two-round majoritarian instrument and the semi-presidential executive. The 2024 snap election disabled the majoritarian instrument in a single evening; the executive that remains has no institutional path to the majority it needs. The current 2027 preparation is the peaceful version of a mechanism the Fifth Republic was built to prevent. The rhyme is imperfect because Algeria is not on the tape. The rhyming part is architectural: a parliamentary aggregation failure that the executive cannot resolve, producing a fiscal instrument that no longer aggregates a majority.

The alternative case is a discrete Italian-style spread event that forces an ECB test of the Transmission Protection Instrument before Christmas; this requires a US Treasury shock or an ECB communication mistake that the July tape does not carry.
