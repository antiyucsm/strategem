I.
International · The Theatre of Nations
Filed El-Obeid / Port Sudan / Abu Dhabi
The El-Obeid Line · The North Kordofan capital of El-Obeid enters the fourth week of a tightening RSF encirclement, with the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights having issued a “red alert” on July 3 warning that approximately five hundred thousand civilians — including one hundred and five thousand already displaced from earlier phases of the war — are trapped inside the perimeter with no functional evacuation corridor, the city entering its third week without electricity, and RSF drone strikes intensifying on the SAF garrison and the residential districts behind it. El-Obeid is the pivot: whoever holds the city holds the road that connects the RSF’s Darfur strongholds to central Sudan and the Nile corridor the SAF-aligned Idris government reclaimed in January. The Stratagem reads the siege not as a humanitarian episode but as the point at which the Sudanese war’s structural geography — a two-belligerent conflict funded and re-supplied from abroad — forces the operational decision the diplomatic track has failed to force: whether the RSF can be permitted to convert the western half of Sudan into a coherent statelet with a road to the capital, or whether the SAF and its external backers must accept the political cost of relieving the city before the pattern of El-Fasher — eighteen months of siege, then storm, then massacre — repeats. The reading draws on Sun Tzu on the siege as the least desirable form of war, Mearsheimer on the regional vacuum through which external backers convert a civil war into a proxy chess-board, Clausewitz on the political sentence a besieging force writes with the pace of its ratchet, and the historical rhyme with Srebrenica, whose 1995 fall was likewise foreseen and unrelieved.

The El-Obeid Line

A Sudanese city of half a million enters the fourth week of a tightening RSF encirclement; the UN issues a red alert on July 3, the electricity has been out for the better part of a month, and the diplomatic track has produced nothing. The siege is the pivot at which the war’s geography stops being ambiguous.

The geography, first.

El-Obeid is not one city among many. It is the road. Kordofan is the connective tissue between the RSF’s Darfur base and the Nile corridor the SAF-aligned Idris government reclaimed in January when Khartoum was formally re-occupied. Whoever holds El-Obeid controls the highway through which fuel, food, weapons, and people move between the western half of the country and the centre. This is a fact of terrain, not of politics; it will remain true whichever flag flies over the garrison.

The RSF understands this. Its encirclement of El-Obeid is now in its nineteenth month if one counts from the initial May 2024 siege, its fourth week if one counts from the final choking of the perimeter that OHCHR marked with the red alert. Half a million civilians are inside. The garrison is the Sudanese Army’s Fifth Infantry Division and whatever mobilised reinforcement Khartoum could push down the Nile before the road out of Sennar became too costly. The city has been without electricity for the better part of a month. Fuel is at the price a rope-and-pulley economy sets when there is no functional road to a functional refinery. Bread is at the price the flour smugglers set.

Why the diplomatic track has produced nothing.

Sudan is one of the clearest present cases of a structural pattern the coordination-layer reading names by default: a civil war in which the belligerents are not their own principals. The RSF is armed, fuelled, and diplomatically underwritten from Abu Dhabi. The SAF is armed and diplomatically underwritten from Cairo and, in a more calibrated register, from Ankara and Riyadh. The United States is an interested but not commanding party; its June diplomatic push produced a communiqué and no ceasefire. The Berlin aid conference in April produced pledges and no monitor. The African Union track is the tacit acknowledgement that no African capital will underwrite an intervention.

The structural obstacle is not that the two Sudanese belligerents cannot reach terms. It is that the four external backers cannot. The war continues at exactly the tempo the Emirati and Egyptian material commitments sustain. Reading the siege as an isolated humanitarian episode — as most Western coverage reads it — is a category error. It is a coordination-layer fact expressed at the tactical register.

Sun Tzu on the siege.

“The worst policy of all is to besiege walled cities,” Sun Tzu writes in the third chapter of the Art of War. He is not offering a humanitarian argument. He is offering a structural one: the siege is the form of war that fixes the besieger’s force in place, exposes his logistics, and consumes his political capital at exactly the rate the besieged’s starvation converts into an image the world reads. The successful besieger — the RSF at El-Fasher, October 2025 — wins only by accepting the cost the image imposes. The unsuccessful besieger loses everything.

The RSF has already made this calculation once and won. It is now making it again. What the reading has to attend to is which of the two calculations is more likely: that Abu Dhabi has decided the RSF may take a second capital and absorb the second image, or that the RSF is running the siege on inertia while its backers negotiate an exit that does not require the storm. The tempo of the drone strikes reads as the former.

The historical rhyme.

Srebrenica is the reading Western coverage will reach for and it is the wrong rhyme by degree. Srebrenica was a UN-declared safe area under UN protection whose fall was the failure of a stated Western commitment. El-Obeid is a Sudanese army garrison in a city with no Western commitment attached to it; its fall would be the ratification of an absence, not the failure of a presence. The closer rhyme is Aleppo, 2016 — a besieged eastern half of a divided city whose fall was foreseen for months, whose external backer (Russia) had made a political decision to sustain the pressure until the political outcome was arithmetically forced, and whose Western witnesses could describe the process in real time without altering it. Aleppo was the moment Syria’s civil war stopped being ambiguous. El-Obeid is now that moment for Sudan.

What the reading expects.

The base case is that El-Obeid holds through the summer at a rising civilian cost and falls in the autumn or the following winter, at which point the RSF’s territorial holding becomes coherent enough to force one of two political outcomes: a de facto partition ratified by the external backers under some form of federal fiction, or an SAF counter-offensive whose logistics the Egyptian and Turkish arithmetic cannot underwrite. The alternative case — a negotiated relief before the storm — requires Abu Dhabi to accept a political cost it has not yet accepted, and there is nothing on the tape to suggest the cost has changed.

The reading Ripperger keeps at the metaphysical register on the vice pattern of concentrated power — the way the tempo of an atrocity accelerates once the material calculation locks — is the register at which the humanitarian dimension of El-Obeid belongs. It is not the reason the siege will succeed or fail. It is the reason the succession will be an image that stays in the record.

“The worst policy of all is to besiege walled cities. The rule is, not to besiege walled cities if it can possibly be avoided. The general, unable to control his irritation, will launch his men to the assault like swarming ants, with the result that one-third of his soldiers are slain, while the town still remains untaken. Such are the disastrous effects of a siege.”
— Sun Tzu · The Art of War, Book III

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What’s driving this. The reading is load-bearing on two layers. Structural: El-Obeid’s geography is the pivot — the road linking Darfur to the Nile is one road, and both belligerents know which garrison controls it. Whoever holds El-Obeid holds Kordofan, and Kordofan is the connective tissue of the Sudanese landmass. The pressure to storm the city is not a policy choice, it is a geographic fact expressed at the operational register. Coordination: the war is not fought by its two Sudanese belligerents alone. It is sustained by an Emirati-Egyptian-Turkish-Saudi backer lattice whose disagreements set the tempo. The diplomatic track has failed because those disagreements have not resolved, not because the two belligerents are unreachable.

What’s not driving this. The individual character of Hemedti or Burhan is not the load-bearing register. Either commander in either position would run the same operational calculus that geography and external supply set for them. Nor is the “international community” a coherent actor here — the OHCHR alert has no instrument attached, and no external principal has committed a force capable of altering the tempo.

What would break this reading.

  • Abu Dhabi publicly withdraws material support from the RSF and a Chad-Libya corridor cargo audit is enforced by a party the RSF cannot ignore.
  • Egyptian air-defence deploys forward inside Sudan under a bilateral status-of-forces arrangement that changes the SAF’s counter-offensive arithmetic.
  • A credible ceasefire monitor deploys before the storm, and the RSF drone tempo demonstrably slows for four consecutive weeks.
  • The city falls in July or August, not the autumn, forcing the reading to concede that the RSF’s calculation on the second-image cost is already made.

Sources used. Sun Tzu on the structural cost of the siege sits under the operational reading; Mearsheimer on regional vacuum and proxy warfare underwrites the coordination reading; the Aleppo 2016 rhyme is the historical precedent the base case tracks; Ripperger on the vice pattern of concentrated power reads the humanitarian tempo without being asked to explain the siege’s success.

Economics · The Ledger of Capital
Filed Boston / San Diego / New York
The Vertex Wager · Vertex Pharmaceuticals on Monday, July 6, announced a definitive agreement to acquire Crinetics Pharmaceuticals for eighty-five dollars per share in cash — a total equity value of approximately ten billion dollars, or eight-point-eight billion net of Crinetics cash, financed with a Bank of America and Morgan Stanley bridge of four-and-a-half billion — the largest acquisition in Vertex’s history, surpassing its 2024 Alpine Immune purchase by more than a factor of two. Crinetics’ commercial asset is PALSONIFY (paltusotine), an oral once-daily therapy for acromegaly launched in late 2025 after a September FDA approval. Crinetics shares closed Monday up ninety-nine per cent; Vertex closed down four. The transaction ratifies a pattern already visible on the biotech M&A ledger: 2026 first-quarter deal value cleared sixty-five billion dollars — the strongest quarter since 2020 — and the shape of demand has decisively rotated away from the mega-merger and toward the ten-billion-dollar-and-under bolt-on. The Stratagem reads the deal not as a corporate transaction but as the moment the industry’s late-cycle capital-allocation logic became explicit: an incumbent whose cystic-fibrosis franchise faces a slowly-declining moat is paying a twenty-nine per cent premium for a rare-endocrine-disease commercial asset with orphan-drug pricing power precisely because the alternative — internal development of a new modality — will not arrive on the schedule its cash-flow window requires. The reading draws on Munger’s mental models on the moat that requires purchase because it cannot be built, Dalio’s Big Cycle on M&A concentration as the signature of a boom’s late stage, Greene’s twenty-third law on the concentration of forces, and the 2000 pharmaceutical consolidation wave as the historical rhyme.

The Vertex Wager

Ten billion dollars for an oral acromegaly drug and a pipeline of endocrine candidates. The size of the cheque and the pattern it sits inside are the story; the specific asset is the excuse.

The shape of the deal.

Vertex is not a natural buyer of endocrine assets. Its franchise is cystic fibrosis, its cash-generation engine is a moat built on Trikafta and its successors, and its recent inorganic history — the 2024 Alpine Immune purchase for four-point-four billion — was the outer bound of what its board had been willing to authorise. Ten billion dollars for a single-asset commercial biotech is a decision that changes the shape of the company; it is not a bolt-on in Vertex’s prior sense of the word.

The asset is PALSONIFY, an oral once-daily paltusotine for acromegaly, approved by the FDA in September 2025 and commercialised through late 2025 into 2026. Acromegaly is an orphan indication with structural pricing power — a small patient population, an unpleasant standard of care (monthly injectable somatostatin analogues), and a payer willingness to pay for the oral alternative. Behind PALSONIFY sit paltusotine label expansions, atumelnant in Cushing’s disease and other endocrinologies, and a lonapegsomatropin combination programme with Ascendis. The pipeline is coherent. The commercial asset is scaling. The multiple at the strike is roughly twelve times consensus 2028 sales — not cheap, not egregious.

Why now.

The Munger model that reads this cleanest is the one about moats you buy versus moats you build. Vertex’s cystic-fibrosis franchise is a purchased-and-then-defended moat — its 2001 Aurora acquisition, followed by the Millennium and Cambridge triangulation, then the disciplined internal development of the CFTR modulators. Every extension since has followed the same pattern: buy the platform, defend the moat, harvest the cash. What has changed is the horizon.

Trikafta faces a slow-declining moat rather than a cliff, but the shape of the cash-flow window is now visible. Internal endocrine development at the scale a Crinetics-comparable pipeline would require is a decade of work Vertex will not survive its window to complete. The cheque is the acknowledgement that the moat inside the window has to be bought, not built. This is Munger’s corollary to Buffett’s inversion: the incumbent who owns the compound-interest engine has to spend the cash on the asset the compound-interest engine cannot generate on its own schedule.

Dalio’s Big Cycle at the capital-allocation register.

The biotech M&A tape in 2026 is the signature of a boom’s late stage. First-quarter deal value cleared sixty-five billion. If the tempo holds through the second half, the year prints north of two hundred and fifty billion — the strongest reading since the 2020 peak. The shape of demand has rotated: the mega-merger is out (no Bristol-Celgene, no AbbVie-Allergan in the queue), and the ten-billion-and-under bolt-on is in. Every large-cap incumbent with a slowly-declining moat is playing the same game.

Dalio’s Big Cycle reading is that the M&A concentration is not the story — it is the visible surface of the story. The underlying phase is the transition inside the long-term debt cycle at which the incumbent, unable to generate the growth its cash-flow model requires organically, converts balance-sheet capacity into acquired growth. The debt component of the Vertex bridge — four-and-a-half billion at present rates — is the cycle’s tell. When rates rise as they are pricing to rise into the second half, the tempo of the M&A tape moderates. Deals struck now will look like the tail of the cycle, not the middle.

The historical rhyme.

The 2000 pharmaceutical consolidation wave — Pfizer-Warner-Lambert (2000), Glaxo-SmithKline (2000), Pfizer-Pharmacia (2003), Sanofi-Aventis (2004) — is the imperfect rhyme. The imperfect part: those were mega-mergers of near-equals whose logic was scale, not asset acquisition. The rhyming part: they were the visible signature of the same underlying pressure — incumbents whose organic pipelines could not match the growth their equity market required, converting balance-sheet capacity into acquired growth at the top of a long expansion. The 2026 wave is bolt-on-shaped instead of mega-shaped for a specific reason: FTC and DOJ pressure since 2021 has raised the transaction cost of the mega-merger to the point at which the ten-billion-dollar bolt-on has become the marginal instrument. The pattern is the same; the size of the vehicle is smaller.

What the reading expects.

The base case is that the deal closes in the third quarter on the announced timeline; that PALSONIFY’s 2028 sales print inside a window of one-and-a-half to two-and-a-half billion; that atumelnant reads out positively on the Cushing’s trial and adds a second commercial asset to Vertex’s endocrine franchise by 2028; and that the acquisition is judged accretive on Vertex’s adjusted-earnings register by 2029. The alternative case — the deal being read as the top-of-cycle over-payment — requires either a PALSONIFY label problem or a step-change in the endocrine competitive landscape (a Novo Nordisk or Lilly entry priced against the injectable comparator) that changes the payer arithmetic.

The Stratagem reads the deal as the marker of a phase, not as the individual bet its board authorised. Every incumbent with a slowly-declining moat and a functional balance sheet will now be judged against the Vertex framework: what did you buy this quarter, and did you buy inside your window. The Bristol-Myers, Amgen, Gilead boards will read the tape.

“All I want to know is where I’m going to die, so I’ll never go there. Turn the thing around all the time; invert. The critical skill is figuring out what you need to avoid. The rare-earth genius is rare; the compound-interest engine is the moat; and the moat you buy is the moat you did not have the decade to build.”
— Charles Munger · Poor Charlie’s Almanack

Europe & Germany · The Continental Ledger
Filed Berlin / Karlsruhe / Magdeburg
The Karlsruhe Question · The Bundestag will debate on Thursday, July 9, a cross-party motion submitted by one hundred and twenty-four members from the SPD, Greens, Left, and a small CDU minority requesting that the Federal Ministry of the Interior prepare a Verbotsantrag — a formal ban application — against Alternative für Deutschland to be filed at the Federal Constitutional Court in Karlsruhe. The motion’s co-sponsors expect no vote at Thursday’s session; the procedural expectation is referral to the Interior Committee ahead of the summer recess. The debate lands nine weeks before the Sachsen-Anhalt Landtag election on September 6, at which the AfD is currently polling above forty per cent on the Landeslist, and inside a Bundestag arithmetic in which Merz’s CDU-CSU-SPD coalition holds a slim majority and the CDU-CSU faction is the fraction least receptive to the ban proposition. The Stratagem reads the motion not as a policy proposal but as the moment the coalition’s two grand-strategic questions — what to do about an AfD that will win at least one state parliament in September, and how to keep the CDU-CSU faction from splitting over the answer — are being publicly named in the chamber for the first time. The reading draws on Aristotle on stasis and the constitutional response to faction, Confucius on the rectification of names in a regime whose central category has become unstable, Talleyrand on the ban proposition as a move that acquires legitimacy by not being taken, and the 2001-2003 NPD ban attempt as the historical rhyme whose failure at Karlsruhe on procedural grounds is the precedent every serious jurist in the chamber will read against.

The Karlsruhe Question

One hundred and twenty-four Bundestag members ask the Interior Ministry to prepare a party-ban application against the AfD. Nine weeks before Sachsen-Anhalt votes. The debate is not the answer; it is the register on which the question now has to be asked.

The motion, first.

The cross-bench motion submitted for Thursday’s session is not itself a ban application. It is a request that the Federal Ministry of the Interior prepare a Verbotsantrag — the formal application that would be submitted to the Second Senate of the Federal Constitutional Court at Karlsruhe under Article 21(2) of the Basic Law. Only the Bundestag, the Bundesrat, or the federal government may file such an application. The vote Thursday, if it happened, would only formalise the Bundestag’s request that the government prepare the case. The government would then decide whether to file it.

No vote is expected at Thursday’s session. The procedural expectation is referral to the Interior Committee. The co-sponsors are one hundred and twenty-four members drawn from the SPD, the Greens, Die Linke, and a small CDU minority; the CDU-CSU faction as a whole has not endorsed the motion; the coalition’s SPD wing supports it under conditions the Merz Chancellery has been careful not to make public. The debate’s purpose — and its co-sponsors understand this — is not to move the ban forward. It is to place the coalition’s two grand-strategic questions on the parliamentary record before the summer recess.

The Karlsruhe record.

The Federal Constitutional Court has been asked to ban a party twice since 1949. The KPD ban of 1956 succeeded. The NPD ban attempts of 2001-2003 and 2013-2017 both failed — the first on procedural grounds (V-Personen inside the party leadership had contaminated the evidentiary record), the second on substantive grounds (the party’s political weight was judged insufficient to threaten the constitutional order the ban was invoked to defend). The AfD case sits at neither precedent’s clean edge. The party’s political weight is not the NPD’s — it is a Bundestag opposition faction and, on current polling, the plurality party of Sachsen-Anhalt. The evidentiary record is not the NPD’s first case — the Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz has classified the AfD as a suspected extremist case (Verdachtsfall) at the federal level and as a confirmed extremist case (Erwiesen-Extremistisch) at three state chapters. The Verbotsantrag would test both prongs at once.

The Court’s doctrine after the 2017 NPD judgment installs a threshold the AfD case would have to clear: a party may only be banned if it presents a “potentially successful” challenge to the free democratic basic order. The 2017 ruling introduced the potentiality test precisely because the NPD failed the “threat” test but the Court did not want to institutionalise a “too small to ban” doctrine. The AfD, unlike the NPD, will not fail the potentiality test; whether it clears the ideological threshold — the party’s doctrine, its language, and its documented programmatic ambitions — is the substantive question.

Aristotle on stasis.

Book V of the Politics is the register on which the Bundestag debate is being asked to sit. Aristotle’s reading is that a constitution facing internal faction (stasis) has a narrow set of responses available to it, and each has its cost: broaden the constitutional coalition, split the faction, exile the faction’s leaders, or absorb the faction’s programme. The ban proposition is the exile move at its constitutional register — the removal of the faction from the space in which the regime deliberates. Aristotle’s warning is that the exile move works when the faction’s programme has not yet been legitimated by the regime’s own institutions and does not work — it accelerates stasis — when the faction’s programme has become inseparable from a widely-shared grievance.

The German answer to Aristotle’s question is not settled. The AfD’s programme on migration, the internal frontier of the Schengen area, and the German economic model has become inseparable from grievances at least a plurality of Sachsen-Anhalt voters share. The ban proposition’s cost is that its filing without a probable success at Karlsruhe converts the AfD into the regime’s martyr and legitimates the very programme it is designed to remove. The 2001-2003 NPD attempt is the empirical precedent for this cost.

Talleyrand’s discipline.

Talleyrand’s dictum — that a diplomatic instrument acquires its power by the credible readiness to use it and not by its use — is the register on which the co-sponsors of the motion are operating. They understand the ban application will not be filed before the Sachsen-Anhalt election and probably not before the next federal election. Its power is in the visible readiness of one hundred and twenty-four Bundestag members to place its architecture on the record. That readiness serves two functions the ban application itself would not serve: it disciplines the CDU-CSU faction (the ban proposition’s public existence raises the internal cost of the CDU-CSU signalling co-operation with the AfD at the state level), and it disciplines the AfD leadership (the party’s more moderate wing has to weigh the cost of the doctrine that would supply the evidentiary record against the political value of the doctrine on the campaign trail).

What the reading expects.

From the Classics

Aristotle & Sun Tzu.

Both oligarchies and democracies are overthrown either by faction inside them or by outside interference. The internal faction is worse for the constitution, for the men outside can only produce revolution by ill-treatment of the ruling class; but the men inside can produce it by taking advantage of grievance and disorder among the citizens. The response of the constitution to internal faction has a narrow set of forms: broaden the coalition to absorb the grievance, split the faction from its leadership, exile the faction’s leaders from the space in which the regime deliberates, or absorb the faction’s programme into the regime’s own settlement. Each has its cost. The move to exile fails and accelerates the stasis when the faction’s programme has already been legitimated by the electorate the exile would need to convince.

The worst policy of all is to besiege walled cities. The rule is, not to besiege walled cities if it can possibly be avoided.
