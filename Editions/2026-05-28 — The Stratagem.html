The Litani Line · On the Eve of the May 29 Pentagon Military-Coordination Meeting Between Lebanese, Israeli, and American Officers, and Ahead of the Fourth Round of Direct US-Mediated Talks Set for June 2-3 in Washington, Israel and Lebanon Negotiate Face to Face for the First Time Since the 1983 May 17 Agreement Collapsed Within a Year — the Agenda the Hard Machinery of an Ending: an Israeli Withdrawal From the South, the Lebanese Army’s Takeover of the Ground Below the Litani River, Western Financing for That Army, the Disarmament of Hezbollah Consistent With UN Security Council Resolution 1701, and the Enforcement Mechanism Every Prior Ceasefire Has Lacked — the Three Rounds Already Held Producing What the State Department Calls “Productive and Positive” Talks Against a Backdrop of Continued Israeli Strikes North of the Buffer Zone, Hezbollah’s Vowed Refusal to Surrender Its Arms, and a Beirut Government Under President Joseph Aoun That Has Staked Its Authority on Reclaiming the State’s Monopoly of Force

The Litani Line

For the first time since 1983, an Israeli delegation and a Lebanese one are sitting at the same table. Three rounds of direct, US-mediated talks have already run in Washington; a military-coordination meeting between Lebanese, Israeli, and American officers convenes at the Pentagon on May 29, with a fourth political round set for June 2-3. The agenda is the hard machinery of an ending — an Israeli withdrawal from the south, the Lebanese army’s takeover of the ground below the Litani, Western money to pay for that army, the disarmament of Hezbollah, and the one piece every prior ceasefire has lacked: an enforcement mechanism. The Stratagem reads the table not as a peace but as a transfer — of the monopoly of force in southern Lebanon, from a militia to a state, under an American guarantee. The question the table cannot say aloud is who still holds the veto, and whether Tehran’s post-war contraction has loosened its grip enough that the veto can be bought out, fenced off, or simply outlasted.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Washington & Beirut·28 May 2026

Washington —The striking thing about the Lebanese-Israeli table is that it exists at all. These two states have been formally at war since 1948. The last time their officials negotiated a withdrawal-for-normalization deal — the May 17 Agreement of 1983, brokered by an American secretary of state — it survived ten months before Beirut abrogated it under Syrian pressure. The talks now under way are narrower and, for that reason, more serious. Nobody at the Pentagon on Friday will be drafting embassies. They will be drawing a line on a map: the Litani, the river some thirty kilometres north of the border, below which the Lebanese Armed Forces are meant to stand alone, and above which Hezbollah’s arms remain the unsolved problem.

Start with the structural reading, because it sets the field everything else moves inside. Mearsheimer’s offensive realism describes a system with no enforcer, in which the strong do what their power permits and bind themselves as little as they can. Israel emerges from the Iran war as the overwhelmingly dominant party, and a dominant party does not trade its dominance for a treaty. This is why Israel’s stated position — that it must retain freedom of operations in Lebanon until the Hezbollah question is settled — is not a bargaining flourish but the whole Israeli theory of the deal. It wants the result of peace — a quiet border, a disarmed adversary — without the constraint of peace, the commitment that would limit its own future action. The Lebanese state wants the mirror image: the constraint without yet being able to deliver the result.

“The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting.” Sun Tzu names what the United States is actually attempting. Having helped break Hezbollah on the battlefield, Washington now wants the result formalized by a Lebanese institution rather than held in place by Israeli firepower — the monopoly of force transferred at the table, not the front. The instrument of that transfer is money: a Lebanese army paid, trained, and equipped by the United States and the Gulf, embedded so deeply in the southern file that its sponsors become indispensable to Lebanon’s own security.

The 1983 ghost.

The May 17 Agreement is the control case, and its lesson is exact. It failed not because the visible belligerents could not agree but because the actor holding the real veto — Damascus, through its Lebanese clients and its army in the Bekaa — was never a party to it. A peace among the parties in the room collapses when the party outside the room can still pull the floor out from under it. Today the veto does not sit in Damascus; Syria is a spent force. It sits in Hezbollah’s remaining arsenal, and behind that, in an Iran whose regional reach has contracted sharply since the Hormuz war. The entire wager of the current table is that the 1983 condition has relaxed — that the external veto is weaker now than it was then. If the wager is right, a durable arrangement is reachable. If it is wrong, Washington is building another May 17, and discovering the fact a year too late.

Greene’s eleventh law — keep people dependent on you — reads the American design from the inside. The prize Washington plays for is not Lebanese gratitude or even a signed document; it is the indispensability that accrues to whoever ends the conflict and underwrites the order afterward. The worldview’s own rule is blunter: power accrues to whoever can stop the fight. The settler, not the belligerent, inherits the order. A Lebanese army that depends on American and Gulf financing for its pay and its rifles is an army whose strategic alignment is fixed in Washington for a generation — and dependence accepted as one’s own choice is the most durable kind, because Beirut will defend the arrangement as sovereignty restored rather than sovereignty leased.

What the line tests.

Hezbollah’s move is the oldest one in the catalogue: Greene’s twenty-second law, surrender to recover. Concede the ground below the Litani, let the Lebanese army deploy into the south, accept the optics of withdrawal — and keep the arms north of the river, where they remain both deterrent and bargaining chip. Full disarmament is strategic suicide for a movement whose entire claim to relevance is its weapons; it will not happen on anyone’s calendar, least of all an American one. So watch the gradations, not the headline. The May 29 Pentagon meeting tests whether “productive and positive” can be converted into a written security framework with named obligations. The June 2-3 round tests whether Israel will trade any of its freedom of operations for a Lebanese commitment it can verify. And the financing tests everything: until the United States and the Gulf actually fund the Lebanese army at the scale required to hold the south, the transfer of the monopoly of force is a communiqué, not a fact. The line on the map is easy to draw. It holds only if someone pays for the soldiers who have to stand on it.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Two layers do the work. The structural one is the war’s outcome: Israel won, Iran contracted, and a dominant power that has just won prefers freedom of action to a binding peace — so the deal on offer is a security arrangement, not a reconciliation. The mechanism is the transfer of the monopoly of force in southern Lebanon from Hezbollah to a Lebanese army that only exists at the needed scale if Washington and the Gulf pay for it. The second layer is the worldview’s rule that power accrues to whoever can stop the fight: the United States is converting its broker role into a permanent guarantor seat, and a US-funded Lebanese army is dependence accepted as sovereignty — the most durable kind, because Beirut will defend it as its own.

What's not driving this: Goodwill. There has been no thaw of sentiment between Jerusalem and Beirut; reading the table as reconciliation misreads it entirely. The talks exist because the war broke Hezbollah and shrank Iran, not because anyone’s feelings changed — which is why the durability question turns on Tehran’s residual grip, not on the warmth of the room.

What would break this reading:

  • Israel accepts a binding withdrawal-for-peace treaty with hard, verifiable limits on its own freedom of operations — the “strong power prefers latitude to commitment” reading was wrong, and Israel valued a settled border over a free hand more than the article credited.
  • Hezbollah disarms substantially north of the Litani before year-end without a fight — the “arms are the veto, surrender-to-recover only” reading was wrong, and Iran’s grip had loosened far more than the article assumed.
  • The talks collapse and Israel resumes a full ground campaign in Lebanon — the “transfer of the monopoly of force under US guarantee” reading was wrong; coercion, not settlement, is the operative mode and the table was theatre.
  • A non-US broker — France, Saudi Arabia, Egypt — delivers and underwrites the enforcement mechanism — the “US as indispensable settler” reading was wrong, and the guarantor seat was contestable.

Sources used: Mearsheimer’s offensive realism sits under the whole reading — an anarchic system with no enforcer, in which the dominant party binds itself as little as possible, explains Israel’s insistence on freedom of operations better than any account of intentions. The worldview’s power claims carry the American side: power accrues to whoever can stop the fight (the settler inherits the order), and voluntary dependence is the most durable form (the LAF on Washington’s payroll). Sun Tzu — subdue the enemy without fighting — and Greene’s twenty-second law, surrender to recover, read Hezbollah’s likely play: concede the south, keep the arms. The 1983 May 17 Agreement is the control case: a deal that excluded the real veto-holder collapsed within a year.

The Sovereign Bid · With the War Premium Draining Out of Bullion as the US-Iran Talks Stay Deadlocked Over Tehran’s Demand to Keep Control of the Strait of Hormuz, Spot Gold Has Eased to Roughly $4,600 an Ounce After Nearly Doubling in the Year to January 2026 — Yet the Bid Beneath It Has Not Broken: the HSBC 2026 Reserve Manager Survey Finds Nearly Forty Percent of Central-Bank Reserve Managers Intending to Add Gold This Year, the Majority Stating That Record Prices Are No Deterrent, First-Quarter Official-Sector Net Purchases Running Near 244 Tonnes, Turkey the Conspicuous Seller as It Sheds 131 Tonnes Through Swaps and Sales to Defend the Lira — the Whole Pattern a Reserve-Diversification Cycle Away From Dollar-Denominated Assets Held in Western Custody, the Risk Calculation Altered Since the February 2022 Freeze of Russia’s Central-Bank Reserves, the Dollar’s Exorbitant Privilege Priced for the First Time in Two Generations Against a Neutral Asset No Issuer Can Switch Off

The Sovereign Bid

Spot gold has eased to about $4,600 an ounce, down from a high above $5,400, as the war premium drains out of the market on hopes that a US-Iran deal eventually reopens the Strait of Hormuz. The price is the least interesting thing about it. Beneath the pullback sits a bid that does not flinch at record levels: the HSBC 2026 Reserve Manager Survey finds nearly forty percent of central banks intending to add gold this year, most of them saying the price is no deterrent, with first-quarter official-sector purchases near 244 tonnes. The Stratagem reads the metal not as an inflation trade or a fear trade but as a dollar trade — the slow, multipolar repricing of the exorbitant privilege by the creditors who learned in 2022 that reserves held in Western custody can be switched off. Gold is the one reserve asset no one else can freeze. That, not the headline tick, is what the sovereigns are buying.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed London & New York·28 May 2026

London —Gold pays no coupon, earns no yield, and costs money to store. By every textbook measure it is an inferior reserve asset to a US Treasury bill — which is precisely why the question worth asking is not why gold fell this month but why central banks keep buying it at prices that have doubled. The answer is not in the gold market. It is in what the dollar became after February 2022, when the United States and its allies froze roughly three hundred billion dollars of Russian central-bank reserves with a keystroke. In that moment the world’s reserve managers learned something they could not unlearn: that the assets they hold for safety are safe only so long as the issuer permits. Gold is the asset that requires no one’s permission.

Dalio’s big cycle is the load-bearing frame. Reserve-currency status, in his reading, runs on a clock measured in centuries, and its late stage carries a signature: the incumbent issuer borrows more than its creditors wish to hold, the privilege of printing the world’s money is overused, and the holders of that money begin, quietly, to diversify out of it. They do not dump it — a run would destroy the value of what they still hold — so they trim at the margin and accumulate the neutral alternative. That is exactly the official-sector pattern: the dollar share of global reserves grinding lower year by year while gold tonnage grinds higher. The metal is not competing with the dollar on yield. It is competing on the single attribute the dollar has spent — the credibility that it will never be turned against its holder.

“Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome.” Munger’s rule explains why the price objection misses the point. On the rational track, buying gold at $4,600 after a double looks like chasing a crowded trade. On the second track — the one Munger insists you analyse beside the first — the reserve manager is not solving for return at all. The lever is security: the avoidance of a tail in which the next set of frozen reserves is your own. When the dominant motive is insurance against confiscation, the entry price is close to irrelevant — which is exactly what the survey respondents mean when they report that record levels are no deterrent.

The exorbitant privilege.

The phrase belonged to de Gaulle’s finance minister, in the 1960s, for the unique advantage the United States enjoyed in issuing the currency the world was obliged to hold. De Gaulle’s answer was to convert France’s dollars into American gold and ship it home — a one-country protest that helped force Nixon to close the gold window in 1971. What is happening now is the same protest, but multipolar and quiet, and with no window left to close because the metal is already dispersed across dozens of national vaults. The worldview’s reading of dependence sharpens the point. The dollar order was the most durable kind of power precisely because it was voluntary — states held dollars because it served them, and defended the arrangement as their own choice. The 2022 freeze converted that voluntary dependence into something closer to coerced dependence, and coerced dependence is the unstable kind: it lasts only until the subject finds an exit. Gold is the exit, and the official sector is walking through it at a measured pace.

Read the exception, because it disciplines the rule. Turkey has been the year’s most conspicuous gold seller, shedding some 131 tonnes in March to defend the lira — not a vote against gold but a demonstration of what gold is for. A central bank under acute currency stress reaches for the asset it can sell to anyone, instantly, without anyone’s leave; that is the same property the buyers are accumulating, used from the other side. So the structural reading does not need gold to keep rising. It needs the official-sector bid to persist through price weakness, and the dollar share of reserves to keep drifting down.

What the bid tests.

That makes the tells unglamorous. The price of gold is a fear gauge and it will swing with the war headlines; the central-bank bid underneath it is a confidence gauge — specifically, the world’s declining confidence that the issuer of the reserve currency will keep its hands off. What would break the reading: a genuine US-Iran settlement that reopens Hormuz, pulls the geopolitical premium out, and is met by central banks selling rather than holding — that would say the bid was a war trade, not a structural one. A stabilising or rising dollar share in the IMF reserve data would say the same. A sharp rotation back into Treasuries as real yields climb, with gold falling hard, would say the security lever was dominated by the return lever after all. Absent those, watch the quarterly tonnage, not the screen. “Gold is money,” J. P. Morgan told Congress in 1912. “Everything else is credit.” The sovereigns are re-learning the distinction one tonne at a time, and the lesson they are paying for is not about gold. It is about the credit.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Two layers carry it. The structural one is Dalio’s reserve-currency cycle in its late stage: an incumbent issuer that borrows more than its creditors wish to hold, and creditors who respond by trimming the dollar at the margin and accumulating a neutral alternative. The driver is custody and confiscation risk, which is a question about the issuer’s restraint, not about the price level — and that is precisely why central banks, the actors most exposed to that risk, are the marginal structural buyer rather than households.

  • A genuine US-Iran settlement reopens the Strait of Hormuz, the geopolitical premium unwinds, and central banks sell into the relief rather than hold — the “structural, not a war trade” reading was wrong, and the official bid was a fear position all along.

  • Official-sector net purchases turn negative for two consecutive quarters — the “price-insensitive structural buyer” reading was wrong, and the security motive bent to the price after all.
  • A sharp rotation back into Treasuries as real yields climb, with gold falling hard — the return lever dominated the security lever, and the confiscation tail was priced as smaller than the article assumed.

“The dollar’s power was never the printing press; it was the promise. The Associate Seat

On May 21 Friedrich Merz put his name to a proposal he had floated at the Cyprus leaders’ retreat: an “associate membership” of the European Union for Ukraine, and after it Moldova and the Western Balkans. The status would seat Ukraine in the European Council, the Commission, and the Parliament — with no vote and no portfolio — and attach a mutual-assistance clause Merz describes as a substantial security guarantee. It is pitched as the way past a two-year deadlock and the unanimity veto that froze it, made newly possible by the departure of Hungary’s Orbán. Kyiv’s answer was cool: Zelensky has rejected “symbolic” membership and wants the real thing. The Stratagem reads the offer not as half a loaf but as a precise instrument — a way to bind Ukraine to Europe while withholding the one attribute, the vote, that would let Ukraine bind Europe back.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Brussels·28 May 2026

Berlin —European enlargement has always run into the same wall: the Union deepens or it widens, and doing both at once strains the machinery that makes it a union at all. Admit a country the size of Ukraine — a vast agricultural economy, a war on its soil, a population that would command a large bloc of votes and a heavy claim on the budget — and every existing member’s voting weight, farm subsidy, and net contribution shifts against it. That is before the procedural problem: accession requires unanimity, and for two years one man in Budapest supplied the veto. Merz’s associate membership is engineered around all of it. It offers Ukraine the chamber without the ballot, presence without the power to decide — and in doing so it converts the hardest question in European politics into a softer one the existing members can actually say yes to.

The structural reading is that enlargement-versus-deepening dilemma, and the move that unlocked the debate is the change in Budapest. Orbán was the reliable veto; his departure, and his successor Péter Magyar’s signal that Hungary is ready to let the first negotiating cluster open, removed the procedural block that had made the whole argument academic. But lifting the veto does not resolve the substantive cost of full membership — it only makes the question live. Associate status is the answer to the cost: it lets the Union act on the strategic imperative of pulling Ukraine westward without paying the budgetary and institutional price of a full seat. The E6 finance ministers — Germany, France, Italy, the Netherlands, Poland, Spain — pushing in parallel on defence and supply chains are the coordination signature: a core composing the moves, the rest invited to follow.

“If names be not correct, language is not in accordance with the truth of things.” Confucius’s rule on the rectification of names is the sharpest lens on what associate membership is. Membership, in the Union, has one defining attribute that distinguishes it from partnership, association, or candidacy: the vote — the seat at the table where binding decisions are made. A membership that withholds the vote is a name severed from the thing it names. The European Economic Area already runs on that severance: Norway and Iceland take the Union’s rules and pay into its budget without a vote on either. Merz is proposing the same architecture under a warmer word, and the warmth of the word is the point — it lets Kyiv claim membership and lets the capitals withhold it, in the same breath.

A seat without a vote.

Greene’s eleventh law — keep people dependent on you — reads the design from the European side, and the worldview’s rule on dependence completes it: the most durable power is the dependence the subject accepts as its own choice. Associate membership offers Ukraine the room, the briefings, the appearance of belonging and the felt status of having arrived — while the lever that would let Kyiv shape the Union’s decisions, or block them, stays in the existing members’ hands. Ukraine would be inside the tent and outside the decision, bound to harmonise its law, its market, and its security with a Union it cannot outvote. That is why Zelensky is cool to it. He can see that an antechamber, however well appointed, is still an antechamber, and that a country with a seat but no vote is a country whose alignment is fixed elsewhere.

The antechamber.

The tell is the security clause. What Ukraine needs above any parliamentary seat is a guarantee that an attack will be answered — an Article 5 in all but name. Mearsheimer’s realism says this is exactly what great powers are most reluctant to grant, because it commits them to a war not of their choosing; so Merz offers a “substantial security guarantee” through a mutual-assistance clause that sounds like the thing without quite being it. Talleyrand would recognise the craft: the whole art is in the gradation, in offering a form precise enough to satisfy and vague enough to bind no one to the worst case. The danger, for Kyiv, is Ankara’s — the antechamber that becomes permanent, the candidacy that never converts, the associate status that hardens into a ceiling rather than a step. So watch whether the other capitals actually back the proposal or merely tolerate it; whether Magyar’s Hungary lets the first accession cluster formally open; whether the security clause acquires teeth or stays a phrase; and whether Zelensky can trade his insistence on full membership for a guarantee real enough to be worth the wait. “Interdependence remains the law,” Greene writes against his own eleventh, “independence the exception.” The question Merz has put to Kyiv is which side of that law a seat without a vote leaves it on.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: The structural layer is the enlargement-versus-deepening dilemma policed by the unanimity veto. Full Ukrainian membership shifts every member’s voting weight, farm subsidy, and net contribution, and for two years Orbán’s veto made the question moot; his exit makes it live without making it cheap. Associate membership is the tactical instrument that resolves the cost — presence without the vote, the strategic pull westward without the budgetary and institutional price. The worldview’s rule on dependence is the second driver: the most durable power is the dependence accepted as one’s own choice, and a seat without a vote institutionalises Ukraine’s alignment as if it were Ukraine’s achievement. The E6 finance bloc moving ahead of the twenty-seven is the coordination signature of a core composing the terms.

What's not driving this: Ukraine’s reform progress. “Merit-based” and the accession benchmarks are the public language, but the binding constraint is the cost of a full vote and a hard security commitment, not whether Kyiv has closed accession chapters — which is why the offer is a status tier, not an accelerated timeline on the existing track.

What would break this reading:

  • The EU opens full accession negotiations on a genuine fast track and treats associate status as a way-station rather than a destination — the “ceiling, not a step” reading was wrong, and the members were willing to pay the full-membership price after all.
  • Ukraine accepts the status and the mutual-assistance clause acquires hard, Article-5-equivalent teeth — the “guarantee without substance” reading was wrong, and Europe was prepared to defend what it admitted.
  • A member other than Hungary — France on farm spending, Poland on grain — emerges as the new veto — the “Orbán’s exit unlocked it” reading was wrong, and the block was substantive rather than personal.
  • Zelensky’s rejection forces Merz to drop the associate concept entirely — the “core composes the moves, the rest follow” reading overstated Berlin’s lead, and the applicant held more leverage than the article credited.

Law of the Day · No. 11

Keep people dependent on you.

Greene’s eleventh law is the cleanest thread through Thursday’s three stories. To make yourself indispensable, entwine the fate of those you serve with yours so they cannot do without you. The worldview sharpens it: the most durable power is not the power that coerces but the power the subject accepts as its own choice. Voluntary dependence outlasts coerced dependence, because the dependent defends the arrangement as freedom. And Greene himself supplies the warning that completes the law — interdependence remains the rule, independence the exception; the patron who makes others depend on him is, in some measure, dependent on them.

Today’s reading: the line, the bid, the seat. In Beirut, the United States offers to settle the war and underwrite the peace, and a Lebanese army on American and Gulf payroll is dependence accepted as restored sovereignty — the settler inheriting the order. In Brussels, Merz offers Ukraine a seat without a vote: inclusion that binds Kyiv to a Union it cannot bind back, dependence in the language of membership.

The Practitioner’s Note

The most durable leash is the one held as freedom.

Each of Thursday’s stories turns on the same instrument: one party makes another depend on it, and the dependence holds best when the dependent calls it something else. Washington wants to end the Lebanon war and pay for the army that keeps the peace, because the settler who restores order inherits the order — and a state whose soldiers are funded abroad is aligned abroad, while it tells itself it has won back its sovereignty. The dollar ran the same play on the whole world for eighty years, voluntary and therefore unbreakable, until it spent the neutrality that made it so; now the central banks accumulate the metal that answers to no one, which is the tell that the leash was felt. Merz offers Kyiv the warm word, “membership,” minus the cold attribute, the vote — the room without the lever, belonging without power.

In your own affairs: the lesson cuts both ways. When you are the patron, the durable hold is the one the other side defends as its own choice — provide value no one else can, and let the dependence feel like freedom. When you are the dependent, read the name against the substance: count the votes you actually hold, not the seats you are offered, and notice when inclusion arrives stripped of the attribute that would make it power.

The hard question: in your own arrangements, where have you accepted a seat without a vote — a title, an alliance, a paycheck — and mistaken presence for power? And on the other side: whose dependence on you would survive the day they discovered it was a dependence? Greene’s eleventh law is the discipline — make yourself indispensable, but remember that the leash runs both ways, and that independence is the exception you must engineer on purpose.
