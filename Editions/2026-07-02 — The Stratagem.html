The Kapotnya Ration

Fifty-five of Russia’s eighty-three federal subjects have introduced mandatory or private-cohort rationing on retail gasoline and diesel by the close of June; refining throughput is running about a quarter below the June 2025 baseline after more than two dozen Ukrainian drone strikes since March have hit eight of the country’s ten largest refineries. From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Kyiv & Moscow·02 July 2026

Kyiv —The useful thing to notice about the Russian fuel numbers is not the throughput. It is the queue. Russia is the world’s third-largest producer of crude oil and the second-largest exporter. Its own population has never, inside living memory, stood in a line to buy gasoline. Fifty-five of the country’s eighty-three federal subjects are now under either state-mandated or private-cohort rationing at the retail pump; drivers in Moscow are being metered at 60, 100, or 150 litres per fill; drivers in Kursk are being metered at 20; Crimea has at points sold no gasoline at all. The commodity that Russia has always sold to the world it is now importing from Belarus and Kazakhstan by rail. The queue is the story.

Begin with Clausewitz. Every attack that goes past its culminating point converts into a defence. The Russian war economy has organised itself for four years around a single premise: that its refining and pipeline geography constitutes a permanent structural depth the Ukrainian side cannot reach. That premise held while Ukrainian strike ranges were measured in the low hundreds of kilometres and Ukrainian drone stockpiles were measured in the low thousands per year. Both variables have compressed. The June cadence of Ukrainian strikes on refineries has hit the Kapotnya complex twice, Ufa twice, Ryazan, Volgograd, Samara, Nizhny Novgorod, and Krasnodar; the Meduza catalogue by June 29 counted only two of Russia’s top ten refineries as untouched. When your enemy can reach every refinery in your European core with a repeatable drone class, your structural depth is no longer where you left it. The Kremlin culminating point on refining is not a future risk. It has been crossed.

Then read Sun Tzu. Attack what the enemy must defend. A refinery is not a battlefield asset the way a tank column or an air-defence battery is. It is a hybrid asset: a war-economy input, a fiscal instrument (fuel duties fund the war), and a civilian-political guarantor (subsidised retail gasoline is the visible sign of a paternal state). Ukraine did not begin the drone campaign in 2022 because it was not yet in range. It scaled the campaign in 2024 because it was starting to be. It escalated in 2026 — twenty-plus strikes since March, eight of the top-ten hit, a quarter of throughput out — because the political-economy assessment inside the General Staff had shifted from “refineries as economic target” to “refineries as political-cohort target.” A city that sells rationed gasoline is not the city Vladimir Putin has spent twenty-six years building a mandate to represent. The strike is the medium; the queue is the message.

What the Ration installs.

Zeihan’s discipline — the specific hardware inside which a resource curse becomes a resource vulnerability — reads the geography at its structural register. Russian oil is not delivered inside Russia in the same way it is delivered to a European or Chinese buyer. Crude moves to a small number of large refineries in the European core; refined product moves back out by rail and truck to a distribution system that has never budgeted redundancy against a hostile air campaign. There is no strategic gasoline reserve at scale. There is no rapid new-refinery build cycle; Russian refineries are Soviet-era assets sustained by Western catalytic-cracker technology that sanctions have already begun to compress. When Kapotnya loses its hydrocracker for the second half of 2026, no other Russian complex has the throughput to backfill Moscow at the price the Moscow retail cohort has been paying. This is precisely why Belarus and Kazakhstan have been asked to send tankers — the request is the admission. The resource superpower has become dependent on its client states for the resource its identity was built on.

Dalio’s late-war regime is the corollary. In the phase in which the elite cohort has been asked to price a war on strategic terms and the domestic cohort has been asked to price it on television and grocery terms, the political durability of a regime rests on the two prices agreeing. They stop agreeing when a domestic scarcity begins to price the war differently than the elite. The 2026 Russian gasoline queue is not yet the 1991 bread line, and the 2026 Russian labour market — unemployment near record lows, wages compressed by conscription-cohort shortage — is not yet the 1990 collapse. But the mechanism is the same: an input the state has always guaranteed is being visibly withdrawn, and every driver at every capped pump is being invited to notice. La Rochefoucauld would read the noticing as the tell: the state that has to insist on a promise is the state that has begun to lose the promise.

What the calendar tests.

Three variables across the next ninety days will reveal whether the Ration holds as the strategic threshold Ukraine has intended. First, the winter-heating cycle: Russian refineries produce not only gasoline and diesel but also the mazut and fuel-oil substrate on which municipal heating networks in the Russian European core depend. If the Kapotnya-and-Ryazan-and-Nizhny compression carries into the October cycle without a Belarusian-and-Kazakh substitution architecture that Belarus and Kazakhstan can politically sustain, the ration crosses from the retail pump into the apartment block, and the domestic-cohort question becomes a domestic-cohort answer. Second, the OPEC-plus-cohort discipline: if the Saudi-Emirati-Kazakh axis holds the September and December OPEC+ meetings on the standing production-schedule cohort against Russian requests for compensation via a lifted export share — Emirati exit from the cartel in May has already reshaped that room — the Russian fiscal-cohort compression tightens rather than loosens. Third, the Ukrainian strike-and-stock cycle: if the Ukrainian General Staff sustains the twenty-plus-strikes-a-month cadence into August and September without Russian air-defence prioritisation forcing a stock-compression cycle inside Kyiv, the strategic threshold is a two-quarter escalation, not a June anomaly. Sun Tzu on the ground on which the enemy must fight cuts at the reading: the Kremlin has been fighting inside Ukraine for four years and inside the standing energy-cohort narrative for twenty-six. It is now being asked to fight, for the first time, inside the Moscow driver’s petrol queue. The battle it did not intend to accept is the battle it will now have to.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What’s driving this: The structural and tactical layers are load-bearing. The structural fact is the geography of Russian refining: a small number of very large, mostly Soviet-era complexes, concentrated in the European core, with no strategic gasoline reserve and no rapid-build cycle because Western catalytic-cracker technology sits inside the sanctions perimeter. That is the hardware. The tactical fact is that Ukrainian long-range drones have compressed the strike radius past every plant in that hardware inventory: by late June 2026, eight of the ten largest refineries had been hit and only two of the top-ten remained untouched. Layer one on layer two: a war-economy input that Moscow has treated as structural depth has been converted into a rationable retail commodity. The queue at the pump is the visible sign of a hardware topology finally meeting a strike topology built to reach it.

What’s not driving this: A reading that treats this as an economic-sanctions story understates the mechanism. Sanctions have been in place since 2022 and have not produced the queue; the strike campaign has. Nor is this primarily a battlefield-attrition story — Russia has more crude than it can move — it is a refined-product-and-distribution story in which the political-cohort visibility of the queue is the load-bearing variable, not the litre count.

What would break this reading:

  • The Belarus-and-Kazakhstan tanker-rail substitution stabilises retail supply inside Moscow, Krasnodar, and Volgograd within sixty days and the rationing schedules quietly unwind — the “client-state dependency admission” reading was overstated; Russia converts the emergency into normal client-state logistics without a durable political-cohort cost.
  • Ukrainian drone strike cadence compresses below ten refinery strikes per month across July-August under Russian air-defence prioritisation — the “strategic threshold rather than June anomaly” reading was wrong; June was a peak the Ukrainian stock-and-launch cohort cannot sustain.
  • Putin’s September Direct Line to the nation acknowledges the rationing as a wartime discipline the population is asked to accept and polling shows the domestic-cohort absorbs the framing without measurable dissent — the “domestic scarcity begins to price the war differently” reading was wrong; the regime has more political runway on this file than the queue suggests.
  • The winter mazut-and-heating-fuel cycle passes without a discrete municipal-heating disruption in a major European-Russian city — the “retail pump crosses into the apartment block” reading was wrong; the ration stays inside the transport-fuel cohort and the political-cohort insulation holds through Q1 2027.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Fremont & Shenzhen·02 July 2026

Fremont —The useful way to read this afternoon’s Tesla print is not against Tesla’s own history. It is against what BYD published on Wednesday morning. Two firms have organised the global battery-electric passenger-vehicle industry between them; both are inside the same technological window; both are exposed to the same demand cycle. In Q2 2026 one of them delivered 557,090 units and the other is expected to deliver 397,000. The delta is 160,000 units, or 40 per cent of Tesla’s entire quarter. And the delta’s trend is not compressing. The interesting question is what a market that has priced Tesla at 175 times earnings has been pricing.

Begin with Munger. Scale, integration, and the compounding cost curve are the three advantages that decide industries. The story of BYD is the story of the three advantages arriving at the same firm at the same time: a battery cohort (through the wholly-owned FinDreams subsidiary that supplies most of BYD’s own cells and increasingly Tesla’s), a chip cohort (in-house IGBT and silicon-carbide power modules), a vehicle-integration cohort (single-firm control from wafer to delivered car), and a distribution cohort (a Chinese domestic market that will absorb 12 million BEVs and PHEVs in 2026 and a European market that has begun to take BYD faster than any legacy import cohort has ever done). Each cohort is a Munger scale advantage; the four together compound. Tesla built one of the four (integration) and rented the other three (batteries from CATL and LG, power semiconductors from Onsemi and STMicro, distribution through direct sales at scale that has now matured). The advantage that decides the industry across the second half of the decade is the advantage BYD has assembled in-house and Tesla has not. That is the structural fact this afternoon’s print will be measured against.

Then read Sun Tzu. You cannot compel the enemy’s approach when his supply lines are secure and his ground is prepared. Tesla’s European campaign has met a BYD that has built its own ro-ro shipping fleet, opened Hungarian, Turkish, and Brazilian assembly, and cut a distribution deal with Stellantis dealer networks across Southern Europe. Its Chinese campaign has met a BYD that outsells it by roughly ten to one inside the domestic passenger-cohort and that has captured the Tier-2-and-3 urban cohort Tesla’s Shanghai gigafactory was designed for. Its North American campaign is being run against a Cybertruck cycle that has compressed to below 5,000 units per quarter and a Model 2 cycle that has been delayed for the third time. The ground on which Tesla is fighting is ground BYD prepared. Elon Musk cannot compel BYD’s approach; BYD does not have to come to Tesla’s ground because Tesla’s ground is already inside BYD’s cost envelope. The moves Tesla has left are the moves a firm makes when ground is no longer a variable it controls: raise the multiple on a story the cash flow will not underwrite, or find a new product-cohort the current one cannot deliver. Both have been attempted. Neither has printed.

What the Verdict installs.

La Rochefoucauld’s observation that the apparent strength of the strong is often the public form of a dependence they cannot publicly acknowledge reads the Tesla multiple at its diagnostic register. Tesla trades at roughly 175 times trailing earnings against BYD’s roughly 21. The gap is not operational strength; it is a robotaxi option, a Full-Self-Driving option, an Optimus humanoid-robot option, and a xAI-inside-Tesla option, priced against a probability the operational cohort has not delivered. Each of the four options is defensible individually; the multiple only makes sense if all four print. This is the register in which Confucius on the rectification of names cuts hardest: the name “technology company” has been carrying a valuation the auto business could not carry, and the auto business is what is delivering the cash. When the name and the practice diverge, the name is the variable that has to move — the market does that quietly, over a period of quarters, and then loudly, in a single week. Tesla’s Q2 2026 print may not be the loud week. It is inside the sequence of quiet quarters that precede it.

Dalio’s late-liquidity regime is the corollary at the market register. In a phase in which passive index flows underwrite the seven names that carry the print, and in which the seven-name concentration has priced each of those seven at a multiple that requires narrative confirmation the fundamentals will not deliver, the arithmetic breaks first at the name whose narrative-to-cash gap is widest. Nvidia’s narrative is being underwritten by hyperscaler capex commitments that are visible and dated; Microsoft’s by Copilot enterprise contracts that are visible and dated; Meta’s by ad-price elasticity that is visible and dated. Tesla’s is being underwritten by a robotaxi date that has been visibly moved, an Optimus date that is not visible, an FSD adoption number the company has not disclosed, and a Cybercab pilot the operational-cohort has not launched. Inside a passive-flow regime, the name with the widest narrative-to-cash gap is the name at which the cohort re-prices first when the re-pricing arrives. Nothing about that is a prediction of a specific week. It is a description of the ordering when the ordering begins.

Brussels —The useful way to read the twenty-first-package delay is not as a Bulgarian or Slovak file. It is as a mechanism file. Since March 2022 the European Union has passed twenty successive Russia sanctions packages by unanimous vote of twenty-seven member states, an instrument no comparable international regime has sustained at that cadence and that duration. The instrument is the single most visible sign of a European foreign-policy machinery that can act in coordinated pressure. It is also, structurally, a unanimity machinery, which means it is only ever as strong as the veto-holder most willing to spend the veto. Twenty rounds is enough data to notice a pattern. The Impasse is what happens when the pattern is finally publicly named.

Begin with Aristotle. The constitution is what the constitution practises. On paper, the EU’s Common Foreign and Security Policy under Article 24 TEU requires unanimity for restrictive measures. In practice, the unanimity requirement has been managed for twenty rounds through a set of informal instruments: side-payments through the Multiannual Financial Framework, discretionary compensation on cohesion and energy files, quiet exemptions in the technical annexes, and periodic Council-presidency pressure that translates the political cost of the veto back to the member-state capital that holds it. The mechanism worked while the veto-holders were peripheral to the EU-average median and while the compensation architecture was still politically underwritten by the German-French axis. Both variables have compressed. Hungary has learned across five successive cycles that the veto itself is the compensation architecture: hold the vote, extract the transit exemption, unlock the funds, cash the discount. Slovakia has learned the same. Bulgaria’s objection this cycle sits inside the same discovered mechanism. The constitution of unanimity is now practising a constitution of purchasable delay.

The Practitioner’s Note

Find the specific point.

Each of Thursday’s stories rewards the principal who found the specific point at which the counterparty’s general defence had never developed. Ukraine did not defeat the Russian army; it found the refinery cohort inside the distribution architecture that had no reserve. BYD did not out-market Tesla on a category-level narrative; it built four scale-and-integration cohorts at once inside the specific cost architecture that decides a commodity vehicle industry. The Central European veto-holders did not attack the sanctions cohort on its stated purpose; they found the unanimity requirement inside a horse-trading architecture the Commission cannot publicly retire. Each is a thumbscrew move at a different register: not the general campaign, but the specific point.

In your own affairs: the law cuts at three registers. At the professional register, the negotiation you have been running on general terms will resolve when you find the specific point at which your counterparty’s general defence stops — the budget cycle they cannot admit to, the boss they cannot cross, the metric their bonus is priced against — and quietly re-frame the ask around it. At the personal register, the pattern in a relationship you have been trying to shift on general grounds will move only when you notice the specific point that carries the pattern (a fear, a habit, a story about the past) and address the point rather than the pattern. At the institutional register, the initiative you have been running against a general resistance will move when you locate the specific institutional pressure — a quarterly board slide, a regulatory calendar, a competitor’s next print — that converts the general resistance into a specific decision.

The hard question: the thirty-third law has an ethical edge that is easier to miss than its tactical one. Discovering a counterparty’s thumbscrew is not the same as pressing it in a register that damages the counterparty; the schooled practitioner discovers the point in order to know where the pressure converts, and then chooses the register at which the pressure can be applied without damaging the counterparty’s longer-term standing. Ukraine is pressing the Russian civilian-visibility thumbscrew in a war where the alternative is more Ukrainian dead; BYD is pressing the Tesla cost-cohort thumbscrew inside a market economy where the counterparty is a rival firm; the Central European vetoes are pressing the unanimity thumbscrew inside an institutional architecture that survives the payment. Before you press the point you have discovered, ask yourself: is the register I am pressing at a register the counterparty can absorb without losing what makes them the counterparty I need? Greene’s thirty-third law is the instrument; the discipline to press only at the register the counterparty can survive is the foundation.
