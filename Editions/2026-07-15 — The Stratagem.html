The Graham Bill

A bipartisan bloc unveiled the Sanctioning Russia Act of 2026 Tuesday afternoon in the Mansfield Room, thirty-two co-sponsors on the release, urging passage inside ten days to honour the late Lindsey Graham. The bill authorises a 100 per cent secondary tariff on any country importing Russian crude, refined product, uranium, or piped gas — explicitly enumerated as China, India, Slovakia, Hungary, and Azerbaijan on the oil side; China, France, Belgium, Japan, and Hungary on the gas side. Trump told reporters the bill has “a good chance,” that Iran and Hezbollah language may be added, and that his signature is conditional on the Senate’s final text. The bill is not another sanctions round. It is the moment the American pressure architecture on Russia pivots from Moscow to Moscow’s customers.

The text, first.

The Sanctioning Russia Act of 2026 — introduced Tuesday at 14:00 ET in the Mansfield Room by Majority Leader John Thune and Senator Richard Blumenthal, with thirty-two additional co-sponsors including seventeen Republicans, fourteen Democrats, and one Independent — is a 61-page bill, roughly double the length of the 2025 predecessor Graham first introduced last April. Its operative provisions run in three registers. First, the mandatory-sanction list: Vladimir Putin, the senior Russian political and military command, thirty-one named oligarchs and their known holding structures, Gazprom, Rosneft, Novatek, and roughly one hundred foreign firms named in the release as suppliers to the Russian defence-industrial base. Second, the financial-plumbing register: mandatory secondary sanctions on any bank or clearing house that processes Russian energy payments outside the price-cap regime, mandatory delisting of Russian-connected instruments from US-registered exchanges, and a formal prohibition on US persons providing services to the “shadow fleet” of aging tankers Moscow uses to evade the existing cap. Third, and the register the market has read as load-bearing: the President is authorised to impose a 100 per cent secondary tariff on any country importing Russian crude, refined product, uranium, or piped gas. The bill explicitly enumerates the five largest purchasers of each. The exemption clause survives from the 2025 version: buyers whose purchases represent less than 15 per cent of Russia’s natural-gas exports and are demonstrably decreasing are exempted. The 500 per cent rate the 2025 version carried is now 100 per cent. The Presidential-discretion clause is retained: Trump may lift or suspend any specific tariff for reasons of “national interest.”

Mearsheimer on secondary leverage as substitute.

Mearsheimer’s offensive-realism discipline reads the pivot at a register the sanctions commentariat is under-articulating. A hegemon whose direct instrument on an adversary has been calibrated over four years to marginal effect — the existing sanctions have reduced Russian oil revenue by roughly 12 per cent while GDP has expanded on defence-industrial mobilisation — either escalates the direct instrument or substitutes. The direct escalation available — kinetic support to Ukraine beyond current levels, direct US-NATO forces on Ukrainian territory, cyber-operations against Russian critical infrastructure — sits at levels the coalition around Trump has explicitly declined for eight months. What the Graham Bill instead does is convert the American trade relationship with third countries into the primary pressure instrument on Russia. The move is legible in Mearsheimer’s frame as a substitution of leverage-on-partners for leverage-on-adversary; and the substitution is only stable if the leverage-on-partners the hegemon holds exceeds the leverage-on-partners the adversary can offer in exchange for the third country’s continued purchase. Beijing’s response function on that trade-off is the operative variable, and no one in the current administration orbit has published a stress-tested read on it. The 2018-2020 Section 301 escalations produced concessions on soybean purchases and pork slaughter; the 2026 test at the 100 per cent register runs at an order of magnitude higher, against a counterparty whose own trade-diversification project is now seven years further advanced.

Sun Tzu on attacking the alliance.

Sun Tzu’s discipline in Chapter III on attacking the enemy’s alliances reads the bill at the strategic register the tactical press is under-articulating. Sun Tzu’s ordering is precise: the supreme excellence is to break the enemy’s alliances; next is to break his army; last is to besiege his walled cities. The Russian war economy has held for four years primarily because its energy revenue has continued to flow to China, India, Turkey, and a floating shadow-fleet buyer set the sanctions apparatus has been eight quarters late in mapping. The Graham Bill’s specific move is to attack that alliance directly — not by persuading the buyers to stop through the diplomatic instrument (which four years of sustained pressure has not produced) but by making the American trade relationship the counter-price. Sun Tzu’s discipline is that the alliance-attack works when the target alliance has weak internal coordination and the alternative to the alliance is credible. Beijing’s alternative to the alliance-with-Russia is the direct US trade relationship at 100 per cent tariff or no tariff, but Beijing’s alliance-with-Russia has been the primary geopolitical hedge under the Xi-Putin coordination architecture for six years and cannot be dropped without an equivalent replacement. The bill’s vulnerability is that the alliance-attack can consolidate the target alliance rather than fracture it; Delhi’s response is the closer test, since India retains the option of visible cooperation on a face-saving tariff schedule that Beijing does not.

Greene’s Law 42 on the shepherd and the sheep.

Greene’s forty-second Law — strike the shepherd and the sheep will scatter — reads the specific enumerative structure of the bill at the tactical register the CNN commentary is missing. The enumeration is not decorative. The bill names the five largest oil buyers (China, India, Slovakia, Hungary, Azerbaijan) and the five largest gas buyers (China, France, Belgium, Japan, Hungary). The enumeration signals to every third country not on the list that they can escape the 100-per-cent instrument by keeping their share below the enumeration threshold, and it signals to every counterparty of an enumerated country that the enumerated country is now trading under a specific American disability. Greene’s discipline reads the enumeration as an attempt to identify the shepherd — the specific counterparty whose continued purchase is holding the rest of the flock in line — and to isolate it. On the oil side, Beijing is the identifiable shepherd; on the gas side, the picture is fractured across five European and Asian counterparties who do not coordinate. The instrument works if the shepherd’s isolation causes the flock to scatter (Slovakia and Hungary are the specific test on the oil side, France and Belgium on the gas side); it fails if the shepherd rallies the flock (Beijing offering a coordinated pass-through mechanism to the enumerated four so no one exits alone). The bill was drafted assuming the scattering; the coordination architecture between Beijing, Moscow, and Delhi over the past four years suggests the rallying is at least as likely.

Dalio on reserve-currency leverage.

Dalio’s Big-Cycle discipline reads the bill at the structural register the tariff-mechanics coverage is missing. The 100 per cent secondary tariff is only an operative instrument to the extent that the American trade relationship remains the counter-price no counterparty can afford to lose. That counter-price is a direct function of the dollar’s reserve-currency position and the American consumer market’s share of global demand — the two variables Dalio’s late-cycle framework identifies as the primary leverage points a reserve-currency issuer holds during the transition period. Both variables are in visible partial decline: dollar reserve share sits at 57 per cent from a 2001 peak of 72 per cent, and the US consumer market’s share of global final demand at 26 per cent from a 2000 peak of 34 per cent. The Graham Bill is a claim on the reserve-currency and consumer-market leverage at exactly the moment the leverage is under structural erosion. The specific worked test is the Beijing counter-price: if Beijing responds by accelerating the CIPS clearing volume, the ruble-yuan-rupee trade settlement pilots, and the direct sourcing of Iranian and Venezuelan crude, the bill accelerates the very leverage erosion it was calibrated to exploit. Dalio’s discipline is not that the leverage is gone; it is that each specific instrument-use of the leverage in the late-cycle phase erodes the underlying capacity at a rate the operator does not price.

The Helms-Burton rhyme.

The imperfect historical rhyme is the Cuba Democracy Act of 1996 — Helms-Burton — in which a bipartisan Congressional bloc, following the shoot-down of two Brothers to the Rescue aircraft, authorised secondary sanctions on any foreign firm trafficking in property confiscated from US persons in Cuba. The rhyme is not the specific target country. It is the structural pattern: a Congressional bipartisan bloc converts an emotional inflection point (Graham’s death in this case; the Brothers to the Rescue shoot-down in 1996) into a secondary-sanctions instrument that reshapes the American trade relationship with every third-country trading with the adversary. Helms-Burton produced immediate friction with Canada, Mexico, and the European Union; the Title III provisions were suspended by every President from Clinton through Obama and reactivated under Trump in 2019. The 2026 bill runs against a substantively different counterparty set (China and India rather than the EU and Canada) at an order of magnitude larger fiscal register (100 per cent tariff rather than corporate liability), but the specific vulnerability the Helms-Burton rhyme illuminates is the vulnerability the Graham Bill shares: a secondary-sanctions regime is only stable if the third-country counterparties conclude the American trade relationship is worth more than the adversary trade relationship, and the counterparties’ conclusion is a function of both the sanction and every other American action running at the same time. The August 1 tariff schedule, the Hormuz Toll of last Monday, and now the Graham Bill are three instruments arriving on the same third-country counterparties inside a 96-hour window. The counterparty aggregate response is priced against the aggregate, not the individual instrument.

What the reading expects.

Base case: the Senate passes the bill inside three weeks by margin above 80-15; the House takes it up in September on a compressed calendar and clears it by October under a rule that limits amendments; Trump signs mid-October with a signing statement asserting the “national interest” discretion clause and immediately suspending the tariff schedule on Slovakia, Hungary, and Azerbaijan under diplomatic cover from Fico, Orbán, and Aliyev respectively; the China and India tariffs are imposed at 25 per cent on a limited product basket in November as a negotiating floor rather than at 100 per cent on the enumerated crude flow; Beijing responds with a symmetric 25 per cent counter-tariff on US soybeans and semiconductor-manufacturing equipment components; Delhi negotiates a face-saving reduction schedule for Russian crude imports of roughly 20 per cent over eighteen months in exchange for tariff suspension on the enumerated product basket. The alternative case is Trump signing at 100 per cent and refusing to invoke the discretion clause on Hungary and Slovakia to preserve the pressure on Beijing; Orbán and Fico withdraw from the coordinated European position on the 21st sanctions package that failed Monday; the Continental split on Russia energy hardens for the remainder of 2026 and the Graham Bill produces its intended pressure on Moscow through the destruction of the European coordination architecture on Russian energy rather than through the direct-tariff impact on Beijing. The base case does not require it; the base case does not preclude it.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What’s driving this. The reading is load-bearing at two layers. Structural: four years of direct sanctions on Moscow have reduced Russian oil revenue by roughly 12 per cent while GDP has expanded on defence-industrial mobilisation, and the coalition around Trump has explicitly declined the direct kinetic escalation available. The remaining instrument is to convert the American trade relationship with third countries — principally China and India — into the pressure instrument on Russia; the Graham Bill is that conversion codified. Tactical: the enumerative structure of the bill (five oil buyers, five gas buyers) is a Sun Tzu alliance-attack that isolates the shepherd (Beijing on oil) and signals to the flock that individual exit is available at the exemption threshold, but the four-year Xi-Putin coordination architecture means Beijing has an equal probability of rallying the flock rather than being isolated by the attack.

What’s not driving this. The bill is not principally a Ukraine-support instrument at the register the Ukraine-friendly commentators are reading it as — Zelensky is not the party the enumerated tariff-schedule was drafted for. And it is not a Congressional-independence move against Trump; the White House sign-off Graham secured the Friday before his death is what makes the bill a live instrument at all, and the “national interest” discretion clause preserves the executive’s ability to gut it at signature.

What would break this reading.

  • Trump publicly refuses to sign inside two weeks and specifies that no bill authorising tariffs on China above 25 per cent will reach his desk; the alliance-attack instrument is de-fanged before it activates, and the bill reverts to a Senate signalling exercise the market discounts to a five-per-cent probability of effective execution.
  • Beijing announces a coordinated pass-through mechanism inside four weeks under which Chinese state refiners maintain Russian crude purchases with a Kremlin-underwritten price discount that offsets the American 100 per cent tariff; the shepherd rallies the flock and the alliance-attack consolidates the counterparty rather than fracturing it.
  • Delhi commits inside six weeks to a demonstrable 30 per cent reduction schedule for Russian crude imports over twelve months in exchange for tariff suspension on a specific US product basket; the alliance-attack works on the softer of the two large buyers and produces the pressure the bill was calibrated to generate, at a moment Beijing is left holding a coordination architecture the second-largest partner has partially exited.
  • Hungary and Slovakia are exempted at Trump’s signature under diplomatic cover from Orbán and Fico that requires them to publicly abandon the coordinated European position on Russian energy; the Continental coordination architecture fractures before the bill’s direct effect on Moscow’s revenue is measurable, and the Graham Bill produces its pressure through European disintegration rather than through the tariff on Beijing.

Levine on the IPO fee cascade.

Matt Levine’s discipline on the specific mechanics of underwriting fees reads the Goldman print at the register the equity press is under-articulating. The 86 per cent year-on-year jump in equity-markets revenue is not a broad-based trading print; it is heavily concentrated in the fee cascade from the SpaceX IPO that priced 27 June at an $86 billion valuation with Goldman as sole global coordinator, followed by three secondary raises Goldman book-ran the week after. The SpaceX book alone paid an estimated $340 million in gross underwriting fees split roughly Goldman 60 / Morgan Stanley 25 / JPMorgan 15; the follow-on ADR programme paid another $180 million; the June-quarter SPAC-adjacent recapitalisations Goldman book-ran added roughly $140 million. Levine’s point — consistent across a decade of Money Stuff columns on the fee complex — is that quarterly underwriting income at that concentration is not a repeatable line; it is a specific vintage event. The Q2 print is legibly one of the specific quarters in which a single mandate reshapes the fee complex’s cadence for the next three quarters and locks in a specific dependency on the pipeline that follows. Goldman’s Q3 pipeline is anchored on the Anthropic Series G lead role (fees roughly $180 million estimated), the OpenAI convertible tranche restructuring (roughly $220 million), and the CoreWeave secondary. The three carry Goldman through Q3 at roughly the same fee run-rate. Q4 depends on the specific set of AI-adjacent IPOs the sell side has been pipelining since March and which the Meta July 8 spare-capacity announcement has already begun to reprice.

Munger on incentive-caused bias.

Munger’s incentive-caused-bias discipline reads the vise’s specific breakout at the market-structure register. The Q2 sell-side estimate distribution for the big-five bank prints was calibrated to net interest income compression and tariff-related consumer-credit deterioration; the actual print delivered on capital-markets fee income at a level the sell side had not modelled. The specific bias Munger’s discipline names is that the sell-side analysts covering the bank complex are compensated on the same underwriting fee cascade the banks themselves are printing on — the same equity capital-markets desks whose bookrunning fees drove the Goldman equity-markets revenue line are the desks whose Q3 outlook is drafted by the same firms whose analyst coverage of Goldman’s Q3 pipeline is being read as a leading indicator by the buy side. Munger’s inversion: what makes the Tuesday print structurally dangerous is not the beat itself but the specific way the beat aligns with the incentive-caused bias of the analysts whose outlook the market is using to price the multiple expansion. When the beat is on a line that the analysts themselves are earning fees on, the outlook the analysts publish for the same line the following quarter is systematically upward-biased; the buy side that reads the outlook without adjusting for the bias pays a multiple against a projection the underlying earnings will not sustain. The 2006 Bear Stearns Q2 print — a beat on structured-credit trading income the sell side extrapolated into 2007 forecasts that priced the stock at 15 times — is the closer version of the pattern than the tape’s Tuesday celebration is registering.

Dalio on bank earnings decoupling.

Dalio’s Big-Cycle discipline reads Tuesday’s prints at the register the historical bank-earnings-as-leading-indicator frame is missing. The pre-2008 relationship between bank earnings and the real-economy cycle was direct: bank earnings tracked loan-book quality and net interest margin, both direct functions of the underlying credit cycle. The post-2010 regulatory architecture — higher capital requirements, Volcker Rule constraints, systemic-risk designation of the largest institutions — combined with the persistent institutional-investor and passive-flow demand for the largest bank equities has produced a divergence: bank earnings now track fee-generation capacity in capital markets far more than loan-book performance, and capital-markets fee generation is itself driven by the private-equity and venture-capital cycle rather than the broader corporate cycle. Dalio’s discipline is that in the late-boom phase, the divergence widens: bank earnings can print records against a real-economy that is deteriorating on the leading indicators (tariff-pass-through, consumer-credit-delinquency, small-business bankruptcy filings), because the fee complex operates on a different cycle. The 2007 analogue is that Goldman, Morgan Stanley, and JPMorgan all printed record Q2 fee income against a mortgage-credit cycle that was already three quarters into deterioration; the divergence was legible in the real-time indicators but not in the earnings tape. The 2026 test is whether the analogous divergence is already visible in the small-business and consumer-credit data the earnings tape is drowning out; the answer, on the credit-card delinquency and small-business bankruptcy filings released the last three Thursdays, is that it is.

The 2006 Q2 rhyme.

The imperfect historical rhyme is Q2 2006, in which Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Merrill Lynch, Lehman Brothers, and Bear Stearns each printed record quarterly earnings on the specific combination of structured-credit trading income, M&A advisory fees, and equity-underwriting activity that would — five quarters later — converge into the specific set of exposures that took the same institutions off the tape. The rhyme is not the credit-cycle catalyst; the 2026 fee cascade is running on IPO underwriting and equity-markets trading rather than structured-credit origination. The rhyme is the pattern of a specific quarter in which the fee complex’s decoupling from the underlying credit and consumer cycle produces an earnings tape whose sell-side extrapolation prices the next four quarters against the print rather than against the underlying cycle. In 2006 the print was celebrated as the sign of a fee complex that had permanently repriced itself higher; in 2007 the print was still being read as the leading indicator by analysts who had not yet re-drafted for the underlying deterioration; in 2008 the entire complex re-rated inside eight weeks. The 2026 rhyme does not require the same magnitude of correction; the regulatory architecture and the systemic-risk architecture are different. But the specific vulnerability of the sell-side extrapolation to the incentive-caused bias Munger’s discipline names is the same.

The mechanism, first.

At the financial register, the position you have taken is often a bet against a coordinated coalition (a hyperscaler cohort, a bank complex, a sanctions coalition); ask which of your positions are less about the substantive call and more about whether the target coalition holds against the specific pressure you are pricing it against. At the personal register, the boundary or ask you are preparing to issue against a specific relational counterparty often assumes that counterparty is not coordinated with a third party who shares their interest; ask which of your relational asks are running against a shadow coalition you have not made explicit. The discipline is not to abandon the alliance-attack instrument. The discipline is to ask, before you fire it, whether the target coalition is structured to fracture under the specific pressure you have chosen — and if not, to identify the specific weak link before you attack, or to reach for a different instrument.

— Question for the Day —

Three alliance-attacks tested against three target-alliance response functions on Wednesday’s tape: an American secondary-tariff bill designed to fracture the Xi-Putin coordination through the enumerated buyer set on which it depends, a bank-earnings vintage-event print anchoring a sell-side Q3 extrapolation whose fee-complex coordination Munger’s incentive-caused-bias discipline names as structurally biased, and a European Council whose unanimity mechanism converts a two-state block posture into an automatic upward revision of the Russian-crude cap on the calendar the block state pair has to do nothing to trigger. Apply the test to your own week — name three alliance-attacks you are preparing to fire — a coalitional professional move, a market position that bets against a coordinated cohort, a relational ask that assumes a counterparty is not shadow-coordinated with a third — and ask honestly whether the target coalition in each case is structured to fracture under the pressure you have chosen or to consolidate. The ones where the target coalition consolidates are the ones that convert your alliance-attack into your own coalition’s fracture. Sun Tzu’s reading is that the supreme excellence is to break the enemy’s alliances, but only where the alliance is more fragile than your own; where it is not, the ordering inverts before it discloses. Confucius’ reading is that the institution whose form has quietly emptied of substance persists in the register of name, and every specific decision it fails to make weakens the authority of every subsequent decision it does. Aristotle’s reading is that the deliberative body’s decision rule must match the substantive coherence of the coalition it decides for; where the coalition’s divergence is a feature of membership, the mechanism itself must recalibrate. Between these three sentences sits the practical instrument: audit each of your alliance-attacks for the consolidation risk, and either identify the specific weak link before you fire or reach for an instrument the target coalition’s response function does not amplify.
