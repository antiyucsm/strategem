Law of the Day · No. 6

Make People Come to You.

When you force the other person to act, you are the one in control. It is always better to make your opponent come to you, abandoning his own plans in the process. Lure him with fabulous gains — then attack.

Yet observe the inversion. When the bait is applied too long or too crudely, the other side stops coming. They build alternative paths. The table empties.

  • Identify what makes others need you. That is your bait.
  • Ask: is the other side still coming — or have they found another table?
  • The most dangerous moment is when you mistake their absence for patience.
From the Classics

Sun Tzu & Clausewitz.

In war, the defensive form of fighting is intrinsically stronger than the offensive. But it has a negative purpose.

— Carl von Clausewitz

He who can modify his tactics in relation to his opponent and thereby succeed in winning may be called a heaven-born captain.

— Sun Tzu

The Practitioner's Note

On bait that stops working.

The blockade is bait. The tariff is bait. The deadline is bait. Each is designed to force the other side to come to you, abandoning their plans in the process.

In your own affairs: what have you been offering — or withholding — that was supposed to bring someone to your table? Is it still working?

The hard question: if they stopped coming and you did not notice, what does that say about your model of the relationship?

Ceasefire, Ticking

Six days remain on the Iran-US truce. Both sides negotiate as though time is the weapon — because it is. Read the clock, not the communiqués.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Islamabad & Washington·16 April 2026

Islamabad —A ceasefire is not peace. It is a clock attached to a negotiation, and the clock is the negotiation’s most powerful participant.

On April 7, the United States and Iran agreed to a two-week truce. On April 22 — six days from now — it expires. In the intervening span, the Islamabad peace talks collapsed after twenty-one hours of fruitless negotiation, the US Navy implemented a full blockade of Iranian ports and turned back ten merchant vessels, Iran threatened Red Sea shipping in retaliation, and mediators from Pakistan, Egypt, and Turkey scrambled to secure what they are calling an “extension in principle.” Day 48 of the conflict. More than three thousand dead in Iran, two thousand in Lebanon, thirteen American service members lost. The clock ticks.

”He who can modify his tactics in relation to his opponent and thereby succeed in winning may be called a heaven-born captain.” — Sun Tzu.

The deadline as revelation.

The power of a deadline in negotiation is not that it forces a decision. It is that it forces disclosure. When time narrows, actors must show what they will and will not accept — and in that showing, the structure of the bargain becomes visible for the first time.

Chris Voss named this the deadline effect in hostage negotiation: the richest information arrives in the final minutes, because that is when the other side can no longer afford to bluff. The same pattern operates between states. Iran’s demand that Israeli strikes on Lebanon cease before any deal was never going to surface in round one of the talks. It surfaced at the collapse of round one because the approaching deadline made deferral costlier than disclosure. Washington’s parallel move — implementing the Hormuz blockade within hours of the talks’ failure — follows the same logic in reverse. It was Law 6 in immediate register: make the other side come to you. By placing the full weight of American naval power at the chokepoint of Iran’s economic survival, Washington converted a diplomatic pause into structural pressure. The message: the longer you wait, the worse it gets.

The extension trap.

Yet the extension now under discussion reveals a deeper architecture. A two-week ceasefire that needs extending is not a ceasefire moving toward peace. It is a ceasefire moving toward itself — a pattern in which the truce becomes the permanent condition because neither side can afford to end it or advance beyond it.

The historical parallel is the Korean armistice of 1953. That “temporary” ceasefire is now seventy-three years old. The pattern is identical: hostilities too costly to resume, peace too structurally complex to achieve, and a frozen line that becomes the de facto settlement. Polymarket prices a permanent peace deal by the end of May at just 31%. The crowd is reading the structure correctly.

The sticking points — zero uranium enrichment, missile-programme caps, nuclear-site dismantlement, end of proxy funding, reopening of Hormuz — are not negotiable positions. They are identity commitments. Iran’s nuclear programme is the regime’s claim to regional-power status. America’s zero-enrichment demand is the condition under which the coalition driving the operation will accept the word “peace.” Structural incompatibility, not tactical disagreement.

The third party at the table.

What the ceasefire clock reveals most clearly is the role of the mediators. Pakistan, Egypt, and Turkey are not disinterested brokers. Each has its own structural reason to be in the room — Pakistan as host and aspirant to diplomatic visibility, Egypt as guardian of its own maritime chokepoint at Suez, Turkey as the NATO member running parallel channels to both sides. Mearsheimer’s offensive realism describes the incentive precisely: small and medium powers do not mediate great-power conflicts from altruism. They mediate to occupy the space between the giants — and the space between giants is where the next generation’s influence is built.

Watch the mediators as closely as the principals. The ceasefire may expire, extend, or collapse. But the realignment of regional power around the negotiation — who brokered what, who held the channel open, who proved indispensable — will outlast whatever document emerges.

The question that remains.

For the student of power, the ceasefire clock teaches one lesson. Deadlines do not resolve conflicts. They surface the information that was always present. The question is not whether the truce extends — it almost certainly will, because the alternative is a resumption neither side’s public can absorb. The question is whether either side is prepared to act on what the clock has now revealed, or whether the clock itself, ticking past April 22 and then past May and then past June, becomes the conflict’s permanent condition.

The Table Empties

America built the tariff wall. The world walked around it. The deals are now being signed at tables where Washington has no seat.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Washington & Brussels·16 April 2026

Washington —The most consequential trade development of 2026 is not a tariff. It is an absence.

On February 20, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in Learning Resources v. Trump that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act does not authorize the president to impose tariffs. Within hours, the administration invoked Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 — a balance-of-payments authority never designed for this purpose — to impose an interim 10% blanket tariff expiring in 150 days unless Congress acts. The effective US tariff rate stands at 17.9%, down from the 28% peak but still the highest since the Smoot-Hawley era. American businesses and consumers absorb nearly 90% of the cost.

”Invert, always invert.” — Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi, via Charlie Munger.

What did the rest of the world do while Washington argued with its own judiciary? It signed deals.

The deals Washington was not invited to.

In the past twelve months, the European Union has finalised free trade agreements with both Mercosur and India — collectively eliminating roughly 90% of bilateral tariffs, covering nearly two billion people and a quarter of global GDP. Canada signed a trade agreement with China. Japan, Vietnam, and India have positioned themselves at the front of a queue for new bilateral arrangements — arrangements negotiated, in every case, without American participation.

McKinsey's March 2026 update on the geometry of global trade documents the shift: more than $165 billion in trade has been redirected away from the US-China corridor since the tariff escalation began. The Cato Institute's assessment is blunter: global trade is leaving the US behind. This is not a temporary adjustment. It is a structural realignment.

The inversion of Law 6.

Greene's sixth law instructs: make people come to you — use bait if necessary. The tariffs were the bait. The logic was straightforward: impose costs high enough that partners would accept American terms to end the pain. It worked once — the 2025 UK and Japan mini-deals were signed under pressure. But the structural error was treating a one-time tactic as a permanent strategy.

When the bait is applied too long, Law 6 inverts. The other side stops coming. They build alternative paths. The EU-India deal is not a response to American tariffs in the defensive sense. It is a response to the recognition that American trade policy has become structurally unpredictable. When partners cannot model what the tariff rate will be in six months, they do not negotiate harder. They negotiate elsewhere.

Lee Kuan Yew understood this from the small-state perspective. The great powers set the weather, but the small states choose their shelter. In 2026, the shelter is being built outside American architecture for the first time since Bretton Woods.

Dalio's Big Cycle in real time.

Ray Dalio's framework describes the pattern with structural precision: in the late stages of a hegemonic order, the declining power's attempts to protect its position accelerate the very shifts it seeks to prevent. The tariffs were designed to preserve American manufacturing and reduce the trade deficit. The trade deficit persists. Manufacturing investment remains flat. What has changed is the global architecture around the tariffs — new agreements, new supply chains, new corridors that route around the United States.

The Section 122 tariff expires in roughly 150 days — August 2026 — unless Congress extends it. This creates a structural clock: if Congress extends, American tariff policy becomes legislatively embedded and the trade realignment accelerates. If Congress declines, the administration loses its remaining tariff authority. Meanwhile, the Court of International Trade processes Judge Eaton's March order requiring refunds of the invalidated IEEPA tariffs — over $130 billion in potential repayments. The market prices court-ordered refunds at 18%.

The second-order consequence.

Munger's discipline is the one that matters here. The first-order effect of the tariffs was to raise prices for American consumers — the average household faces $1,500 in additional costs in 2026. The second-order effect was to push trading partners into new arrangements. The third-order effect — the one now arriving — is that those arrangements, once signed, do not reverse when the tariffs come down. The EU-Mercosur deal does not contain a sunset clause tied to American trade policy. The EU-India agreement does not expire when Section 122 does.

The table, once emptied, does not automatically refill.

Blueprint, Unbuilt

Germany's €500 billion infrastructure fund is one year old. The money has been allocated. The question is whether it has been spent on the right things.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Frankfurt·16 April 2026

Berlin —One year ago, the German Bundestag adopted the largest fiscal commitment in the Federal Republic's history — a €500 billion special fund for infrastructure and climate transition, accompanied by more than €200 billion for defence and €100 billion for green energy. The reform required amending the constitutional debt brake, a move Friedrich Merz's government accomplished with a two-thirds parliamentary majority in what commentators called Germany's "fiscal reawakening."

Twelve months later, €24 billion has been disbursed. And the question is no longer whether Germany can spend the money. It is whether the money is being spent on the right things.

"To govern is to choose." — Pierre Mendès-France.

The diffusion problem.

Greene's Law 23 is the law of concentration: find the single point whose absence decides the contest and place your weight there. Germany's infrastructure fund does the opposite. It distributes resources across bridges, railways, digital infrastructure, grid modernisation, hydrogen, and defence simultaneously — a breadth that gives every ministry a claim and no ministry a mandate.

The criticism arriving from economists and business leaders is not that the spending is wrong. It is that the spending is undirected. Implementation has been hampered by procurement bottlenecks, planning-law delays, and the absence of a single coordinating authority. The money exists. The institutional capacity to deploy it at speed does not.

This is the gap Aristotle named between potentia and actus — between capacity and its realisation. Germany has the fiscal capacity. What it lacks is the institutional machinery to convert that capacity into built infrastructure within the political timeline Merz requires. By the time the bridges are repaired and the grid is modernised, the next election may already have passed.

The external shock.

Compounding the execution challenge is the crisis the previous edition analysed: the Iran conflict and the Hormuz blockade. Oil prices have pushed German energy costs higher at precisely the moment when the infrastructure plan was supposed to demonstrate that fiscal expansion could outrun structural headwinds. Manufacturers in chemicals, steel, and automotive — the sectors most sensitive to energy input costs — are calling for emergency relief measures that would compete with the fund's own priorities for attention and resources.

This is Munger's Lollapalooza pattern arriving on schedule. The infrastructure fund alone was manageable. The energy shock alone was manageable. The state elections approaching in autumn alone were manageable. Stacked together — fiscal ambition, external price shock, and electoral clock — they produce a compound pressure that no single policy response can address.

Merz warned lawmakers this week that the outlook remains "very critical" and that "radical" political and legal steps would be needed in 2026 to reverse the slide. The word radical from a CDU chancellor is itself a signal worth reading. It suggests the institutional track — incremental disbursement, committee-stage procurement, planning-law review — is too slow for the political reality.

The AfD's opening.

The AfD does not need to build anything. It needs only to point at what has not been built. With state elections approaching in autumn 2026, the gap between the €500 billion headline and the €24 billion reality provides a narrative requiring no explanation: they promised half a trillion — where is it?

This is the same structural dynamic the previous edition analysed through the "silent chair" framework, now applied domestically. The fund's success depends on conditions Merz does not fully control — procurement reform, municipal planning capacity, energy price stability — and each uncontrolled variable that fails becomes ammunition for a party that has built its strategy on naming failures rather than proposing alternatives.

Talleyrand's counsel.

The discipline required is what the previous edition named: Talleyrand's above all, not too much zeal. The temptation will be to accelerate disbursement before the autumn elections — announce flagship projects, cut planning stages, bypass review. The risk is that speed without direction produces waste that becomes its own scandal.

The alternative is harder and less visible: concentrate the spending on a small number of high-impact projects that can show tangible progress within the electoral timeline, accept that the full fund will take a decade to deploy, and communicate the constraint honestly. Law 23 applies to governance as it does to war: concentrate your forces at the decisive point.

The decisive point for Merz is not the total amount spent. It is the visible evidence that spending has changed something specific — a rail line opened, a bridge replaced, a grid connection completed — that voters can see and touch before they vote. Everything else is a number on a spreadsheet, and numbers on spreadsheets do not win elections.
