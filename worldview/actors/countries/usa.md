# United States

## Core interests (durable)

- **Dollar primacy.** The ability to issue the world's reserve currency is the top-line privilege. Every other interest is subordinate to preserving this.
- **Energy self-sufficiency.** Shale flipped the US from net importer to net exporter. Continental energy independence means the US can act in the Persian Gulf without paying a price at its own pump.
- **Sea-lane dominance.** The US Navy underwrites global trade. Its willingness to police chokepoints (Hormuz, Malacca, Bab el-Mandeb) is why containerized trade is affordable.
- **Technology leadership.** Semiconductors, AI, biotech — each treated as civilizational leverage. Export controls are the enforcement mechanism.
- **Alliance loyalty (ambiguous).** NATO, AUKUS, the Quad. Each alliance is priced for US leadership; deliberate ambiguity about US commitment is itself strategic.

## Durable constraints

- **Electoral cycle.** Every four years the country auditions a new regime, regardless of ongoing external crises. Allies and adversaries both plan against this metronome.
- **Two-party polarization.** Any policy requiring continuity across administrations — climate, debt, Iran, China — is structurally fragile.
- **Debt trajectory.** Interest payments now exceed defense spending. Eventually forces the choice between inflation, austerity, or growth. Each has different foreign-policy consequences.
- **Cultural exhaustion with overseas commitments.** "Endless wars" framing is bipartisan. Wars must now be explained as short, decisive, or existential.

## Reading guide

- When the US announces a position, treat it as the opening offer — not the commitment.
- Watch the Gulf and the Pacific simultaneously. These are the two boards; news from one affects the other.
- The president matters more than the party for short-term direction; the party matters more than the president for what survives.
- Oil price above $120 sustained is the political breaking point. Below that, most foreign-policy costs are absorbable.
