Law of the Day · No. 23

Concentrate Your Forces.

Power itself always exists in concentrated forms. Single-mindedness, against opponents who are less focused — such an arrow finds its mark every time.

Yet know the limit. When tied to a single source, if that source falls, you fall with it. Concentration is a sword with two edges.

  • Find the single point whose absence decides the contest.
  • Place your weight there. Do not thin it across fronts.
  • Identify what happens if that point fails — plan for it.
From the Classics

Clausewitz & Sun Tzu.

Be very strong; first in general, then at the decisive point. There is no higher and simpler law of strategy than that of keeping one's forces concentrated.

— Carl von Clausewitz

Attack where he is unprepared. Appear where you are not expected.

— Sun Tzu

The Practitioner's Note

On your own Hormuz.

Each life contains choke points. A single client, a single relationship, a single income — points whose pressure decides the whole.

Of your adversary: where is their Hormuz? What single point, if squeezed, decides more than its size?

Of yourself: where is your Hormuz? Diversify that point — or fortify it until it cannot be squeezed.

Concentration of Force

Washington closes Iran's arteries at Hormuz. Read it not as crisis, but as case study — a demonstration of where power is applied, not how much of it.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Islamabad & Washington·15 April 2026

Islamabad —A state with superior force rarely uses all of it. It places it where absence would cost the other side everything, and waits.

On Monday, after twenty-one hours of talks in Pakistan ended without agreement, U.S. Central Command began enforcing a blockade of Iranian ports. No ship passed in the first twenty-four hours. Six merchant vessels complied with direction and turned back. No shots were fired.

"The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting." — Sun Tzu.

That is what this move is. That it works is not in doubt; the material logic of a chokepoint through which a fifth of the world's oil transits compels it. What makes it instructive is where it sits in the long tradition of such moves — and where its vulnerabilities lie.

The chokepoint across centuries.

Chokepoints recur across the historical record for the same reason. They concentrate force at a single narrow point — Clausewitz called this the highest law of strategy: be very strong, first in general, then at the decisive point — while exposing the weaker party to pressure their broader position cannot answer. The Athenians closed the Hellespont to Sparta's grain in 405 BC and ended a war that had run for a generation. The British blockade of the Central Powers in the First World War accomplished more than any battle of it. The American blockade of Japan in 1941 produced Pearl Harbor. Chokepoints are where small states break and large states overreach.

Hormuz is not merely geographic. It is the point at which Iran's economic capacity to exist as a sovereign state narrows. To negotiate over the strait is to admit its significance. To refuse is to watch the structural pressure compound day by day. This is Greene's Law 23 made concrete — single-mindedness of purpose and total concentration on the goal — and it is the oldest available move in the strategist's repertoire.

Tempo as weapon.

The move's second feature is timing. Within a single news cycle, Washington shifted from blockade enforced to talks might resume within forty-eight hours. That is not indecision. It is Law 17 — cultivate unpredictability — applied in motion. Iran must calibrate not against a position but against a moving pattern. Proxies find the rules altered mid-response; allies watch ground shift before they commit. The news cycle becomes the instrument, not the record.

The inversion, and the moral question.

Munger's discipline applies before the admiration settles. What would make this a disaster?

The first answer is structural. If Brent crude sustains above one-hundred-and-twenty dollars for a quarter, the hegemon pays at its own pump. American voters do not distinguish between a war abroad and a price at home; the political cost accrues to Washington faster than the strategic benefit does.

The second is coalition. The United States is pursuing tactical objectives — no enrichment, reduced proxy support, preserved navigation. Israel is pursuing regime change. These are not the same war, and they do not end on the same terms. Law 20 warns against this precisely: do not commit inadvertently to anyone. The coalition that presses hardest in the opening days produces the disagreement that breaks the operation in the middle weeks.

The third is the one Sun Tzu placed first — before terrain, before tactics, before force. Which of the two rulers acts in accordance with the law of morality? It is the question operational discourse has forgotten how to ask. If the operation's aims cannot be defended to the American population as their own, and if the alliance driving its timing pursues ends the American population would not endorse if asked, then the operation is structurally fragile in the sense Sun Tzu meant. A population that cannot explain its own cause does not sustain it.

Observe what happens at Hormuz across the coming weeks not for the headline but for the canonical pattern: pressure applied, results achieved, alliance tension emerging, and the question of when to stop becoming — as it always does — the question that decides the whole. Law 47: do not go past the mark you aimed for; in victory, learn when to stop. This is the line history judges every chokepoint operation against.

The Circular $122 Billion

OpenAI closes the largest private round in history — funded, in part, by the very hardware vendor it most depends upon. Read the ledger, not the headline.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed San Francisco·15 April 2026

San Francisco —When the supplier funds the purchaser, one should pause before admiring the deal.

OpenAI's record one-hundred-and-twenty-two-billion-dollar round — valuing the company at eight-hundred-and-fifty-two billion — was led by Amazon at fifty billion, Nvidia at thirty, SoftBank at thirty. Each has a different reason to be in the room. Only one has a reason that circles back to its own income statement.

"Never, ever think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives." — Charles T. Munger.

The circle at the heart of the round.

Nvidia sells the chips OpenAI buys. OpenAI spends what it raises on Nvidia chips. Nvidia's revenue rises. Nvidia's stock rises. The stake's value rises. Each link is defensible. The chain is circular.

This is not new. It is how the railroads were financed in the 1870s, when the builders owned the bond-holders and the bond-holders owned the land the railroads crossed. It is how the dotcoms were financed in 1999, when the advertising revenue of one internet company was the paid-customer-acquisition spend of another, and both reported the same dollars as growth. Each era produces its own version of the same structure because the structure is profitable at every link — until the marginal buyer stops buying.

Dependence as the real product.

Underneath the capital structure sits the real asset. It is not the model, not the compute, not the brand. It is dependence. Nine hundred million people use OpenAI's tools each week. The four largest hyperscalers have eighty billion dollars co-staked in the company. Enterprises embed it in their operations in ways they cannot reverse within a quarter.

Greene named this Law 11 — learn to keep people dependent on you. It is the most durable form of economic power there is, and it is what OpenAI has actually been building. The model is merely how.

Munger's questions.

Munger's inversion asks what breaks this. Three answers.

If hyperscaler capital spending decelerates by even thirty percent — if AWS and Google recognize that they are the paying intermediary in a supplier-plus-customer axis — the circular flow reverses at each link. Nvidia's revenue falls. The stakes mark down. OpenAI's next round prices differently. The structure unwinds faster than it built because each participant was individually justified in joining but collectively cannot sustain the pattern alone.

If a rival architecture displaces the incumbent one — open-weights models, efficient-compute designs, specialized inference chips from firms not yet at the table — the concentration the round assumes no longer holds. Technology transitions have reshaped capital structures like this one many times. Railroads lost to the telegraph. Dotcoms lost to mobile. At each transition, the capital bets of the prior era had to clear before the new era could proceed.

If regulators begin to treat the cross-holding pattern as concerted action — not antitrust in the traditional sense, but the question of whether the four hyperscalers and their suppliers are in fact a single economic bloc — the game changes materially. Munger's discipline here is the one he used on himself: model the pressures, not the actors. The pressures on regulators in 2026 are not the same as they were in 2024.

At every point in the economic history of concentrated technological power — steam, rail, electricity, telephone, automobile, semiconductor, internet, social network, now artificial intelligence — the period of concentrated capital formation ended in a reset that the investors of the moment could not imagine from the inside. The technologies were all real. The question was whether the capital structure around each was calibrated to the technology's actual output over the decade that followed. Mostly, it wasn't. Munger's question is the one that matters: who is the marginal buyer, and on what day does that buyer stop?

Berlin, Bystander

Germany's "fiscal reawakening" meets the strait. Merz learns what every medium power eventually learns — sovereignty ends at the price of Brent.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Brussels·15 April 2026

Berlin —The most consequential economic decision of the German quarter was not made in Berlin. It was made aboard a United States carrier west of the Strait of Hormuz.

Growth forecasts for 2026 and 2027 have been cut. Inflation, which the Merz government had planned to outrun with an eight-and-a-half-percent minimum-wage rise, returns through the fuel pump. The fiscal pivot S&P called the story of 2026 is being judged against external conditions its authors did not set and cannot reverse.

"Above all, not too much zeal." — Talleyrand.

The silent chair.

The position Germany occupies is older than the Federal Republic. A mid-sized export economy in a long peace is structurally exposed to the wars of others — to decisions taken at tables where it has no seat. Germany writes the rules of its labor market; it does not write the price of Brent crude. It does not decide when the Straits of Hormuz close. It does not choose the timing of the American pivot from offshore balancing to direct intervention. These decisions arrive on its balance sheet regardless.

We have named this position the silent chair. Where the United States is the agent of the Hormuz story and Iran the target, Germany occupies a third category: the state whose structural position means consequential decisions about it are made without its participation. France, Japan, Korea, and increasingly Britain all occupy variants. It is the common condition of the medium state in late-cycle great-power competition. It is not weakness. It is position. But position untended becomes liability.

Compounded commitments.

The relevant discipline is Greene's Law 20 — do not commit inadvertently to anyone. Germany's energy dependence on reliable imports, its export dependence on China, its security reliance on the United States — each was, at the moment it was entered, a reasonable individual commitment. Compounded across four decades, the sum of them is a structural constraint no single commitment would have warranted.

Law 20's deeper form is this: commitments accumulate silently. They reveal their cost only when the environment changes. The environment has now changed, and the bill arrives in one quarter for commitments dating to the 1970s.

Underneath sits a domestic political question of different magnitude. The AfD does not need to author a policy to benefit from this moment. It needs only to name the cost. Law 32 — play to people's fantasies — operates in its darker register: inflation imported through oil is politically indistinguishable, in voters' daily experience, from inflation created at home. Voters feel the pump. Causation is a story for editors. The AfD runs against the pump, and the pump does not care who runs against it. This is how Merz loses ground he did not choose to cede.

The discipline of restraint.

Munger's Lollapalooza — crises rarely arrive through a single variable but through the alignment of several — describes the German condition precisely. Oil shock. Regional elections. Fiscal pivot mid-flight. EU fragmentation. Each alone would be manageable; the stack is not.

The question for Merz is the one Talleyrand answered for every French regime he served across four revolutions: above all, not too much zeal. The temptation will be to do too much — to pass emergency subsidies that bind Berlin's budget to conditions it cannot predict, to commit European partners to positions that will strand them when the American position shifts again. The alternative — the older discipline — is to do less, endure more, accept the cost of a position one did not choose, and preserve capacity for the decisions that remain to be made.

For the student of power, three readings follow.

First: identify the silent chairs in your own affairs. The contracts whose terms are set by parties you will not meet. The suppliers whose pricing is set by commodity prices you do not see. The reputations shaped by decisions made in rooms you are not in. The silent-chair position is not the exception of life but its common condition.

Second: audit the compound commitments whose costs reveal themselves only in crises. The discipline is not to refuse commitment — one cannot live that way — but to periodically review the total, because the sum differs from the parts when the environment shifts.

Third, and hardest: do not confuse calm with control. Much of what feels stable is the absence, for now, of a pressure test. A fiscal plan that works at forty-dollar oil is not the same plan at one-hundred-twenty-dollar oil. What breaks in the latter case is not the plan. It is the assumption that the former conditions were permanent.
