The Cancelled Flight

Three African states revoked overflight permits for Taiwan's president on the same day, hours before he was to depart. Eswatini was the destination; airspace was the weapon. Beijing has now demonstrated that the One-China policy can be invoked at altitude — and that the price of crossing a sovereign sky can be set in Beijing rather than Antananarivo, Port Louis, or Victoria.

From the Desk of Statecraft·Filed Taipei & Beijing·25 April 2026

Taipei —On 21 April, hours before President Lai Ching-te was scheduled to depart for the coronation anniversary of King Mswati III in Eswatini — Taiwan's last diplomatic ally on the African continent — Madagascar, Mauritius, and the Seychelles each revoked the overflight permits previously issued for the presidential aircraft. The three rescissions arrived inside the same news cycle. None of the three countries is large; together they form an arc of airspace through which a flight from Taipei to Mbabane must pass. Taiwan's foreign ministry announced the cancellation later that afternoon, naming the source of pressure plainly: "the Chinese authorities, including economic coercion." A special envoy will attend in Lai's place. The trip will not happen.

Read the instrument. The blockade is not naval, not aerial in the kinetic sense, not declared. It is a coordinated revocation of routine sovereign permissions, executed simultaneously across three independent jurisdictions. Beijing did not need to fire anything. It needed only to call in receivables: debt relief held over Madagascar, infrastructure financing extended to Mauritius, the implicit terms of belt-and-road inclusion across the Indian Ocean rim. Each of the three governments faced a choice between issuing a routine permit and forfeiting concessions worth several years of fiscal headroom. Each chose airspace.

"The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting." — Sun Tzu. An aircraft that does not fly because three governments simultaneously withdrew their permission is a perfect expression of the maxim. There is no battle. There is also no destination.

The encirclement, route by route.

The Chinese strategic vocabulary calls this weiqi: the patient placement of stones around a position until the position has no liberty left. The discipline is structural, not tactical. Taiwan has now twelve diplomatic allies, most of them small Pacific or Caribbean states; Eswatini is the last in Africa. Each of those twelve is independently surrounded by a perimeter of states that do recognise Beijing — and each of those perimeter states is independently exposed to Chinese debt, port concessions, fishing-fleet permissions, vaccine deliveries, or some combination thereof. The 21 April move was not the first stone. It was the stone that revealed how many had already been placed.

Mearsheimer reads regional hegemony as the structural goal of any rising great power, and his framework predicts exactly this kind of escalation in the gray zone short of war. Beijing is converting commercial leverage into diplomatic instruments at the speed at which the receivables come due. The Indian Ocean rim — Madagascar, Mauritius, Seychelles, and behind them Mozambique, Tanzania, the Maldives — has been a Chinese investment field for fifteen years. The 21 April action is the first time those investments have been redeemed, in cash, for a single political deliverable.

Lee Kuan Yew's arithmetic.

Lee Kuan Yew used to say that small states have only two options when a great power demands their alignment: take the demand and survive, or refuse it and demonstrate independence at a cost the small state has to be willing to absorb. Madagascar's external debt to China is roughly a billion dollars; Mauritius runs a current-account deficit that Chinese tourism and Chinese-financed infrastructure mitigate; Seychelles has accepted Chinese coast-guard cooperation in waters where its own enforcement capacity is thin. The arithmetic for each, on 20 April, was not difficult. Issuing the overflight permit costs nothing administratively and several years of concessions in Beijing's books. Refusing it costs the inverse.

What changed on 21 April was not the arithmetic; it was the public coordination. Three identical decisions delivered inside one news cycle is the signature of network coordination — what Catherine Austin Fitts calls a "switchboard" move, in which the routing is invisible and the synchronisation is the proof. The countries did not need to agree among themselves. They needed only to receive the same call from the same creditor on the same morning. The pattern is observable; the mechanism is inferable; the attribution is what Beijing has been content to leave ambiguous, because ambiguity is part of the deterrent.

What Taipei cannot retaliate against.

The asymmetry of the move is its design. Taiwan cannot reciprocate because Taiwan has nothing comparable to revoke. It can issue a démarche; it has. It can lobby Washington for a stronger statement; the State Department response on 22 April was muted. It can use the moment to publicise the pattern, and it has — the KMT and TPP joined the DPP in a rare cross-partisan resolution condemning the coercion, the first such alignment under the Lai presidency. Inside Taiwan the move tightened the political consensus. Outside Taiwan it widened the gap between recognition and capability.

Greene's Law 31 — control the options: get others to play with the cards you deal — describes the Beijing posture more precisely than any framing about red lines. Taiwan was not denied a destination; it was denied a route. Lai could have flown by the long way around — over the Atlantic — and the cost would have been visible enough to function as the answer Beijing wanted reported. The choice between humiliating routing and cancellation was the choice Beijing wrote. Taipei picked the less embarrassing of two embarrassments. Both were inside the frame.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the analysis. Structural: a creditor with fifteen years of accumulated leverage across the Indian Ocean rim was always going to redeem some of it for a political deliverable; the timing of that redemption is set in Beijing, not Taipei. Coordination: three independent governments do not synchronise to the hour without a switchboard, and the switchboard's signature is the simultaneity itself. Tactical follows from both — the airspace permit is the cheapest instrument that produces the most visible humiliation per dollar of leverage burned. What is not load-bearing today is consciousness-state. Lai is a careful operator; the move was not aimed at his personality. It was aimed at the category his office occupies.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Fifteen years of accumulated Chinese leverage across the Indian Ocean rim — debt, infrastructure, fisheries cooperation, vaccine flows — meeting one political deliverable Beijing has decided to redeem. Each of the three African states faced the same arithmetic: issue a routine permit, or forfeit concessions worth several years of fiscal headroom. The synchronisation across three independent jurisdictions is the structural-and-coordination layers in plain sight. Taiwan has nothing of comparable weight to offer in counter-bid, so the asymmetry decides the outcome before the day begins.

What's not driving this: Eswatini's status. The destination is largely incidental. Beijing was not trying to detach Eswatini; it was demonstrating that the route to any of Taiwan's twelve allies can be closed at the choice of the creditor, not the choice of the traveller.

What would break this reading:

  • One of the three African states publicly rescinds the revocation and re-issues the permit before mid-May — the coordination did not hold, and the leverage is shallower than priced.
  • Eswatini switches recognition to Beijing inside Q3 — the move was about Eswatini specifically and the encirclement reading is wrong.
  • The US Senate moves a Taiwan Allies Protection Act with airspace-cooperation provisions and a debt-relief facility for the three states before the August recess — the asymmetry reverses partially and the next attempt becomes more expensive for Beijing.
  • Beijing does not repeat this instrument the next time a Lai trip is scheduled — the 21 April action was an experiment that did not pencil at scale.

Sources used: Sun Tzu on subduing the enemy without fighting, because the move is bloodless by design; Mearsheimer on the structural pressure on a regional hegemon to convert commercial leverage into diplomatic instruments; Lee Kuan Yew on small-state arithmetic, because that arithmetic is exactly what Madagascar, Mauritius, and Seychelles ran on the morning of 20 April; Greene's Law 31 on dealing the cards rather than playing them, for the chosen-between-two-losses structure of Taipei's reply.

What we think — and what the crowd prices.

The kinetic markets stay quiet; the gray-zone instrument quietly extends.

The Comeback Trade

Intel reported $0.29 of non-GAAP earnings against a consensus of $0.02 and the stock closed up 23.6%. AMD rose 13% on no news of its own. The S&P and Nasdaq printed records. The CPU lane is being rerated — but the prices are running ahead of an industry whose customer base has not yet decided whether the AI workload is one box, two boxes, or a different box altogether.

From the Desk of Capital·Filed Santa Clara & New York·25 April 2026

Santa Clara —Intel reported its first quarter on the evening of 23 April. Adjusted earnings per share came in at $0.29 against a consensus of $0.02 — a 14× beat against guidance the company itself set three months ago. Data-centre and AI revenue grew 22% year-on-year. Server-CPU unit growth, which Intel had guided to "slight" in January, was now expected to be "double-digit" for the year. The stock closed Friday at $82.55, up 23.61% on the day, near a record. AMD rose 13.29% on no company news at all. The S&P 500 closed at 7,165.08; the Nasdaq at 24,836.60. Both at all-time highs.

Read the move. The headline says Intel's turnaround is real. The tape says the market has rerated an entire CPU lane — not just Intel — on the implication that AI workloads are pulling general-purpose silicon into the same demand curve that Nvidia has been riding for two years. The "real beneficiary" framing for AMD, repeated through Friday's session, is itself the tell: investors are not buying Intel's execution; they are buying a thesis about the structure of AI compute that says CPUs and GPUs both win, simultaneously, on different lines of the same data-centre BOM. That thesis may be right. It is also the thesis that pays the most people the most money the fastest, which is a separate observation.

"To finish first, you must first finish." — a saying old enough to be a Buffett. Intel's beat is the news; the stock's response is the market's view of the news two layers removed. The two should not be confused.

What the beat says — and does not say.

The beat is real. Intel's data-centre unit posted its second consecutive quarter of double-digit revenue growth; Granite Rapids and Sierra Forest server SKUs are clearing inventory faster than the company guided; the 18A process is shipping at a 65% yield, well above the 50% level analysts had penciled. The Trump administration's August 2025 conversion of $8.9 billion in CHIPS Act grants into a 9.9% federal equity stake — at $20.47 a share, struck near the 52-week low — has now reverted enough that the holding sits roughly four times in the money before the company has shipped a single 14A wafer. The political-economy backdrop is favourable. The execution is improving. None of that is in dispute.

What the beat does not say is that the structural question — does the world need this many CPUs in 2027? — has been answered. It says the customer base in Q1 2026 was buying server CPUs at a rate the sell-side had under-modelled. Charlie Munger's rule applies cleanly here: invert, always invert. Why was the consensus $0.02? Because Intel itself guided breakeven, knowing the market would mark a beat. Why was the actual print $0.29? Because the sandbag was deeper than the analysts modelled around. Why did AMD rise 13% without a print? Because the same model now applies to AMD's coming guide, which the market has decided in advance to disbelieve in the same direction.

The flywheel from the side.

Helen Thompson's framework on the political economy of energy reads onto silicon better than most. Compute is the new energy bottleneck; the flow rate of GPUs and CPUs to data centres is set by power, not by chips. Intel's 18A node coming online is one piece of a flywheel in which Nvidia ships H200s and B200s, hyperscalers absorb them into clusters that need new transformers, transformers need three years of permitting, and the CPUs that orchestrate the GPU calls go in alongside. The market's Friday rerating implicitly priced in two more years of that flywheel — that the inventory build is structural, not cyclical, and that AMD and Intel both benefit from a workload pattern that has not yet stabilised.

Ray Dalio's Big Cycle is a useful corrective. We are mid-cycle in a debt-financed capex boom whose returns are real to the participants and unverified at the system level. Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon are spending a combined $400 billion of capex into 2026, financed by record cash flows and, increasingly, by debt issuance. The boom has the shape of a real one — productivity growth in the firms doing the spending is plausibly there — and the shape of a credit one, in which the marginal dollar of compute is being financed by the marginal dollar of investment-grade bond issuance. Both can be true. Both have been true at every previous capex peak. The question for the bondholder is the same question as for the equity holder: which year's earnings is the spend supposed to clear against?

The concentration story.

Greene's Law 23 — concentrate your forces — describes Intel's posture better than any operational frame. The company has narrowed: divested NAND in 2021, is exiting parts of the foundry roadmap, is funding 18A through a federal stake rather than a syndicated bond, has handed the consumer-PC tail to AMD's mercy. What remains is server CPUs, foundry for high-trust customers (Apple, Microsoft, Pentagon), and the AI accelerator line that has been one quarter from cancellation for three years. The Friday tape rewarded the narrowing. Whether the narrowing translates into a 2027 revenue line that justifies a $400 billion market cap is a different question than whether it justified a 23% one-day move.

The AMD lift on no news is the more interesting capital-structure observation. Lisa Su's company has been carrying a thesis — that MI300 and MI355 displace some part of Nvidia's data-centre share — for eighteen months without it printing in revenue at the speed the multiple required. Friday's move was the market deciding that Intel's beat was sufficient evidence that AMD's beat is also coming, three weeks ahead of AMD's print. That is a reflexive trade, not a fundamental one. It can be right and still be reflexive — the two are not exclusive.

The reading.

Two layers carry it. Structural: AI workloads are pulling general-purpose silicon into the same compute-as-utility demand curve, and the political economy — federal CHIPS equity, foundry sovereignty, hyperscaler capex commitments — is built to keep the flywheel running through 2027 regardless of any single quarter's print. Tactical: Intel sandbagged the guide, the consensus pencilled the sandbag, and the beat-on-a-low-bar architecture rewarded the stock and the lane in one tape. The reading we resist is that this rerating is itself the signal. The signal will be in two prints from now — AMD's late-April number, Nvidia's late-May number — at which point either the demand curve persists, or the rerating begins to unwind on the same reflexivity that drove it. Until then, the comeback trade is a real trade, priced ahead of the verification.

The Brandmauer Inverts

For the first time, both ZDF's Politbarometer and INSA show the AfD ahead of the CDU/CSU. Friedrich Merz, ten months into his chancellorship, has the lowest approval rating of any leader in the twenty-four largest democracies. The "firewall" against the populist right was conceived as a shared establishment commitment to keep one party out. It is now a wall around a party that no longer holds the median voter.

From the Desk of Europe·Filed Berlin & Karlsruhe·25 April 2026

Berlin —In the ZDF Politbarometer fielded 14–16 April, Alternative für Deutschland reached 26% to the CDU/CSU's 25%. The INSA series, fielded 11–14 April, showed AfD at 27% and the Union bloc at 24%. Both polls flipped the rank order of German politics for the first time since the Federal Republic was founded. Chancellor Friedrich Merz's personal approval, in the same survey instrument used across two dozen democracies, sits at 19% satisfied and 76% dissatisfied — the lowest of any of the world's elected leaders. The "Brandmauer," the cross-party firewall that the CDU, SPD, Greens, and FDP committed to in 2024 to keep the AfD out of any governing coalition, was conceived as a defensive instrument. It is now defending a position that has slipped beneath the line it was meant to hold.

Read what changed. The Brandmauer was never a constitutional rule; it was an inter-party convention dependent on the CDU's confidence that it could win without AfD votes. That confidence is the asset that has now eroded. The 113-MP motion to ask the Bundesverfassungsgericht to ban the AfD has not advanced in eight months, and Merz himself has expressed doubts about the proposal. Interior Minister Alexander Dobrindt has done the same. The Cologne administrative court suspended the Bundesamt für Verfassungsschutz's "right-wing extremist" classification in February. Each of these is a separate fact; together they describe an establishment that has decided, in successive small movements, that the AfD is too large to ban and too unpopular to coalition with — leaving only the option of containment, which is precisely the option whose viability has just collapsed in the polling.

"He who knows when he can fight and when he cannot will be victorious." — Sun Tzu. The CDU is in the dilemma of a fighter who has just been told he cannot win the next round, by an opponent he is not allowed to negotiate with. Both routes out are foreclosed. That is not a stable equilibrium; it is the moment before a strategic choice must be admitted.

Why the polls hold.

The conventional reading attributes the AfD lead to grievance: energy prices, migration, the fading post-pandemic recovery. Each of those factors is real and each is partial. The structural reading is sharper. Germany's economy grew 0.2% in 2025; consensus 2026 forecasts cluster between 0.5% and 1.0%. Unemployment sits above 6%. The Sondervermögen — Merz's €500 billion infrastructure and rearmament fund — is increasing borrowing without yet showing on factory floors. The AfD's polling lead is not a protest spike; it is the consolidation of a vote that is no longer trusting the establishment's promise that the slowdown is cyclical. Twelve months of policy-and-no-recovery has produced exactly the read that twelve months of structural pressure usually produces. Personality is the foreground. The drift is the substance.

Lee Kuan Yew's observation on small-state politics — that voters tolerate a great deal as long as they can see the elite-class is also paying — applies here in inverse. German voters can see, in the Sondervermögen, that the elite class is borrowing to spend on its own priorities (rearmament, EU defence, Ukraine) and is asking households to absorb the energy cost in the meantime. The AfD's vote is not a vote for an alternative programme. It is a vote against the implicit settlement under which the elite-class's choices are also the household's bill.

The Brandmauer's three exits.

Three doors are theoretically open. The first is to ban the party — the constitutional path that requires the Bundestag, Bundesrat, or federal government to file a motion at Karlsruhe. Only 113 MPs of 734 have backed the existing motion; few of them are leading figures; Merz himself is sceptical. The constitutional bar is high (the AfD must be shown to actively pursue the elimination of the constitutional order, not merely to oppose it), and the Cologne court ruling has weakened the evidentiary base. Door one is unlikely without a precipitating event the establishment has not yet experienced.

The second door is to coalition with the AfD at the federal level — the move East European parties (FPÖ-ÖVP in Austria, FdI-Lega in Italy) have already taken with their populist counterparts. Inside the CDU this remains taboo, formally; informally, several Landesverband heads in Saxony, Thuringia, and Brandenburg have said quietly that the math after the next state elections may not leave another option. Door two requires the CDU to break a public commitment, which has its own electoral cost. The third door — Merz's stated strategy — is to outlast the AfD by delivering enough on the Sondervermögen, energy, and security that the protest vote returns home. That door requires a recovery that the IMF, Bundesbank, and the major economic institutes do not yet see.

The coordination signature.

Watch the second derivative. Inside the past month, the CDU's general secretary has tested a "more conservative on migration" message; the SPD has floated a more restrictive citizenship reform; Friedrich Merz has spoken approvingly of certain AfD policy positions while reaffirming the no-coalition rule. These are not random. They are the visible signature of an establishment beginning to triangulate against a competitor it cannot defeat directly. Greene's Law 11 — learn to keep people dependent on you — is what the Brandmauer was supposed to do; the more interesting Law for this week is Law 22, surrender to recover: pay the symbolic concession early, on your own terms, before the structural pressure forces a worse one.

Talleyrand's maxim that treason is a matter of dates applies to the German political establishment with the lightness it deserves. Whatever the CDU does inside 2026 to absorb AfD voters short of coalition will be reported, eventually, as the moment the Brandmauer cracked. Whatever they do not do will be reported, on the same timeline, as the moment they refused to. The interesting question is which framing the CDU prefers — and the leadership's behaviour over the past month suggests they are still hoping a third reading is available.

What the reading names.

Two layers carry the analysis. Structural: Germany's growth, energy, and demographic profile have moved enough that the median voter is no longer reachable through a programme that promises a return to the 2010s. Coordination: the Brandmauer is an inter-elite convention, and inter-elite conventions decay when the underlying electoral arithmetic shifts. The tactical layer follows — Merz triangulating on migration, Dobrindt declining the ban motion, Landesverband chairs floating informal AfD cooperation. What we resist is the consciousness-state framing imported from English-language coverage that reads the AfD vote as exclusively cultural reaction. It is partly that. It is more importantly the structural read that twelve months of stagnation has now hardened into a political fact, and the fact is not going to soften before the autumn state elections.

Reading Note

The lenses used.

What's driving this: Twelve months of stagnation in a country whose social contract assumed cyclical recovery. The AfD's lead is not personality-driven; it is the consolidation of a structural protest vote that has stopped trusting the establishment's read of the economy. The Brandmauer was a defensive instrument designed for a CDU that could win the median voter without AfD votes. That CDU no longer reliably exists. The wall now defends a position smaller than itself, which is the configuration in which inter-elite conventions decay — slowly, then suddenly.

What's not driving this: A single migration crisis, energy spike, or scandal. English-language coverage tends to find a triggering event; the German polling shows a slow drift across the entire post-2024 period. Personality is the foreground; structure is the substance.

What would break this reading:

  • The CDU recovers a polling lead over the AfD inside Q3 — the protest vote was thinner than assumed and the Brandmauer is not under structural pressure.

Law of the Day · No. 33

Discover each man's thumbscrew.

Everyone has a weakness, a gap in the castle wall. That weakness is usually an insecurity, an uncontrollable emotion or need; it can also be a small secret pleasure. Either way, once found, it is a thumbscrew you can turn to your advantage.

Today's reading: the day's three stories are not about new ideas. They are about leverage that was already accumulated being redeemed for a specific, visible outcome. Beijing pressed three African states on the same morning — Madagascar's debt, Mauritius's tourism, Seychelles's fisheries — and got a Taiwanese flight cancelled. Intel printed an earnings beat against a guide it had set itself low enough that the rerating was waiting; AMD's 13% lift on no news redeemed the same setup. Twelve months of stagnation in Germany have now redeemed into an AfD polling lead the Brandmauer was not designed for. Each operator turned a thumbscrew that had been quietly tightened over years.

  • A thumbscrew is set long before it is turned. The 21 April airspace move was the redemption of fifteen years of belt-and-road conditional finance, not a new instrument.
  • An earnings beat against a self-set low guide is a thumbscrew on consensus — the analysts who modelled the company's own number become the people who must explain the beat.
  • A polling lead is a thumbscrew on coalition math. Ban or coalesce — the establishment must now choose, and the choice is not free.

The Practitioner's Note

Read the redemption, not the news.

Today's three stories are alike in one respect: each is a story about long-accumulated leverage being redeemed in a single visible move. The headline is the redemption. The story is what was already there. Beijing did not invent airspace coercion on 21 April; it spent fifteen years of debt to make it possible. Intel did not improve operations on 23 April; it cashed a sandbagged guide. The AfD did not win this week; it consolidated twelve months of stagnation into a polling fact.

In your own affairs: when something dramatic happens, ask what was being accumulated quietly that this event has now redeemed. The visible move is the noisiest moment of a longer process. The longer process is the data; the move is the bookmark. Scrolling backward from the move to the accumulation is usually a more honest exercise than predicting the next move forward.

The hard question: what leverage are you accumulating right now whose redemption you have not yet decided when to ask for? And on which counterparts have you already agreed, implicitly, that they may turn the screw on you when they choose?
